</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_roading">Use in roading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobblestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use in roading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cobblestones are typically either set in sand or similar material, or are bound together with <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>. Paving with cobblestones allows a road to be heavily used all year long. It prevents the build-up of <a href="/wiki/Rut_(roads)" title="Rut (roads)">ruts</a> often found in <a href="/wiki/Dirt_road" title="Dirt road">dirt roads</a>. It has the additional advantage of not getting muddy in wet weather or dusty in dry weather. Shod horses are also able to get better traction on stone cobbles, pitches or setts than tarmac or asphalt. The fact that <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a> <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a> <a href="/wiki/Hooves" class="mw-redirect" title="Hooves">hooves</a> and even modern automobiles make a lot of noise when rolling over cobblestone paving might be thought a disadvantage, but it has the advantage of warning pedestrians of their approach. In England, the custom was to strew the cobbles outside the house of a sick or dying person with straw to dampen the sound.
</p><p>Cobblestones set in sand have the environmental advantage of being <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">permeable paving</a>, and of moving rather than cracking with movements in the ground.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_today">Use today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobblestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cobblestones were largely replaced by quarried granite <a href="/wiki/Sett_(paving)" title="Sett (paving)">setts</a> (also known as Belgian block<sup id="cite_ref-belgianblock_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belgianblock-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>) in the nineteenth century. The word cobblestone is often used to describe such treatment. Setts were relatively even and roughly rectangular stones that were laid in regular patterns. They gave a smoother ride for carts than cobbles, although in heavily used sections, such as in yards and the like, the usual practice was to replace the setts by parallel granite slabs set apart by the standard axle length of the time.
</p><p>
In England, it was commonplace since ancient times for flat stones with a flat narrow edge to be set on edge to provide an even paved surface. This was known as a 'pitched' surface and was common all over Britain, as it did not require rounded pebbles. Pitched surfaces predate the use of regularly-sized granite setts by more than a thousand years. Such pitched paving is quite distinct from that formed from rounded stones, although both forms are commonly referred to as 'cobbled' surfaces. Most surviving genuinely old 'cobbled' areas are in reality pitched surfaces. A cobbled area is known as a "causey", "cassay" or "cassie" in <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> (probably from <i>causeway</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ItalianStones.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/ItalianStones.jpg/220px-ItalianStones.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/ItalianStones.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="320" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ItalianStones.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italian cobblestone-covered street in <a href="/wiki/Isolabella,_Turin" title="Isolabella, Turin">Isola Bella</a>. Cobblestones such as these are designed for horses to get a good grip.</div></div></div>
<p>Cobblestoned and "setted" streets gradually gave way to <a href="/wiki/Macadam" title="Macadam">macadam</a> roads, and later to <a href="/wiki/Tarmacadam" title="Tarmacadam">tarmac</a>, and finally to <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">asphalt concrete</a> at the beginning of the 20th century. However, cobble&#173;stones are often retained in historic areas, even for streets with modern vehicular traffic. Many older villages and cities in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> are still paved with cobblestones or pitched.
</p><p>In recent decades, cobblestones have become a popular material for paving newly <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrianised</a> streets in Europe. In this case, the noisy nature of the surface is an advantage as pedestrians can hear approaching vehicles. The visual cues of the cobblestones also clarify that the area is more than just a normal street.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The use of cobblestones/setts is also considered to be a more "upmarket" roadway solution, having been described as "unique and artistic" compared to the normal asphalt road environment.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In older U.S. cities such as <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-belgianblock_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belgianblock-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Castle,_Delaware" title="New Castle, Delaware">New Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland (Maine)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, many of the older streets are paved in cobblestones and setts (mostly setts); however, many such streets have been paved over with asphalt, which can crack and erode away due to heavy traffic, thus revealing the original stone pavement.
</p><p>In some places such as <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon,_Saskatchewan" class="mw-redirect" title="Saskatoon, Saskatchewan">Saskatoon, Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, as late as the 1990s some busy intersections still showed cobblestones through worn down sections of pavement. In Toronto streets using setts were used by streetcar routes and disappeared by the 1980s, but are still found in the <a href="/wiki/Distillery_District" title="Distillery District">Distillery District</a>.
</p><p>Many cities in Latin America, such as <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>; <a href="/wiki/Zacatecas,_Zacatecas" class="mw-redirect" title="Zacatecas, Zacatecas">Zacatecas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guanajuato,_Guanajuato" class="mw-redirect" title="Guanajuato, Guanajuato">Guanajuato</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>; <a href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan" title="Old San Juan">Old San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>; <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vigan" title="Vigan">Vigan</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, are well known for their many cobblestone streets, which are still operational and in good condition. They are still maintained and repaired the traditional manner, by placing and arranging granite stones by hand.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, there are old cobblestone paths with colored <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marbles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a>. The design with three colors (red/limestone, black/limestone, white/marble) has a long tradition in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. The cubes of the old ways are handmade.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_architecture">Use in architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobblestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Classical_School,_Alexander,_NY.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg/220px-Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg/330px-Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg/440px-Alexander_Classical_School%2C_Alexander%2C_NY.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3468" data-file-height="2532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Classical_School,_Alexander,_NY.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Alexander Classical School three-story cobblestone building in Alexander, New York.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone_architecture" title="Cobblestone architecture">Cobblestone architecture</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Finger_Lakes" title="Finger Lakes">Finger Lakes</a> Region of New York State, the retreat of the glaciers during the last ice age left numerous small, rounded cobblestones available for building.  Pre-Civil War architecture in the region made heavy use of cobblestones for walls.  Today, the fewer than 600 remaining cobblestone buildings are prized as historic locations, most of them private homes. They are clustered south of Lake Ontario, between Buffalo and Syracuse. There is also a cluster of cobblestone buildings in the Town of <a href="/wiki/Paris,_Ontario" title="Paris, Ontario">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. In addition to homes, cobblestones were used to build barns, stagecoach taverns, smokehouses, stores, churches, schools, factories, and cemetery markers.
</p><p>The only public cobblestone building in the US is the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Classical_School" title="Alexander Classical School">Alexander Classical School</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Alexander_(village),_New_York" title="Alexander (village), New York">Alexander, New York</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_cycling">Use in cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobblestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use in cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In cycling road races, cobblestones are used as an additional difficulty for the riders. It requires a certain skill to ride cobblestones efficiently, without falling or getting a flat tire. <a href="/wiki/Tour_of_Flanders" title="Tour of Flanders">Tour of Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris%E2%80%93Roubaix" title="Paris–Roubaix">Paris–Roubaix</a> are notable <a href="/wiki/Cobbled_classics" title="Cobbled classics">cobbled classics</a>.
</p>
