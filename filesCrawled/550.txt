<p><b>British Asians</b> (also referred to as <b>South Asians in the United Kingdom</b>, <b>Asian British people</b> or <b>Asian Britons</b>) are persons of South Asian descent who reside in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> usage, the term <i>Asian</i> usually refers to people who trace their ancestry to <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> in particular (former <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Ceylon" title="British Ceylon">Ceylon</a>); i.e. modern countries of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>. 
</p><p>Immigration of small numbers of <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_ethnic_groups" title="South Asian ethnic groups">South Asians</a> to England began with the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> in the 17th century. Indians came to Britain, for educational or economic reasons, during the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>, with most returning to India after a few months or years,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and in greater numbers as the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a> led to <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">the partition of 1947</a>, eventually creating the separate countries of India, Pakistan and Bangladesh.
</p><p>The most significant wave of Asian immigration to and settlement in the United Kingdom came following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and the independence of Pakistan, India, Sri Lanka and later Bangladesh, especially during the 1950s and 1960s. An influx of Asian immigrants also took place following the <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Asians_from_Uganda" title="Expulsion of Asians from Uganda">expulsion</a> or flight of <a href="/wiki/Indian_diaspora_in_East_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian diaspora in East Africa">Indian communities</a> (then holders of <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passports</a>) from the newly independent <a href="/wiki/Indians_in_Uganda" title="Indians in Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indians_in_Kenya" title="Indians in Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indians_in_Tanzania" title="Indians in Tanzania">Tanzania</a> in the early 1970s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Britain, the word "Asian" usually refers specifically to people of <a href="/wiki/South_Asian" class="mw-redirect" title="South Asian">South Asian</a> ancestry (<a href="/wiki/British_Pakistanis" title="British Pakistanis">Pakistanis</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Indian" title="British Indian">Indians</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Bangladeshi" title="British Bangladeshi">Bangladeshis</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Sri_Lankans" title="British Sri Lankans">Sri Lankans</a>). This usage contrasts to that in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where it is used to refer to people of <a href="/wiki/East_Asian" class="mw-redirect" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> origin.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BSA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSA-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Sociological_Association" title="British Sociological Association">British Sociological Association</a>'s guidelines on equality and diversity suggest that "South Asian" is more precise than "Asian", and that the latter should not be used where there is a risk of it conflating South Asians with people from elsewhere in Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-BSA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSA-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_1991" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 1991">United Kingdom Census 1991</a> was the first to include a question on ethnicity (apart from in Northern Ireland, where the question was not asked until 2001). The question had tick-boxes for "Indian", "Pakistani" and "Bangladeshi". There was also a tick box, as well as a general "Any other ethnic group (please describe)" option for those not wishing to identify with any of the pre-set tick boxes. For the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001 Census</a>, in England and Wales, "Indian", "Pakistani" and "Bangladeshi" and "Any other Asian background (please write in)" options were grouped under an "Asian or Asian British" heading, with appearing under a separate heading. In Scotland, all of these tick-boxes were grouped together under an "Asian, Asian Scottish or Asian British " heading, and in Northern Ireland no broad headings were used, just tick-boxes for each of the Asian groups.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a> questionnaire was more consistent with regard to the grouping of Asian ethnicities, such that Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Chinese and any other Asian background options appeared under a broad "Asian/Asian British" ("Asian, Asian Scottish or Asian British" in Scotland) heading in all parts of the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography_and_religion">Demography and religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demography and religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg/220px-British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg/330px-British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg/440px-British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="652" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Asian_professionals_at_a_networking_event_in_the_City_of_London.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British Asian professionals at a networking event in the City of London</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 UK Census</a> recorded 1,451,862 residents of Indian, 1,174,983 of Pakistani and 451,529 of Bangladeshi ethnicity, making a total South Asian population of 3,078,374 (4.9 per cent of the total population), excluding other Asian groups and people of mixed ethnicity.<sup id="cite_ref-2011census_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011census-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>South Asian ethnic groups mostly originate from a few select places in South Asia, these are known as place of origins. British Indians tend to originate mainly from the two Indian States, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Evidence from Bradford and Birmingham have shown, Pakistanis originate largely from the <a href="/wiki/Mirpur_District" title="Mirpur District">Mirpur District</a> in <a href="/wiki/Azad_Kashmir" title="Azad Kashmir">Azad Kashmir</a>. The second largest ethnic group of British Pakistanis are the Punjabi people, largely from <a href="/wiki/Attock_District" title="Attock District">Attock District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(Pakistan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (Pakistan)">Punjab</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Pashtuns" title="Pashtuns">Pashtuns</a> and other ethnic groups from the districts of <a href="/wiki/Nowshera,_Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa" class="mw-redirect" title="Nowshera, Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa">Nowshera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peshwar" class="mw-redirect" title="Peshwar">Peshwar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghazi,_NWFP" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghazi, NWFP">Ghazi</a> in province of <a href="/wiki/Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa" class="mw-redirect" title="Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber-Pakhtunkhwa</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Waltham_Forest" title="London Borough of Waltham Forest">London Borough of Waltham Forest</a> there are substantial numbers of Pakistani people originating from <a href="/wiki/Jhelum" title="Jhelum">Jhelum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(Pakistan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (Pakistan)">Punjab</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Studies have shown 95 per cent of Bangladeshis originate from the <a href="/wiki/Sylhet_Division" title="Sylhet Division">Sylhet Division</a>, located in the Northeastern part of Bangladesh. Districts include <a href="/wiki/Sylhet_District" title="Sylhet District">Sylhet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habiganj_District" title="Habiganj District">Habiganj</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moulvibazar_District" title="Moulvibazar District">Moulvibazar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sunamganj_District" title="Sunamganj District">Sunamganj</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-95percent_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95percent-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-spitafields_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spitafields-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Tower_Hamlets" class="mw-redirect" title="Tower Hamlets">Tower Hamlets</a>, people have origins mainly from <a href="/wiki/Jagannathpur_Upazila" title="Jagannathpur Upazila">Jagannathpur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beanibazar_Upazila" title="Beanibazar Upazila">Beanibazar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishwanath_Upazila" title="Bishwanath Upazila">Bishwanath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sylhet_Sadar" class="mw-redirect" title="Sylhet Sadar">Sylhet Sadar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-transnational_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transnational-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Due to a growing sense of affiliation with Britain, many third generation South Asians chose to not mark "Asian or British Asian" and instead marked "British Asian" in the "Other Asian" write in section.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardener_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardener-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg/220px-Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg/330px-Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg/440px-Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="682" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Members_of_the_Asian_community_with_David_Cameron.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Members of the Asian community with the then prime minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> in 2014</div></div></div>
<p>The language spoken by Indians are, <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kutchi_language" title="Kutchi language">Kutchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malayalam_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Malayalam language">Malayalam</a>. People from Pakistan speak <a href="/wiki/Urdu_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potwari_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Potwari language">Mirpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindko_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindko language">Hindko</a> (dialects of Punjabi), <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_language" title="Sindhi language">Sindhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashmiri_language" title="Kashmiri language">Kashmiri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pashto_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seraiki_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Seraiki language">Seraiki</a>. Gujaratis who emigrated from India and East Africa speak <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kutchi_language" title="Kutchi language">Kutchi</a> (a dialect of Sindhi), while a sizeable number of <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_Muslims" title="Gujarati Muslims">Gujarati Muslims</a> speak <a href="/wiki/Urdu_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a> for religious and cultural reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Bangladeshis speak <a href="/wiki/Sylheti_language" title="Sylheti language">Sylheti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>. People from Sri Lanka speak <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinhala_language" title="Sinhala language">Sinhala</a>. Those who speak dialects mainly refer their language to the main language, for example Sylheti speakers say they speak Bengali or Mirpuri speakers say they speak Punjabi. The reason for this is because they do not expect outsiders to be well informed about dialects.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The unemployment rate among Indian men was only slightly higher than that for White British or White Irish men, 7 per cent compared with 5 per cent for the other two groups. On the other hand, Pakistanis have higher unemployment rates of 13-14%, and Bangladeshis have one of the highest rates, around 23%.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Some surveys also revealed the Indian unemployment rate to be 6-7%<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Persons of Indian or mixed Indian origin are more likely than <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">White British</a> to have university degrees, whereas Pakistanis and Bangladeshis are less likely.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> With the exception of Bangladeshi women, every other group of South Asians, have higher attendance at university than the national average.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> GCSE pass rates have been rising for all South Asians.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been three waves of migration of Hindus in the United Kingdom.The first wave was before India's independence in 1947. In the early 1950s the Conservative Health Minister, <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">Enoch Powell</a> recruited a large number of doctors from the Indian sub-continent. The second wave occurred in the 1970s mainly from <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>. The later communities included those from <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>. The last wave of migration began in the 1990s and included Tamil refugees from Sri Lanka and professionals including doctors and software engineers from India.
</p><p>British Pakistanis and Bangladeshis tend to be religiously homogeneous, with Muslims accounting for 92% of each group while their counterparts of Indian and Sri Lankan origin are more religiously diverse, with 55% Hindus, 29% Sikhs, and 15% Muslims. British Gujaratis are predominantly Hindu, belonging to various caste organizations, with large minorities of <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_Muslims" title="Gujarati Muslims">Muslims</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jains</a>, and smaller numbers of Christians and <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoroastrians">Zoroastrians</a>. Notable religious buildings are the <a href="/wiki/East_London_Mosque" title="East London Mosque">East London Mosque</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Central_Mosque" title="London Central Mosque">London Central Mosque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Central_Mosque" title="Birmingham Central Mosque">Birmingham Central Mosque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baitul_Futuh" class="mw-redirect" title="Baitul Futuh">Baitul Futuh</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAPS_Shri_Swaminarayan_Mandir_London" title="BAPS Shri Swaminarayan Mandir London">BAPS Shri Swaminarayan Mandir London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Lakshmi_Narayan_Hindu_Temple" title="Bradford Lakshmi Narayan Hindu Temple">Bradford Lakshmi Narayan Hindu Temple</a>, Shikharbandi Jain Derasar in <a href="/wiki/Potters_Bar" title="Potters Bar">Potters Bar</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha" title="Gurdwara Sri Guru Singh Sabha">Gurdwara Sri Guru Singh Sabha</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southall" title="Southall">Southall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guru_Nanak_Darbar_Gurdwara" title="Guru Nanak Darbar Gurdwara">Guru Nanak Darbar Gurdwara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gravesend" title="Gravesend">Gravesend</a>.
</p><p>The publication of <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Satanic_Verses" title="The Satanic Verses">The Satanic Verses</a></i> in 1988 caused <a href="/wiki/The_Satanic_Verses_controversy" title="The Satanic Verses controversy">major controversy</a>. Muslims condemned the book for <a href="/wiki/Islam_and_blasphemy" title="Islam and blasphemy">blasphemy</a>. On 2 December 1988 the book was publicly burned at a demonstration in <a href="/wiki/Bolton" title="Bolton">Bolton</a> attended by 7,000 Muslims, followed by a similar demonstration and book-burning in <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> on 14 January 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 1989 <a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">Ayatollah</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruhollah_Khomeini" title="Ruhollah Khomeini">Ruhollah Khomeini</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Fatw%C4%81" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatwā">fatwā</a> ordering Muslims to kill Rushdie.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Britain is also home of notable Asian religious leaders and scholars. Some of them are <a href="/wiki/Mirza_Masroor_Ahmad" title="Mirza Masroor Ahmad">Mirza Masroor Ahmad</a> (Caliph of the <a href="/wiki/Ahmadiyya" title="Ahmadiyya">Ahmadiyya</a> Community), <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Qayum_(imam)" title="Abdul Qayum (imam)">Sheikh Abdul Qayum</a> (one of the best known scholars in Europe and Chief Imam of <a href="/wiki/East_London_Mosque" title="East London Mosque">East London Mosque</a>), <a href="/wiki/Abu_Yusuf_Riyadh_ul_Haq" title="Abu Yusuf Riyadh ul Haq">Abu Yusuf Riyadh ul Haq</a> (Khateeb of <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Central_Mosque" title="Birmingham Central Mosque">Birmingham Central Mosque</a>), Dr. Mahmudul Hasan (Khateeb of Essex Mosque), Abdur Rahman Madani (Chairman of Global Eid Trust and Chief Imam of Darul Ummah Mosque), <a href="/wiki/Faiz-ul-Aqtab_Siddiqi" title="Faiz-ul-Aqtab Siddiqi">Faiz-ul-Aqtab Siddiqi</a> (principal of <a href="/wiki/Hijaz_College" title="Hijaz College">Hijaz College</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ajmal_Masroor" title="Ajmal Masroor">Ajmal Masroor</a> (Imam and Liberal Democrats politician) and <a href="/wiki/Pramukh_Swami_Maharaj" title="Pramukh Swami Maharaj">Pramukh Swami Maharaj</a> (fifth spiritual successor of Hindu <a href="/wiki/Swaminarayan" title="Swaminarayan">Swaminarayan</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_in_Britain">History in Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History in Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_Gandhi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Portrait_Gandhi.jpg/220px-Portrait_Gandhi.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Portrait_Gandhi.jpg/330px-Portrait_Gandhi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Portrait_Gandhi.jpg/440px-Portrait_Gandhi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2024" data-file-height="3040" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Portrait_Gandhi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mohandas_Karamchand_Gandhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi">Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi</a> who studied in England played a pivotal role in ending the British Raj in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a></div></div></div> 
<p>The earliest date at which South Asians settled in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> is not clear.
</p><p>If the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romany</a> (Gypsies) are included, then the earliest arrivals were in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. DNA surveys have linked Romanies to present-day South Asian populations and the <a href="/wiki/Romany_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romany language">Romany language</a> is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan</a> language family. Romanies are believed to have begun travelling westward around 1000 CE, and have mixed with <a href="/wiki/South-west_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South-west Asia">South-west Asian</a> and European populations over many centuries.
</p><p>Romani began arriving in sizeable numbers in parts of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> in the 16th century. The Romani who settled in Britain are known as <a href="/wiki/Romanichal" title="Romanichal">Romanichal</a>.
</p><p>The first educated South Asian to travel to Europe and live in Britain was <a href="/wiki/I%27tisam-ud-Din" title="I&#39;tisam-ud-Din">I'tisam-ud-Din</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bengali_Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali Muslim">Bengali Muslim</a> cleric, <a href="/wiki/Munshi" title="Munshi">munshi</a> and diplomat to the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a> who arrived in 1765 with his servant Muhammad Muqim.<sup id="cite_ref-buckland_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buckland-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He wrote of his experiences and travels in his <a href="/wiki/Farsi" class="mw-redirect" title="Farsi">Persian</a> book, <i>Shigurf-nama-i-Wilayat</i> (or 'Wonder Book of Europe').<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This is also the earliest record of literature by a British Asian.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lascar">Lascar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lascar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> arrived in India in 1498, he opened a direct maritime route between South Asia and Europe. In the following century many South Asians arrived in Europe by sea as sailors, slaves and servants. Trade and English <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracy</a> brought some of these people to Britain and four South Asian men in London answered the call for sailors for the first <a href="/wiki/English_East_India_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="English East India Company">English East India Company</a> fleet to Asia. Their Portuguese names identifies them as mixed-race Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Luso-Asians" title="Luso-Asians">Luso-Asians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 17th century, the East India Company employed thousands of South Asian <i><a href="/wiki/Lascar" title="Lascar">lascars</a></i>, scholars and workers (who were mostly <a href="/wiki/British_Bangladeshi" title="British Bangladeshi">Bengali</a> or <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Islam in the United Kingdom">Muslim</a>) mainly to work on British ships and ports around the world. The first group of South Asians to migrate in notable numbers, in the 18th century, were <i><a href="/wiki/Lascar" title="Lascar">lascars</a></i> (sailors) recruited from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> (largely from the <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> region) to work for the <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">British East India Company</a>, some, despite prejudice and a language barrier, settled down, often forcibly after ill treatment and being abandoned by ship masters. Many were forced into poverty and starved.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160307001450/http://www.learningzone.cityoflondon.gov.uk/schoolmate/Bal/sm_bal_timeline_detail.asp?ID=24">[2]</a><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nijhar_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nijhar-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Letters to newspapers in 1785 talked of “the number of miserable objects, Lascars, … shivering and starving in the streets”.<sup id="cite_ref-learningzone.cityoflondon.gov.uk_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learningzone.cityoflondon.gov.uk-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Some lascars took British wives, and some converted to Anglican Christianity (at least nominally) in order to marry,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> possibly due to a lack of South Asian women in Britain at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Most Indians during this period would visit or reside in Britain temporarily, returning to India after months or several years, bringing back knowledge about Britain in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>38 lascars were reported arriving in British ports in 1760.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Between 1803 and 1813, there were more than 10,000 lascars from the Indian subcontinent visiting British port cities and towns.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher2004_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher2004-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>140, 154–6, 160–8, 172</span></sup> By 1842, 3,000 lascars visited the UK annually, and by 1855, 12,000 lascars were arriving annually in British ports. In 1873, 3,271 lascars arrived in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Ansari_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ansari-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>35</span></sup> Throughout the early 19th century lascars visited Britain at a rate of 1,000 every year,<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher2004_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher2004-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>140,54–6,60–8,72</span></sup> which increased to a rate of 10,000 to 12,000 every year throughout the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-behal_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-behal-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the majority being <i>lascars</i>, the earliest Muslim communities were found in port towns, found living in barracks, Christian charity homes and hostels.<sup id="cite_ref-Nijhar_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nijhar-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The first and most frequent South Asian travelers to Britain were Christian Indians and those of European-Asian mixed race. For Muslim Indians considerations about how their dietary and religious practices would alienate them from British society were brought into question but these considerations were often outweighed by economic opportunities. Those that stayed often took British names, dress and diet.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Naval cooks also came, many of them from the <a href="/wiki/Sylhet_Division" title="Sylhet Division">Sylhet Division</a> of what is now <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. One of the most famous early <a href="/wiki/Bengali_Muslims" title="Bengali Muslims">Bengali Muslim</a> immigrants to England was <a href="/wiki/Sake_Dean_Mahomet" class="mw-redirect" title="Sake Dean Mahomet">Sake Dean Mahomet</a>, a captain of the British East India Company who in 1810 founded London's first <a href="/wiki/Indian_restaurant" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian restaurant">Indian restaurant</a>, the Hindoostane Coffee House. He is also reputed for introducing <a href="/wiki/Shampoo" title="Shampoo">shampoo</a> and therapeutic massage to the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In 1784 he migrated to Ireland where he fell in love with a woman called Jane Daly. He converted to Anglicanism in order to marry her, as it was illegal at the time for non-Protestants to marry Protestants. They later moved to Brighton.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After reports of lascars starving and suffering from poverty the East India Company responded by making available lodgings for them, but no checks were kept on the boarding houses and barracks they provided. The Lascars were made to live in cramped, dreadful conditions which resulted in the deaths of many each year, with reports of Lascars being locked in cupboards and whipped for misbehavior (by owners) which was reported by the Society for the Protection of Asiatic Sailors (founded in 1814).<sup id="cite_ref-learningzone.cityoflondon.gov.uk_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learningzone.cityoflondon.gov.uk-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1842, the Church Missionary Society reported on the dire ″state of the Lascars in London″<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> it was reported in the winter of 1850, 40 Asian men, also known as 'sons of India', were found dead of cold and hunger on the streets of London. Shortly after these reports evangelical Christians proposed the construction of a charity house and gathered £15,000 pounds in assistance of the Lascars . In 1856 <i>The Strangers' Home for Asiatics, Africans and South Sea Islanders</i> was opened in Commercial Road, Limehouse under the manager Lieutenant-Colonel R. Marsh Hughes.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Navigation_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Navigation Act">Navigation Act</a> of 1660 restricted the employment of non-English sailors to a quarter of the crew on returning East India Company ships. Baptism records in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">East Greenwich</a> suggest that young Indians from the <a href="/wiki/Malabar_Coast" title="Malabar Coast">Malabar Coast</a> were being recruited as servants at the end of the seventeenth century, and records of the EIC also suggest that Indo-Portuguese cooks from <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a> were retained by captains from voyage to voyage.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In 1797, 13 were buried in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_Nicholas" class="mw-redirect" title="St Nicholas">St Nicholas</a> at <a href="/wiki/Deptford" title="Deptford">Deptford</a>.
</p><p>It is estimated 8,000 Indians (a large proportion being lascar sailors) lived in Britain permanently prior to the 1950s. Although, the comparatively few lascars that gained work often opened shops and helped initiate social and political community associations.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Indians were less likely to settle permanently because of wage differentials.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Due to the majority of early South Asian immigrants being <i>lascars</i>, the earliest South Asian communities were found in port towns
</p><p>The small, often transitory presence of Lascars continued into the 1930s, with the <i>Port of London Authority mentioning Lascars in a February 1931 article writing that ''</i>Although appearing so out of place in the East End, they are well able to look after themselves, being regular seamen who came to the Docks time after time and have learnt a little English and know how to buy what they want.''<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1932, the Indian National Congress survey of 'all Indians outside India' estimated that there were 7,128 Indians in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that from 1800 to 1945, 20,000 South Asians emigrated to Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_World_war_II_migration">Postwar World war II migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Postwar World war II migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> and the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, South Asian migration to the UK increased through the 1950s and 1960s from Pakistan (including present-day Bangladesh), India and Sri Lanka (who are all members of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>). Additionally immigrants from former <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> colonies (including <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indo-Caribbeans</a>) were also moving to Britain.
</p><p>Although this immigration was continuous, several distinct phases can be identified:
</p>
<ul><li>Manual workers, mainly from Pakistan, were recruited to fulfill the labour shortage that resulted from World War II. These included <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Indian" title="Anglo-Indian">Anglo-Indians</a> who were recruited to work on the railways as they had done in India.</li>
<li>Workers mainly from the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab region">Punjab region</a> of India and Pakistan arrived in the late 1950s and 1960s. Many worked in the foundries of the <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">English Midlands</a> and a large number worked at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a> in west London. This created an environment to where the next generation of families did not lose their identity as easily. An example would be <a href="/wiki/Southall" title="Southall">Southall</a> which is populated by many <a href="/wiki/Sikhs" title="Sikhs">Sikhs</a>.</li>
<li>During the same time, medical staff from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> were recruited for the newly formed <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a>. These people were targeted as the British had established medical schools in the Indian subcontinent which conformed to the British standards of medical training.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_migration_from_East_Africa">Asian migration from East Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asian migration from East Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Beginning around 1964 <a href="/wiki/Africanization" title="Africanization">Africanization</a> policies in East Africa prompted the arrival of Asians with British passports from Kenya, Uganda and Tanzania. At first these were the people employed in government and administrative roles, but this was expended to include those Asians engaged in commerce. The movement was called the "Exodus".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1972, all South Asians were <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Asians_from_Uganda" title="Expulsion of Asians from Uganda">expelled from Uganda</a> by the controversial figure <a href="/wiki/Idi_Amin" title="Idi Amin">Idi Amin</a>, then president of Uganda. Those holding British passports came to Britain. Many such displaced people who were predominantly of <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarati</a> origins had left behind successful businesses and vast commercial empires in Uganda, but built up their lives all over again in Britain, starting from scratch. Some of these "twice-over" migrants became retailers, while others found suitable employment in white-collar professions.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1962" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962">Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962</a> and <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_1971" title="Immigration Act 1971">Immigration Act 1971</a> largely restricted any further <a href="/wiki/Primary_immigration" title="Primary immigration">primary immigration</a>, although family members of already-settled migrants were still allowed. In addition, much of the subsequent growth in the South Asian community has come from the births of second and third-generation South Asian Britons.
</p><p>British Asians faced discrimination and racism following <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">Enoch Powell</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_Blood_speech" title="Rivers of Blood speech">Rivers of Blood speech</a> and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(UK)" title="National Front (UK)">National Front</a> in the late 1960s. This included overt racism in the form of <a href="/wiki/Paki_bashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Paki bashing">Paki bashing</a>, predominantly from <a href="/wiki/White_power_skinhead" title="White power skinhead">white power skinheads</a>, the National Front, and the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Party" title="British National Party">British National Party</a>, throughout the 1970s and 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Drawing inspiration from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Black power">black power</a> movement, and the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Apartheid_Movement" title="Anti-Apartheid Movement">Anti-Apartheid Movement</a>, young British Asian activists began a number of <a href="/wiki/Anti-racist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-racist">anti-racist</a> Asian youth movements in the 1970s and 1980s, including the Bradford Youth Movement in 1977, the Bangladeshi Youth Movement following the murder of <a href="/wiki/Altab_Ali" class="mw-redirect" title="Altab Ali">Altab Ali</a> in 1978, and the <a href="/wiki/Newham_Monitoring_Project" title="Newham Monitoring Project">Newham Youth Movement</a> following the murder of Akhtar Ali Baig in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic">Economic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, the Centre for Social Markets estimated that British Asian businesses contributed more than £5 billion to GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Many British Asians are regarded as affluent middle-class people.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> British Asians  are celebrated for revolutionizing the corner shop, and energising the British economy which changed Britain's antiquated retail laws forever.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lakshmi_Mittal" title="Lakshmi Mittal">Lakshmi Mittal</a> is one of  Britain's richest men. In 2017, Forbes ranked him as the 56th-richest person in the world with a net worth of US$20.4 billion. He is also the "57th-most powerful person" of the 72 individuals named in <i>Forbes'</i> "Most Powerful People" list for 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004 it was reported that UK Sikhs had the highest percentage of home ownership, at 82%, out of all UK religious communities.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The biggest influence of South Asians on popular British culture has probably been the spread of <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">Indian cuisine</a>, though of the 9,000 Indian restaurants in the UK, most are run by Bangladeshis; their ancestral home was part of British India's Bengal province until <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Bengal_(1947)" title="Partition of Bengal (1947)">partition in 1947</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performing_arts">Performing arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Performing arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg/220px-Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg/330px-Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg/440px-Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asian_Professionals_at_a_Summer_Ball_in_London_dancing_to_Bhangra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Asian Professionals at a Summer Ball in London dancing to Bhangra</div></div></div>
<p>As in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(music)" title="Bhangra (music)">Bhangra</a> music has become popular among many in Britain<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> not only from the works of <a href="/wiki/List_of_British_Asian_people#Music" class="mw-redirect" title="List of British Asian people">British South Asian musicians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Panjabi_MC" title="Panjabi MC">Panjabi MC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swami_(band)" title="Swami (band)">Swami</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rishi_Rich" title="Rishi Rich">Rishi Rich</a> but also incorporated into the works of a number of non-South Asian musicians not only British but including North American artists such as Canadian <a href="/wiki/Shania_Twain" title="Shania Twain">Shania Twain</a>, who created a whole alternate version of her multi-platinum album <a href="/wiki/Up!_(album)" title="Up! (album)">Up!</a> with full Indian instrumentation, produced by South Asian producers <a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_%26_Diamond&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simon &amp; Diamond (page does not exist)">Simon &amp; Diamond</a>. Diamond, better known as <a href="/wiki/DJ_Swami" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Swami">DJ Swami</a> has also collaborated with rapper <a href="/wiki/Pras" title="Pras">Pras</a>, of <a href="/wiki/The_Fugees" class="mw-redirect" title="The Fugees">The Fugees</a>, and his band <a href="/wiki/Swami_(band)" title="Swami (band)">Swami</a> have become one of the most renowned acts in South Asian music history, having had songs in major Hollywood movies and best-selling video games. One of the first artists of South Asian <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> origin to achieve mainstream success was <a href="/wiki/Apache_Indian" title="Apache Indian">Apache Indian</a> who infused reggae and hip hop with Indian popular music to create a sound that transcended genre and found a multicultural audience. He is the only Indian artist to have achieved 7 top forty hits in the National UK charts.  A subsequent wave of "<a href="/wiki/Asian_Underground" title="Asian Underground">Asian Underground</a>" artists went on to blend elements of western underground dance music and the traditional music of their home countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitin_Sawhney" title="Nitin Sawhney">Nitin Sawhney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talvin_Singh" title="Talvin Singh">Talvin Singh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_Dub_Foundation" title="Asian Dub Foundation">Asian Dub Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panjabi_MC" title="Panjabi MC">Panjabi MC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raghav" title="Raghav">Raghav</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rishi_Rich_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="Rishi Rich Project">Rishi Rich Project</a> (featuring Rishi Rich, <a href="/wiki/Jay_Sean" title="Jay Sean">Jay Sean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juggy_D" title="Juggy D">Juggy D</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg/220px-British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg/330px-British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg/440px-British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Asian_musicians_combining_Eastern_and_Western_musical_traditions.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British Asian musicians combining Eastern and Western musical traditions</div></div></div>
<p>The influence of South Asian music has not only been from South Asians living in the UK, but also from some UK artists that were starting using South Asian instruments creating a new sound that was a mixture of sitars and tablas with more rock-based western instruments like drums and guitar.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The films <i><a href="/wiki/East_Is_East_(1999_film)" title="East Is East (1999 film)">East is East</a></i>, Chicken Tikka Masala and <i><a href="/wiki/Bend_It_Like_Beckham" title="Bend It Like Beckham">Bend It Like Beckham</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Television_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Television program">TV shows</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Goodness_Gracious_Me_(TV_%26_radio)" class="mw-redirect" title="Goodness Gracious Me (TV &amp; radio)">Goodness Gracious Me</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kumars" class="mw-redirect" title="The Kumars">The Kumars at No. 42</a></i> have managed to attract large, multi-ethnic audiences. The success and popularity of British Pakistani boxer <a href="/wiki/Amir_Khan_(British_boxer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amir Khan (British boxer)">Amir Khan</a> influenced the revival of <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a> on <a href="/wiki/ITV_Sport" title="ITV Sport">ITV Sport</a>.
</p><p>Although there are roughly double the number of South Asians in the UK today compared to people of African descent, South Asians are less represented in global and British media than any other major group; in the UK there is less than half the amount of South Asians represented in the media than those of African and Caribbean descent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisations_honouring_Asian_achievements">Organisations honouring Asian achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Organisations honouring Asian achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg/220px-Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg/330px-Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg/440px-Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5472" data-file-height="3648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Organisers_and_Hosts_of_the_Asian_Professional_Awards.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Asian Professional Awards from left to right: Onkardeep Singh MBE; <a href="/wiki/Jasvir_Singh_(barrister)" title="Jasvir Singh (barrister)">Jasvir Singh</a> OBE; Sunny &amp; Shay Grewal; Harry Virdee; <a href="/wiki/Param_Singh_(property_developer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Param Singh (property developer)">Param Singh</a> MBE</div></div></div>
<p>With the increasing number of high achievers and trail blazers within the Asian community across a variety of professions, the British Asian community has over the years setup a variety of high-profile Award ceremonies to recognise Asian achievements across the full spectrum of professions and industries. These organisations and ceremonies include: 
</p>
<ul><li>Asian Achievers Awards organised by <a href="/wiki/Asian_Voice" title="Asian Voice">Asian Voice</a> since 2000 with women dominating the nominee list for the first time in 2017 <sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Asian Women of Achievement Awards organised by <a href="/wiki/Pinky_Lilani" title="Pinky Lilani">Pinky Lilani</a> CBE DL since 1999 <sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Asian Legal Awards organised by the Society for Asian Lawyers since 1994 making it one of the oldest Asian awards ceremonies <sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Asian Curry Awards celebrating the best of <a href="/wiki/Asian_cuisine" title="Asian cuisine">Asian restaurants</a> since 2010 <sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Asian_Awards" title="The Asian Awards">The Asian Awards</a> organised by the Lemon Group since 2010 and usually attended by a host of leading celebrities <sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Asian Professional Awards organised by <a href="/wiki/Jasvir_Singh_(barrister)" title="Jasvir Singh (barrister)">Jasvir Singh</a> OBE and <a href="/wiki/Param_Singh_(property_developer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Param Singh (property developer)">Param Singh</a> since 2014 aimed at celebrating success within the City professions <sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArcelorMittal_Orbit,_April_2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg/220px-ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg/330px-ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg/440px-ArcelorMittal_Orbit%2C_April_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArcelorMittal_Orbit,_April_2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/ArcelorMittal_Orbit" title="ArcelorMittal Orbit">ArcelorMittal Orbit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Park,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic Park, London">London Olympic Park</a>, designated by the Indian <a href="/wiki/Anish_Kapoor" title="Anish Kapoor">Anish Kapoor</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anish_Kapoor" title="Anish Kapoor">Anish Kapoor</a> is an Indian-born British sculptor. Born in Mumbai, Kapoor has lived and worked in London since the early 1970s when he moved to study art, first at the Hornsey College of Art and later at the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_College_of_Art_and_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea College of Art and Design">Chelsea College of Art and Design</a>. Kapoor received the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> in 1991.  Born in London and of Asian origin, <a href="/wiki/Shezad_Dawood" title="Shezad Dawood">Shezad Dawood</a> became known for this work in various media in the early 2000s. Also born in London and of Pakistani origin, <a href="/wiki/Haroon_Mirza" title="Haroon Mirza">Haroon Mirza</a> emerged as an artist in the late 2000s. Best known for his sculptural installations that generate sound, Mirza was awarded the Silver Lion for the Most Promising Artist at the 54th <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a> in 2011.
</p><p>Theatre company <a href="/wiki/RIFCO_Arts" title="RIFCO Arts">RIFCO Arts</a> has been producing and touring productions based on the British Asian experience since 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Well-known South Asian writers include <a href="/wiki/H.S._Bhabra" class="mw-redirect" title="H.S. Bhabra">H.S. Bhabra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghulam_Murshid" title="Ghulam Murshid">Ghulam Murshid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tahir_Shah" title="Tahir Shah">Tahir Shah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gurinder_Chadha" title="Gurinder Chadha">Gurinder Chadha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazrin_Choudhury" title="Nazrin Choudhury">Nazrin Choudhury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rekha_Waheed" title="Rekha Waheed">Rekha Waheed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanif_Kureshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanif Kureshi">Hanif Kureshi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monica_Ali" title="Monica Ali">Monica Ali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meera_Syal" title="Meera Syal">Meera Syal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gautam_Malkani" title="Gautam Malkani">Gautam Malkani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bali_Rai" title="Bali Rai">Bali Rai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raman_Mundair" title="Raman Mundair">Raman Mundair</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_and_Interfaith">Charity and Interfaith</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Charity and Interfaith">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a growing number of young British Asians who are making a mark in the charity and interfaith sectors. A recent example is Onkardeep Singh who became the youngest person of South Asian heritage in 2018 to be awarded an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> for his interfaith and voluntary works. <sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/British_Asians_in_association_football" title="British Asians in association football">British Asians in association football</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:247px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg/245px-Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="245" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg/368px-Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg/490px-Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Khan_and_Malignaggi_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Amir_Khan_(boxer)" title="Amir Khan (boxer)">Amir Khan</a> (left), with American boxer <a href="/wiki/Paulie_Malignaggi" title="Paulie Malignaggi">Paulie Malignaggi</a> (right).</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jawaid_Khaliq" title="Jawaid Khaliq">Jawaid Khaliq</a>, the first world champion boxer of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistani</a> origin, was born in <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amir_Khan_(British_boxer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Amir Khan (British boxer)">Amir Khan</a>, the silver medallist at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">2004 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, has become a cultural icon in the UK with TV audiences of up to 8 million watching him fight. Khan represents Britain in <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a> and is the former <a href="/wiki/World_Boxing_Association" title="World Boxing Association">WBA</a> world <a href="/wiki/Light_welterweight" title="Light welterweight">light welterweight</a> champion. The boxer <a href="/wiki/Haider_Ali_(boxer)" title="Haider Ali (boxer)">Haider Ali</a> won the first ever gold medal for Pakistan in boxing at the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth games">commonwealth games</a> in Manchester in 2002 in the <a href="/wiki/Featherweight" title="Featherweight">featherweight</a> division.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Chopra" title="Michael Chopra">Michael Chopra</a> played for the <a href="/wiki/England_national_under-21_football_team" title="England national under-21 football team">England national under-21 football team</a> and became the first <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballer</a> of Indian descent to play and score in the <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a>. In 2006 he scored one of the fastest goals in Premier League history, as Chopra had only been on the pitch for fifteen seconds after coming on as a substitute.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa_F.C." title="Aston Villa F.C.">Aston Villa</a> defender, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Taylor_(footballer)" title="Neil Taylor (footballer)">Neil Taylor</a> is also of Indian descent. Currently in the Premier League, <a href="/wiki/Hamza_Choudhury" title="Hamza Choudhury">Hamza Choudhury</a>, a British Footballer whose parents are of Bangladeshi descent, plays for <a href="/wiki/Leicester_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Leicester City">Leicester City</a>, making him the first player of Bangladeshi descent to play in the Premier League, and has also made appearances for the England Under-21 Team.
</p><p>Just as in South Asia, the most popular sport among the British Asian community is cricket; as much as third of the players of the sport at recreational level are of South Asian descent.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> This has not translated into equal levels of success professionally however, with only 4.2% of cricketers being of British South Asian descent in first-class cricket across the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Regardless, many British South Asians have gone on to represent <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England</a> in cricket internationally. <a href="/wiki/Nasser_Hussain" title="Nasser Hussain">Nasser Hussain</a>, who was the captain of the <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England cricket team</a>, was born in Madras, India.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Other success stories of the past have included <a href="/wiki/Mark_Ramprakash" title="Mark Ramprakash">Mark Ramprakash</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indo-Caribbean</a> descent, <a href="/wiki/Isa_Guha" title="Isa Guha">Isa Guha</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Bengalis" title="Bengalis">Bengali</a> descent, and <a href="/wiki/Monty_Panesar" title="Monty Panesar">Monty Panesar</a>, of Indian Sikh descent. Currently, <a href="/wiki/Moeen_Ali" title="Moeen Ali">Moeen Ali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adil_Rashid" title="Adil Rashid">Adil Rashid</a> are the only players in the England mens squad, both of Pakistani (Mipuri) descent. Ali, affectionately known by fans as 'the beard's that's feared', is currently ranked the 7th best all-rounder in ODI cricket and 8th-best in test cricket worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p><p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrities_in_popular_culture">Celebrities in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Asian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Celebrities in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shazia_Mirza.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Shazia_Mirza.jpg/280px-Shazia_Mirza.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Shazia_Mirza.jpg/420px-Shazia_Mirza.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Shazia_Mirza.jpg/560px-Shazia_Mirza.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shazia_Mirza.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Shazia_Mirza" title="Shazia Mirza">Shazia Mirza</a> is a popular British comedian.</div></div></div>
<p>Early South Asian stars to break into English and Hollywood films include <a href="/wiki/Sabu_Dastagir" title="Sabu Dastagir">Sabu</a>, remembered for his lead roles in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thief_of_Bagdad_(1940_film)" title="The Thief of Bagdad (1940 film)">The Thief of Bagdad</a></i> (1940), <i><a href="/wiki/Jungle_Book_(1942_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jungle Book (1942 film)">Jungle Book</a></i> (1942), and <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Narcissus" title="Black Narcissus">Black Narcissus</a></i> (1947).
</p><p>Since the 1970s, South Asian performers and writers have achieved significant mainstream cultural success. The first South Asian musician to gain wide popularity in the UK and worldwide fame was <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> lead singer <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a>, born Farrokh Bulsara in <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a>, East Africa, to parents of <a href="/wiki/Parsi_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Parsi people">Parsi</a> descent from <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Bombay</a> (now Mumbai). In 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time Asia</a></i> magazine voted him as one of the most influential South <a href="/wiki/Asian_people" title="Asian people">Asians</a> in the past 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-TimeAsia2006_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimeAsia2006-79">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> At around the same time, music producer, composer and songwriter <a href="/wiki/Biddu" title="Biddu">Biddu</a> gained worldwide fame for a number of hit songs, including "<a href="/wiki/Kung_Fu_Fighting" title="Kung Fu Fighting">Kung Fu Fighting</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Douglas" title="Carl Douglas">Carl Douglas</a> and "<a href="/wiki/I_Love_to_Love_(But_My_Baby_Loves_to_Dance)" title="I Love to Love (But My Baby Loves to Dance)">I Love to Love (But My Baby Loves to Dance)</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Tina_Charles_(singer)" title="Tina Charles (singer)">Tina Charles</a>. In the 1990s the South Asian artists who gained mainstream success included <a href="/wiki/Apache_Indian" title="Apache Indian">Apache Indian</a>, whose 1993 single "<a href="/wiki/Boom_Shack-A-Lak" title="Boom Shack-A-Lak">Boom Shack-A-Lak</a>" was used in many <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> movies, and <a href="/wiki/Jas_Mann" title="Jas Mann">Jas Mann</a>, who headed <a href="/wiki/Babylon_Zoo" title="Babylon Zoo">Babylon Zoo</a> and whose 1996 single "<a href="/wiki/Spaceman_(Babylon_Zoo_song)" title="Spaceman (Babylon Zoo song)">Spaceman</a>" set a UK chart record when it sold 418,000 copies in its first week of release.
</p><p>Prominent South Asian actors in the 1980s included <a href="/wiki/Art_Malik" title="Art Malik">Art Malik</a>, for his roles in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jewel_in_the_Crown_(TV_series)" title="The Jewel in the Crown (TV series)">The Jewel in the Crown</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Living_Daylights" title="The Living Daylights">The Living Daylights</a></i>, and Sir <a href="/wiki/Ben_Kingsley" title="Ben Kingsley">Ben Kingsley</a> (born Krishna Pandit Bhanji), one of Britain's most acclaimed and well-known performers. Kingsley is one of few actors to have won all four major motion picture acting awards, receiving <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award">Oscar</a>, <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="BAFTA Award">BAFTA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Award" title="Screen Actors Guild Award">Screen Actors Guild</a> awards throughout his career, including the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> for his performance in <i><a href="/wiki/Gandhi_(film)" title="Gandhi (film)">Gandhi</a></i> (1982).<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The actress <a href="/wiki/Parminder_Nagra" title="Parminder Nagra">Parminder Nagra</a> has a prominent role in the US TV series <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>, and played the lead role in the successful British film <i>Bend It Like Beckham</i> (2002). The actor <a href="/wiki/Naveen_Andrews" title="Naveen Andrews">Naveen Andrews</a> plays the role of <a href="/wiki/Sayid_Jarrah" title="Sayid Jarrah">Sayid Jarrah</a> in the popular US TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_(TV_series)" title="Lost (TV series)">Lost</a></i>, and also had a prominent role in the award-winning film <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_Patient_(film)" title="The English Patient (film)">The English Patient</a></i> (1996). The actor <a href="/wiki/Kunal_Nayyar" title="Kunal Nayyar">Kunal Nayyar</a> plays the character of <a href="/wiki/Raj_Koothrappali" title="Raj Koothrappali">Raj Koothrappali</a> in the popular US sitcom, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bang_Theory" title="The Big Bang Theory">The Big Bang Theory</a></i>. Long-running British soap operas such as <i><a href="/wiki/Coronation_Street" title="Coronation Street">Coronation Street</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/EastEnders" title="EastEnders">EastEnders</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Emmerdale" title="Emmerdale">Emmerdale</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hollyoaks" title="Hollyoaks">Hollyoaks</a></i> have all had a number of South Asian characters.
</p><p>The comedians <a href="/wiki/Sanjeev_Bhaskar" title="Sanjeev Bhaskar">Sanjeev Bhaskar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meera_Syal" title="Meera Syal">Meera Syal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papa_CJ" title="Papa CJ">Papa CJ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shazia_Mirza" title="Shazia Mirza">Shazia Mirza</a> are all well-recognised figures in British <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>. The presenter and match maker of the BBC marriage arranging show <i><a href="/wiki/Arrange_Me_a_Marriage" title="Arrange Me a Marriage">Arrange Me a Marriage</a></i> is a South Asian-Scot <a href="/wiki/Aneela_Rahman" class="mw-redirect" title="Aneela Rahman">Aneela Rahman</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hardeep_Singh_Kohli" title="Hardeep Singh Kohli">Hardeep Singh Kohli</a> is a presenter, reporter and comedian on British television and radio. British Bangladeshi, Pakistani and Indian contestants have appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Apprentice_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Apprentice (UK)">The Apprentice</a></i> including <a href="/wiki/Syed_Ahmed_(businessman)" title="Syed Ahmed (businessman)">Syed Ahmed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tre_Azam" class="mw-redirect" title="Tre Azam">Tre Azam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lohit_Kalburgi" class="mw-redirect" title="Lohit Kalburgi">Lohit Kalburgi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghazal_Asif" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghazal Asif">Ghazal Asif</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shazia_Wahab" class="mw-redirect" title="Shazia Wahab">Shazia Wahab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sara_Dhada" class="mw-redirect" title="Sara Dhada">Sara Dhada</a>, and most notably <a href="/wiki/Saira_Khan" title="Saira Khan">Saira Khan</a>, who is now a British TV presenter. The broadcasters <a href="/wiki/Daljit_Dhaliwal" title="Daljit Dhaliwal">Daljit Dhaliwal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishnan_Guru-Murthy" title="Krishnan Guru-Murthy">Krishnan Guru-Murthy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samira_Ahmed" title="Samira Ahmed">Samira Ahmed</a> are known for working on <i><a href="/wiki/Channel_4_News" title="Channel 4 News">Channel 4 News</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:217px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg/215px-Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg" decoding="async" width="215" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg/323px-Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg/430px-Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1127" data-file-height="1076" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Save_The_World_Awards_2009_show24_-_Suleman_Mirza_and_Madhu_Singh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The award-winning dance act <a href="/wiki/Signature_(dance_group)" title="Signature (dance group)">Signature</a> involved a <a href="/wiki/British_Pakistani" class="mw-redirect" title="British Pakistani">British Pakistani</a> and a <a href="/wiki/British_Indian" title="British Indian">British Indian</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The term South Asian was given the tag "Br-Asian" around the turn of the millennium by media businessmen Moiz Vas and Nav Sagoo. Vas and Sagoo were responsible for the South Asian Music awards which aired on ITV1 in the UK. Sagoo conceived the Br-Asian stage at Glastonbury Festival in 2004 and 2005 which featured acts such as Rishi Rich, Jay Sean, Swami, Raghav and Pentagram.
</p><p>In 2008, in the second season of reality television <i><a href="/wiki/Britain%27s_Got_Talent" title="Britain&#39;s Got Talent">Britain's Got Talent</a></i>, one of the country's most successful <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a> shows, the South Asian dance duo <i><a href="/wiki/Signature_(dance_group)" title="Signature (dance group)">Signature</a></i>, consisting of Suleman Mirza (a British Pakistani) and Madhu Singh (a <a href="/wiki/British_Indian" title="British Indian">British Indian</a>) performing a fusion of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(music)" title="Bhangra (music)">Bhangra</a> music and dance styles, came second on the show. The most successful South Asian musician in 2008 was the <a href="/wiki/British_Tamils" class="mw-redirect" title="British Tamils">British Tamil</a> artist <a href="/wiki/M.I.A._(artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="M.I.A. (artist)">M.I.A.</a>, who was nominated for two <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Awards</a> for her single "<a href="/wiki/Paper_Planes_(M.I.A._song)" title="Paper Planes (M.I.A. song)">Paper Planes</a>", and has been nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Academy Award for Best Original Score</a> for "<a href="/wiki/O..._Saya" title="O... Saya">O... Saya</a>", from the <a href="/wiki/Slumdog_Millionaire_(soundtrack)" class="mw-redirect" title="Slumdog Millionaire (soundtrack)"><i>Slumdog Millionaire</i> soundtrack</a>. The actor <a href="/wiki/Dev_Patel" title="Dev Patel">Dev Patel</a>, who played the role of <a href="/wiki/Anwar_Kharral" title="Anwar Kharral">Anwar Kharral</a> in the teen drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Skins_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Skins (TV series)">Skins</a></i>, also played the leading role in <a href="/wiki/Danny_Boyle" title="Danny Boyle">Danny Boyle</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Slumdog_Millionaire" title="Slumdog Millionaire">Slumdog Millionaire</a></i>, for which he received several awards and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/62nd_British_Academy_Film_Awards" title="62nd British Academy Film Awards">2009 BAFTA Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Actor_in_a_Leading_Role" title="BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Leading Role">Best Leading Actor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Mumzy_Stranger" title="Mumzy Stranger">Mumzy Stranger</a>, an R&amp;B and hip-hop music artist, became the first British Bangladeshi to release a music single, called "One More Dance".<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In October 2009, Jay Sean's single "<a href="/wiki/Down_(Jay_Sean_song)" title="Down (Jay Sean song)">Down</a>" reached the #1 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> and sold four million copies in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-riaa_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-riaa-84">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> making him the first South Asian-origin solo artist and "the first UK <a href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">Urban</a> act to top <i>Billboard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s Hot 100,"<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> "the most successful male UK urban artist in US chart history,"<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> and the most successful <a href="/wiki/List_of_songs_by_British_artists_which_reached_number-one_on_the_Hot_100_(USA)" class="mw-redirect" title="List of songs by British artists which reached number-one on the Hot 100 (USA)">British male artist in the US charts</a> since <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> in 1997. A new generation of British Asian musicians have followed, such as Shizzio, 21 Perspective and Raxstar. In the early 2010s, Asian boy band members, <a href="/wiki/Siva_Kaneswaran" class="mw-redirect" title="Siva Kaneswaran">Siva Kaneswaran</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Wanted" title="The Wanted">The Wanted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zayn_Malik" title="Zayn Malik">Zayn Malik</a> of <a href="/wiki/One_Direction" title="One Direction">One Direction</a>, have gained considerable mainstream popularity worldwide; The Wanted reached #3 on the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 with "<a href="/wiki/Glad_You_Came" title="Glad You Came">Glad You Came</a>" while One Direction topped the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Up_All_Night_(One_Direction_album)" title="Up All Night (One Direction album)">Up All Night</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Humza_Arshad" title="Humza Arshad">Humza Arshad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ali_Shahalom" title="Ali Shahalom">Ali Shahalom</a> are well known British Asian Comedians for their YouTube careers which normally consists of stereotyping British Pakistani and Bangladeshi Muslim Culture. In 2011, one of the <a href="/wiki/Humza_Arshad" title="Humza Arshad">Humza Arshad</a>'s video was the seventh most viewed on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sky_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-88">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thenational_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thenational-89">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
