<p><br />
Fixed <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a> were adopted in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> from the 15th century onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Until this point, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> had a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> naming system.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1292, 48 per cent of Welsh names were patronymics, and in some parishes over 70 per cent. Other names were derived from <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a>, (rarely) occupational names, and a few non-hereditary personal names.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Patronymic names changed from generation to generation, with a person's <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptismal</a> name being linked by <i>ap</i>, <i>ab</i> (son of) or <i>ferch</i> (daughter of) to the father's baptismal name.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For example, Evan son of Thomas would be known as Evan (ap) Thomas; Evan's son, John, would be John (ab) Evan; and John's son Rees would be Rees (ap) John.
</p><p>Patronymics could be extended with names of grandfathers and earlier ancestors, to perhaps the seventh generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Names such as Llewelyn ap Dafydd ab Ieuan ap Gruffudd ap Meredydd were not uncommon.  Those extended patronymics were essentially a <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogical history</a> of the person's ancestry along the male line. The <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Encyclopedia of Wales">Encyclopedia of Wales</a> surmises that the system arose from <a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a>, which made it essential for people to know how people were descended from an ancestor.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> These laws were decaying by the later Middle Ages, and the patronymic system was gradually replaced by fixed surnames, although the use of patronymic names continued up until the early 19th century in some rural areas. In the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> surnames became hereditary amongst the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_peers_and_baronets" title="Welsh peers and baronets">Welsh gentry</a>, and the custom spread slowly amongst commoners.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Areas where <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> influence was strong abandoned patronymics earlier, as did town families and the wealthy.
</p><p>New surnames retained the "ap" in several cases, mainly in reduced form at the start of the surname, as in Upjohn (from ap John), Powell (from ap Hywel), Price (from ap Rhys), Pritchard (from ap Richard), and Bowen (from ab Owen). Alternatively, the <i>ap</i> was simply dropped entirely. The most common surnames in modern Wales result from adding an <i>s</i> to the end of the name, as in Jones, Roberts and Edwards.  Patronymic surnames with the short -s form are recorded in various parts of England dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, and the Welsh practice was presumably in imitation of this.  As most Welsh surnames, however, are derived from patronymics, and often based on a small set of first names, Welsh communities are full of families bearing the same surnames, but who are completely unrelated; it cannot be assumed that two people named <a href="/wiki/Jones_(surname)" title="Jones (surname)">Jones</a>, even in the same village, must have inherited the surname from a common ancestor. It is not uncommon for five or more of the starting fifteen for the Wales international rugby team to be named Jones (all of the following played in the same period and are not immediately related to any of the others: <a href="/wiki/Adam_Jones_(rugby_union,_born_1981)" title="Adam Jones (rugby union, born 1981)">Adam R. Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Jones" title="Dafydd Jones">Dafydd Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Jones" title="Ryan Jones">Ryan Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jones_(rugby_player)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stephen Jones (rugby player)">Stephen Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Jones_(rugby_player_born_1979)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark Jones (rugby player born 1979)">Mark Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Jones_(rugby_union,_born_1980)" title="Adam Jones (rugby union, born 1980)">Adam M. Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alun_Wyn_Jones" title="Alun Wyn Jones">Alun Wyn Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Jones_(rugby_player)" title="Duncan Jones (rugby player)">Duncan Jones</a>). The prevalence of names such as Jones, Williams and Thomas brought a need for further distinction and in the 19th century a trend started for double surnames, created by prefixing the name of a house, parish or the mother's surname, as in "Cynddylan Jones". A hyphen was sometimes later introduced, for example "<a href="/wiki/Nash-Williams" title="Nash-Williams">Nash-Williams</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The stock of Welsh surnames is very small, which is partly attributable to the reduction in the variety of baptismal names after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The typical Welsh surnames Evans, Jones, Williams, Davies and Thomas were all found in the top ten surnames recorded in England and Wales in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An analysis of the geography of Welsh surnames commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Government">Welsh Assembly Government</a> found that 718,000 people in Wales, nearly 35% of the Welsh population, have a family name of Welsh origin, compared with 5.3% in the rest of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, 4.7% in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, 4.1% in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and 3.8% in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. A total of 16.3 million people in the countries studied had a name of Welsh origin.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_revival_of_patronymics">Modern revival of patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern revival of patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the vast majority of Welsh surnames are now family names as in the rest of the UK, there has been a limited revival of <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> in modern Wales, especially among Welsh speakers. Alternatively, given surnames are used, as in the case of the folk singer and political figure <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Iwan" title="Dafydd Iwan">Dafydd Iwan</a> (Dafydd Iwan Jones), opera singer <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Terfel" title="Bryn Terfel">Bryn Terfel</a> (Bryn Terfel Jones), classical singer <a href="/wiki/Sh%C3%A2n_Cothi" title="Shân Cothi">Shân Cothi</a>, and the late actress <a href="/wiki/Myfanwy_Talog" title="Myfanwy Talog">Myfanwy Talog</a> (Myfanwy Talog Williams).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
