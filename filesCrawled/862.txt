<p>The <b>ceremonial counties</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> also referred to as the <b>lieutenancy areas of England</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> are areas of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> to which a <a href="/wiki/Lord-lieutenant" title="Lord-lieutenant">lord-lieutenant</a> is appointed. Legally, the areas in England, as well as in Wales and Scotland, are defined by the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancies_Act_1997" title="Lieutenancies Act 1997">Lieutenancies Act 1997</a> as "counties and areas for the purposes of the lieutenancies in Great Britain", in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">areas used for local government</a>. They are also informally known as "geographic counties",<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as often representing more permanent features of English geography, and to distinguish them from other types of <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">counties of England</a> which have a present-day administrative function.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceremonial_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg/220px-English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg/330px-English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg/440px-English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_geographical_counties_1889_with_ridings.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ceremonial counties before the creation of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> in 1965 (showing <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">counties corporate</a> as part of the main counties)</div></div></div>
<p>The distinction between a county for purposes of the lieutenancy and a county for administrative purposes is not a new one; in some cases, a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county corporate</a> that was part of a county was appointed its own lieutenant (although the lieutenant of the containing county would often be appointed to this position, as well), and the three Ridings of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> had been treated as three counties for lieutenancy purposes since the 17th century.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> established <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> to assume the administrative functions of <a href="/wiki/Quarter_Sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter Sessions">Quarter Sessions</a> in the counties. It created new entities called "<a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative counties">administrative counties</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An administrative county comprised all of the county apart from the <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a>; also, some traditional subdivisions of counties were constituted administrative counties, for instance the <a href="/wiki/Soke_of_Peterborough" title="Soke of Peterborough">Soke of Peterborough</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>. The act further stipulated that areas that were part of an administrative county would be part of the county for all purposes. The greatest change was the creation of the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a>, which was made both an administrative county and a "county"; it included parts of the historic counties of <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>. Other differences were small and resulted from the constraint that <a href="/wiki/Urban_sanitary_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban sanitary district">urban sanitary districts</a> (and later <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal boroughs</a>) were not permitted to straddle county boundaries.
</p><p>Apart from Yorkshire, counties that were subdivided nevertheless continued to exist as ceremonial counties. For example, the administrative counties of <a href="/wiki/East_Suffolk_(county)" title="East Suffolk (county)">East Suffolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Suffolk_(county)" title="West Suffolk (county)">West Suffolk</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/County_borough_of_Ipswich" class="mw-redirect" title="County borough of Ipswich">county borough of Ipswich</a>, were considered to make up a single ceremonial county of <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, and the administrative county of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> was part of the ceremonial county of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>.
</p><p>The term "ceremonial county" is an anachronism; at the time they were shown on <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> maps as "counties" or "geographical counties", and were referred to in the Local Government Act 1888 simply as "counties".
</p><p>Apart from minor boundary revisions (for example, <a href="/wiki/Caversham,_Berkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Caversham, Berkshire">Caversham</a>, a town in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, becoming part of <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a> county borough and thus of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, in 1911), these areas changed little until the 1965 creation of Greater London and of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a>, which resulted in the abolition of the offices of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Middlesex" title="Lord Lieutenant of Middlesex">Lord Lieutenant of Middlesex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_the_County_of_London" title="Lord Lieutenant of the County of London">Lord Lieutenant of the County of London</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Huntingdonshire" title="Lord Lieutenant of Huntingdonshire">Lord Lieutenant of Huntingdonshire</a> and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Greater_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant of Greater London">Lord Lieutenant of Greater London</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Lord Lieutenant of Huntingdon and Peterborough">Lord Lieutenant of Huntingdon and Peterborough</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_counties_1974.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/English_counties_1974.svg/220px-English_counties_1974.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/English_counties_1974.svg/330px-English_counties_1974.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/English_counties_1974.svg/440px-English_counties_1974.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_counties_1974.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ceremonial counties from 1974 to 1996 (City of London not shown)</div></div></div>
<p>In 1974, administrative counties and county boroughs were abolished, and a major reform was instituted. At this time, lieutenancy was redefined to use the new <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties</a> directly.
</p><p>Following a further rearrangement in 1996, <a href="/wiki/County_of_Avon" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Avon">Avon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_England" title="Cleveland, England">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a> were abolished. This led to a resurrection of a distinction between the local government counties and the ceremonial or geographical counties used for lieutenancy, and also to the adoption of the term "ceremonial counties", which although not used in statute, was used in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> before the arrangements coming into effect.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The County of Avon that had been formed in 1974 was mostly split between <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, but its city of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> regained the status of a county in itself, which it had lost upon the formation of Avon. Cleveland was partitioned between <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a>. Hereford and Worcester was divided into the restored counties of <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>. Humberside was split between <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and a new ceremonial county of <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> was restored as a ceremonial county. Many county boroughs were re-established as "unitary authorities"; this involved establishing the area as an administrative county, but usually not as a ceremonial county.
</p><p>Most ceremonial counties are, therefore, entities comprising local authority areas, as they were from 1889 to 1974. The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_British_Counties" title="Association of British Counties">Association of British Counties</a>, a traditional counties lobbying organisation, has suggested that ceremonial counties be restored to their ancient boundaries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shrieval_counties">Shrieval counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceremonial_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shrieval counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In present-day England, the ceremonial counties correspond to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_shrievalties" title="List of shrievalties">shrieval counties</a>, each with a <a href="/wiki/High_sheriff" title="High sheriff">high sheriff</a> appointed (except the City of London, which has its <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_the_City_of_London" title="Sheriffs of the City of London">two sheriffs</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ceremonial_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancies_Act_1997" title="Lieutenancies Act 1997">Lieutenancies Act 1997</a> defines counties for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Lord-lieutenant" title="Lord-lieutenant">lieutenancies</a> in terms of metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties (created by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, as amended) as well as Greater London and the Isles of Scilly (which lie outside the 1972 Act's new system). Although the term is not used in the act, these counties are sometimes known as "ceremonial counties". The counties are defined in Schedule 1, paragraphs 2–5<sup id="cite_ref-sched_1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sched_1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> as amended<sup id="cite_ref-amend_1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amend_1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> (most recently in 2009<sup id="cite_ref-amend_2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amend_2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and 2019<sup id="cite_ref-Dorset_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorset-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>) — these amendments have not altered the actual areas covered by the counties as set out in 1997, only their composition in terms of local government areas, as a result of structural changes in local government.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;N 1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
