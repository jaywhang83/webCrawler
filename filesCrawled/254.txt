<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_given_names">Dutch given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dutch given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Dutch child's birth and given name(s) must be officially registered by the parents within 3 days after birth. It is not uncommon to give a child several given names. Usually the first one is for daily use, often in a diminutive form. Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a> often chose <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_of_names" title="Latinisation of names">Latinized</a> names for their children, such as <i>Catharina</i> and <i>Wilhelmus</i>, while <a href="/wiki/Protestants" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> more commonly chose simple Dutch forms such as <i>Trijntje</i> and <i>Willem</i>. In both cases, names were often shortened for everyday use (<i>Wilhelmus</i> and <i>Willem</i> became <i>Wim</i>). In 2014 39% of Dutch children received one name, another 38% were given two names, 20% had three names, 2% got four names and only a few hundred children had five or more given names.<sup id="cite_ref-BloothooftOnland2016_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BloothooftOnland2016-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Dutch_.28Netherlands.29_naming_law_.28given_names.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_(Netherlands)_naming_law_(given_names)">Dutch (Netherlands) naming law (given names)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dutch (Netherlands) naming law (given names)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> naming legislation allows nearly all given names unless they are too similar to an existing surname, or if the name is inappropriate.  There is no legal limit on the number of given names for one child.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Dutch_given_names">History of Dutch given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of Dutch given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Dutch given names can roughly be divided in four main periods:
</p>
<ol><li>The domination of Germanic names. (<a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> and before until the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>)</li>
<li>The high Middle Ages, when Germanic-based personal names were losing ground to non-native <a href="/wiki/Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Saints">holy names</a>. (High Middle Ages until the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> era)</li>
<li>A period of stability, when a very strong naming habit emerged. (Early Modern era–1960s)</li>
<li>The post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> period, characterised by previously unknown personal names. (1960s–present)</li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_period">Germanic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Germanic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Germanic names are the names with the longest <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> in the Dutch-speaking area; they form the oldest layer of the given names known in Dutch. The Germanic names were characterised by a rich diversity, as there were many possible combinations.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> name is composed of two parts, the latter of which also indicates the gender of the person. A name like Adelbert or Albert is composed of <i>"adel"</i> (meaning <i>"noble"</i>) and <i>"bert"</i> which is derived from <i>"beracht"</i> (meaning <i>"bright"</i> or <i>"shining"</i>) hence the name means something in the order of <i>"Bright/Shining through noble behaviour";</i> the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> name <i>"Albright"</i>, now only seen as a surname, is a cognate with the same origin.
</p><p>Combining these parts was used when the child was named after family or other relatives. For example, the child would receive two parts from different family members, in this way a father named <i>"Hildebrant"</i> and a mother called <i>"Gertrud"</i> would call their son <i>"Gerbrant"</i> and their daughter <i>"Hiltrud".</i>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_names">Medieval names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medieval names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Through the course of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> names derived from <a href="/wiki/Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Saints">Christian Saints</a> became more common than Germanic ones. From the 12th century onwards it became custom for the child to receive a Christian name, although some names of Germanic origin like <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_of_Nivelles" title="Gertrude of Nivelles">Gertrude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hubertus" title="Hubertus">Hubertus</a> remained prevalent as these too became names of Christian saints.
</p><p>The direct influence of the church on the transition from Germanic to Christian names must not be overestimated. Before the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">council of Trent</a> (1545–1563), the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic church">Roman Catholic church</a> did not have any regulation of the practice of naming children.
</p><p>There are thought to have been a number of reasons the Christian names gained the upper hand, such as the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">crusades</a>, the larger ecclesiastical influence and the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Mendicant_Orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Mendicant Orders">mendicant orders</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a>) and most importantly, the <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">veneration</a> of saints and the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saints</a>.
</p><p>Besides religious influence it is believed that <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> was the main reason to give children a Christian name. With larger cities starting to flourish all across the Low Countries, wealthy citizens in particular became <i>trend-setters</i> in this regard.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In these times typical Dutch names such as <i>"Kees"</i> <i>(<a href="/wiki/Cornelis_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelis (disambiguation)">Cornelis</a>),</i> <i>"Jan"</i> <i>(<a href="/wiki/Johannes" title="Johannes">Johannes</a>)</i> and <i>"Piet"</i> <i>(<a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Petrus</a>)</i> emerged.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When the conversion was made from Germanic to Christian names, most parents just picked a name they liked best or would be most helpful in their child's later life, for example if the child would come from a butcher's family and he himself would one day become a <a href="/wiki/Butcher" title="Butcher">butcher</a>, the child would probably be called after <i>"Sint Joris"</i> (the Dutch name for "<a href="/wiki/Saint_George" title="Saint George">Saint George</a>"), the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of the butchers.
</p><p>The Dutch habit of naming newborns after another family member originates with a then-widespread <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">superstition</a> that the name in some way contributed to some form of <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a> of the person the child was named after, who was usually much older. This superstition disappeared after some time, even though a certain Le Francq van Berkeij writes the following in 1776: "<i>bij veelen, een oud, overgeloovig denkbeeld, dat iemand weldra sterft, wanneer hij, gelijk men zegt, vernoemd is</i>" ("many have a superstitious belief that a person will soon die when someone, as they say, has been named after him").
</p><p>As the centuries passed, this practice became so standard that the names of the children were practically known at the marriage of the future parents. The rules for naming were the following:
</p>
<ul><li>First-born son is named after paternal grandfather</li>
<li>First-born daughter is named after maternal grandmother</li>
<li>Second son is named after maternal grandfather</li>
<li>Second daughter is named after paternal grandmother</li>
<li>Subsequent children were often named after uncles and aunts – there was some liberty of choice here.</li></ul>
<p>The infant mortality rate was high. If a son had died before his next brother was born, this younger brother was usually given the same name. The same goes, <i><a href="/wiki/Mutatis_mutandis" title="Mutatis mutandis">mutatis mutandis</a></i>, for a daughter. When the father died before the birth of a son, the son was usually named after him. When the mother died at the birth of a daughter, the daughter was usually named after the mother.
</p>
<h4><span id="Post-World_War_II_period_.281945.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-World_War_II_period_(1945–present)">Post-World War II period (1945–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-World War II period (1945–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Traditionally there was little difference between the <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian name</a> ("doopnaam") and the name used in domestic spheres ("roepnaam"). If someone's Christian name was <i><a href="/wiki/Johannes" title="Johannes">Johannes</a></i>, domestically he was called <i>Johan</i>, <i>Jan</i> or <i>Hans</i>.
</p><p>After the war, the Dutch became less religious. Thus the Christian name and given name started to diverge, as personal names of foreign origin were adopted. In some cases these names are written more or less <a href="/wiki/Phonetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonetic">phonetically</a>, for example <i>Sjaak</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>Jacques</i>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i>Jack</i>) and <i>Sjaan</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>Jeanne</i>). (See also <a href="/wiki/Sjors_%26_Sjimmie" title="Sjors &amp; Sjimmie">Sjors &amp; Sjimmie</a>.) Working-class names Jan, Piet and Klaas (the Dutch proverbial equivalent to "Tom, Dick and Harry") were often replaced by middle-class Hans, Peter and Nico. Also, the urge to name children after their grandparents lessened dramatically. The change in naming also led to a new law on naming in 1970, replacing the old one, which had been in force since 1803.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nowadays, traditional official names are found, but often only as an addition to the modern name. Boys are more often given a traditional Dutch name than girls. Boys are also more commonly named after a family member while girls are simply named for the sound of the name. As in the past there is a certain difference between working class names and middle class names. As working class tend to adopt more modern names and names of celebrities as middle class names are more traditional.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a great variety of Dutch surnames (over 100,000), partly because of an influx due to a forced official registration of surnames in 1811,<sup id="cite_ref-Schulze2008_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> hence there have been few generations in which names could become extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In practice, the great majority of Dutch people had had family surnames for centuries, and the adoption of new names was limited to some Jewish citizens and some people in rural communities in the north east of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-naammythe_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naammythe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a persistent myth that some Dutch citizens, as a way of protest, chose humorous names during the forced registration. Examples often given are <i>Naaktgeboren</i> ("born naked") or <i>Zondervan</i> ("without surname"). However these names are in fact far older;<sup id="cite_ref-naammythe_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naammythe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Naaktgeboren</i> for example is from the German <i>Nachgeboren</i> (born after the father was deceased, also one of the origins of the name <i><a href="/wiki/Posthumus" title="Posthumus">Posthumus</a></i>).
</p><p>Many Dutch surnames originated from different personal qualities, geographical locations, and occupations. However, Dutch names in English directories (e.g., reference lists of scientific papers) may be ordered on the full name including all prefixes (Van Rijn would be ordered under 'V'), partly because many Dutch emigrant families to English-speaking countries have had their prefixes capitalized for them, whether they liked it or not, like <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steve_Van_Dyck" title="Steve Van Dyck">Steve Van Dyck</a>, and normal practise in English is to order on the first capitalized element.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Dutch_naming_law_.28surnames.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_naming_law_(surnames)">Dutch naming law (surnames)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dutch naming law (surnames)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In line with Dutch tradition, marriage used to require a woman to precede her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> with her husband's name and add a hyphen between the two. Thus, when Anna Pietersen married Jan Jansen, she became Anna Jansen-Pietersen.
</p><p>However, this did not become her legal name. Her legal name did not change at all. Passports, and other official documents, continued to name her Anna Pietersen, even though there might have been "spouse of Jan Jansen" added.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current law in the Netherlands gives people more freedom: upon marriage within the Netherlands, both partners default to keeping their own surnames, but both are given the choice of using their partner's surname, or a combination of the two. For example, if a person called Jansen marries someone called Smit, each partner has the choice to call himself or herself Jansen, Smit, Jansen-Smit or Smit-Jansen. The preferred option will be registered with the municipal registration, without giving up the right to use one's original name, which remains the legal name. However, in practice, the standard procedure is that when a woman marries, she either keeps her maiden name or has a double surname, for example, Miss Jansen marries Mr Smit she either chooses to become Mrs Jansen or Mrs Smit-Jansen.  It is not common to only take the partner's surname.
</p><p>This can cause problems for foreign national females living in the country, as they may be required to present their passport as proof of identification. If they changed their surname on marriage, then in municipal records the surname as it appears on their passport takes precedence. While name changes due to marriages performed in the Netherlands cannot be processed, it is certainly possible in the Netherlands to process name changes due to marriages performed outside the Netherlands, provided certain conditions are met. These conditions are that the marriage must be registered abroad, the application for a name change abroad must be requested on the same date as the marriage date, the changed name must be recorded abroad on a certificate in accordance with the local rules of the foreign country and the marriage and name change, as well as proof of application as of the date of the marriage, must be legalized or apostilled and provided to the Dutch consulate or Dutch municipality upon return to the Netherlands.
</p><p>This stems from the fact that international marriages are not necessarily governed by Dutch law but by private international law which is codified in the Netherlands in the Civil Code (Burgerlijk Wetboek), Book No. 10, Private International Law, Title 2 - The Name, Article 24.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Parents can choose to give their children either their father's or mother's family name, as long as the parents are married or are living together and the father has acknowledged the child. The surname of younger siblings must be the same as the surname of the oldest child.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymics">Patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although most people had family surnames before 1811, the use of <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> was common, including for those with established last names . The oldest form used the possessive of the father's name along with the word for son or daughter. Examples would be a boy born to Jan being named Pieter Janszoon while his daughter might be named Geertje Jansdochter. These forms were commonly shortened, to Janszn./Jansz and Jansdr., or to Jansse, and finally to Jans which could be used for both male or female children. These patronymic names were official and even used on legal documents where inheritances can be seen to pass from father to son with different "last names".
</p><p>In the North and East of the Netherlands, many people were between 1000 and 1800 A.D.  named after their ancestors, sometimes after the place where they lived,  by the <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> -ma or -stra (of <a href="/wiki/Frisian_languages" title="Frisian languages">Frisian</a>), or -ing or -ink (of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Low_Saxon" title="Dutch Low Saxon">Low Saxon</a> origin).  Examples: Dijkstra (after a dyke near the place they came from);  Halbertsma (after an ancestor called Halbert); Wiebing (after an ancestor called Wiebe); Hesselink (after an ancestor called Hessel). 
After 1811, many patronymics became permanent surnames such that Peeters, Jansen, Willems are common surnames today.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Most_common_Dutch_surnames">Most common Dutch surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Most common Dutch surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_most_common_surnames_in_Europe#Netherlands" title="List of most common surnames in Europe">List of most common surnames in Europe §&#160;Netherlands</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_most_common_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="List of most common surnames">The most common Dutch surnames</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> (as of 1947) and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> are listed to the right. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.meertens.knaw.nl/nfd/nrf.php">Meertens' Dutch surname database</a> lists 94143 different family names; the total <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch speaking population</a> in Europe is estimated to be about 23 million people. The most common Dutch names in Belgium are nearly all <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> <i>"father-based"</i> names in which they are composed with the following formula <i>name of father + "-son",</i> the only exceptions being <i>"De Smet"</i> (the <a href="/wiki/Smith_(metalworker)" class="mw-redirect" title="Smith (metalworker)">Smith</a>) and - to a certain extent, because it is also a patronymic ("Thomas") - <i>"Maes"</i> (<a href="/wiki/Meuse" title="Meuse">Meuse</a>). The most common Dutch names in the Netherlands are more diverse, with names ranging from <i>"Visser"</i> (<a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fisherman</a>) to <i>"Van Dijk"</i> ((living along) the <a href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dike (construction)">dike</a>) and <i>"De Jong"</i> (the young (one)). It should be remembered however that these figures are based on the data of an entire country, and on a smaller scale other names tend to dominate certain <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">regions</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tussenvoegsels">Tussenvoegsels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tussenvoegsels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tussenvoegsel" title="Tussenvoegsel">Tussenvoegsel</a></div>
<p>Over 20,000 surnames in The Netherlands start with <i>tussenvoegsels</i>, <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">prepositions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">articles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbg_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbg-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Tussenvoegsel#Common_tussenvoegsels" title="Tussenvoegsel">large number</a> of prepositions and combinations are possible, but the vast majority of such names start with just a few: 99% of the people with tussenvoegsel-names (including foreign names with "von", "de la", "dos" etc.) have names starting with <i>van</i> ("from / of"; 45%), <i>de / den</i> ("the", 21.5% &amp; 1.6%), <i>van der / van den / van de / van 't</i> ("from the" with different inflections; 16.6%, 7.2%, 4.3%, 0.5%), <i>ten / ter</i> ("at the"; 1.1%, 0.8%) and <i>te</i> ("at"; 0.4%).<sup id="cite_ref-cbg_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbg-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Netherlands the tussenvoegsels are rarely capitalized, while, since the 19th century, in Belgium they usually are. In the Netherlands, as for example in <a href="/wiki/German_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="German surname">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish surname">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese surname">Portugal</a> and (considering prepositions) <a href="/wiki/French_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="French surname">France</a>, the tussenvoegsels are not a part of the indexing process, and in encyclopedias, telephone books, etc. surnames are sorted starting with the first capitalized letter. In Belgium, as in <a href="/wiki/English_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="English surname">English speaking countries</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian surname">Italy</a> and (considering articles) France, indexing includes the tussenvoegsels, leading to large sections under "D" and "V". In Belgium, primarily in <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a>, prepositions and articles can be merged with the surname (such as <i>Vandecasteele</i>) and a few combinations occur (<i>Vande Casteele</i>).
</p><p>In the Netherlands, the first tussenvoegsel is capitalized if no name or initial precedes it. For example: <i>Jan van den Berg</i>, <i>J. van den Bergs</i>, but <i>Mijn naam is Van den Berg</i> ("my name is Van den Berg") and <i>de heer Van den Berg</i> ("Mr. Van den Berg"). In <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, tussenvoegsels of personal names always keep their original <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>: "mevrouw van der Velde", "A. van der Velde", and "Jan Van den Broeke".
</p><p>While in <a href="/wiki/German_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="German surname">German surnames</a> the addition of <i>von</i> to a name often indicates that a person belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> or <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royalty</a>, <i>van</i> is too common in The Netherlands to make such a connection. This usage does exist in Flemish names, though its nobility usually obtained the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> prefix 'de', specifically without capitalization.
</p>
