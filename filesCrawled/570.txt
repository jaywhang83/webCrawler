	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Colonnade</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg/350px-Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg" decoding="async" width="350" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg/525px-Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg/700px-Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6020" data-file-height="4016" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikimedia_Conference_2015_photo_by_Pine_-_28.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colonnade at the <a href="/wiki/Belvedere_on_the_Pfingstberg" title="Belvedere on the Pfingstberg">Belvedere on the Pfingstberg</a> palace in Germany</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">classical architecture</a>, a <b>colonnade</b> is a long sequence of <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columns</a> joined by their <a href="/wiki/Entablature" title="Entablature">entablature</a>, often free-standing, or part of a building.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Paired or multiple pairs of columns are normally employed in a colonnade which can be straight or curved.  The space enclosed may be covered or open. In <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Square" title="St. Peter&#39;s Square">St. Peter's Square</a> in Rome, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Lorenzo_Bernini#Architecture" title="Gian Lorenzo Bernini">Bernini's great colonnade</a> encloses a vast open elliptical space.
</p><p>When in front of a building, screening the door (Latin <i>porta</i>), it is called a <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a>, when enclosing an open court, a <a href="/wiki/Peristyle" title="Peristyle">peristyle</a>. A portico may be more than one rank of columns deep, as at the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in Rome or the <a href="/wiki/Stoa" title="Stoa">stoae</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>. 
</p><p>When the <a href="/wiki/Intercolumniation" title="Intercolumniation">intercolumniation</a> is alternately wide and narrow, a colonnade may be termed <em>araeosystyle</em> (Gr. αραιος, "widely spaced", and συστυλος, "with columns set close together"), as in the case of the western porch of <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul&#39;s Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a> and the east front of the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Perrault" title="Claude Perrault">Perrault</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colonnade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Colonnades have been built since ancient times and interpretations of the classical model have continued through to modern times, and Neoclassical styles remained popular for centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Doremus_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doremus-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>, for example, porticos are continued along the front as a colonnade.  The porch of columns that surrounds the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> (in style a <i>peripteral</i> classical temple) can be termed a colonnade.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As well as the traditional use in buildings and monuments, colonnades are used in sports stadiums such as the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Stadium" title="Harvard Stadium">Harvard Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, where the entire horseshoe-shaped stadium is topped by a colonnade. The longest colonnade in the United States, with 36 <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_column" class="mw-redirect" title="Corinthian column">Corinthian columns</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Education_Building" title="New York State Education Building">New York State Education Building</a> in Albany, New York.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
