<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ribbleheadstation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ribbleheadstation.jpg/220px-Ribbleheadstation.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ribbleheadstation.jpg/330px-Ribbleheadstation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ribbleheadstation.jpg/440px-Ribbleheadstation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="864" data-file-height="560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ribbleheadstation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ribblehead_railway_station" title="Ribblehead railway station">Ribblehead station</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Settle–Carlisle line</b> (also known as the <i>Settle and Carlisle</i> (S&amp;C)) is a 73-mile-long (117&#160;km) main railway line in northern England. The route, which crosses the remote, scenic regions of the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Pennines" title="North Pennines">North Pennines</a>, runs between <a href="/wiki/Settle_Junction_railway_station" title="Settle Junction railway station">Settle Junction</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to <a href="/wiki/Morecambe" title="Morecambe">Morecambe</a> line, and <a href="/wiki/Carlisle_railway_station" title="Carlisle railway station">Carlisle</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/English-Scottish_border" class="mw-redirect" title="English-Scottish border">English-Scottish borders</a>. The historic line was constructed in the 1870s and has several notable tunnels and viaducts such as the imposing <a href="/wiki/Ribblehead_Viaduct" title="Ribblehead Viaduct">Ribblehead</a>.
</p><p>The line is a part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Rail" title="National Rail">National Rail</a> network that is managed by <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a>. All passenger services are operated by <a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a> apart from those temporary diverted services (due to closures of the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Main_Line" title="West Coast Main Line">West Coast Main Line</a>). Stations serve towns such as <a href="/wiki/Settle,_North_Yorkshire" title="Settle, North Yorkshire">Settle</a> in North Yorkshire, <a href="/wiki/Appleby-in-Westmorland" title="Appleby-in-Westmorland">Appleby-in-Westmorland</a> in Cumbria and small rural communities along its route.
</p><p>In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a> intended to close the Settle - Carlisle line. This prompted rail groups, enthusiasts, local authorities and residents along the route to fight a successful campaign to save the railway. In 1989 the UK government announced it would not close the line. Since then, passenger numbers have grown steadily to 1.2 million in 2012. Eight formerly closed stations have been reopened and several quarries have been reconnected to the line. It remains one of the most popular railway routes in the UK for <a href="/wiki/Railtour" title="Railtour">charter trains</a> and specials. After damage by a landslip, part of the line was closed from February 2016 to March 2017.  To celebrate the reopening, the first regular mainline scheduled service in England for nearly half a century ran with a <a href="/wiki/LNER_Peppercorn_Class_A1_60163_Tornado" title="LNER Peppercorn Class A1 60163 Tornado">steam engine</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Settle–Carlisle line (S&amp;C) had its origins in railway politics; the expansion-minded <a href="/wiki/Midland_Railway" title="Midland Railway">Midland Railway</a> company was locked in dispute with the rival <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Western_Railway" title="London and North Western Railway">London and North Western Railway</a> (LNWR) over access rights to the latter’s tracks to Scotland.
</p><p>The Midland's access to Scotland was via the <a href="/wiki/%22Little%22_North_Western_Railway" title="&quot;Little&quot; North Western Railway">"Little North Western" route</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ingleton,_North_Yorkshire" title="Ingleton, North Yorkshire">Ingleton</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ingleton_branch_line" title="Ingleton branch line">Ingleton branch line</a> from Ingleton to <a href="/wiki/List_of_closed_railway_stations_in_Britain:_K-L" title="List of closed railway stations in Britain: K-L">Low Gill</a>, where it joined the <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_and_Carlisle_Railway" title="Lancaster and Carlisle Railway">Lancaster and Carlisle Railway</a>, was under the control of the rival LNWR. Initially the routes, although physically connected at Ingleton, were not logically connected, as the LNWR and Midland could not agree on sharing the use of <a href="/wiki/Ingleton_(Midland)_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingleton (Midland) railway station">Ingleton station</a>. Instead the LNWR terminated its trains at <a href="/wiki/Ingleton_(L%26NW)_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingleton (L&amp;NW) railway station">its own station</a> at the end of Ingleton Viaduct, and Midland Railway passengers had to change into LNWR trains by means of a walk of about a mile over steep gradients between the two stations.<sup id="cite_ref-forgottenrelics_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forgottenrelics-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An agreement was reached over station access, enabling the Midland to attach through carriages to LNWR trains at Ingleton. Passengers could continue their journey north without leaving the train. The situation was not ideal, as the LNWR handled the through carriages of its rival with deliberate obstructiveness, for example attaching the coaches to slow goods trains instead of fast passenger workings.<sup id="cite_ref-settlecarlisledotcocotuk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-settlecarlisledotcocotuk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The route through Ingleton is closed, but the major structures, Low Gill and Ingleton viaducts, remain. It was a well-engineered line suitable for express passenger running, however its potential was never realised due to the rivalry between the companies. The Midland board decided that the only solution was a separate route to Scotland. Surveying began in 1865, and in June 1866, Parliamentary approval was given to the Midland’s bill, for which <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Carter_(Coventry_MP)" title="Samuel Carter (Coventry MP)">Samuel Carter</a> was solicitor.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Soon after, the <a href="/wiki/Overend,_Gurney_and_Company" title="Overend, Gurney and Company">Overend-Gurney</a> banking failure sparked a financial crisis in the UK. Interest rates rose sharply, several railways went bankrupt and the Midland's board, prompted by a shareholders' revolt, began to have second thoughts about a venture where the estimated cost was £2.3&#160;million (equivalent to £210&#160;million in 2018).<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As a result, in April 1869, with no work started, the company petitioned Parliament to abandon the scheme it had earlier fought for. However Parliament, under pressure from other railways which would benefit from the scheme that would cost them nothing, refused, and construction commenced in November that year.
</p><p>As this date falls between the publication of the 1st Edition 1:2500 <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> map and its 1st Revision, the impact of construction can be observed by studying those maps.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The line was built by over 6,000 <a href="/wiki/Navvy" title="Navvy">navvies</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> who worked in remote locations, enduring harsh weather conditions. Large camps were established to house the navvies, most of them Irish, with many becoming complete townships with post offices and schools. They were named Inkerman, Sebastapol and Jericho. The remains of one camp—Batty Green—where over 2,000 navvies lived and worked, can be seen near Ribblehead. The Midland Railway helped pay for scripture readers to counteract the effect of drunken violence in these isolated communities.
</p><p>A plaque in the church at <a href="/wiki/Chapel-le-Dale" title="Chapel-le-Dale">Chapel-le-Dale</a> records the workers who died—both from disease and accidents—building the railway. The death toll is unknown but 80 people died at Batty Green alone following a <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> epidemic.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A memorial stone was laid in 1997 in the churchyard of St Mary's Church, <a href="/wiki/Mallerstang" title="Mallerstang">Mallerstang</a> to commemorate the 25 railway builders and their families who died during the construction of this section of the line, and who were buried there in unmarked graves.
</p><p>The engineer for the project was John Crossley from <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, a veteran of other Midland schemes.
The terrain traversed is among the bleakest and wildest in England, and construction was halted for months at a time due to frozen ground, snowdrifts and flooding. One contractor had to give up as a result of underestimating the terrain and the weather—Dent Head has almost four times the rainfall of London. Another long-established partnership dissolved under the strain; that of Eckersley and Bayliss. They were contracted to construct the 23&#160;mi (37&#160;km) section from Kirkby Thore to Petteril Bridge in Carlisle.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The line was engineered to express standards throughout—local traffic was secondary and many stations were miles from the villages they purported to serve. The railway's summit at 1,169 feet (356&#160;m) is at <a href="/wiki/Aisgill" title="Aisgill">Ais Gill</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/Garsdale" title="Garsdale">Garsdale</a>. To keep the gradients to less than 1 in 100 (1%), a requirement for fast running using steam traction, huge engineering works were required. Even so, the terrain imposed a 16-mile (26&#160;km) climb from Settle to Blea Moor, almost all of it at 1 in 100, and known to enginemen as ‘the long drag’.
</p><p>The line required 14 tunnels and 22 <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaducts</a>, the most notable is the 24 arch <a href="/wiki/Ribblehead_Viaduct" title="Ribblehead Viaduct">Ribblehead Viaduct</a> which is 104&#160;ft (32&#160;m) high and 440 yards (402&#160;m) long. The swampy ground meant that the piers had to be sunk 25&#160;ft (8&#160;m) below the peat and set in concrete in order to provide a suitable foundation. Soon after crossing the viaduct, the line enters Blea Moor tunnel, 2,629&#160;yd (2,404&#160;m) long and 500&#160;ft (152&#160;m) below the moor, before emerging onto <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dent_Head_Viaduct&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dent Head Viaduct (page does not exist)">Dent Head Viaduct</a>. The summit at <a href="/wiki/Aisgill" title="Aisgill">Aisgill</a> is the highest point reached by main line trains in England. The tunnel at Lazonby was constructed at the request of a local vicar as he did not want the railway to run past the vicarage.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Water_trough" class="mw-redirect" title="Water trough">Water troughs</a> were laid between the tracks at Garsdale enabling steam engines to take water without losing speed.
</p><p>The remains of the navvies' camp at Rise Hill tunnel were investigated by <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Team" title="Time Team">Time Team</a></i> in 2008, for a programme that was broadcast on 1 February 2009.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The line opened for goods traffic in August 1875 with the first passenger trains starting in April 1876. The cost of the line was £3.6&#160;million (equivalent to £330&#160;million in 2018)<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> – 50 per cent above the estimate and a colossal sum for the time.
</p><p>For some time the Midland dominated the market for London-Glasgow traffic, providing more daytime trains than its rival. In 1923 the Midland and the LNWR were both merged into the new <a href="/wiki/London_Midland_%26_Scottish_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="London Midland &amp; Scottish Railway">London Midland &amp; Scottish Railway</a>. In the new company, the disadvantages of the Midland’s route were clear – its steeper gradients and greater length meant it could not compete on speed from London to Glasgow, especially as Midland route trains had to make more stops to serve major cities in the Midlands and Yorkshire. The Midland had long competed on the extra comfort it provided for its passengers but this advantage was lost in the merged company.
</p><p>After nationalisation in 1948, the pace of rundown quickened. It was regarded as a duplicate line, and control over the through London-Glasgow route was split over several regions which made it hard to plan popular through services. Mining subsidence affected speeds through the East Midlands and Yorkshire. In 1962, the <a href="/wiki/Thames%E2%80%93Clyde_Express" title="Thames–Clyde Express">Thames–Clyde Express</a> travelling via the S&amp;C took almost nine hours from London to Glasgow – over the West Coast Main Line the journey length was 7 hours 20 minutes.
</p><p>
In the 1963, <a href="/wiki/Beeching_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Beeching Report">Beeching Report</a> into the restructuring of <a href="/wiki/British_Railways" class="mw-redirect" title="British Railways">British Rail</a> recommended the withdrawal of all passenger services from the line. Some smaller stations had closed in the 1950s. Although the Beeching recommendations were shelved, it is clear that closure of the line was planned as early as the late 1960s. Such closure is referred to in paragraph 40 of the official report into the accident involving two goods trains between Horton-in-Ribblesdale and Selside on 30 October 1968, by Lt. Colonel I.K.A. McNaughton: </p><blockquote><p>"... Even if the Settle and Carlisle line were planned to form part of the long term railway network of the country, it would still come fairly low in the priority list for installation of AWS; this route, however, is planned for closure within the next few years ..."</p></blockquote><p> In May 1970 all stations except for Settle and Appleby West were closed, and its passenger service cut to two trains a day in each direction, leaving mostly freight.
</p><p>Few express passenger services continued to operate, <a href="/wiki/The_Waverley" class="mw-redirect" title="The Waverley">The Waverley</a> from <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_railway_station" title="St Pancras railway station">London St Pancras</a> to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh Waverley</a> via <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nottingham railway station">Nottingham</a> ended in 1968, while the <a href="/wiki/Thames%E2%80%93Clyde_Express" title="Thames–Clyde Express">Thames–Clyde Express</a> from London to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Central_station" title="Glasgow Central station">Glasgow Central</a> via <a href="/wiki/Leicester_railway_station" title="Leicester railway station">Leicester</a>, lasted until 1975. Night sleepers from London to Glasgow continued until 1976. After that a residual service from Glasgow – cut back at Nottingham (three trains each way) – survived until May 1982.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threat_of_closure">Threat of closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Threat of closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s, the S&amp;C suffered from a lack of investment, and most freight traffic was diverted onto the electrified <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Main_Line" title="West Coast Main Line">West Coast Main Line</a>. The condition of many viaducts and tunnels deteriorated due to lack of investment. Dalesrail began operating services to closed stations on summer weekends in 1974. These were promoted by the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales_National_Park_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire Dales National Park Authority">Yorkshire Dales National Park Authority</a> to encourage ramblers.
</p><p>In the early 1980s, the S&amp;C was carrying only a handful of trains per day, and British Rail decided the cost of renewing the viaducts and tunnels would be prohibitively expensive, given the small amount of traffic carried on the line. In 1981 a protest group, the Friends of the Settle–Carlisle Line (FoSCL), was established, and campaigned against the line's closure even before it was officially announced.
</p><p>In 1984, closure notices were posted at the S&amp;C's remaining stations. However, local authorities and rail enthusiasts joined together and campaigned to save the S&amp;C, pointing out that British Rail was ignoring the S&amp;C's potential for <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, ignoring the need for a diversionary route to the West Coast main line, and failing to promote through traffic from the Midlands and Yorkshire to Scotland. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg/220px-1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg/330px-1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg/440px-1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1111" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1986_Settle_and_Carlisle_railway_closure_enquiry.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1986 Transport Users Consultative Committee hearing in Carlisle</div></div></div>
<p>There was outrage over the closure plan: critics pointed out that this was a main line, not a small branch railway. The campaign uncovered evidence that British Rail had mounted a dirty tricks campaign against the line,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> exaggerating the cost of repairs (£6&#160;million for Ribblehead Viaduct alone)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and diverting traffic away from the line in order to justify its closure plans, a process referred to as <a href="/wiki/Closure_by_stealth" title="Closure by stealth">closure by stealth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Publicity over British Rail's tactics succeeded in a huge increase in traffic. Journeys per year were 93,000 in 1983 when the campaign began, 450,000 by 1989. As late as August 1988, the British Rail Board posted adverts stating they had appointed Lazard Brothers to 'advise on the sale of the Settle–Carlisle line'.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> As a result of the successful campaign, the government finally refused consent to close the line in 1989, and British Rail started to repair the deteriorating tunnels and viaducts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statue_of_Ruswarp">Statue of Ruswarp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Statue of Ruswarp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009 a statue of the <a href="/wiki/Border_collie" class="mw-redirect" title="Border collie">border collie</a> Ruswarp (pronounced Russup) was sited on the platform of the refurbished <a href="/wiki/Garsdale_railway_station" title="Garsdale railway station">Garsdale railway station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The commemorative sculpture, funded by public subscription, was made by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Joel_Walker_(sculptor)" title="Joel Walker (sculptor)">Joel Walker</a> and cast in <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>. It celebrates the saving of the railway line which was coordinated by the Friends of the Settle to Carlisle Line, whose first secretary, Graham Nuttall, was a keen <a href="/wiki/Hillwalking" class="mw-redirect" title="Hillwalking">hillwalker</a>; his dog Ruswarp signed the petition to save the line with his paw print.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  On 20 January 1990 Graham Nuttall had gone missing. He and Ruswarp had bought day return tickets from Burnley to <a href="/wiki/Llandrindod_Wells" title="Llandrindod Wells">Llandrindod Wells</a> to go walking in the Welsh Mountains, but they never returned. Searches in the Elan Valley and <a href="/wiki/Rhayader" title="Rhayader">Rhayader</a> found nothing until on 7 April 1990, a lone walker found Nuttall's body beside a stream. The 14-year-old Ruswarp was nearby, having stayed by his master's body for 11 weeks in winter weather; he was so weak that he had to be carried down the mountain. His veterinary fees were paid by the <a href="/wiki/RSPCA" class="mw-redirect" title="RSPCA">RSPCA</a>, who awarded him their Animal Medallion and collar for 'vigilance' and Animal Plaque for 'intelligence and courage'. He died shortly after Nuttall's funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Settle-Carlisle_Line_poster_2010-11.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Settle-Carlisle_Line_poster_2010-11.jpg/220px-Settle-Carlisle_Line_poster_2010-11.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="345" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cf/Settle-Carlisle_Line_poster_2010-11.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="252" data-file-height="395" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Settle-Carlisle_Line_poster_2010-11.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northern Rail advertisement for the Settle–Carlisle line</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg/220px-Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg/330px-Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg/440px-Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruswarp_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1259798.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Ruswarp at <a href="/wiki/Garsdale_railway_station" title="Garsdale railway station">Garsdale railway station</a></div></div></div>
<p>Anglo-Scottish expresses have not been fully restored. The former regional franchisee <a href="/wiki/Arriva_Trains_Northern" title="Arriva Trains Northern">Arriva Trains Northern</a> initiated a twice daily Leeds–Glasgow Central service in 1999 (calling at Settle, Appleby, Carlisle, Lockerbie and Motherwell). The service was withdrawn at the behest of the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Rail_Authority" title="Strategic Rail Authority">Strategic Rail Authority</a> in 2003,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and there remains no link from Yorkshire or the East Midlands to Glasgow over the line. The link from <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> operates on Sundays during the summer months for the benefit of ramblers under the DalesRail brand.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> due to congestion on the West Coast Main Line, much rail-freight traffic is using the S&amp;C once again.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Coal from the <a href="/wiki/Hunterston_Terminal" title="Hunterston Terminal">Hunterston coal terminal</a> in Scotland is carried to power stations in Yorkshire, and <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> is transported from <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Hull" title="Port of Hull">Hull Docks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drax_Power_Station" title="Drax Power Station">Drax Power Station</a> to the British Gypsum plasterboard factory at <a href="/wiki/Kirkby_Thore" title="Kirkby Thore">Kirkby Thore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Major engineering work was needed to upgrade the line to the standards required for such heavy freight traffic and additional investment made to reduce the length of signal sections.
</p><p>In July 2009, work to stabilise a length of embankment near Kirkby Thore and remove a long-standing permanent speed restriction was undertaken.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>As of 2013, the line has experienced an upturn in fortunes. Eight formerly closed stations have reopened and in 2012 1.2 million passenger journeys were recorded compared with just 90,000 in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ribblehead_railway_station" title="Ribblehead railway station">Ribblehead station</a> features a special visitor centre. The line is an important diversionary route from the electrified <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_Main_Line" title="West Coast Main Line">West Coast Main Line</a> during engineering works. However, as the line is not electrified, electric trains such as <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_390" title="British Rail Class 390"><i>Pendolinos</i></a> need to be hauled by diesel locomotives (typically a <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_57#Virgin_Trains" title="British Rail Class 57">Class 57 <i>Thunderbird</i></a>) along the diversion section.
</p><p>In April 2014, the 25th anniversary of the line's reprieve was celebrated by the running of a special train from Leeds to Carlisle over the route.  This conveyed many of the campaigners who fought to save the line and called at Settle station, where a ceremony was held to commemorate the announcement made on 11 April 1989 that the line would be kept open. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Portillo" title="Michael Portillo">Michael Portillo</a>, the Minister of State with responsibility for railways in the Thatcher government of the time (and who made the official announcement regarding the line in parliament) attended the celebrations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconnection_to_quarries">Reconnection to quarries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reconnection to quarries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2015 it was announced that the stone quarries at Arcow and Dry Rigg would be reconnected to the line via north facing points. Stone from both of these quarries is in demand for road building due to its high Polished Stone Value (PSV) and would be taken out of the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales_National_Park" title="Yorkshire Dales National Park">Yorkshire Dales National Park</a> by freight train instead of lorries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The work was undertaken during the last quarter of 2015 with the link opening to traffic in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Northern_franchise">New Northern franchise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New Northern franchise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2015, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Arriva_Rail_North" class="mw-redirect" title="Arriva Rail North">Arriva Rail North</a> Ltd had been awarded the rights to operate the new Northern Rail franchise from April 2016 (replacing current franchisees Serco &amp; Abellio)<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> As part of the new franchise agreement with the DfT, the new operator will run one additional return journey over the line on weekdays (leaving Leeds in the late afternoon and returning from Carlisle later in the evening than the current last train) and two extra services each way on Sundays.
</p>
<h3><span id="2015.E2.80.9317_temporary_closures"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2015–17_temporary_closures">2015–17 temporary closures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2015–17 temporary closures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The winter of 2015–16 saw services over the route repeatedly disrupted by flooding and a serious <a href="/wiki/Landslip" class="mw-redirect" title="Landslip">landslip</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Armathwaite_railway_station" title="Armathwaite railway station">Armathwaite</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Storm_Desmond" title="Storm Desmond">Storm Desmond</a> saw the line closed for several days at the beginning of December by flooding at several different locations, whilst the landslip at Eden Brows near Armathwaite resulted in the closure of the southbound line between Cumwhinton and <a href="/wiki/Culgaith_railway_station" title="Culgaith railway station">Culgaith</a> from 29 January 2016 to allow the damaged <a href="/wiki/Embankment_(transportation)" title="Embankment (transportation)">embankment</a> to be inspected and stabilised.  Problems had first been reported in mid-December 2015, but repairs were carried out and services resumed on 22 December.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Single line working was in place for several days over the northbound line whilst the remedial work continued and an emergency timetable was in operation.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  Further ground movement at the site (due to the base of the embankment being eroded by the river and the saturated nature of the fill material originally used to construct the embankment) led to the complete closure of the line between Appleby &amp; Carlisle on 9 February 2016, with buses replacing trains over this section.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Repairs to the affected section entailed building a 100m-long piled retaining wall and support platform for the track and stabilising the embankment beneath it; work began in July 2016 and was completed in March 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The line between Appleby and Armathwaite was reopened to traffic on 27 June 2016 on a temporary timetable;<sup id="cite_ref-Rail280616_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rail280616-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> the repair project was estimated to cost £23&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In February 2017, to celebrate the forthcoming reopening of the line on 31 March, scheduled trains drawn by <a href="/wiki/LNER_Peppercorn_Class_A1_60163_Tornado" title="LNER Peppercorn Class A1 60163 Tornado">60163 <i>Tornado</i></a> ran in February, the first regular mainline scheduled service in England using steam for more than half a century.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The service carried more than 5,500 passengers during its three days of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, work on the piled wall and trackbed at Eden Brows was completed and the work site was handed back to Network Rail, allowing the infrastructure operator to recommission and test the track and signalling system over the affected section ahead of the reopening date. On 31 March 2017 LNER Class A3 Pacific <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_A3_4472_Flying_Scotsman" title="LNER Class A3 4472 Flying Scotsman">No. 60103 <i>Flying Scotsman</i></a> operated a special trip to Carlisle and back to celebrate the full opening to traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rolling_stock">Rolling stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rolling stock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Passenger services are usually operated by <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_158" title="British Rail Class 158">Class 158</a> <a href="/wiki/Diesel_Multiple_Units" class="mw-redirect" title="Diesel Multiple Units">Diesel Multiple Units</a>, although <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_153" title="British Rail Class 153">Class 153</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_156" title="British Rail Class 156">Class 156</a> units can also be used (the former to add additional capacity on certain services and the latter on the seasonal DalesRail trains from Preston and Blackpool). <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_142" title="British Rail Class 142">Class 142</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_144" title="British Rail Class 144">Class 144</a> units can make occasional appearances on the route, but only as short-notice replacements for the booked units or on transfer moves between depots. <a href="/wiki/British_Rail_Class_150" title="British Rail Class 150">Class 150</a> units have also begun to appear occasionally<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> (as substitutes for the booked 158s) since a batch of the units were transferred to Northern Rail from London Midland in the autumn of 2011.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents">Accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Settle%E2%80%93Carlisle_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>
