<p><b>British Home Stores</b>, commonly abbreviated to <b>BHS</b> and latterly legally styled <b>BHS Ltd</b>, was a British <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department store</a> chain, primarily selling clothing and household items. In its later years, the company began to expand into furniture, electronics, entertainment, convenience groceries and fragrance and beauty products.
</p><p>The company was founded in 1928 by a group of U.S. entrepreneurs,<sup id="cite_ref-herald_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and had a total of 163 stores mainly located in high streets or <a href="/wiki/Shopping_centres" class="mw-redirect" title="Shopping centres">shopping centres</a> by the time of its closure in 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> as well as 74 international stores across 18 separate territories.
</p><p>BHS was previously a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>, but was bought by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Green" title="Philip Green">Sir Philip Green</a> in 2000 and taken private. The company became part of Green's <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_Group" title="Arcadia Group">Arcadia Group</a> in 2009. Following a number of loss-making years, the company was sold to the consortium <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Chappell" title="Dominic Chappell">Retail Acquisitions Ltd</a> lead by serial bankrupt Dominic Chappell, in March 2015 for the nominal price of £1.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2016, just 13 months after the purchase by Retail Acquisitions, the company entered <a href="/wiki/Administration_(law)" title="Administration (law)">administration</a> following unsuccessful attempts to continue trading.<sup id="cite_ref-ft.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was eventually wound down, and all stores were closed by late August 2016 following failed attempts to find a buyer.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The overseas franchises and digital business were sold during the administration period to Al Mana Group.
</p><p>In June 2018 Al Mana Group closed down the BHS online business, having traded for just 20 months.<sup id="cite_ref-retail-week.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retail-week.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of April 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the bhs.com website is once again trading. Litecraft Group Limited are trading with the BHS - "British Home Store" brand name under licence. The website offers some of the lighting and home-wares, for which British Home Stores had a strong reputation - stating that they are "back by popular demand." Fashion and clothing have been removed from the range.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>British Home Stores was founded in 1928 by a group of U.S. entrepreneurs<sup id="cite_ref-herald_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> who wanted to follow the successful model set by <a href="/wiki/Woolworths_Group" title="Woolworths Group">Woolworths</a>. They did not want go into direct competition with Woolworths, so set their highest price at a <a href="/wiki/Shilling_(British_coin)" title="Shilling (British coin)">shilling</a>. The first store opened in <a href="/wiki/Brixton" title="Brixton">Brixton</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-herald_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and by 1929 the price limit had been lifted to five shillings to allow the business to offer more goods.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The business expanded by opening further branches, all offering small cafeterias and grocery departments, and in 1933 the business went public. After the war, the business continued to grow, and by the end of the 1960s had 94 stores nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BHS,_Darlington.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg/220px-BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg/330px-BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg/440px-BHS%2C_Darlington.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BHS,_Darlington.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A BHS branch in pre-1995 branding seen in <a href="/wiki/Darlington" title="Darlington">Darlington</a> in 2009.</div></div></div>
<p>The company expanded in the 1970s and 1980s, including the opening of stores in the newly-developing wave of indoor shopping centres (such as <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Shopping_Centre,_Bexleyheath" title="Broadway Shopping Centre, Bexleyheath">Broadway Shopping Centre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lakeside_Shopping_Centre" title="Lakeside Shopping Centre">Lakeside Shopping Centre</a>). A joint venture was launched with supermarket retailer <a href="/wiki/Sainsbury%27s" title="Sainsbury&#39;s">Sainsbury's</a> to create hypermarkets using the <a href="/wiki/SavaCentre" title="SavaCentre">SavaCentre</a> brand.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Sainsbury's took full control of SavaCentre in 1989, and later converted the stores to the Sainsbury's branding.
</p><p>A downturn in business in the early 1980s was fought with a revamp of the stores and the selling of goods with higher profit margins. The company closed its only overseas store, in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, during this time (although there was a rapidly aborted re-entry in 1996 via a franchise store in Dublin's <a href="/wiki/Jervis_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Jervis Centre">Jervis Centre</a>). In 1985, the first overseas <a href="/wiki/Franchising" title="Franchising">franchise</a> store opened in <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Such stores, not directly owned by the BHS company itself, have operated over Europe and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-cc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, BHS merged with <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(retailer)" title="Habitat (retailer)">Habitat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mothercare" title="Mothercare">Mothercare</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Storehouse_plc" title="Storehouse plc">Storehouse plc</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-herald_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Soon afterwards, the British Home Stores registered company name and branding across its shops was replaced with "BhS" (later "Bhs", and since reverted to the all-caps "BHS", which the company used in addition to the full British Home Stores name prior to the full rebrand) and a new <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">corporate logo</a>. The exception was in stores that displayed a "historic" fascia, such as that in Edinburgh's Princes Street, which continued to feature the British Home Stores name in its original Roman type etched into the granite shop front.
</p><p>British Home Stores, like many other major retailers, followed a trend of opening stores at out-of-town locations since the 1980s. One of these was the two-level store at <a href="/wiki/Merry_Hill_Shopping_Centre" title="Merry Hill Shopping Centre">Merry Hill Shopping Centre</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> (which formed part of an <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Enterprise Zone">Enterprise Zone</a>). This store opened on 14 November 1989, ultimately replacing the store in nearby <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a>, which closed in June 1990 after a directly related sharp fall in turnover. The nearby <a href="/wiki/West_Bromwich" title="West Bromwich">West Bromwich</a> store closed around the same time, its fortunes also affected by the Merry Hill development and smaller developments around nearby <a href="/wiki/Oldbury,_West_Midlands" title="Oldbury, West Midlands">Oldbury</a>, which had begun with the <a href="/wiki/SavaCentre" title="SavaCentre">SavaCentre</a> hypermarket in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Takeover_by_Philip_Green">Takeover by Philip Green</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Takeover by Philip Green">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the mid-1990s, the brand saw a further reinvention under guidance from retail design house '20:20'. The new look was showcased with the launch of the "millennium concept" shopfit, initially at the <a href="/wiki/Grafton_Centre" title="Grafton Centre">Grafton Centre</a>, Cambridge during 1995. With its softer <i>Bhs</i> "signature" logo and warm interior lighting, the concept attempted with varying degrees of success to meet the needs of the modern, more sophisticated (female) shopper. During the late 1990s, the stores which formed Storehouse Plc fell on hard times; BHS and <a href="/wiki/Mothercare" title="Mothercare">Mothercare</a> were the worst affected. Following a number of years of tough trading, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Green" title="Philip Green">Philip Green</a> bought BHS from Storehouse Plc in May 2000 for £200&#160;million. He then changed the company from public (Plc) to private (Ltd). In 2002, Green went on to acquire the <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_Group" title="Arcadia Group">Arcadia Group</a> of high street retailers, which included <a href="/wiki/Topshop" title="Topshop">Topshop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burton_(clothing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burton (clothing)">Burton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evans_(clothing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Evans (clothing)">Evans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Perkins" title="Dorothy Perkins">Dorothy Perkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wallis_(retailer)" title="Wallis (retailer)">Wallis</a> among others, to form Britain's second largest clothes retailer, after <a href="/wiki/Marks_and_Spencer" class="mw-redirect" title="Marks and Spencer">Marks and Spencer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Alan Smith, chairman of Storehouse at the time of the Bhs sale, commented, "He [Philip Green] had a crystal-clear vision and strategy. He had the guts to do the deal, to make it work when nobody else thought he could."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_British_Home_Stores">Return to British Home Stores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to British Home Stores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2005, Green, owner of BHS, purchased Etam UK from its French owner, <a href="/wiki/Etam_D%C3%A9veloppement" title="Etam Développement">Etam Développement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Etam UK brands included Etam, Amelie May, and Tammy. The girls' fashion retailer Tammy was the strongest brand in terms of sales and consumer recognition. For this reason, and to help improve girls' perception of BHS as a whole, from September 2005 stand-alone stores were closed and the brand integrated into BHS stores.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road,_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg/220px-British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg/330px-British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg/440px-British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road%2C_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="361" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_Homes_Stores_-_Bridge_Road,_Kirkstall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_597968.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A now closed branch in 'British Home Stores' branding in <a href="/wiki/Kirkstall" title="Kirkstall">Kirkstall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, BHS resurrected its British Home Stores fascia, more than 20 years after it had disappeared from the UK high street.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The move followed the purchase of several former <a href="/wiki/Allders" title="Allders">Allders at Home</a> sites from the department store chain. These projects were designed to build upon the success of the homewares and lighting that BHS stores currently offered and to tap into new areas of business such as furniture, curtains, rugs, and wall art. Brands sold included Denby, Maxwell Williams, Typhoon, Brabantia, Terence Conran and Jasper Conran.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 2005, Green's wife, a <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> resident, received a £1.2 billion <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a> from Arcadia.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In early 2006, Green considered a sale of the business, contacting rivals including <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debenhams" title="Debenhams">Debenhams</a>. A joint venture was also considered as an option alongside an outright sale, but did not progress due to the tough condition of the market at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2007, there were rumours of talks with Icelandic retail investor <a href="/wiki/Baugur_Group" title="Baugur Group">Baugur Group</a> regarding a sale of the chain and that Green was hoping to raise around £450 million from selling the chain which would then be used towards the continuation of Arcadia's Topshop international roll-out. Analysts said Green was in a dilemma over whether he should sell the chain to rivals of his other Arcadia chains.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 27 February 2009, it was announced that the company would be integrated into the Arcadia Group. Central support functions were merged and selected BHS stores housed selected Arcadia brands; for example, in July 2009, BHS stores in <a href="/wiki/Solihull" title="Solihull">Solihull</a> in the West Midlands and <a href="/wiki/Bexleyheath" title="Bexleyheath">Bexleyheath</a> in South London<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> both opened Evans and Wallis concessions. In August 2009, the Canterbury branch opened Wallis and Evans concessions within the store. Other stores with Arcadia insertions included Tunbridge Wells, Oxford, Peterborough, Watford, Kilmarnock, Nottingham, Camberley, Norwich and Aberdeen.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership_changes">Leadership changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leadership changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BHSLogo.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BHSLogo.svg/150px-BHSLogo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BHSLogo.svg/225px-BHSLogo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/BHSLogo.svg/300px-BHSLogo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BHSLogo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Logo used from 2010 to 2015 (2010-2013 variant had no stripe)</div></div></div>
<p>Mike Goring was appointed managing director to the chain in May 2009, and in July, Jacquie Gray was appointed Creative Director. In 2010, BHS changed its logo, resurrecting the uppercase form of the abbreviation that had not been used since the Storehouse rebrand and the later rebrand in the 1990s. A new e-commerce website was launched, and a new store design was gradually introduced across the estate.
</p><p>Goring left BHS in 2012 to take up the position of Retail Director for <a href="/wiki/Debenhams" title="Debenhams">Debenhams</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> After he left, former <a href="/wiki/Marks_and_Spencer" class="mw-redirect" title="Marks and Spencer">Marks and Spencer</a> Menswear Trading Director, Richard Price was appointed managing director.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Gray left in 2014.
</p><p>In January 2015, Green confirmed that he was considering selling the company following sustained losses, and that he had received a number of approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_and_restructuring">Sale and restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sale and restructuring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 12 March 2015, it was announced that BHS had been sold to Retail Acquisitions for a nominal price of £1.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It was also confirmed that Richard Price had left his post as managing director for a position with the clothing arm of <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>; former BHS <a href="/wiki/Chief_Operating_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Operating Officer">Chief Operating Officer</a> Darren Topp was confirmed as the interim Chief Executive.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Shortly after the takeover, 51 of the company's 171 stores were reported as being under threat of closure.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BHS,_High_Street,_Lincoln_(13th_December_2015).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG/220px-BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG/330px-BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG/440px-BHS%2C_High_Street%2C_Lincoln_%2813th_December_2015%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="5129" data-file-height="3414" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BHS,_High_Street,_Lincoln_(13th_December_2015).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A branch with post-2015 branding seen in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a> in 2015.</div></div></div>
<p>In September 2015, BHS owners Retail Acquisitions announced another rebrand, as part of a £60m rejuvenation plan, which resulted in the full British Home Stores name being resurrected on the high street for the first time since 1986. A programme of modernising stores with the new branding was announced, while plans to roll out food halls were to continue.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By early 2016, periodic store closures had seen the company withdraw entirely from several city centres including Bath,<sup id="cite_ref-2015closurelist_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2015closurelist-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Cardiff, Carlisle,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Oxford,<sup id="cite_ref-2015closurelist_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2015closurelist-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Reading<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and Southampton.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Plans were also being made to reduce the size of the flagship Oxford Street branch by leasing excess space to other retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, the company sought a <a href="/wiki/Company_voluntary_arrangement" title="Company voluntary arrangement">company voluntary arrangement</a> (CVA) to allow it to restructure the business.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> As part of its application, it revealed a deficit in its pension scheme of £207 million, and sought to transfer its schemes to the <a href="/wiki/Pension_Protection_Fund" title="Pension Protection Fund">Pension Protection Fund</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> A consortium lead by banker <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_de_Scossa" title="Nicholas de Scossa">Nicholas de Scossa</a> was involved in negotiations to buy the company.<sup id="cite_ref-times_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration_and_closure">Administration and closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Administration and closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BHSChester3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/BHSChester3.jpg/220px-BHSChester3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/BHSChester3.jpg/330px-BHSChester3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/BHSChester3.jpg/440px-BHSChester3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BHSChester3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BHS on Foregate Street, <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> in July 2016, during their closing down sale</div></div></div>

<p>On 24 April 2016, <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Chappell" title="Dominic Chappell">Dominic Chappell</a> (owner of Retail Acquisitions) announced that <a href="/wiki/Administration_(law)" title="Administration (law)">administrators</a> would be appointed the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-ft.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It was announced that the chain had entered administration on 25 April 2016, putting 11,000 jobs at risk. <a href="/wiki/Duff_%26_Phelps" title="Duff &amp; Phelps">Duff &amp; Phelps</a> were appointed administrators and sought to sell the business as a going concern. It at this point had debts of £1.3 billion including £571 million in pension liabilities, meaning either individual assets (such as stores) would have to be sold or the chain would be in new ownership. UK sports chain <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct" title="Sports Direct">Sports Direct</a> was reported to be in talks to buy a number of BHS stores, but no buyer was found for the company, resulting in the closure of BHS stores. Head office staff were made redundant on the announcement that no buyer had been found, with stores expected to last up to 8 weeks selling the remaining stock.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On 23 July 2016, the administrators Duff and Phelps shut 20 stores, and the next week another 30.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Closure of the final outlets was on 28 August 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The insolvent part of the company finally went into liquidation on 2 December 2016, with the remainder of winding up proceedings commencing on that date.
</p>

<p>The Qatari Al Mana Group purchased the company's international franchise stores and online operations in June 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The group formed a new business, BHS International (UK) Limited, based in London. It launched a new website, bhs.com, under the new brand name "The British Home Store" in September 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Al Mana announced that BHS.com will close by June 27, 2018, shifting focus to their international franchise business.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clothing">Clothing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Clothing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2014, New Zealand-based childrenswear brand <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin_Patch_(retailer)" title="Pumpkin Patch (retailer)">Pumpkin Patch</a> was introduced into larger stores and online.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Furniture">Furniture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Furniture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company had an independent furniture website, bhsfurniture.co.uk.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The stores and website sold a number of leading brands including <a href="/wiki/Natuzzi" title="Natuzzi">Italsofa</a>, <a href="/wiki/G-Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="G-Plan">G-Plan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Relyon" title="Relyon">Relyon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silentnight" title="Silentnight">Silentnight</a>. Larger homes stores, such as the <a href="/wiki/Trafford_Centre" title="Trafford Centre">Barton Square</a> branch in the Trafford Centre, Greater Manchester, also sold the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Welle_High_Five&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Welle High Five (page does not exist)">Welle</a> cabinet range and had <a href="/wiki/Sharps_Bedrooms" title="Sharps Bedrooms">Sharps</a> showrooms.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_goods">Electrical goods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electrical goods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its stores, BHS sold a mixture of basic electricals based primarily on kitchen products such as kettles and toasters. Latterly the range had begun to increase, particularly in the designated Home stores and larger high street branches. Ranges introduced included <a href="/wiki/Breville" title="Breville">Breville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russell_Hobbs" title="Russell Hobbs">Russell Hobbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/De%27Longhi" title="De&#39;Longhi">De'Longhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vax_(vacuum)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vax (vacuum)">Vax</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In addition, BHS began to sell larger electrical items through a separate website, bhsdirect.co.uk. The service was run through a third party company, Buy it Direct, and was not directly controlled by BHS.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> This allowed the company to expand its product range to laptops, tablets, large kitchen appliances such as fridge-freezers, TVs and air-conditioners.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_store">Food store</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Food store">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In January 2014, it was announced that the chain would sell branded food products; the service was to be trialled in 50 stores with the intention of making this a permanent addition in up to 150 stores.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The first of three trial stores opened in <a href="/wiki/Staines" class="mw-redirect" title="Staines">Staines</a> in March 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and was shortly followed by another in <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a> and a third in <a href="/wiki/Romford" title="Romford">Romford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_franchises">International franchises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Home_Stores&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: International franchises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The BHS brand has been franchised since 1985 to stores around the world and, although they are not directly owned, products and support were supplied by BHS. The Tammy brand was available as a separate franchise. In early 2006 a new franchise, "Bhs Kids", was launched in the Middle East, carrying many best-selling children's lines from BHS stores.
</p><p>BHS was the first high street retailer in 1995 to open a branch in Moscow. The £3&#160;million venture was the largest in the international portfolio and was quickly followed by the opening of a second store in <a href="/wiki/St_Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petersburg">St Petersburg</a>. Further stores opened in Uzbekistan and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> in 1998. The Middle East remained the key focus, with stores in Saudi Arabia, Kuwait and Dubai, Oman, Qatar, Bahrain and Abu Dhabi. By 2000 the chain also had stores in Greece, Tenerife, Gibraltar, Malta and the Far East. In 2011, a store in Armenia opened.  In 2013, a franchised concession opened in the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and a store was opened in <a href="/wiki/Ulaanbaatar" title="Ulaanbaatar">Ulaanbaatar</a>, Mongolia.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
