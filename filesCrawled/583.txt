<p><b>Princess Helena of Waldeck and Pyrmont</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (Helene Friederike Auguste; later Duchess of Albany; 17 February 1861 – 1 September 1922), who became a member of the <a href="/wiki/British_royal_family" title="British royal family">British royal family</a> by marriage, was the fifth daughter and child of <a href="/wiki/George_Victor,_Prince_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont" title="George Victor, Prince of Waldeck and Pyrmont">George Victor, Prince of Waldeck and Pyrmont</a> and his first wife, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Helena_of_Nassau" title="Princess Helena of Nassau">Princess Helena of Nassau</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Helena_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was born in	<a href="/wiki/Arolsen" class="mw-redirect" title="Arolsen">Arolsen</a>, capital of <a href="/wiki/Waldeck_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Waldeck (state)">Waldeck</a> principality, in Germany.
She was the sister of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich,_Prince_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont" title="Friedrich, Prince of Waldeck and Pyrmont">Friedrich</a>, last reigning Prince of Waldeck and Pyrmont; <a href="/wiki/Marie_of_Waldeck-Pyrmont" class="mw-redirect" title="Marie of Waldeck-Pyrmont">Marie</a>, the first wife of <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="William II of Württemberg">William II of Württemberg</a>; and of <a href="/wiki/Emma_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont" title="Emma of Waldeck and Pyrmont">Emma</a>, Queen consort of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_the_Netherlands" title="William III of the Netherlands">William III of the Netherlands</a> (and mother of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmina_of_the_Netherlands" title="Wilhelmina of the Netherlands">Queen Wilhelmina</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Helena_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wedding_dress_of_Princess_Helena_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont" title="Wedding dress of Princess Helena of Waldeck and Pyrmont">Wedding dress of Princess Helena of Waldeck and Pyrmont</a></div>
<p>Along with Emma and a third sister, Pauline, Helena was considered as a second wife for their distant cousin <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_the_Netherlands" title="William III of the Netherlands">William III of the Netherlands</a>. She later met with another distant cousin <a href="/wiki/Prince_Leopold,_Duke_of_Albany" title="Prince Leopold, Duke of Albany">Prince Leopold, Duke of Albany</a>, youngest son of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, at the suggestion of his mother. The two became engaged in November 1881. 
</p><p>On 27 April 1882, Leopold and Helena married in <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_Chapel,_Windsor_Castle" class="mw-redirect" title="St. George&#39;s Chapel, Windsor Castle">St. George's Chapel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">Windsor Castle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After their wedding, Leopold and Helena resided at <a href="/wiki/Claremont_(country_house)" title="Claremont (country house)">Claremont House</a>.  The couple had a brief, but happy marriage, ending in the <a href="/wiki/Hemophiliac" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemophiliac">hemophiliac</a> Leopold's death from a fall in Cannes, France, in March 1884.  At the time of Leopold's death, Helena was pregnant with their second child.
</p><p>The couple had two children:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Alice,_Countess_of_Athlone" title="Princess Alice, Countess of Athlone">Princess Alice of Albany</a> (1883-1981), later Countess of Athlone</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward,_Duke_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Charles Edward, Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Prince Charles, Duke of Albany</a> (1884-1954), born posthumously, later Duke of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personality_and_social_work">Personality and social work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Helena_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personality and social work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the memoirs of Helena's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Alice,_Countess_of_Athlone" title="Princess Alice, Countess of Athlone">Princess Alice of Albany</a>, Helena was very intelligent, had a strong sense of duty, and a genuine love of welfare work. <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, initially worried that Helena might turn out to be a stereotypically-remote German Princess, remarked in a letter to her eldest daughter, <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Princess_Royal" title="Victoria, Princess Royal">German Crown Princess Victoria</a>, that she was pleased Helena liked 'to go among the people.' The Queen soon came to regard her young daughter-in-law with great respect and affection, notwithstanding her initial concerns upon hearing from the match-making Vicky that Helena was an "intellectual", being unusually well-educated for a princess. Before her marriage, Helena's father had made her superintendent of the infant schools in his principality, and in this position the Princess had devised the pupils' educational curriculum. Helena particularly enjoyed solving mathematical problems and reading philosophy: during their tragically brief marriage, Prince Leopold proudly introduced his wife to the circle of academics he'd befriended at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>. Helena maintained these friendships for the rest of her life.
</p><p>In 1894, Helena was one of the founders of the Deptford Fund.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Originally dedicated to helping find alternative work for women and girls employed in the dangerous cattle slaughter business, the Fund soon expanded, with many projects instigated to help the local community. In 1899 Helena opened the Albany Institute.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This later expanded into a combined community/performance centre with the theatre venue known as the Albany Empire. A centre of 1970s anti-fascist activity and <a href="/wiki/Rock_Against_Racism" title="Rock Against Racism">Rock Against Racism</a>, the Empire and Institute buildings were destroyed in an arson attack in 1978. A new <a href="/wiki/Albany_Theatre" title="Albany Theatre">Albany Theatre</a> was opened by <a href="/wiki/Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Diana, Princess of Wales">the Princess of Wales</a> in 1982 and the Deptford Fund continues to this day.
</p><p>Helena was also involved in several hospital charities and with those dedicated to ending human trafficking. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, she organised much of her charity work along with that of her sister-in-law <a href="/wiki/Princess_Beatrice_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Princess Beatrice of the United Kingdom">Princess Beatrice</a> and husband's niece <a href="/wiki/Princess_Marie_Louise_of_Schleswig-Holstein" title="Princess Marie Louise of Schleswig-Holstein">Princess Marie-Louise</a> to avoid the not-uncommon problem of conflicting (and sometimes misguided) royal war-work projects.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Princess_Helena_of_Waldeck_and_Pyrmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Leopold's death, Helena and her two children, Alice and Charles Edward, continued to reside at Claremont House. After the death of her nephew, the <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Hereditary_Prince_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="Alfred, Hereditary Prince of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">Hereditary Prince of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a> in 1899, Helena's sixteen-year-old son was selected as the new heir to the German duchy, and was parted from his mother and sister in order to take up residence there. When the First World War broke out 14 years later, Charles Edward found himself fighting in the German Army. As a result, he was stripped of his British titles by an <a href="/wiki/Titles_Deprivation_Act_1917" title="Titles Deprivation Act 1917">act of Parliament in 1917</a>. By contrast, her daughter <a href="/wiki/Princess_Alice,_Countess_of_Athlone" title="Princess Alice, Countess of Athlone">Alice</a> remained in England and became a sister-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Teck" title="Mary of Teck">Queen Mary</a>, consort of <a href="/wiki/King_George_V" class="mw-redirect" title="King George V">King George V</a>.
</p><p>In 1901, it was speculated that Helena would marry <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Primrose,_5th_Earl_of_Rosebery" title="Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery">Archibald Primrose, 5th Earl of Rosebery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Helena died on 1 September 1922 of a heart attack in Hinterriss in Tyrol, Austria, while visiting her son.
</p><p>Through her son Charles Edward, Helena is the great-grandmother of <a href="/wiki/Carl_XVI_Gustaf_of_Sweden" title="Carl XVI Gustaf of Sweden">Carl XVI Gustav</a>, the current King of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.
</p>
