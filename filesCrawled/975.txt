<p><b>Professor</b> (commonly abbreviated as <b>Prof.</b><sup id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is an academic rank at <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Post-secondary_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-secondary education">post-secondary education</a> and research institutions in most countries. Literally, <i>professor</i> derives from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as a "person who professes" being usually an <a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">expert</a> in arts or sciences, a teacher of the highest rank.<sup id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In most systems of <a href="/wiki/List_of_academic_ranks" title="List of academic ranks">academic ranks</a> the word "professor" only refers to the most senior academic position, sometimes informally known as "full professor".<sup id="cite_ref-macleans.ca_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macleans.ca-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  In some countries or institutions, the word "professor" is also used in titles of lower ranks such as <a href="/wiki/Associate_professor" title="Associate professor">associate professor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assistant_professor" title="Assistant professor">assistant professor</a>; this is particularly the case in the United States, where the word professor is sometimes used <a href="/wiki/Colloquially" class="mw-redirect" title="Colloquially">colloquially</a> to refer to anyone in an academic post.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  This colloquial usage would be considered incorrect among most other academic communities.  However, the unqualified title Professor designated with a capital letter usually refers to a full professor also in English language usage.
</p><p>Professors often conduct <a href="/wiki/Original_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Original research">original research</a> and commonly teach <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate" class="mw-redirect" title="Undergraduate">undergraduate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Professional_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Professional school">professional</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate</a> courses in their fields of expertise.  In universities with <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate schools</a>, professors may mentor and supervise graduate students conducting research for a <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis or dissertation</a>.  In many universities, 'full professors' take on senior managerial roles, leading departments, research teams and institutes, and filling roles such as <a href="/wiki/University_president" class="mw-redirect" title="University president">president</a>, principal or <a href="/wiki/Vice-chancellor" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice-chancellor">vice-chancellor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-prom_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prom-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The role of professor may be more public facing than that of more junior staff, and professors are expected to be national or international leaders in their field of expertise.<sup id="cite_ref-prom_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prom-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Socrates_Louvre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/150px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/225px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Socrates_Louvre.jpg/300px-Socrates_Louvre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Socrates_Louvre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Ancient Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> was one of the earliest recorded professors.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The term "professor" was first used in the late 14th century to mean "one who teaches a branch of knowledge".<sup id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The word comes "...from Old French professeur (14c.) and directly from [the] Latin professor[, for] 'person who professes to be an expert in some art or science; teacher of highest rank'"; the Latin term came from the "...<a href="/wiki/Agent_noun" title="Agent noun">agent noun</a> from <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/profiteri" class="extiw" title="wikt:profiteri">profiteri</a> 'lay claim to, declare openly'." As a title that is "prefixed to a name, it dates from 1706". The "[s]hort form prof is recorded from 1838". The term "professor" is also used with a different meaning: "[o]ne professing religion. This canting use of the word comes down from the Elizabethan period, but is obsolete in England."<sup id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A professor is an accomplished and recognized academic. In most <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> nations, as well as northern Europe, the title <i>professor</i> is the highest academic rank at a university. In the <a href="/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States" title="Professors in the United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academic_ranks_in_Canada#Professorship" title="Academic ranks in Canada">Canada</a>, the title of <i>professor</i> applies to most post-doctoral academics, so a larger percentage are thus designated.  In these areas, professors are scholars with doctorate degrees (typically <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> degrees) or equivalent qualifications who teach in four-year colleges and <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>.  An <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">emeritus</a> professor is a title given to selected retired professors with whom the university wishes to continue to be associated due to their stature and ongoing research.  Emeritus professors do not receive a salary, but they are often given office or lab space, and use of libraries, labs, and so on.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The term professor is also used in the titles <i><a href="/wiki/Assistant_professor" title="Assistant professor">assistant professor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Associate_professor" title="Associate professor">associate professor</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> which are not considered professor-level positions in all European countries.  In Australia, the title <i>associate professor</i> is used in place of the term <a href="/wiki/Reader_(academic_rank)" title="Reader (academic rank)"><i>reader</i></a> as used in the United Kingdom and other Commonwealth countries; ranking above senior lecturer and below full professor.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond holding the proper academic title, universities in many countries also give notable artists, athletes and foreign dignitaries the title <i><a href="/wiki/Honorary_title_(academic)" title="Honorary title (academic)">honorary</a> professor</i>, even if these persons do not have the academic qualifications typically necessary for professorship and they do not take up professorial duties.  However, such "professors" usually do not undertake academic work for the granting institution.  In general, the title of professor is strictly used for academic positions rather than for those holding it on honorary basis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tasks">Tasks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tasks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg/220px-Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg/330px-Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg/440px-Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2108" data-file-height="2004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Toni_Morrison_2008-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Toni_Morrison" title="Toni Morrison">Toni Morrison</a>, Emeritus Professor at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Professors are qualified experts in their field who generally perform some or all the following tasks:
</p>
<ul><li>Managing <a href="/wiki/Teaching" class="mw-redirect" title="Teaching">teaching</a>, research and publications in their departments (in countries where a professor is head of a department);</li>
<li>Presenting <a href="/wiki/Lecture" title="Lecture">lectures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seminar" title="Seminar">seminars</a> in their specialties (i.e., they "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/profess" class="extiw" title="wikt:profess">profess</a>");</li>
<li>Performing, leading and publishing advanced <a href="/wiki/Original_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Original research">original research</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer-reviewed journal">journals</a> in their fields;</li>
<li>Providing <a href="/wiki/Pro_bono" title="Pro bono">pro bono</a> <a href="/wiki/Community_service" title="Community service">community service</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> including consulting functions (such as advising government and nonprofit organizations) or providing expert commentary on TV or radio news or public affairs programs;</li>
<li>Mentoring <a href="/wiki/Graduate_students" class="mw-redirect" title="Graduate students">graduate students</a> in their academic training;</li>
<li>Mentoring more junior academic staff;</li>
<li>Conducting administrative or managerial functions, usually at a high level (e.g. deans, heads of departments, research centers, etc.); and</li>
<li>Assessing students in their fields of expertise (e.g., through grading <a href="/wiki/Test_(assessment)" title="Test (assessment)">examinations</a> or <a href="/wiki/Thesis#Thesis_examinations" title="Thesis">viva voce</a> defenses).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<p>Other roles of professorial tasks depend on the institution, its legacy, protocols, place (country), and time.  For example, professors at research-oriented universities in North America and, generally, at European universities, are promoted primarily on the basis of research achievements and external grant-raising success.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salary">Salary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Salary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States#Salary" title="Professors in the United States">Professors in the United States §&#160;Salary</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Professor_salaries.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Professor_salaries.svg/220px-Professor_salaries.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Professor_salaries.svg/330px-Professor_salaries.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Professor_salaries.svg/440px-Professor_salaries.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="791" data-file-height="486" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Professor_salaries.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Salary of professors, as reported in the 2005 report the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_Hochschulverband&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deutscher Hochschulverband (page does not exist)">Deutscher Hochschulverband</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutscher_Hochschulverband" class="extiw" title="de:Deutscher Hochschulverband">de</a>&#93;</span> DHV. Bars are for assistant professor, associate professor and full professor, respectively.</div></div></div>
<p>A professor typically earns a base salary and a range of benefits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What benefits? (December 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In addition, a professor who undertakes additional roles in their institution (e.g., department chair, dean, head of graduate studies, etc.) earns additional income.  Some professors also earn additional income by moonlighting in other jobs, such as <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">consulting</a>, publishing academic or popular press books, giving speeches, or coaching executives. Some fields (e.g., business and computer science) give professors more opportunities for outside work.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany_and_Switzerland">Germany and Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Germany and Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A report from 2005 by the "Deutscher Hochschulverband DHV",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a lobby group for German professors, the salary of professors, the annual salary of a German professor is <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>46,680 in group "W2" (mid-level) and €56,683 in group "W3" (the highest level), without performance-related bonuses. The anticipated average earnings with performance-related bonuses for a German professor is €71,500. The anticipated average earnings of a professor working in Switzerland vary for example between 158,953 <a href="/wiki/CHF_(currency)" class="mw-redirect" title="CHF (currency)">CHF</a> (€102,729) to 232,073 CHF (€149,985) at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zurich" title="University of Zurich">University of Zurich</a> and 187,937 CHF (€121,461) to 247,280 CHF (€159,774) at the <a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">ETH Zurich</a>; the regulations are different depending on the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">Cantons of Switzerland</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Academic_ranks_in_Spain" title="Academic ranks in Spain">Academic ranks in Spain</a></div>
<p>The salaries of civil servant professors in Spain are fixed on a nationwide basis, but there are some bonuses related to performance and seniority and a number of bonuses granted by the Autonomous Regional governments. These bonuses include three-year premiums (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">trienios</i>, according to seniority), five-year premiums (<i lang="es">quinquenios</i>, according to compliance with teaching criteria set by the university) and six-year premiums (<i lang="es">sexenios</i>, according to compliance with research criteria laid down by the national government). These salary bonuses are relatively small. Nevertheless, the total number of <i lang="es">sexenios</i> is a prerequisite for being a member of different committees.
</p><p>The importance of these <i lang="es">sexenios</i> as a <a href="/wiki/Reputation" title="Reputation">prestige</a> factor in the university was enhanced by legislation in 2001 (LOU). Some indicative numbers can be interesting, in spite of the variance in the data. We report net monthly payments (after taxes and social security fees), without bonuses: <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ayudante" class="extiw" title="wikt:ayudante">Ayudante</a>, €1,200; <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ayudante_doctor" class="extiw" title="wikt:ayudante doctor">Ayudante Doctor</a>, €1,400; <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contratado_doctor" class="extiw" title="wikt:contratado doctor">Contratado Doctor</a>; €1,800; <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/professor_titular" class="extiw" title="wikt:professor titular">Professor Titular</a>, €2,000; <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/catedr%C3%A1tico" class="extiw" title="wikt:catedrático">Catedrático</a>, €2,400. There are a total of 14 payments per year, including 2 extra payments in July and December (but for less than a normal monthly payment).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_professors">Education professors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education professors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Professors in <a href="/wiki/Teacher_education" title="Teacher education">teacher education</a> sometimes earn less than they would if they were still elementary classroom teachers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2018)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In one case study report, it was shown that a beginning full-time tenure-track assistant professor in elementary teacher education at <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Northridge" title="California State University, Northridge">California State University, Northridge</a> was hired in 2002 at a salary of $53,000, which was $15,738 less than she would have earned in her previous position as a 9-month public school kindergarten teacher, $68,738.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

To improve comparability, adjustments have been made to correct for <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power" title="Purchasing power">purchasing power</a> and taxes. Because of differences between institutions in the US and UK these countries have two listings of which one denotes the salary in top-tier institutions (based on the <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_ranking" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanghai ranking">Shanghai-ranking</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_professor">Research professor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Research professor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a number of countries, the title "research professor" refers to a professor who is exclusively or mainly engaged in research, and who has few or no teaching obligations. For example, the title is used in this sense in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (where it is known as research professor at some universities and professorial research fellow at some other institutions) and in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>. Research professor is usually the most senior rank of a research-focused career pathway in those countries, and regarded as equal to the ordinary full professor rank. Most often they are permanent employees, and the position is often held by particularly distinguished scholars; thus the position is often seen as more prestigious than an ordinary full professorship. The title is used in a somewhat similar sense in the <a href="/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States#Research_professor" title="Professors in the United States">United States</a>, with the exception that research professors in the United States are often not permanent employees and often must fund their salary from external sources,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> which is usually not the case elsewhere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_professors" title="List of fictional professors">List of fictional professors</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg/220px-The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="416" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg/330px-The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg/440px-The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="1153" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Adventure_of_the_Final_Problem_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Professor_Moriarty" title="Professor Moriarty">Professor Moriarty</a> from the Sherlock Holmes story "<a href="/wiki/The_Final_Problem" title="The Final Problem">The Final Problem</a>"</div></div></div>
<p>Traditional fictional portrayals of professors, in accordance with a <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotype</a>, are shy, absent-minded individuals often lost in thought. In many cases, fictional professors are socially or physically awkward. Examples include the 1961 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Absent-Minded_Professor" title="The Absent-Minded Professor">The Absent-Minded Professor</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Professor_Calculus" title="Professor Calculus">Professor Calculus</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">The Adventures of Tintin</a></i> stories. Professors have also been portrayed as being misguided into an evil pathway, such as Professor Metz, who helped <a href="/wiki/Bond_villain" class="mw-redirect" title="Bond villain">Bond villain</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Stavro_Blofeld" title="Ernst Stavro Blofeld">Blofeld</a> in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Diamonds_Are_Forever_(film)" title="Diamonds Are Forever (film)">Diamonds Are Forever</a></i>; or simply evil, like <a href="/wiki/Professor_Moriarty" title="Professor Moriarty">Professor Moriarty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archenemy" title="Archenemy">archenemy</a> of British detective <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a>. The modern animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i> has <a href="/wiki/Professor_Hubert_Farnsworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Professor Hubert Farnsworth">Professor Hubert Farnsworth</a>, a typical absent-minded but genius-level professor. A related stereotype is the <a href="/wiki/Mad_scientist" title="Mad scientist">mad scientist</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>, author and professor of <a href="/wiki/English_studies" title="English studies">English</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell</a>, frequently used professors as the <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonists</a> in his novels. Professor <a href="/wiki/Henry_Higgins_(character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Higgins (character)">Henry Higgins</a> is a main character in <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_(play)" title="Pygmalion (play)">Pygmalion</a></i>. In the <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> series, set at the wizard school <a href="/wiki/Hogwarts" title="Hogwarts">Hogwarts</a>, the teachers are known as professors, many of whom play important roles, notably Professors <a href="/wiki/Dumbledore" class="mw-redirect" title="Dumbledore">Dumbledore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minerva_McGonagall" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerva McGonagall">McGonagall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Severus_Snape" title="Severus Snape">Snape</a>. In the board game <i><a href="/wiki/Cluedo" title="Cluedo">Cluedo</a></i>, Professor Plum has been depicted as an absent-minded academic. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lloyd" title="Christopher Lloyd">Christopher Lloyd</a> played Plum's <a href="/wiki/Clue_(film)" title="Clue (film)">film</a> counterpart, a <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologist</a> who had an affair with one of his patients.
</p><p>Since the 1980s and 1990s, various stereotypes were re-evaluated, including professors. Writers began to depict professors as just normal human beings and might be quite well-rounded in abilities, excelling both in intelligence and in physical skills. An example of a fictional professor not depicted as shy or absent-minded is <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones" title="Indiana Jones">Indiana Jones</a>, a professor as well as an <a href="/wiki/Archeologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Archeologist">archeologist</a>-<a href="/wiki/Adventurer" class="mw-redirect" title="Adventurer">adventurer</a>, who is skilled at both scholarship and fighting. The popularity of the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_(franchise)" title="Indiana Jones (franchise)"><i>Indiana Jones</i> movie franchise</a> had a significant impact on the previous stereotype, and created a new archetype which is both deeply knowledgeable and physically capable. The character generally referred to simply as <a href="/wiki/The_Professor_(Gilligan%27s_Island)" title="The Professor (Gilligan&#39;s Island)">the Professor</a> on the television <a href="/wiki/Sit_com" class="mw-redirect" title="Sit com">sit com</a> series, <i><a href="/wiki/Gilligan%27s_Island" title="Gilligan&#39;s Island">Gilligan's Island</a></i>, although described alternatively as a high-school science teacher or research scientist, is depicted as a sensible advisor, a clever inventor, and a helpful friend to his fellow <a href="/wiki/Castaway" title="Castaway">castaways</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Houseman" title="John Houseman">John Houseman</a>'s portrayal of law school professor Charles W. Kingsfield, Jr., in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Paper_Chase_(film)" title="The Paper Chase (film)">The Paper Chase</a></i> (1973) remains the <a href="/wiki/Epitome" title="Epitome">epitome</a> of the strict, authoritarian professor who demands perfection from students. <a href="/wiki/Annalise_Keating" title="Annalise Keating">Annalise Keating</a> (played by <a href="/wiki/Viola_Davis" title="Viola Davis">Viola Davis</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">American Broadcasting Company</a> (ABC) <a href="/wiki/Legal_drama" title="Legal drama">legal drama</a> <a href="/wiki/Mystery_(fiction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mystery (fiction)">mystery</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/How_to_Get_Away_with_Murder" title="How to Get Away with Murder">How to Get Away with Murder</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> professor at the fictional Middleton University.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Early in the series, Annalise is a self-sufficient and confident woman, respected for being a great law professor and a great lawyer, feared and admired by her students,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> whose image breaks down as the series progresses.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mysterious, older men with magical powers (and unclear academic standing) are sometimes given the title of "Professor" in literature and theater. Notable examples include Professor Marvel in <i>The Wizard of Oz</i><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and Professor Drosselmeyer (as he is sometimes known) from the ballet <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nutcracker" title="The Nutcracker">The Nutcracker</a></i>. Also, the magician played by Christian Bale in the film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prestige" title="The Prestige">The Prestige</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> adopts 'The Professor' as his <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a>. A variation of this type of non-academic professor is the "crackpot inventor", as portrayed by Professor Potts in the film version of <i><a href="/wiki/Chitty_Chitty_Bang_Bang" title="Chitty Chitty Bang Bang">Chitty Chitty Bang Bang</a></i> or the Jerry Lewis-inspired <i><a href="/wiki/Professor_Frink" title="Professor Frink">Professor Frink</a></i> character on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>. Other professors of this type are the thoughtful and kind Professor Digory Kirke of <a href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicles_of_Narnia" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronicles of Narnia">Chronicles of Narnia</a>.</i>
</p><p>The title has been used by <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedians</a>, such as "Professor" <a href="/wiki/Irwin_Corey" title="Irwin Corey">Irwin Corey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soupy_Sales" title="Soupy Sales">Soupy Sales</a> in his role as "The Big Professor". In the past, <a href="/wiki/Pianist" title="Pianist">pianists</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_saloon" title="Western saloon">saloons</a> and other rough environments have been called "professor".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Puppeteer" title="Puppeteer">puppeteer</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Punch_and_judy" class="mw-redirect" title="Punch and judy">Punch and Judy</a> show is also traditionally known as a "professor".
</p>
