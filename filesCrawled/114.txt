<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Guipuscoa</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">province of Spain</a> and a historical territory of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">autonomous community of the Basque Country</a>. Its capital city is <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">Donostia-San Sebastián</a>. Gipuzkoa shares borders with the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">department</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es-Atlantiques" title="Pyrénées-Atlantiques">Pyrénées-Atlantiques</a> at the northeast, with the province and autonomous community of <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a> at east, <a href="/wiki/Biscay" title="Biscay">Biscay</a> at west, <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lava" title="Álava">Álava</a> at southwest and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> to its north. It is located at the easternmost extreme of the <a href="/wiki/Cantabric_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantabric Sea">Cantabric Sea</a>, in the Bay of Biscay. It has 66 kilometres (41 miles) of coast land.
</p><p>With a total area of 1,980 square kilometres (760 square miles), Gipuzkoa is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_provinces_by_area" title="List of Spanish provinces by area">smallest province of Spain</a>. The province has 89 <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Spain" title="Municipalities of Spain">municipalities</a> and a population of 720,592 inhabitants (2018), from which more than half live in the <a href="/wiki/San_Sebastian_metropolitan_area" class="mw-redirect" title="San Sebastian metropolitan area">Donostia-San Sebastián metropolitan area</a>. Apart from the capital, other important cities are <a href="/wiki/Irun" title="Irun">Irun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Errenteria" title="Errenteria">Errenteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zarautz" title="Zarautz">Zarautz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mondrag%C3%B3n" title="Mondragón">Mondragón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eibar" title="Eibar">Eibar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">Hondarribia</a>, <a href="/wiki/O%C3%B1ati" title="Oñati">Oñati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tolosa,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Tolosa, Spain">Tolosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beasain" title="Beasain">Beasain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pasaia" title="Pasaia">Pasaia</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> gives the province an intense green colour with little thermic oscillation. Gipuzkoa is the province of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Country</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque language</a> is most extensively used: 49.1% of the population spoke Basque in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first recorded name of the province was <i>Ipuscoa</i> in a document from the year 1025.<sup id="cite_ref-etymology_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymology-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During the following years, and in various documents, several similar names appear, such as <i>Ipuzcoa</i>, <i>Ipuçcha</i>, <i>Ipuzka</i>, among others.
</p><p>The full etymology the word Gipuzkoa has not been fully ascertained, but links have been made with the Basque word <i>Giputz</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-etymology_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymology-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> containing the root <i>ip-</i> which is related to the word <i>ipar</i> (north), <i>ipurdi</i> (back) and <i>ipuin</i> (tale). According to this, <i>ipuzko</i> (one of the several first known denominations) might refer to something "to the north" or "in the north".<sup id="cite_ref-etymology_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymology-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denominations">Denominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Denominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gipuzkoa"><i>Gipuzkoa</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gipuzkoa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gipuzkoa is the <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> spelling recommended by the <a href="/wiki/Euskaltzaindia" title="Euskaltzaindia">Royal Academy of the Basque language</a>, and it is commonly used in official documents in that language. The Basque spelling is also mandatory in official texts from the various Spanish public administrations, even in documents written in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>. It is the spelling most frequently used by the Spanish-language media in the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>.
</p><p>It is also the spelling used in the Basque version of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1978" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Constitution of 1978">Spanish constitution</a> and in the Basque version of the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy_of_the_Basque_Country" title="Statute of Autonomy of the Basque Country">Statute of Autonomy of the Basque Country</a>. Gipuzkoa is also the only official spelling approved for the historical territory by the <a href="/wiki/Juntas_Generales" title="Juntas Generales">Juntas Generales</a> of the province.
</p>
<h3><span id="Guip.C3.BAzcoa"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Guipúzcoa"><i>Guipúzcoa</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Guipúzcoa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Guipúzcoa</i> is the spelling in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and it has been determined by the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Spanish_Language_Academies" class="mw-redirect" title="Association of Spanish Language Academies">Association of Spanish Language Academies</a> as being the only correct use outside official Spanish documents, where the use of the Basque spelling is mandatory.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup> It is also the Spanish spelling used in the Spanish version of the Constitution and in the Spanish version of the Statute of Autonomy of the Basque Country.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_demographics">Geography and demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography and demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At 1,980&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (764&#160;sq&#160;mi) Gipuzkoa is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_provinces_by_area" title="List of Spanish provinces by area">smallest province in Spain</a>. The province has 88 <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a> and 709,607 inhabitants (2011), a quarter of whom live in the capital, <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">San Sebastián</a>. Other important towns are <a href="/wiki/Irun" title="Irun">Irun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Errenteria" title="Errenteria">Errenteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zarautz" title="Zarautz">Zarautz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arrasate" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrasate">Arrasate</a>, <a href="/wiki/O%C3%B1ati" title="Oñati">Oñati</a> with an old university, <a href="/wiki/Eibar" title="Eibar">Eibar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tolosa,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Tolosa, Spain">Tolosa</a> which was the provincial capital for a short time, <a href="/wiki/Beasain" title="Beasain">Beasain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pasaia" title="Pasaia">Pasaia</a>, the main port and <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">Hondarribia</a>, an old fort town across from the French Atlantic coast.
</p><p>Gipuzkoa is hilly and green linking mountain and sea, and heavily populated with numerous urban nuclei that dot the whole territory. The conspicuous presence of hills and rugged terrain has added to a special leaning towards <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, nature and mountains on the part of Gipuzkoans. Some mountains have an emblematic or iconic significance in the local tradition, their summits being topped with crosses, memorials and mountaineer postboxes. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimages</a> which have gradually lost their former religious zeal and taken on a more secular slant are sometimes held to their summits. Some renowned mountains are <a href="/wiki/Aiako_Harria" class="mw-redirect" title="Aiako Harria">Aiako Harria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hernio" title="Hernio">Hernio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Txindoki" title="Txindoki">Txindoki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aizkorri" title="Aizkorri">Aizkorri</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Izarraitz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Izarraitz (page does not exist)">Izarraitz</a>, amongst others.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Aralar_Natural_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Aralar Natural Park">Aralar Natural Park</a> is a conservation area on the border of Gipuzkoa and Navarre in the <a href="/wiki/Aralar_Range" title="Aralar Range">Aralar Range</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rivers of Gipuzkoa are distinctly different from other <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">Bay of Biscay</a> rivers. They arise in the hilly <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque</a> inland landscape (<i>Basque Mountains</i>), flow in a south- north direction, forming close, narrow  valleys before joining the ocean. The rivers extend for a short length with only a small fluctuation in the volume of water thanks to the stable rainfall all year round, and they show an abrupt drop between origin and mouth as far as the length of the river is concerned. From west to east the rivers are the <a href="/wiki/Deba_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deba (river)">Deba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urola" class="mw-redirect" title="Urola">Urola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oria_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oria River">Oria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urumea" title="Urumea">Urumea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oiartzun_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oiartzun River">Oiartzun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bidasoa" title="Bidasoa">Bidasoa</a>. Except for a narrow strip extending east from the hamlet <i>Otzaurte</i> (<a href="/wiki/Zegama" title="Zegama">Zegama</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/San_Adrian_(tunnel)" title="San Adrian (tunnel)">tunnel of San Adrian</a>, the province drains its waters to the Atlantic basin.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The region's communication layout is in step with its geographical features, with the main lines of infrastructure along a north -south axis up to recent times along the rivers heading to the ocean. Accordingly, the inland <a href="/wiki/Way_of_St._James" class="mw-redirect" title="Way of St. James">Way of St. James</a>, i.e. the Tunnel Route penetrated the province via <a href="/wiki/Irun" title="Irun">Irun</a> and turned south-west along the <a href="/wiki/Oria_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oria River">Oria River</a> towards the provincial limits at the tunnel of San Adrian. This stretch was in operation up to 1765 when the Royal Road moved to the western <a href="/wiki/Deba_(river)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deba (river)">Deba</a> Valley. A minor St. James route crossed Gipuzkoa east to west along the coast.
</p><p>Currently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2015)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the main road cutting through Gipuzkoa largely follows that layout, i.e. the N-1 E-5 from Irun to Donostia and on to <a href="/wiki/Altsasu" class="mw-redirect" title="Altsasu">Altsasu</a> all along the Oria River for the most part (gateway to Navarre through the pass of Etxegarate). Also, the major <a href="/wiki/RENFE" class="mw-redirect" title="RENFE">Irun-Madrid railway</a> runs close to the river up to its very origin on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Aizkorri" title="Aizkorri">Aizkorri</a>  at train stop Otzaurte in <a href="/wiki/Zegama" title="Zegama">Zegama</a>. By 1973 engineering works for the Bilbao-Behobia A-8 E-70 motorway had been completed, with the new road cutting across the valleys east to west and turning into the main axis between Donostia and Bilbao, besides enabling heavy load traffic to access the west and south of Spain as a toll road. The regional railway network <a href="/wiki/Euskotren" class="mw-redirect" title="Euskotren">Euskotren</a> also follows along the coast from east to west, while it proves impractical for long distance on the grounds of its tortuous layout and as it serves small towns. The railway network reaches the border town <a href="/wiki/Hendaia" class="mw-redirect" title="Hendaia">Hendaia</a> before it changes to <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF</a>. Within the <a href="/wiki/Donostialdea" title="Donostialdea">Donostialdea</a> region, the <a href="/wiki/Metro_Donostialdea" title="Metro Donostialdea">Metro Donostialdea</a> provides service.
</p><p>The stretch of the A-15 motorway serving Gipuzkoa and Pamplona opened in 1995 embroiled in controversy under protest and an attack campaign led by ecologists, <a href="/wiki/Batasuna" title="Batasuna">Basque leftist nationalists</a> and eventually ETA. Conflict resolved after a deal about the definitive layout was struck . In January 2010, following a scheme drawn up by the regional government of Gipuzkoa to improve provisions for the ever-increasing road traffic, the Maltzaga-Urbina AP-1 motorway stretch leading to <a href="/wiki/Vitoria,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitoria, Spain">Gasteiz</a> was completed, providing likewise access to the industrial areas of <a href="/wiki/Arrasate" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrasate">Arrasate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bergara" title="Bergara">Bergara</a>, the gateway to <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lava" title="Álava">Álava</a> by village Landa). AP-1 and N-1 E-5 are connected by a highway east to west from <a href="/wiki/Bergara" title="Bergara">Bergara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beasain" title="Beasain">Beasain</a>. European traffic to south of Spain avoids <a href="/wiki/Donostia" class="mw-redirect" title="Donostia">Donostia</a>'s metropolitan area by means of the southern outer road ring.
</p><p>Currently it is under construction the AVE <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed rail</a> with an Y-type layout and links to the SNCF network in Hendaia (NE), Vitoria (SE), Bilbao (W) and <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">Pamplona</a> (S) to be completeted by 2022. Contractors were appointed, works are in place although past schedule, while strong opposition (ecologists, Basque leftist nationalists,...) and serious financial tensions made its future uncertain.
</p><p>The only airport in Gipuzkoa serving just domestic flights is the <a href="/wiki/San_Sebastian_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="San Sebastian Airport">San Sebastian Airport</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">Hondarribia</a>, while the air transportation needs are usually served by the nearby <a href="/wiki/Bilbao_Airport" title="Bilbao Airport">Bilbao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biarritz_%E2%80%93_Anglet_%E2%80%93_Bayonne_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Biarritz – Anglet – Bayonne Airport">Biarritz</a> airports.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_traits">Cultural traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gipuzkoa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural traits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gipuzkera" class="mw-redirect" title="Gipuzkera">Gipuzkera</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque language</a> spoken in most of the region, shows a considerable vitality and holds a prominent position among other dialects. The Basque cultural element is apparent, including traditional dances and singing, <a href="/wiki/Bertsolaritza" title="Bertsolaritza">bertsolaritza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trikiti" title="Trikiti">trikiti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Txistu" title="Txistu">txistu</a> music, <a href="/wiki/Baserri" title="Baserri">baserris</a> dotting the rural landscape, town festivals, and its signature heavy sculptures (stone, steel, iron) from the industrial tradition, all blending with the latest Basque, Spanish and international pop culture events and design trends centred in major urban areas (<a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">Donostia</a>, Tolosa, etc.).
</p><p>Traditionally a Catholic province, its patron <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> are <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Loyola" title="Ignatius of Loyola">Ignatius of Loyola</a>, born in the neighborhood of Loyola (<a href="/wiki/Azpeitia" title="Azpeitia">Azpeitia</a>) and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Society of Jesus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_of_Arantzazu" title="Sanctuary of Arantzazu">Our Lady of Arantzazu</a>.
</p><p>The region has produced many famous Basque athletes for example: <a href="/wiki/Jose_Maria_Olazabal" class="mw-redirect" title="Jose Maria Olazabal">Jose Maria Olazabal</a> (golfer), <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_%C3%81ngel_Iribar" title="José Ángel Iribar">José Ángel Iribar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xabi_Alonso" title="Xabi Alonso">Xabi Alonso</a> (footballers), <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Olano" title="Abraham Olano">Abraham Olano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Perurena" title="Domingo Perurena">Domingo Perurena</a> (cyclists), <a href="/wiki/Edurne_Pasaban" title="Edurne Pasaban">Edurne Pasaban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberto_I%C3%B1urrategi" title="Alberto Iñurrategi">Alberto Iñurrategi</a> (mountaineers), <a href="/wiki/I%C3%B1aki_Urdangarin" title="Iñaki Urdangarin">Iñaki Urdangarin</a> (handball player), <a href="/wiki/Maite_Z%C3%BA%C3%B1iga" title="Maite Zúñiga">Maite Zúñiga</a> (runner) and <a href="/wiki/Paulino_Uzcudun" title="Paulino Uzcudun">Paulino Uzcudun</a> (boxer).
</p>
