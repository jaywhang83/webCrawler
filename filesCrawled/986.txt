<p>The <b>University of Kent</b> (formerly the <b>University of Kent at Canterbury</b>, abbreviated as <b>UKC</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_university" title="Collegiate university">semi-collegiate</a> <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Research_university" title="Research university">research university</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It was founded in 1965 and is recognised as a Beloff's <a href="/wiki/Plate_glass_university" title="Plate glass university">plate glass university</a>. The University was granted its <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> on 4 January 1965 and the following year <a href="/wiki/Princess_Marina_of_Greece_and_Denmark" title="Princess Marina of Greece and Denmark">Princess Marina, Duchess of Kent</a> was formally installed as the first <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin11-36_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin11-36-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The university has a rural campus north of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> situated within 300 acres (1.2&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of park land, housing over 6,000 students, as well as campuses in <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tonbridge" title="Tonbridge">Tonbridge</a> in Kent and European postgraduate centres in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The University is international, with students from 158 different nationalities and 41% of its academic and research staff being from outside the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2019, the University of Kent is ranked within the top 55 universities in the UK by the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/The_Complete_University_Guide" class="mw-redirect" title="The Complete University Guide">Complete University Guide</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and scores 85% for overall satisfaction in the 2018 <a href="/wiki/National_Student_Survey" title="National Student Survey">National Student Survey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  In 2016, over 28,000 students applied to the University through <a href="/wiki/UCAS" title="UCAS">UCAS</a> and 4000 accepted an offer.<sup id="cite_ref-hitcreative1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hitcreative1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Indeed, almost three-quarters of the work submitted for the 2014 research assessments by the University was judged to be world-leading or internationally excellent.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Santander_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Santander Network">Santander Network</a> of European universities encouraging social and economic development.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A university in the city of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> was first considered in 1947, when an anticipated growth in student numbers led several residents to seek the creation of a new university, including Kent. However, the plans never came to fruition.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin14_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin14-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A decade later both population growth and greater demand for university places led to a re-consideration. In 1959 the Education Committee of Kent County Council explored the creation of a new university,<sup id="cite_ref-about59_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about59-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> formally accepting the proposal unanimously on 24 February 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-times250280_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times250280-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Two months later the Education Committee agreed to seek a site at or near Canterbury, given the historical associations of the city, subject to the support of Canterbury City Council.<sup id="cite_ref-times260460_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times260460-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1962 a site was found at Beverley Farm, straddling the then boundary between the City of Canterbury and the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative county</a> of Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-times010262_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times010262-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The university's original name, chosen in 1962, was the <i>University of Kent at Canterbury</i>, reflecting the fact that the campus straddled the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Council" title="Kent County Council">Kent County Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-about59_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about59-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> At the time it was the normal practice for universities to be named after the town or city whose boundaries they were in, with both "University of Kent" and "University of Canterbury" initially proposed. The name adopted reflected the support of both the city and county authorities; as well as the existence of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Canterbury" title="University of Canterbury">University of Canterbury</a> in New Zealand, which officially opposed the use of a name too similar to its own.<sup id="cite_ref-times171062_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times171062-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The abbreviation "UKC"<b> </b>became a popular abbreviation for the university.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin29-30_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin29-30-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1965_to_2000">1965 to 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1965 to 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The University of Kent at Canterbury was granted its <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> on 4 January 1965 and the first batch of 500 students arrived in the October of that year. On 30 March 1966 <a href="/wiki/Princess_Marina_of_Greece_and_Denmark" title="Princess Marina of Greece and Denmark">Princess Marina, Duchess of Kent</a> was formally installed as the first <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin11-36_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin11-36-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University was envisaged as being a <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_university" title="Collegiate university">collegiate</a> establishment, with most students living in one of the colleges on campus, and as specialising in <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinarity" title="Interdisciplinarity">inter-disciplinary</a> studies in all fields.<sup id="cite_ref-times040463_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times040463-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Over the years, changes in government policy and other changing demands have largely destroyed this original concept, leading to the present state, which is nearer the norm for a British University. However, the four original colleges â Darwin, Eliot, Keynes and Rutherford â remain, together with the newer Woolf and Turing colleges, each with their own masters.<sup id="cite_ref-collegemasters_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegemasters-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The university grew at a rapid rate throughout the 1960s, with three colleges and many other buildings on campus being completed by the end of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-KentLife4_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KentLife4-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The 1970s saw further construction, but the university also encountered the biggest physical problem in its history.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin225-231_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin225-231-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The university had been built above a tunnel on the disused <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_and_Whitstable_Railway" title="Canterbury and Whitstable Railway">Canterbury and Whitstable Railway</a>. In July 1974 the tunnel collapsed, damaging part of the Cornwallis Building, which sank nearly a metre within about an hour on the evening of 11 July.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin228_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin228-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Fortunately, the university had insurance against subsidence, so it was able to pay for the south-west corner of the building to be demolished and replaced by a new wing at the other end of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin231_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin231-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Building elsewhere included the Park Wood accommodation village and the Darwin houses in 1989.
</p><p>In 1982 the university opened the University Centre at Tonbridge (now the University of Kent at Tonbridge) for its School of <a href="/wiki/Continuing_education" title="Continuing education">Continuing education</a>, helping to enhance the availability of teaching across the county.<sup id="cite_ref-KentLife5_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KentLife5-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1990s and 2000s the University expanded beyond its original campus, establishing campuses in <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonbridge" title="Tonbridge">Tonbridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, and partnerships with <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_College,_Kent" title="Canterbury College, Kent">Canterbury College</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Kent_College" class="mw-redirect" title="West Kent College">West Kent College</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Kent_College" title="South Kent College">South Kent College</a> and <a href="/wiki/MidKent_College" title="MidKent College">MidKent College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-KentLife5_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KentLife5-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_present">2000 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG/220px-School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG/330px-School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG/440px-School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:School_of_Arts_-_UKC.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The School of Arts Building at Kent's Canterbury campus; it is one of several buildings constructed by the university in recent years</div></div></div>
<p>In the 2000s the university entered a collaboration named <a href="/wiki/Universities_at_Medway" title="Universities at Medway">Universities at Medway</a> with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Greenwich" title="University of Greenwich">University of Greenwich</a>, <a href="/wiki/MidKent_College" title="MidKent College">MidKent College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Christ_Church_University" title="Canterbury Christ Church University">Canterbury Christ Church University</a> to deliver university provision in the <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a> area.<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This led to the development of the <i>University of Kent at Medway</i>, opened from 2001. Initially based at Mid-Kent College, a new joint campus opened in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Small postgraduate centres opened in Paris in 2009, and later in Rome and Athens.
</p><p>As a consequence of the expansion outside Canterbury the university's name was formally changed to the <i>University of Kent</i> on 1 April 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Part of the original reasoning for the name disappeared when <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">local government reforms in the 1970s</a> resulted in the Canterbury campus falling entirely within the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Canterbury" title="City of Canterbury">City of Canterbury</a>, which no longer has county borough status, and Kent County Council.
</p><p>In 2007 the university was rebranded with a new logo and website. The logo was chosen following consultation with existing university students and those in <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form" title="Sixth form">sixth forms</a> across the country.<sup id="cite_ref-identity_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University of Kent set its tuition fees for UK and European Union undergraduates at Â£9,000 for new entrants in 2012, which was approved by the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_Fair_Access" title="Office for Fair Access">Office for Fair Access</a> (OFFA). The fee was approved by Council on 1 April 2011 and was confirmed by OFFA in July 2011. The proposed changes to UK and EU undergraduate tuition fees did not apply to international student fees.
</p><p>Following the extension of Keynes College in 2001, two new colleges opened on the Canterbury campus, Woolf College for postgraduates in 2008 and Turing College for undergraduates in 2015. Several other new buildings were also added, including the Jarman School of Arts Building in 2009, the Colyer-Fergusson Music Building, a performing arts space, in 2012, and the Sibson building, housing maths and the business school, in 2017. A major Â£27m project to extend and refurbish the Templeman Library began in 2013, was completed in 2017 and formally opened in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Additional accommodation was provided for students at the Medway Campus with the completion of Liberty Quays in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-2000sBuildings_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2000sBuildings-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, the University held a number of events to celebrate its 50th anniversary. Festivals were held in Canterbury and Medway, a summer festival, the funding of twelve Beacon Projects and the temporary erection of a Ferris Wheel on the Canterbury campus.<sup id="cite_ref-50festival_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50festival-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, a consultation was launched on a masterplan for future development of the Canterbury campus.<sup id="cite_ref-masterplan_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-masterplan-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In March 2017 it was announced that, in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Christ_Church_University" title="Canterbury Christ Church University">Canterbury Christ Church University</a>, the University of Kent had been given funding to develop <a href="/wiki/Kent_and_Medway_Medical_School" title="Kent and Medway Medical School">Kent and Medway Medical School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UnivKentMedSchAnnounce_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UnivKentMedSchAnnounce-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campuses">Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Campuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canterbury_campus">Canterbury campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canterbury campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> campus covers 300 acres (1.2&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) and is situated in parkland in an elevated position just over two miles (3&#160;km) from the city centre, with views over the city and <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a> UNESCO world heritage site. The campus currently has approximately 12,000 full-time and 6,200 part-time students, with accommodation for over 5000, in addition to 600 academic and research staff. Residential and academic buildings are intermingled in the central part of the campus, science building are clustered west of Giles Lens and there is a dedicated student village on the western edge, several minutesâ walk from the main campus. The campus is ecologically diverse and home to a number of protected species, including Great Crested Newts. The North West of the site is heavily forested, including pockets of ancient woodland, while the Southern Slopes contain a mix of wildflower and hay meadows, and there are seven ponds spread across the campus.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG/220px-Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG/330px-Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG/440px-Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Campus_shops_-_UKC.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The shops at the Canterbury campus, with the Kent Union offices housed in the offices above.</div></div></div>
<p>The campus has a selection of shops, including a grocery store, bookshop, pharmacy and launderettes. Food and drink is provided by range of cafes and bars run either by the University or the student union. Bars include K-bar, in Keynes College, Mungoâs, in Eliot College, Origins, in Darwin College, and Woodyâs in the Park Wood Student Village. Cafeteria style food is available in Rutherford College, fine dining at the Beagle Restaurant in Darwin College, and food is served at the bars and other cafes around campus.
</p><p>The campus nightclub, The Venue, was refurbished and modernised in 2010 and is open Wednesday to Saturday. The upstairs area was originally used a live music venue, known as The Lighthouse and then the Attic, but has since been replaced with the Student Media Centre which hosts Inquire, KTV and CSR. Club nights and live music are also held at various bars on campus.
</p><p>Sporting facilities are spread across two main sites: the sports centre, which contains several multi-purpose sports halls, a fitness suite, squash courts and climbing wall, and the Sports Pavilion site, with a variety of indoor and outdoor sports pitches and training facilities, including 3G and astroturf.
</p><p>The Gulbenkian arts complex includes a theatre and cinema, as well as a small stage which hosts monthly comedy nights as well as occasional shows such as Jazz at Five and The Chortle Student Comedy Awards. The adjacent Colyer-Fergusson Building, which opened in 2013, includes an adaptable format concert/rehearsal hall with retractable seating and variable acoustics and practice rooms. The Gulbenkian Theatre seats 340 and presents student, professional and amateur shows throughout the year. The theatre was opened in 1969 and was named after the <a href="/wiki/Calouste_Gulbenkian_Foundation" title="Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation">Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation</a> which helped fund its construction. The Gulbenkian Cinema is an independent cinema in the Gulbenkian complex open to students and the general public. It is Kent's regional film theatre showing new mainstream and non-mainstream releases as well as archive and foreign language films. In the daytime the cinema is used as a lecture theatre for University students.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Gulbenkian complex also hosts a cafe/ bar and restaurant facility open to students, staff and the general public.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_and_access">Transport and access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transport and access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The campus is accessed by road from either the West, with two entrances on the A290 Whitstable Road, or the East, via St Stephenâs Hill. An off-road foot and cycle route connects the central campus to the Northern edge of the city, and a regular bus services (âUniBusâ) is also in operation, although with a more limited service outside of term time. The <a href="/wiki/A2_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A2 road (Great Britain)">A2</a> dual carriageway links the campus and city to London, the port at <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> and the national motorway network. The campus also lies at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Crab_and_Winkle_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Crab and Winkle Line">Crab and Winkle Way</a>, a 7-mile off-road foot and cycle path running through farm and woodland to the coastal fishing town of <a href="/wiki/Whitstable" title="Whitstable">Whitstable</a>, providing a link for cycle commuters.
</p><p>The closest railway station to the campus is <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Canterbury West">Canterbury West</a> which is, as of 2009, served by <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_(train_operating_company)" title="Southeastern (train operating company)">Southeastern</a> High Speed trains, connecting Canterbury with <a href="/wiki/London_St_Pancras_International" class="mw-redirect" title="London St Pancras International">London St Pancras International</a>  in 56 minutes. These services stop at <a href="/wiki/Ashford_International_railway_station" title="Ashford International railway station">Ashford International</a> en route, thus providing a direct connection to <a href="/wiki/Eurostar" title="Eurostar">Eurostar</a> services to France and Belgium. Standard Southeastern services also connect Canterbury West and <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Canterbury East">Canterbury East</a> Stations with London <a href="/wiki/London_Victoria_station" title="London Victoria station">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charing_Cross_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Charing Cross Station">Charing Cross</a>. Both of the Canterbury stations can be accessed by the UniBus service. The nearest international air services are provided from the London airports, <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow</a>, with indirect <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express coach</a> services to both from Canterbury bus station with one transfer at London Victoria coach station. The campus is also served by two coach services (Route 007) to/from London each day, with further services operating from Canterbury bus station.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medway_campus">Medway campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Medway campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000 the University joined with other educational institutes to form the "Universities for Medway" initiative, aimed at increasing participation in higher education in the <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway Towns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The following year the <i>University of Kent at Medway</i> formally opened, initially based at <a href="/wiki/Mid-Kent_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-Kent College">Mid-Kent College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> By 2004 a new campus for the university had been established in the old <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Dockyard" title="Chatham Dockyard">Chatham Dockyard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-about00_29-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about00-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> sharing a campus with <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Christchurch_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Canterbury Christchurch University">Canterbury Christchurch University</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Greenwich" title="University of Greenwich">University of Greenwich</a>.
</p><p>The University of Kent and Medway Park Leisure Centre have gone into a multimillion-pound partnership to provide high quality leisure facilities for university students and the general public. Medway Park (formerly the Black Lion Leisure Centre) was re-opened in 2011 by <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Princess_Royal" title="Anne, Princess Royal">Princess Anne</a> for use as a training venue for the 2012 London Olympics, as well as a training venue for the Egyptian and Congo National teams.
</p><p>The campus accommodation was finished in late 2009 (called Liberty Quays), and caters for over 600 students. The accommodation building includes a <a href="/wiki/Tesco_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Tesco Express">Tesco Express</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subway_(restaurant)" title="Subway (restaurant)">Subway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Domino%27s_Pizza" title="Domino&#39;s Pizza">Domino's Pizza</a>, and Cargo, a bar showing sports, live music and entertainment
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonbridge_campus">Tonbridge campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tonbridge campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982 the university established the School of <a href="/wiki/Continuing_education" title="Continuing education">continuing education</a> in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Tonbridge" title="Tonbridge">Tonbridge</a>, extending its coverage to the entire county of Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-about80_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about80-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Many buildings were added in the 1980s and 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-KentLife5_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KentLife5-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The campus is now called the <i>University of Kent at Tonbridge</i>. It collaborates with the Kent Business School and Kent Innovation and Enterprise.

<p>The original plan was to have no academic sub-divisions within the three faculties (initially Humanities, Social Sciences and Natural Sciences) and to incorporate an interdisciplinary element to all degrees through common first year courses ("Part I") in each faculty, followed by specialist study in the second and final years ("Part II").<sup id="cite_ref-times040463_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times040463-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The lack of Departments encouraged the development of courses that crossed traditional divides, such as Chemical Physics, Chemistry with Control Engineering, Biological Chemistry and Environmental Physical Science.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin39-54_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin39-54-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the interdisciplinary approach proved increasingly complex for two reasons. The levels of specialisation at <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">A Levels</a> meant that many students had not studied particular subjects for some years and this made it impossible to devise a course that both covered areas unstudied by some and did not bore others. This proved an especial problem in Natural Sciences, where many Mathematics students had not studied Chemistry at A Level and vice versa.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg/220px-Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg/330px-Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg/440px-Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kent_Business_School_-_UKC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kent_Business_School" title="Kent Business School">Kent Business School</a></div></div></div><p> Additionally many subjects, particularly those in the Social Sciences, were not taught at A Level and required the first year as a grounding in the subject rather than an introduction to several different new subjects. Problems were especially encountered in the Faculty of Natural Sciences where the differing demands of Mathematics and physical sciences led to two almost completely separate programmes and student bases.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin39-54_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin39-54-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In 1970 this led to the creation of the School of Mathematical Studies, standing outside the Faculties.<sup id="cite_ref-about70_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about70-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The addition of other subjects led to increased pressure on common Part I programmes and increasingly students took more specialised Part I courses designed to prepare them for Part II study.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin39-54_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin39-54-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Substantial change to this structure did not come until the 1990s, driven more by national government policy than curricular demands, which were, after all, very flexible by nature. In 1989 the Universities Funding Council, which was merged into the <a href="/wiki/Higher_Education_Funding_Council_for_England" title="Higher Education Funding Council for England">Higher Education Funding Council for England</a> (HEFCE) in 1992, was charged by the UK Government to determine the cost for teaching each subject. To meet these accountancy requirements, Kent required for the first time that each member of staff declare a single discipline they would be affiliated with in future. When departments were formed in the early 1990s this led to a great deal of reorganisation of staff, and destroyed many existing inter-disciplinary relationships. Following the formation of departments, finance was devolved to departments based on how many students were taught. This quickly evolved into undermining the interdisciplinary context further, as departments sought to control finance by increasing the amount of specialist teaching in the first year.
</p><p>The university now has the Faculties further divided into 18 Departments and Schools, ranging from the School of English to the Department of Biosciences, and from the Kent Law School to the Department of Economics. Also of note is the University's <a href="/wiki/Brussels_School_of_International_Studies" title="Brussels School of International Studies">Brussels School of International Studies</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, Belgium. The school offers master's degrees in <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a> theory and international conflict analysis, along with an LLM in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. In 2005 a new department, the Kent School of Architecture, began teaching its first students. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Wye_College" title="Wye College">Wye College</a> came under Kent's remit, in joint partnership with <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG/220px-Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG/330px-Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG/440px-Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Darwin_College_-_UKC.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Darwin_College,_Kent" title="Darwin College, Kent">Darwin College</a></div></div></div>

<p>The university also has an associate college named <a href="/wiki/Chaucer_College" title="Chaucer College">Chaucer College</a>.
</p><p>There was much discussion about the names adopted for most of the colleges with the following alternative names all in consideration at one point or another: for Eliot: Caxton, after <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a>; for Keynes: <a href="/wiki/Richborough" title="Richborough">Richborough</a>, a town in Kent; <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>; and for Darwin: Anselm (again); Attlee, after <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>, the post war Prime Minister; Becket, after <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a>, another former Archbishop (this was the recommendation of the college's provisional committee but rejected by the Senate); Conrad; Elgar, after <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Edward Elgar</a>; Maitland; Marlowe, after <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Marlowe" title="Christopher Marlowe">Christopher Marlowe</a>; Russell, after <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a> (this was the recommendation of the Senate but rejected by the Council); Tyler, after both <a href="/wiki/Wat_Tyler" title="Wat Tyler">Wat Tyler</a> and Tyler Hill on which the campus stands. The name for the College proved especially contentious and was eventually decided by a postal ballot of members of the Senate, choosing from: Attlee, Conrad, Darwin, Elgar, Maitland, Marlowe and Tyler.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin122-126_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin122-126-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
(Both Becket and Tyler were eventually used as the names for residential buildings on campuses and the building housing both the Architecture and Anthropology departments is named Marlowe.)
</p><p>Each college has residential rooms, lecture theatres, study rooms, computer rooms and social areas. The intention of the <i>colleges</i> was that they should not be just <a href="/wiki/Halls_of_residence" class="mw-redirect" title="Halls of residence">Halls of residence</a>, but complete <i>academic</i> communities. Each college (except Woolf) has its own bar, all rebuilt on a larger scale, and originally its own dining hall (only Rutherford still has a functioning dining hall; Darwin's is hired out for conferences and events; Keynes's was closed in 2000 and converted into academic space, but in 2011 Dolche Vita was expanded and became the dining hall for Keynes students in catered accommodation after Keynes's expansion in 2011; and Eliot's was closed in 2006). It was expected that each college (more were planned) would have around 600 students as members, with an equivalent proportion of staff, with half the students living within the college itself and the rest coming onto campus to eat and study within their colleges. Many facilities, ranging from accommodation, tutorials and alumni relations, would be handled on a college basis. With no planned academic divisions below the Faculty level, the colleges would be main focus of students' lives and there would be no units of a similar or smaller size to provide a rival focus of loyalties.
</p><p>This vision of a collegiate university has increasingly fallen away. The funding for colleges did not keep pace with the growth in student numbers, with the result that only four colleges were built. In later years when there was heavy student demand for scarce accommodation in Canterbury the solution was found in building additional on-campus accommodation but not in the form of further colleges. The hopes that students living off campus would stay around to eat dinner in their colleges were not met, whilst the abolition of college amenities fees removed students' direct stake in their colleges. With the growth of specialist subject departments as well as of other university wide facilities, more and more of the role of colleges was transferred to the central university. Accommodation and catering were transferred to the centralised <i>University of Kent at Canterbury Hospitality</i> (UKCH).<sup id="cite_ref-MartinGen_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MartinGen-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today the University does not operate as a traditional collegiate university â applications are made to the University as a whole, and many of the colleges rely on each other for day-to-day operation. Academic departments have no formal ties to colleges other than those that are located within particular college buildings due to availability of space, with lectures, seminars and tutorials taking place wherever there is an available room rather than on a college basis. Many students are allocated accommodation in their respective college, but some are housed in developments with no defined collegiate link whilst others are housed in different colleges.
</p><p>Despite this the six College Student Committees, volunteer groups made up of elected officers and supporting volunteers, have retained a reasonably strong presence on campus. They run fundraising events and welfare campaigns throughout the academic year, and organise student events for their colleges during Welcome Week. Every student in the University retains a college affiliation to either Keynes, Eliot, Rutherford, Darwin or Park Wood even if they do not live in college accommodation. Students are encouraged to stay engaged with their College Committees throughout their time at the University.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms_and_logo">Coat of arms and logo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Coat of arms and logo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The University of Kent's <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> was granted by the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Arms" title="College of Arms">College of Arms</a> in September 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-about59_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about59-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/White_horse_of_Kent" title="White horse of Kent">white horse of Kent</a> is taken from the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Council" title="Kent County Council">County of Kent</a> (and can also be seen on the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Kent" title="Flag of Kent">Flag of Kent</a>). The three Cornish <a href="/wiki/Red-billed_chough" title="Red-billed chough">choughs</a>, originally belonging to the arms of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a>, were taken from the arms of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Canterbury" title="City of Canterbury">City of Canterbury</a>. The Crest depicts the West Gate of Canterbury with a symbolic flow of water, presumably the <a href="/wiki/River_Stour,_Kent" title="River Stour, Kent">Great Stour</a>, below it. Two golden Bishops' <a href="/wiki/Crosier" title="Crosier">Crosiers</a> in the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Saltire" title="Saltire">St. Andrews Cross</a> are shown in front of it. The supporters â lions with the sterns of golden ships â are taken from the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin33-36_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin33-36-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Coat of Arms is now formally used only for degree certificates, degree programmes and some merchandise, as a result of the University seeking a consistent identity branding.<sup id="cite_ref-identity_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-identity-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kent is a research-led university with 24 schools and 40 specialist research centres spanning the sciences, technology, medical studies, the social sciences, arts and humanities.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Research_Assessment_Exercise" title="Research Assessment Exercise">Research Assessment Exercise</a> the University of Kent was ranked 40th out of 128 participating institutions in a 'grade point average' league table in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_Higher_Education_Supplement" class="mw-redirect" title="The Times Higher Education Supplement">The Times Higher Education Supplement</a></i> (falling from 31st in 2008),<sup id="cite_ref-timeshighereducation1_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeshighereducation1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> 30th in terms of 'Research Power' (rising from 40th in 2008),<sup id="cite_ref-timeshighereducation1_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeshighereducation1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and 19th in terms of 'Research Intensity' (rising from 49th in 2008).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The University had a total research income of Â£17 million in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Library">Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG/220px-Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG/330px-Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG/440px-Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Templeman_Library_-_UKC.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Templeman Library, which sits at the heart of the Canterbury Campus. The upper floors offer views of the city of Canterbury.</div></div></div>
<p>The Templeman Library (named after <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Templeman" title="Geoffrey Templeman">Geoffrey Templeman</a>, the University's first Vice-Chancellor) contains over a million items in stock including books, journals, videos, DVDs, and archive materials (for example, a full text of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> from 1785 onwards), yet it is still only half its planned size. It has a materials fund of approximately Â£1million a year, and adds 12,000 items every year. It is open every day in term time, on a 24/7 basis. It receives 800,000 visits a year, with approximately half a million loans <i>per annum</i>.
</p><p>The library also houses the British Cartoon Archive,<sup id="cite_ref-cartoon_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cartoon-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> (established 1975<sup id="cite_ref-about70_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about70-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>) a national collection of, mainly, newspaper cartoons, with over 90,000 images catalogued.
</p><p>In 2013 work began to extend, refurbish and completely modernise the Templeman Library, including the addition of study space, along with the creation of a new purpose built lecture theatre. Additionally, the Library facade underwent major renovation. This work was completed in 2017, with additional refurbishment work planned for 2018.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The student population is mixed, with around 15,000 undergraduates and 4,000 postgraduates, with approximately 22% of students coming from overseas.<sup id="cite_ref-HESA2_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HESA2-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Approximately 128 different nationalities are currently represented, and the female to male ratio is 55 women to every 45 men.<sup id="cite_ref-HESA2_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HESA2-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span id="Students.27_Union"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Students'_Union">Students' Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Students&#039; Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Students' Union, officially known as "Kent Union", is the <a href="/wiki/Students%27_union" title="Students&#39; union">student representative body</a> for students at the university. It is led by five elected full-time officers (the '<a href="/wiki/Sabbatical_officer" title="Sabbatical officer">sabbatical team</a>'), a Board of trustees, part-time student officers and 'lay' members of the local community and business selected for their specialist expertise.
</p><p>The Union runs two shops on campus, <i>Essentials</i>, an all-purpose food and essentials store, and <i>Parkwood Essentials</i>, in the Park Wood student village. The Union also operates the Park Wood bar <i>Woody's</i> and a 1,500 capacity nightclub <i>The Venue</i> which, unusually, is located on the central campus. Essentials, the Venue and other shops and Union offices are located in purpose-made buildings completed in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-about90_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about90-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>  Kent Union also co-ordinates over 200 sports clubs and societies, as well as media outlets, volunteering and charity activities, and provides student welfare services.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstrations">Demonstrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Demonstrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kent_Union_Banner.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kent_Union_Banner.jpg/220px-Kent_Union_Banner.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kent_Union_Banner.jpg/330px-Kent_Union_Banner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Kent_Union_Banner.jpg/440px-Kent_Union_Banner.jpg 2x" data-file-width="519" data-file-height="356" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kent_Union_Banner.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former banner of Kent Union, featuring the Union's old logo.</div></div></div>
<p>In early March 1970 a General Meeting of the University of Kent at Canterbury Students' Union voted to occupy the Cornwallis Building as part of a national student movement to open personal records to individual student scrutiny.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian1_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian1-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The occupation lasted about two weeks, with a majority vote ending the occupation on 18 March. Approximately 400 students marched out of the Cornwallis Building to present a set of demands that were handed by Union President David Lawrence to the University Registrar Mr Eric Fox. The demands had been drawn up and debated by groups of up to 300 students at a time in meetings and seminars held throughout the occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian2_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early to mid-1970s, along with other plate-glass universities, the Union had a reputation for revolutionary politics, leading to demands for law changes from some staff trade unionists.<sup id="cite_ref-times021674_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times021674-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> It was active in anti-poll tax, anti-student loans and anti-racism campaigns as well as safety campaigns on campus in the late 1980s.
</p><p>In 2003, ahead of the 'Top Up Fees' vote, the Union took 300 students to take part in the NUS UK Student Demonstrations on three double decker buses.  The Union covered all the transport costs to the demonstration. In 2010, ahead of a parliamentary vote on issues concerning raising tuition fees, the Union took part in the <a href="/wiki/2010_UK_student_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2010 UK student protests">national demonstrations</a>. The union heavily subsidised the transport for 500 students to get to London.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chaplaincy">Chaplaincy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Chaplaincy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whilst the University is <a href="/wiki/Secularity_(non-religiosity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularity (non-religiosity)">secular</a>, there is a chaplaincy consisting of permanent <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholic</a> priests and a <a href="/wiki/Pentecostal" class="mw-redirect" title="Pentecostal">Pentecostal</a> <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">minister</a>, as well as part-time chaplains from other denominations and faiths. The chaplaincy runs the annual Carol Service that takes place every year in <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Cathedral" title="Canterbury Cathedral">Canterbury Cathedral</a> at the end of Autumn Term.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="InQuire">InQuire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: InQuire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The University has a student newspaper named InQuire and an online news website InQuire Media<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> (launched in January 2008). The newspaper is published every two weeks and is edited by a group of student volunteers. While the newspaper and website are funded by the Students' Union, the selection of content is independent but subject to moderation by Kent Union before physical publication. Content within InQuire is focused on campus issues and national news that affects students.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kent_Television">Kent Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Kent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Kent Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kent_Television" title="Kent Television">Kent Television</a></div>
<p>Kent Television or KTV is the newest member of the Kent Union student media family.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Founded in May 2012 it has already obtained nearly 200,000 views and has live broadcasting capabilities. KTV is run by volunteers and aims to provide students with regular entertaining and informative televisual content.
