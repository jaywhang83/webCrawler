<p><b>Sir Nikolaus Bernhard Leon Pevsner</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a></span> (30 January 1902 – 18 August 1983) was a German-British <a href="/wiki/History_of_art" title="History of art">art historian</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_architecture" title="History of architecture">architectural historian</a> best known for his monumental 46-volume series of county-by-county guides, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Buildings_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="The Buildings of England">The Buildings of England</a></i> (1951–74). 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nikolaus Pevsner was born in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saxony" title="Kingdom of Saxony">Saxony</a>, the son of Anna and her husband Hugo Pevsner, a Russian-Jewish fur merchant. He attended <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas_School,_Leipzig" title="St. Thomas School, Leipzig">St. Thomas School, Leipzig</a>, and went on to study at several universities, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilian_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Berlin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goethe_University_Frankfurt" title="Goethe University Frankfurt">Frankfurt am Main</a>, before being awarded a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> by <a href="/wiki/Leipzig_University" title="Leipzig University">Leipzig</a> in 1924 for a thesis on the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque architecture</a> of Leipzig.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEngel2004_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEngel2004-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1923, he married Carola ("Lola") Kurlbaum, the daughter of distinguished Leipzig lawyer, Alfred Kurlbaum.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGames2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGames2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He worked as an assistant keeper at the <a href="/wiki/Gem%C3%A4ldegalerie_Alte_Meister" title="Gemäldegalerie Alte Meister">Dresden Gallery</a> (1924–28). He converted to <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> early in life.
</p><p>During this period he became interested in establishing the supremacy of German modernist architecture after becoming aware of <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a>'s Pavillon de l'Esprit Nouveau at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Internationale_des_Arts_D%C3%A9coratifs_et_Industriels_Modernes" class="mw-redirect" title="Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes">Paris Exhibition of 1925</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGames2010_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGames2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1928 he contributed the volume on <a href="/wiki/Italian_baroque" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian baroque">Italian baroque</a> painting to the <i>Handbuch der Kunstwissenschaft</i>, a multi-volume series providing an overview of the history of European art. He taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a> (1929–33), offering a specialist course on <a href="/wiki/English_art" title="English art">English art</a> and architecture.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg/220px-BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg/330px-BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg/440px-BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BirminghamUniversityChancellorsCourt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Birmingham_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Birmingham University">Birmingham University</a></div></div></div>
<p>According to biographer Stephen Games, Pevsner welcomed many of the economic and cultural policies of the early <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Hitler regime</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGames2010_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGames2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamesPevsner2002_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamesPevsner2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, due to <a href="/wiki/Law_for_the_Restoration_of_the_Professional_Civil_Service" title="Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service">Nazi race laws</a> he was forced to resign his lectureship in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarrison2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarrison2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Later that year Pevsner moved to England, settling in <a href="/wiki/Hampstead" title="Hampstead">Hampstead</a>, where poet <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Grigson" title="Geoffrey Grigson">Geoffrey Grigson</a> was his next-door neighbour in Wildwood Terrace.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Pevsner's first post was an 18-month research fellowship at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Birmingham" title="University of Birmingham">University of Birmingham</a>, found for him by friends in Birmingham and partly funded by the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Assisting_Refugee_Academics" class="mw-redirect" title="Council for Assisting Refugee Academics">Academic Assistance Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bham_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bham-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A study of the role of the designer in the industrial process, the research produced a generally critical account of design standards in Britain which he published as <i>An Enquiry into Industrial Art in England</i> (Cambridge University Press, 1937). He was subsequently employed as a buyer of modern textiles, glass and ceramics for the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Gordon_Russell" class="mw-redirect" title="Sydney Gordon Russell">Gordon Russell</a> furniture showrooms in London.
</p><p>By this time Pevsner had also completed <i>Pioneers of the Modern Movement: from William Morris to Walter Gropius</i>, his influential pre-history of what he saw as <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gropius" title="Walter Gropius">Walter Gropius</a>'s dominance of contemporary design. <i>Pioneers</i> ardently championed Gropius's first two buildings (both pre–First World War) on the grounds that they summed up all the essential goals of 20th-century architecture; in England, however, it was widely taken to be the history of England's contribution to international modernism, and a manifesto for <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>) modernism, which it was not.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In spite of that, the book remains an important point of reference in the teaching of the history of modern design, and helped lay the foundation of Pevsner's career in England as an architectural historian. Since its first publication by <a href="/wiki/Faber_%26_Faber" class="mw-redirect" title="Faber &amp; Faber">Faber &amp; Faber</a> in 1936, it has gone through several editions and been translated into many languages. The English-language edition has also been renamed <i>Pioneers of Modern Design</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pevsner was "<a href="/wiki/More_German_than_the_Germans" title="More German than the Germans">more German than the Germans</a>" to the extent that he supported "<a href="/wiki/Goebbels" class="mw-redirect" title="Goebbels">Goebbels</a> in his drive for 'pure' non-decadent German art".<sup id="cite_ref-Pearman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He was reported as saying of the <a href="/wiki/Nazis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazis">Nazis</a> (in 1933): "I want this movement to succeed. There is no alternative but chaos.... There are things worse than <a href="/wiki/Hitlerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Hitlerism">Hitlerism</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGames2010_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGames2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, he was included in the Nazi <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Book" title="The Black Book">Black Book</a> as hostile to the Hitler regime.
</p><p>In 1940, Pevsner was taken to the <a href="/wiki/Internment_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Internment camp">internment camp</a> at <a href="/wiki/Huyton#Second_World_War" title="Huyton">Huyton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, as an enemy alien. Geoffrey Grigson later wrote in his <i>Recollections</i> (1984): "When at last two hard-faced <a href="/wiki/Bow_Street_Runners" title="Bow Street Runners">Bow Street runners</a> arrived in the early hours of the morning to take [him] ... I managed, clutching my pyjama trousers, to catch them up with the best parting present I could quickly think of, which was an elegant little edition, a new edition, of Shakespeare's Sonnets."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Pevsner was released after three months on the intervention of, among others, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Pick" title="Frank Pick">Frank Pick</a>, then Director-General of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Information_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Information (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Information</a>. He spent some time in the months after <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">the Blitz</a> clearing bomb debris, and wrote reviews and art criticism for the Ministry of Information's <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Zeitung" title="Die Zeitung">Die Zeitung</a></i>, an anti-Nazi publication for Germans living in England. He also completed for <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> the Pelican paperback <i>An Outline of European Architecture</i>, which he had begun to develop while in <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a>. <i>Outline</i> would eventually go into seven editions, be translated into 16 languages, and sell more than half a million copies.
</p><p>In 1942, Pevsner finally secured two regular positions. From 1936 onwards he had been a frequent contributor to the <i><a href="/wiki/Architectural_Review" title="Architectural Review">Architectural Review</a></i> and from 1943 to 1945 he stood in as its acting editor while the regular editor <a href="/wiki/James_Maude_Richards" title="James Maude Richards">J. M. Richards</a> was on active service. Under the <i>AR</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s influence, Pevsner's approach to modern architecture became more complex and more moderate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPevsner2010_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPevsner2010-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Early signs of a lifelong interest in Victorian architecture, also influenced by the <i><a href="/wiki/Architectural_Review" title="Architectural Review">Architectural Review</a></i>, appeared in a series written under the pseudonym of "Peter F. R. Donner": Pevsner's "Treasure Hunts"  guided readers down selected London streets, pointing out architectural treasures of the 19th century. He was also closely involved with the <i>Review</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s proprietor, <a href="/wiki/H._de_C._Hastings" title="H. de C. Hastings">H. de C. Hastings</a>, in evolving the magazine's theories on <a href="/wiki/Picturesque" title="Picturesque">picturesque</a> planning.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEErten2004_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEErten2004-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1942, Pevsner was also appointed a part-time lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Birkbeck,_University_of_London" title="Birkbeck, University of London">Birkbeck College</a>, London; he would eventually retire from the college in 1969 as its first professor of art history. He lectured at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> for almost 30 years, having been <a href="/wiki/Slade_Professor_of_Fine_Art" title="Slade Professor of Fine Art">Slade professor</a> there for a record six years from 1949 to 1955, and would also become the <a href="/wiki/Slade_Professor_of_Fine_Art" title="Slade Professor of Fine Art">Slade professorship</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford</a> in 1968.
</p><p>Framing all this was his career as a writer and editor. After moving to England, Pevsner had found that the study of architectural history had little status in academic circles, and the amount of information available, especially to travellers wanting to inform themselves about the architecture of a particular district, was limited. Invited by <a href="/wiki/Allen_Lane" title="Allen Lane">Allen Lane</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a>, for whom he had written his <i>Outline</i> and also edited the <a href="/wiki/King_Penguin_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="King Penguin Books">King Penguin series</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> to suggest ideas for future publications, he proposed a series of comprehensive county guides to rectify this shortcoming.
</p><p>Work on the <i><a href="/wiki/Buildings_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Buildings of England">Buildings of England</a></i> series began in 1945, and the first volume was published in 1951. Pevsner wrote 32 of the books himself and 10 with collaborators, with a further four of the original series written by others. Since his death, work has continued on the series, which has been extended to cover the rest of the United Kingdom, under the title <a href="/wiki/Pevsner_Architectural_Guides" title="Pevsner Architectural Guides">Pevsner Architectural Guides</a> (now published by <a href="/wiki/Yale_University_Press" title="Yale University Press">Yale University Press</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> After updating and correcting <i>London 1: the Cities of London and Westminster</i> for its reissue in 1962, Pevsner delegated the revision and expansion of further volumes to others, beginning with Enid Radcliffe for <i>Essex</i> (1965).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarries2011_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarries2011-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The gazetteer descriptions of revised volumes do not routinely distinguish between Pevsner's original text and any new writing, but more recent books sometimes supply his words in quotation when the revising author's judgement differs, where a building has since been altered, or where the old text is no longer topical.
</p><p>As well as <i>The Buildings of England</i>, Pevsner proposed the <i>Pelican History of Art</i> series (1953– ), a multi-volume survey on the model of the German <i>Handbuch der Kunstwissenschaft</i>, which he would himself edit. Many individual volumes are regarded as classics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar">Postwar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Postwar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1946, Pevsner made the first of several broadcasts on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Third_Programme" title="BBC Third Programme">BBC Third Programme</a>, presenting nine talks in all up to 1950, examining painters and European art eras. By 1977 he had presented 78 talks for the BBC, including the <a href="/wiki/Reith_Lectures" title="Reith Lectures">Reith Lectures</a> in 1955 – a series of six broadcasts, entitled <i>The Englishness of English Art</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Reith_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reith-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> for which he explored the qualities of art which he regarded as particularly English, and what they said about the English national character.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGamesPevsner2002_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGamesPevsner2002-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/A._W._Mellon_lectures" title="A. W. Mellon lectures">A. W. Mellon lectures</a> in Fine Art at the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a>, Washington, D.C., were published in 1976 as <i>A History of Building Types</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarries2011715_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarries2011715-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pevsner was a founding member in 1957 of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Society">Victorian Society</a>, the national charity for the study and protection of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_architecture" title="Edwardian architecture">Edwardian architecture</a> and other arts. In 1964 he was invited to become its chairman, and steered it through its formative years, fighting alongside <a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Casson" title="Hugh Casson">Hugh Casson</a> and others to save houses, churches, railway stations and other monuments of the Victorian age. He served for ten years (1960–70) as a member of the National Advisory Council on Art Education (or Coldstream Committee), campaigning for art history to be a compulsory element in the curriculum of art schools. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy" title="British Academy">British Academy</a> in 1965 and awarded the Gold Medal of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">Royal Institute of British Architects</a> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarries2011672–3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarries2011672–3-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Having assumed British citizenship in 1946, Pevsner was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> in 1953 and was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> in 1969 "for services to art and architecture".
</p><p>Pevsner also received an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">Honorary Doctorate</a> from <a href="/wiki/Heriot-Watt_University" title="Heriot-Watt University">Heriot-Watt University</a> in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-HW_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HW-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pevsner died at home in Wildwood Terrace,<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hampstead" title="Hampstead">Hampstead</a>, London, in August 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-AJ_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJ-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> His wife predeceased him by 20 years.
</p><p>His memorial service was held at the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ_the_King,_Bloomsbury" title="Church of Christ the King, Bloomsbury">Church of Christ the King, Bloomsbury</a>, the following December, with the memorial address being given by <a href="/wiki/Alec_Clifton-Taylor" title="Alec Clifton-Taylor">Alec Clifton-Taylor</a>, a friend of 50 years. He is buried in the churchyard of St Peter, <a href="/wiki/Clyffe_Pypard" title="Clyffe Pypard">Clyffe Pypard</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>. His elder son, Dieter, was an editor at <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> and co-founder with Oliver Caldecott of the publishing company Wildwood House in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> His younger son, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Pevsner" title="Tom Pevsner">Tom</a>, was a film producer and director who went on to work on several <a href="/wiki/James_Bond_films" class="mw-redirect" title="James Bond films">James Bond films</a>.
</p><p>In 2007, a <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> was erected by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> at the house that had been Pevsner's home since 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AJ_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJ-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Pevsner oversaw the publication of the initial volumes of the Scottish, Welsh and Irish counterparts of <i>The Buildings of England</i> (and in each was credited as "Editor-in-Chief", "Founding Editor" and "Editorial Adviser" respectively) he did not write any of them. As with the revisions of his earlier works, many of these volumes were the work of several contributors. Coverage of the whole of Great Britain was completed in 2016, with the Irish series still in progress.
</p><p>A fictionalised Pevsner appears in the 1998 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spell_(novel)" title="The Spell (novel)">The Spell</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hollinghurst" title="Alan Hollinghurst">Alan Hollinghurst</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_ideas_and_theories">Notable ideas and theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notable ideas and theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>"A bicycle shed is a building; <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cathedral" title="Lincoln Cathedral">Lincoln Cathedral</a> is a piece of architecture. Nearly everything that encloses space on a scale sufficient for a human being to move in is a building; the term architecture applies only to buildings designed with a view to aesthetic appeal." From <i>An Outline of European Architecture</i>, 1943.</li>
<li>Pevsner also described the three ways aesthetic appeal could manifest itself in architecture: in a building's façade, the material volumes, or the interior.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archive">Archive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1984 the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Research_Institute" title="Getty Research Institute">Getty Research Institute</a> acquired the Nikolaus Pevsner Papers,<sup id="cite_ref-Nikolaus_Pevsner_Papers_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nikolaus_Pevsner_Papers-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> an archive that includes 143 boxes of typed and handwritten notes, clippings, photographs, books, lecture notes, and manuscripts.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Papers">Papers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nikolaus_Pevsner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Papers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Papers relating to Pevsner's departure from Germany and efforts to obtain work in England are contained within the archives of the Society for the Protection of Science and Learning (now the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Assisting_Refugee_Academics" class="mw-redirect" title="Council for Assisting Refugee Academics">Council for Assisting Refugee Academics</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Bodleian_Library" title="Bodleian Library">Bodleian Library</a>. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bodley.ox.ac.uk/dept/scwmss/wmss/online/modern/spsl/spsl.html">Index to the Catalogue of the SPSL.</a><br /> A substantial collection of his papers is held at the Pevsner archive in the <a href="/wiki/Getty_Research_Institute" title="Getty Research Institute">Getty Research Institute</a>, Los Angeles. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://archive.is/20121214182313/http://library.getty.edu/cgi-bin/Pwebrecon.cgi?BBID=76714">Nikolaus Pevsner papers, 1919–1979</a>. Research Library at the Getty Research Institute, Los Angeles, California.<br />
Documents relating to his various projects for Penguin, including the King Penguin series, the <i>Pelican History of Art</i> and the <i>Buildings of England</i>, are held by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20100517093139/http://www.bristol.ac.uk/is/library/collections/specialcollections/archives/penguin/">Penguin Archive</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bristol" title="University of Bristol">University of Bristol</a>.<br />Papers relating to the work of the Victorian Society during his years as chairman are held by the Victorian Society themselves and the London Metropolitan Archives. (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20091203112854/http://www.victoriansociety.org.uk/about/our-archives/">Victorian Society archives</a>)
</p>
