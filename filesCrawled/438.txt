	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Standard English</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">normative form of the English language</div>
<p><b>Standard English</b> (<b>SE</b>, also <b>standardised English</b> or <b>standardized English</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Dialect#Standard_and_non-standard_dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> that is used as the national norm—the <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a>—in an <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">English-speaking country</a>, especially as the language for public and formal usage.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  In England and Wales, the term <i>standard English</i> is associated with <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> accent, and the United Kingdom Standard English (UKSE) grammar and vocabulary. In Scotland, the standard dialect is <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Standard_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Standard English">Scottish Standard English</a>; in the United States, <a href="/wiki/General_American" class="mw-redirect" title="General American">General American</a> is the standard variety spoken; and in Australia, the national standard is called <a href="/wiki/General_Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="General Australian">General Australian</a> English.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a standard English is generally used in public and official settings, a range of <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">registers</a> (stylistic levels) exists within any standardized English, as is often seen when comparing a newspaper article with an <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic paper</a>, for example. A distinction also may be drawn between spoken and written forms of standard English, which tend to follow different norms of formality. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">vernacular dialects</a> are looser than <a href="/wiki/Codification_(linguistics)" title="Codification (linguistics)">codified</a> standards, and quicker to accept new grammatical forms and vocabulary. The various local standard varieties are characterized by a generally accepted set of rules, often those established by <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">prescriptive grammarians</a> of the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>English originated in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon period</a>, and is now spoken as a <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first</a> or <a href="/wiki/English_as_a_second_language" class="mw-redirect" title="English as a second language">second language</a> in many countries of the world, many of which have developed one or more "national standards" (though this does not refer to <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">published standards documents</a>, but to frequency of consistent usage). English is the first language of the majority of the population <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_English-speaking_population" title="List of countries by English-speaking population">in a number of countries</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and is an official language in <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_English_is_an_official_language" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries where English is an official language">many others</a>, including; <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>. The English that is widely accepted as "standard" in many of these countries will have a number of unique rules with regards to spelling, pronunciation and grammar. 
</p><p>As the result of <a href="/wiki/Colonisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonisation">colonisation</a> and historical migrations of English-speaking populations, and the predominant use of English as the international language of trade and commerce (a <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i>), English has also become the most widely used second language.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In countries where English is neither a native language nor widely spoken, a non-native<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> variant (typically <a href="/wiki/English_language_in_England" title="English language in England">English English</a> or <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a>) might be considered "standard" for teaching purposes. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Typically, English English is taught as standard across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">Southern Asia</a> and North American English is taught as standard across <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. This does, however, vary between regions and individual teachers. In some areas a <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a>, blends English with one or more native languages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_grammar" title="English grammar">English grammar</a></div>
<p>Although the standard Englishes of the various <a href="/wiki/Anglosphere" title="Anglosphere">anglophone countries</a> are very similar, often there are minor grammatical differences between them, as well as numerous <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a> divergences. In American and Australian English, for example, "sunk" and "shrunk" as past tense forms of "sink" and "shrink" are beginning to become acceptable as standard forms, whereas standard British English still insists on "sank" and "shrank".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In South African English, the deletion of verbal complements is becoming common. This phenomenon sees the objects of transitive verbs being omitted: "Did you get?", "You can put in the box".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This kind of construction is not accepted in most other standardized varieties of English.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English orthography</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">American and British English spelling differences</a></div>
<p>With rare exceptions, Standard Englishes use either American or British spelling systems, or a mixture of the two (such as in <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">Indian English spelling</a>). British spellings usually dominate in <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries.
</p>
