	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Toll road</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Roadway for which a fee (or toll) is assessed for passage</div>

<p>A <b>toll road</b>, also known as a <b>turnpike</b> or <b>tollway</b>, is a public or private <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a> (almost always a <a href="/wiki/Controlled-access_highway" title="Controlled-access highway">controlled-access highway</a> in the present day) for which a <a href="/wiki/Fee" title="Fee">fee</a> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">toll</a></i>) is assessed for passage. It is a form of <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">road pricing</a> typically implemented to help recoup the cost of <a href="/wiki/Road_construction" class="mw-redirect" title="Road construction">road construction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_maintenance" class="mw-redirect" title="Road maintenance">maintenance</a>.
</p><p>Toll roads have existed in some form since <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a>, with tolls levied on passing travellers on foot, wagon, or horseback; but their prominence increased with the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and many modern tollways charge fees for <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a> exclusively. The amount of the toll usually varies by vehicle type, weight, or number of <a href="/wiki/Axles" class="mw-redirect" title="Axles">axles</a>, with freight <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a> often charged higher rates than <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">cars</a>.
</p><p>Tolls are often collected at toll booths, <a href="/wiki/Toll_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Toll house">toll houses</a>, plazas, stations, bars, or gates. Some toll collection points are autonomous, and the user deposits money in a machine which opens the gate once the correct toll has been paid. To cut costs and minimise time delay, many tolls are collected with <a href="/wiki/Electronic_toll_collection" title="Electronic toll collection">electronic toll collection</a> equipment which automatically communicates with a toll payer's <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponder</a> or uses <a href="/wiki/Automatic_number_plate_recognition" class="mw-redirect" title="Automatic number plate recognition">automatic number plate recognition</a> to charge drivers by debiting their accounts.
</p><p>Criticisms of toll roads include the time taken to stop and pay the toll, and the cost of the toll booth operators—up to about one-third of revenue in some cases. Automated toll-paying systems help minimise both of these. Others object to paying "twice" for the same road: in fuel taxes and with tolls.
</p><p>In addition to toll roads, <a href="/wiki/Toll_bridge" title="Toll bridge">toll bridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toll_tunnel" title="Toll tunnel">toll tunnels</a> are also used by public authorities to generate funds to repay the cost of building the structures. Some tolls are set aside to pay for future maintenance or enhancement of infrastructure, or are applied as a general fund by local governments, not being earmarked for transport facilities. This is sometimes limited or prohibited by central government legislation. Also <a href="/wiki/Road_congestion_pricing" class="mw-redirect" title="Road congestion pricing">road congestion pricing</a> schemes have been implemented in a limited number of urban areas as a <a href="/wiki/Transportation_demand_management" title="Transportation demand management">transportation demand management</a> tool to try to reduce <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tailpipe_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Tailpipe emissions">air pollution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FHWA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FHWA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_times">Ancient times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Toll roads have existed for at least the last 2,700 years, as tolls had to be paid by travellers using the <a href="/wiki/Susa" title="Susa">Susa</a>–<a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a> highway under the regime of <a href="/wiki/Ashurbanipal" title="Ashurbanipal">Ashurbanipal</a>, who reigned in the 7th century BC.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> refer to tolls in Arabia and other parts of Asia. In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, before the 4th century BC, the <a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a> notes the use of tolls. Germanic tribes charged tolls to travellers across <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">mountain passes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Road_toll_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Road toll (historic)">Road toll (historic)</a></div>
<p>A 14th-century example (though not for a road) is Castle <a href="/wiki/Loevestein" class="mw-redirect" title="Loevestein">Loevestein</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, which was built at a strategic point where two rivers meet. <a href="/wiki/River_toll" class="mw-redirect" title="River toll">River tolls</a> were charged on boats sailing along the river. The <a href="/wiki/%C3%98resund" title="Øresund">Øresund</a> in Scandinavia was once subject to <a href="/wiki/Sound_dues" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound dues">a toll to the Danish Monarch</a>, who derived a sizable portion of his revenue from it.
</p><p>Many modern European roads were originally constructed as toll roads in order to recoup the costs of construction, maintenance and as a source of tax money that is paid primarily by someone other than the local residents. In 14th-century England, some of the most heavily used roads were repaired with money raised from tolls by <a href="/wiki/Pavage" title="Pavage">pavage</a> grants. Widespread toll roads sometimes restricted traffic so much, by their high tolls, that they interfered with trade and cheap transportation needed to alleviate local famines or shortages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tolls were used in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in the 14th and 15th centuries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Industrialisation in Europe needed major improvements to the transport infrastructure which included many new or substantially improved roads, financed from tolls. The <a href="/wiki/A5_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A5 road (Great Britain)">A5 road</a> in Britain was built to provide a robust transport link between Britain and Ireland and had a toll house every few miles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 20th century, road tolls were introduced in Europe to finance the construction of motorway networks and specific transport infrastructure such as bridges and tunnels. <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> was the first European country to charge motorway tolls, on a 50 kilometres (31&#160;mi) motorway section near <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> in 1924. It was followed by <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, which made users pay for the network of motorways around and between its cities in 1927. Later in the 1950s and 1960s, France, Spain, and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> started to build motorways largely with the aid of concessions, allowing rapid development of this infrastructure without massive state debts. Since then, road tolls have been introduced in the majority of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> member states.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, prior to the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a> and the large federal grants supplied to states to build it, many states constructed their first <a href="/wiki/Controlled-access_highway" title="Controlled-access highway">controlled-access highways</a> by floating <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> backed by toll revenues. Starting with the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a> in 1940, and followed by similar roads in New Jersey (<a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Turnpike" title="New Jersey Turnpike">New Jersey Turnpike</a>, 1952 and <a href="/wiki/Garden_State_Parkway" title="Garden State Parkway">Garden State Parkway</a>, 1954), New York (<a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Thruway" title="New York State Thruway">New York State Thruway</a>, 1954), Massachusetts (<a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Turnpike" title="Massachusetts Turnpike">Massachusetts Turnpike</a>, 1957), and others, numerous states throughout the 1950s established major toll roads. With the establishment of the Interstate Highway System in the late 1950s, toll road construction in the U.S. slowed down considerably, as the federal government now provided the bulk of funding to construct new freeways, and regulations required that such Interstate highways be free from tolls. Many older toll roads were added to the Interstate System under a <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_clause" title="Grandfather clause">grandfather clause</a> that allowed tolls to continue to be collected on toll roads that predated the system. Some of these such as the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Turnpike" title="Connecticut Turnpike">Connecticut Turnpike</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Richmond%E2%80%93Petersburg_Turnpike" title="Richmond–Petersburg Turnpike">Richmond–Petersburg Turnpike</a> later removed their tolls when the initial bonds were paid off. Many states, however, have maintained the tolling of these roads as a consistent source of revenue.
</p><p>As the Interstate Highway System approached completion during the 1980s, states began constructing toll roads again to provide new controlled-access highways which were not part of the original interstate system funding. <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Highway_Beltway_8" title="Texas State Highway Beltway 8">Houston's outer beltway</a> of interconnected toll roads began in 1983, and many states followed over the last two decades of the 20th century adding new toll roads, including the <a href="/wiki/Central_Florida_Expressway_Authority" title="Central Florida Expressway Authority">tollway system around Orlando, Florida</a>, Colorado's <a href="/wiki/E-470" title="E-470">E-470</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_State_Route_400" title="Georgia State Route 400">Georgia State Route 400</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>London, in an effort to reduce traffic within the city, instituted the <a href="/wiki/London_congestion_charge" title="London congestion charge">London congestion charge</a> in 2003, effectively making all roads within the centre of the city tolled.
</p><p>In the United States, as states looked for ways to construct new freeways without federal funding again, to raise revenue for continued road maintenance, and to control congestion, new toll road construction saw significant increases during the first two decades of the 21st century. Spurred on by two innovations, the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_toll_collection" title="Electronic toll collection">electronic toll collection</a> system, and the advent of <a href="/wiki/High-occupancy_toll_lane" title="High-occupancy toll lane">high occupancy and express lane tolls</a>, many areas of the U.S saw large road building projects in major urban areas. Electronic toll collection, first introduced in the 1980s, reduces operating costs by removing toll collectors from roads. Tolled express lanes, by which certain lanes of a freeway are designated "toll only", increases revenue by allowing a free-to-use highway collect revenue by allowing drivers to bypass traffic jams by paying a toll. The <a href="/wiki/E-ZPass" title="E-ZPass">E-ZPass</a> system, compatible with many state systems, is the largest ETC system in the U.S., and is used for both fully tolled highways and tolled express lanes. <a href="/wiki/Maryland_Route_200" title="Maryland Route 200">Maryland Route 200</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Expressway" title="Triangle Expressway">Triangle Expressway</a> in North Carolina were the first toll roads built without toll booths, with drivers charged via ETC or by optical license plate recognition and are billed by mail.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_turnpikes">United Kingdom turnpikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom turnpikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Toll_roads_in_Great_Britain" title="Toll roads in Great Britain">Toll roads in Great Britain</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trusts_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Turnpike trusts in the United Kingdom">Turnpike trusts</a> were established in England and Wales from about 1706 in response to the need for better roads than the few and poorly-maintained tracks then available. Turnpike trusts were set up by individual <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">Acts of Parliament</a>, with powers to collect road tolls to repay loans for building, improving, and maintaining the principal roads in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Britain</a>. At their peak, in the 1830s, over 1,000 trusts<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> administered around 30,000 miles (48,000&#160;km) of turnpike road in England and Wales, taking tolls at almost 8,000 toll-gates.<sup id="cite_ref-Searlep798_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Searlep798-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The trusts were ultimately responsible for the maintenance and improvement of most of the main roads in England and Wales, which were used to distribute agricultural and industrial goods economically. The tolls were a source of revenue for road building and maintenance, paid for by road users and not from general taxation. The turnpike trusts were gradually abolished from the 1870s. Most trusts improved existing roads, but some new roads, usually only short stretches, were also built. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Holyhead" title="Holyhead">Holyhead</a> road followed <a href="/wiki/Watling_Street" title="Watling Street">Watling Street</a> from London but was exceptional in creating a largely new route beyond <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a>, and especially beyond <a href="/wiki/Llangollen" title="Llangollen">Llangollen</a>. Built in the early 19th century, with many toll booths along its length, most of it is now the <a href="/wiki/A5_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A5 road (Great Britain)">A5</a>. In the modern day, one major toll road is the <a href="/wiki/M6_Toll" title="M6 Toll">M6 Toll</a>, relieving traffic congestion on the <a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6</a> in Birmingham. A few notable bridges and tunnels continue as toll roads including the <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Mersey_Gateway" class="mw-redirect" title="Mersey Gateway">Mersey Gateway</a> bridge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toll_roads_elsewhere">Toll roads elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Toll roads elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some cities in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> had toll roads in the 19th century. Roads radiating from <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> required users to pay at <a href="/wiki/Tollkeeper%27s_cottages_in_Ontario" title="Tollkeeper&#39;s cottages in Ontario">toll gates</a> along the street (<a href="/wiki/Yonge_Street" title="Yonge Street">Yonge Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bloor_Street" title="Bloor Street">Bloor Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davenport_Road" title="Davenport Road">Davenport Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Road_(Toronto)" title="Kingston Road (Toronto)">Kingston Road</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and disappeared after 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>19th-century <a href="/wiki/Plank_road" title="Plank road">plank roads</a> were usually operated as toll roads. One of the first U.S. motor roads, the <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Motor_Parkway" title="Long Island Motor Parkway">Long Island Motor Parkway</a> (which opened on October 10, 1908) was built by William Kissam Vanderbilt II, the great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Vanderbilt" title="Cornelius Vanderbilt">Cornelius Vanderbilt</a>. The road was closed in 1938 when it was taken over by the state of New York in lieu of back taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charging_methods">Charging methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Charging methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Road_pricing" title="Road pricing">Road pricing</a></div>
<p>Road tolls were levied traditionally for a specific access (e.g. city) or for a specific infrastructure (e.g. roads, bridges). These concepts were widely used until the last century. However, the evolution in technology made it possible to implement road tolling policies based on different concepts. The different charging concepts are designed to suit different requirements regarding purpose of the charge, charging policy, the network to the charge, tariff class differentiation
etc.:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Time Based Charges and Access Fees</b>: In a time-based charging regime, a road user has to pay for a given period of time in which they may use the associated infrastructure. For the practically identical access fees, the user pays for the access to a restricted zone for a period or several days.
</p><p><b>Motorway and other Infrastructure Tolling</b>: The term tolling is used for charging a well-defined special and comparatively costly infrastructure, like a bridge, a tunnel, a mountain pass, a motorway concession, or the whole motorway network of a country. Classically a toll is due when a vehicle passes a tolling station, be it a manual barrier-controlled toll plaza or a free-flow multi-lane station.
</p><p><b>Distance or Area Charging</b>: In a distance or area charging system concept, vehicles are charged per total distance driven in a defined area.
</p><p>Some toll roads charge a toll in only one direction. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Harbour_Bridge" title="Sydney Harbour Bridge">Sydney Harbour Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Harbour_Tunnel" title="Sydney Harbour Tunnel">Sydney Harbour Tunnel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Distributor" title="Eastern Distributor">Eastern Distributor</a> (these all charge tolls city-bound) in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, crossings between <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River_Port_Authority" title="Delaware River Port Authority">Delaware River Port Authority</a> and crossings between New Jersey and <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/Port_Authority_of_New_York_and_New_Jersey" title="Port Authority of New York and New Jersey">Port Authority of New York and New Jersey</a>. This technique is practical where the <a href="/wiki/Shunpiking" title="Shunpiking">detour to avoid the toll</a> is large or the toll differences are small.
</p>

<p>Traditionally tolls were paid by hand at a toll gate. Although payments may still be made in cash, it is more common now to pay using an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_toll_collection" title="Electronic toll collection">electronic toll collection</a> system. In some places, payment is made using <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponders</a> which are affixed to the windscreen.
</p>
<h3><span id="Open.2C_closed_and_open_road_toll_systems"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Open,_closed_and_open_road_toll_systems">Open, closed and open road toll systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Open, closed and open road toll systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three systems of toll roads exist: <b>open</b> (with mainline barrier toll plazas); <b>closed</b> (with entry/exit tolls); and <b>open road</b> (no toll booths, only electronic toll collection gantries at entrances and exits or at strategic locations on the median of the road). Modern toll roads often use a combination of the three: for example, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florida%27s_Turnpike" title="Florida&#39;s Turnpike">Florida's Turnpike</a> implement all three systems in different sections.
</p><p>On an open toll system, all vehicles stop at various locations along the highway to pay a toll. (This is different from "open road tolling", where <i>no</i> vehicles stop to pay a toll.)  While this may save money from the lack of need to construct toll booths at every exit, it can cause traffic congestion while traffic queues at the mainline toll plazas (toll barriers). It is also possible for motorists to enter an 'open toll road' after one toll barrier and exit before the next one, thus travelling on the toll road toll-free. Most open toll roads have ramp tolls or partial access junctions to prevent this practice, known in the U.S. as "<a href="/wiki/Shunpiking" title="Shunpiking">shunpiking</a>".
</p><p>With a closed toll system, vehicles collect a ticket when entering the highway. In some cases, the ticket displays the toll to be paid on exit. Upon exit, the driver must pay the amount listed for the given exit. Should the ticket be lost, a driver must typically pay the maximum amount possible for travel on that highway. Short toll roads with no intermediate entries or exits may have only one toll plaza at one end, with motorists travelling in either direction paying a flat fee either when they enter or when they exit the toll road. In a variant of the closed toll system, mainline barriers are present at the two endpoints of the toll road, and each interchange has a ramp toll that is paid upon exit or entry. In this case, a motorist pays a flat fee at the ramp toll and another flat fee at the end of the toll road; no ticket is necessary. In addition, with most systems, motorists may pay tolls only with cash and/or change; debit and credit cards are not accepted. However, some toll roads may have travel plazas with ATMs so motorists can stop and withdraw cash for the tolls.
</p><p>The toll is calculated by the distance travelled on the toll road or the specific exit chosen. In the United States, for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Turnpike" title="Kansas Turnpike">Kansas Turnpike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Turnpike" title="Ohio Turnpike">Ohio Turnpike</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Turnpike" title="New Jersey Turnpike">New Jersey Turnpike</a>, most of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Toll_Road" title="Indiana Toll Road">Indiana Toll Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Thruway" title="New York State Thruway">New York State Thruway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida%27s_Turnpike" title="Florida&#39;s Turnpike">Florida's Turnpike</a> currently implement closed systems.
</p><p>The Union Toll Plaza on the <a href="/wiki/Garden_State_Parkway" title="Garden State Parkway">Garden State Parkway</a> was the first ever to use an automated toll collection machine. A plaque commemorating the event includes the first quarter collected at its toll booths.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first major deployment of an <a href="/wiki/RFID" class="mw-redirect" title="RFID">RFID</a> <a href="/wiki/Electronic_toll_collection" title="Electronic toll collection">electronic toll collection</a> system in the United States was on the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_North_Tollway" title="Dallas North Tollway">Dallas North Tollway</a> in 1989 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amtech&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Amtech (page does not exist)">Amtech</a> (see <a href="/wiki/TollTag" class="mw-redirect" title="TollTag">TollTag</a>). The Amtech RFID technology used on the Dallas North Tollway was originally developed at Sandia Labs for use in tagging and tracking livestock. In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Telepass" title="Telepass">Telepass</a> active transponder RFID system was introduced across Italy. Several US states now use <a href="/wiki/US_Mobile_Tolling_Platforms" title="US Mobile Tolling Platforms"> mobile tolling platforms</a> to facilitate use of payment via smartphones. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_407" title="Ontario Highway 407">Highway 407</a> in the province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada, has no toll booths, and instead reads a transponder mounted on the windshields of each vehicle using the road (the rear licence plates of vehicles lacking a transponder are photographed when they enter and exit the highway). This made the highway the first all-automated toll highway in the world. A bill is mailed monthly for usage of the 407. Lower charges are levied on frequent 407 users who carry electronic transponders in their vehicles. The approach has not been without controversy: In 2003 the 407 ETR settled<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> a class action with a refund to users.
</p><p>Throughout most of the East Coast of the United States, <a href="/wiki/E-ZPass" title="E-ZPass">E-ZPass</a> (operated under the brand <a href="/wiki/I-Pass" title="I-Pass">I-Pass</a> in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>) is accepted on almost all toll roads. Similar systems include <a href="/wiki/SunPass" title="SunPass">SunPass</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/FasTrak" title="FasTrak">FasTrak</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Good_to_Go_(toll_collection_system)" title="Good to Go (toll collection system)">Good to Go</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington State</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=ExpressToll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ExpressToll (page does not exist)">ExpressToll</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. The systems use a small radio <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponder</a> mounted in or on a customer's vehicle to deduct toll fares from a pre-paid account as the vehicle passes through the toll barrier. This reduces manpower at toll booths and increases traffic flow and fuel efficiency by reducing the need for complete stops to pay tolls at these locations.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg/220px-New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="103" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg/330px-New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg/440px-New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1998" data-file-height="933" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Jersey_Turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>E-ZPass lanes at a <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Turnpike" title="New Jersey Turnpike">New Jersey Turnpike</a> (<a href="/wiki/Interstate_95" title="Interstate 95">I-95</a>) Toll Gate for Exit 8A in <a href="/wiki/Monroe_Township,_Middlesex_County,_New_Jersey" title="Monroe Township, Middlesex County, New Jersey">Monroe Township, New Jersey</a>, United States</div></div></div>
<p>By designing a tollgate specifically for electronic collection, it is possible to carry out open-road tolling, where the customer does not need to slow at all when passing through the tollgate. The U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> is testing a system on a stretch of Texas 121 that has no toll booths. Drivers without a TollTag have their license plate photographed automatically and the registered owner will receive a monthly bill, at a higher rate than those vehicles with TollTags.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A similar variation of automatic collection is the Toll Roads in Orange County, CA, US wherein all entry or collection points are equipped with high-speed cameras which read license plates and users will have 7 calendar days to pay online using their plate number or else setup an account for automatic debits.
</p><p>The first all-electronic toll road in the northeastern United States, the InterCounty Connector (<a href="/wiki/Maryland_Route_200" title="Maryland Route 200">Maryland Route 200</a>) was partially opened to traffic in February 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-sun_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the final segment was completed in November 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  The first section of another all-electronic toll road, the <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Expressway" title="Triangle Expressway">Triangle Expressway</a>, opened at the beginning of 2012 in North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing_and_management">Financing and management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Financing and management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some toll roads are managed under such systems as the <a href="/wiki/Build-Operate-Transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Build-Operate-Transfer">Build-Operate-Transfer</a> (BOT) system. Private companies build the roads and are given a limited franchise. Ownership is transferred to the government when the franchise expires. This type of arrangement is prevalent in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. The BOT system is a fairly new concept that is gaining ground in the United States, with <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> already building and operating toll roads under this scheme. <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> are also considering the BOT methodology for future highway projects.
</p><p>The more traditional means of managing toll roads in the United States is through semi-autonomous <a href="/wiki/Public_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Public authority">public authorities</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> manage their toll roads in this manner. While most of the toll roads in California, Delaware, Florida, Texas, and Virginia are operating under the BOT arrangement, a few of the older toll roads in these states are still operated by public authorities.
</p><p>In France, some toll roads are operated by private or public companies, with specific taxes collected by the state.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toll_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Toll roads have been criticised as being inefficient in various ways:<sup id="cite_ref-Roth_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roth-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>They require vehicles to stop or slow down (except <a href="/wiki/Open_road_tolling" title="Open road tolling">open road tolling</a>); manual toll collection wastes time and raises vehicle operating costs.</li>
<li>Collection costs can absorb up to one-third of revenues, and revenue theft is considered to be comparatively easy.</li>
<li>Where the tolled roads are less congested than the parallel "free" roads, the traffic diversion resulting from the tolls increases congestion on the road system and reduces its usefulness.</li>
<li>By tracking the vehicle locations, their drivers are subject to an effectual restriction of their freedom of movement and freedom from excessive surveillance.</li></ol>
<p>A number of additional criticisms are also directed at toll roads in general:
</p>
<ol><li>Toll roads are a form of <a href="/wiki/Regressive_tax" title="Regressive tax">regressive taxation</a>; that is, compared to conventional taxes for funding roads, they benefit wealthier citizens more than poor citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>If toll roads are owned or managed by private entities, the citizens may lose money overall compared to conventional public funding because the private owners/operators of the toll system will naturally seek to profit from the roads.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The managing entities, whether public or private, may not correctly account for the overall social costs, particularly to the poor, when setting pricing and thus may hurt the neediest segments of society.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>People who have to use a toll road when driving to work, might have to pay fairly much on an annual basis, while colleagues living elsewhere do not have to pay any toll.</li></ol>
