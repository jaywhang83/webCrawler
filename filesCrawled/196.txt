	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Nobiliary particle</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>nobiliary particle</b> is used in a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> or family name in many <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western cultures</a> to signal the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> of a family. The <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">particle</a> used varies depending on the country, language and period of time. However, in some languages the nobiliary particle is the same as a regular <a href="/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition" title="Preposition and postposition">prepositional particle</a> that was used in the <a href="/wiki/Surname#History" title="Surname">creation of many surnames</a>. In some countries, it became customary to distinguish the nobiliary particle from the regular one by a different spelling, although in other countries these conventions did not arise, occasionally resulting in ambiguity. The nobiliary particle can often be omitted in everyday speech or certain contexts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark_and_Norway">Denmark and Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Denmark and Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Danish_nobility" title="Danish nobility">Danish nobility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_nobility" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian nobility">Norwegian nobility</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_(Sparre)_(1517-1605).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg/220px-G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg/330px-G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg/440px-G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_%28Sparre%29_%281517-1605%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2204" data-file-height="2880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_(Sparre)_(1517-1605).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6rvel_Fadersdotter_(Sparre)" title="Görvel Fadersdotter (Sparre)">Gørvel Fadersdotter of Giske</a> (1517–1605). In the painting: 'Fru Gørvel Fadersdatter til Giedske'</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, there is a distinction between (1) nobiliary particles in family names and (2) prepositions denoting an individual person's place of residence.
</p><p>Nobiliary particles like <i>af</i>, <i>von</i>, and <i>de</i> (English: <i>of</i>) are integrated parts of family names. The use of particles was not a particular privilege for the nobility. On the other hand, particles were almost exclusively used by and associated with them. Especially in the late 17th and 18th centuries, a person would often receive a particle along with his or her old or new family name when ennobled. Examples are families like <a href="/wiki/Gyldenpalm_(noble_family)" title="Gyldenpalm (noble family)">de Gyldenpalm</a> (lit. 'of Goldenpalm') and <a href="/wiki/Munthe_af_Morgenstierne_(noble_family)" class="mw-redirect" title="Munthe af Morgenstierne (noble family)">von Munthe af Morgenstierne</a> (lit. 'of Munthe of Morningstar'). Otherwise, particles would arrive together with immigrants. Examples are families like <a href="/wiki/Preben_von_Ahnen" title="Preben von Ahnen">von Ahnen</a>. Prominent non-noble families having used particles are <a href="/wiki/Cappelen_(family)" title="Cappelen (family)">von Cappelen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Von_der_Lippe_(Norwegian_family)" class="mw-redirect" title="Von der Lippe (Norwegian family)">von der Lippe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A9quy_family" title="Créquy family">de Créqui dit la Roche</a>.
</p><p>The preposition <i>til</i> (English: <i>to</i>, but translates as <i>of</i>; comparable with German <i>zu</i>) is placed behind a person's full name in order to denote his or her place of residence, for example <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Jonsson" title="Sigurd Jonsson">Sigurd Jonsson til Sudreim</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/French_nobility" title="French nobility">French nobility</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, largely as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>) the particle <i>de</i> precedes a <i>nom de terre</i> ("name of land") in many families of the French nobility (for example, <a href="/wiki/Maximilien_de_B%C3%A9thune,_Duke_of_Sully" title="Maximilien de Béthune, Duke of Sully">Maximilien de Béthune</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>   A few do not have this particle (for example, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_S%C3%A9guier" title="Pierre Séguier">Pierre Séguier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chancellor of France">Lord Chancellor of France</a>).  The particle can also be <i>du</i> ("of the" in the masculine form), <i>d' </i> (employed, in accordance with the rules of <a href="/wiki/Elision_(French)" title="Elision (French)">orthography</a>, when the <i>nom de terre</i> begins with a vowel; for example, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ferdinand_Philippe,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Ferdinand Philippe, Duke of Orléans">Ferdinand d'Orléans</a>), or <i>des</i> ("of the" in the plural).  In French, <i>de</i> indicates a link between the land and a person—either <a href="/wiki/Landlord" title="Landlord">landlord</a> or <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasant</a>.
</p><p>Never in French history was this particle proof of nobility.  The nobleman was always designated an <i>escuyer</i> (<i>dapifer</i> in Latin, for "<a href="/wiki/Squire" title="Squire">squire</a>") or, better, a <i>chevalier</i> (<i>miles</i> in Latin, for "<a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>").  Only knights could be designated by the spoken style <i>monseigneur</i> or <i>messire</i> (<i>dominus</i> in Latin, for "<a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">sir</a>"), as, for example, "monseigneur <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_du_Guesclin" title="Bertrand du Guesclin">Bertrand du Guesclin</a>, chevalier" (in English form, "Sir Bertrand du Guesclin, knight").
</p><p>So, in fact and by convention, surnames with the nonnoble use of the particle <i>de</i> are spelled as a single word (e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Pierre_Dupont" title="Pierre Dupont">Pierre Dupont</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> though many such conserved the <i>de</i> as a separate word.
</p><p>From the sixteenth century, surnames among the French nobility have often been composed of a combination of <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> names, titles, or <i>noms de terres</i> ("names of lands" or estates) joined by the preposition <i>de</i>, as in "<a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord" title="Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord">Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The use of this particle began to be an essential appearance of nobility.  But, after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">kingdom of France</a>, the use of <i>de</i> has not invariably evidenced nobility, as shown in <a href="/wiki/Val%C3%A9ry_Giscard_d%27Estaing" title="Valéry Giscard d&#39;Estaing">Valéry Giscard d'Estaing</a>'s grandfather's change of name in the early twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Even earlier in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries many middle-class families simply adopted the particle without being ennobled; <a href="/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre" title="Maximilien Robespierre">Maximilien Robespierre</a>'s family, for example, used the particle for some generations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany_and_Austria">Germany and Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Germany and Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/German_nobility" title="German nobility">German nobility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Austrian_nobility" title="Austrian nobility">Austrian nobility</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <b>von</b> (descending <i>from</i>) or <b>zu</b> (resident <i>at</i>) generally precedes the surname of a noble family (in, for example, the names of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Heinrich_Graf_zu_Pappenheim" title="Gottfried Heinrich Graf zu Pappenheim">Gottfried Heinrich Graf zu Pappenheim</a>). If it is justified, they can be used together ("von und zu"): the present ruler of Liechtenstein, for example, is <a href="/wiki/Hans-Adam_II,_Prince_of_Liechtenstein" title="Hans-Adam II, Prince of Liechtenstein">Johannes Adam Ferdinand Alois Josef Maria Marko d'Aviano Pius von und zu Liechtenstein</a>.
</p><p>As in France and Spain, not all noble families use a nobiliary particle. The names of the most ancient nobility, the <i><a href="/wiki/Uradel" title="Uradel">Uradel</a></i>, but also names of some old untitled nobility, often do not contain the particle von or zu, such as <i>Grote</i>, <i>Knigge</i> or <i>Vincke</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nichtadeliges_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nichtadeliges-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, the prefix <i>von</i> occurs, in the names of 200 to 300 non-noble families,<sup id="cite_ref-Adelszeichen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adelszeichen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> much like <i>van</i> in the Netherlands. Especially in northwestern Germany (Bremen, Hamburg, Holstein, Lower Saxony, Schleswig, Westphalia) and in German-speaking Switzerland, von is a frequent element in non-noble surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-Adelszeichen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adelszeichen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In Austria and Bavaria, non-noble surnames containing von were widely altered by compounding it to the main surname element in the 19th century, such as <i>von Werden</i> → <i>Vonwerden</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Adelszeichen_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adelszeichen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Hungary was part of the Austrian-Hungarian Empire, Hungarian nobility used a different particle, <i>de</i>, which had no meaning in Hungarian and was apparently borrowed from French. Sometimes it preceded last names of Slovak or Ukrainian origin (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Yuriy_Fedkovych" title="Yuriy Fedkovych">Hordynskyi de Fed'kovyč</a>), which made these surnames sound especially comical for outsiders.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_nobility" title="Portuguese nobility">Portuguese nobility</a></div>
<p>In Portugal there are not, and never were, any special naming conventions to show nobility. Personal titles like <i><a href="/wiki/Dom_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dom (title)">Dom</a></i> (and its female variant <i>Dona</i>) may be used by the clergy, for instance, before their Christian name, not implying nobility, except if one previously knows the name as belong to a commoner.
</p><p>Furthermore, Portuguese nobility is traditionally recognised just to people being born to four noble quarters: both grandfathers and both grandmothers must have been noble for their grandson or granddaughter to be considered a noble at birth, independently of any noble name, with or without particle.
</p><p>Portuguese surnames do not indicate nobility, as usually, the same surnames exist in noble and non-noble families. The restriction to nobility and the clergy of bearing arms at the beginning of the 16th century, when king <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I</a> extinguished the previous <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> armorial, usually shows someone to be noble if he or she bears personal or family arms. But nobility in Portugal was never restricted to the bearers of arms, and many Portuguese nobles did not or do not have arms at all.
</p><p>The preposition <i>de</i>, and its different orthographic forms (<i>do</i>, <i>dos</i>, <i>da</i> and <i>das</i>), like in France, do not indicate nobility in the bearer. Portuguese modern law recognises to any citizen the right not to sign those particles, even if they are present in their identification documents, and the opposite right is legally allowed to those Portuguese citizens who, not having in their documentation any such prepositions, are able to sign it if they wish. In fact, articles and prepositions are considered in Portuguese nomenclature just as an embellishment to any name.
</p><p>Good taste made usually Portuguese nobility reduce prepositions linking their many surnames, signing just one at the beginning of the name, and then the last surname being preceded by <i>e</i> (and), not to repeat the preposition. For instance, the name <i>João Duarte da Silva dos Santos da Costa de Sousa</i> may also legally be signed <i>João Duarte Silva Santos Costa Sousa</i>. Tradition and good taste should make him sign just <i>João Duarte da Silva Santos Costa e Sousa</i>. The last "and" (<i>e</i>) substitutes all previous surnames' prepositions except the first one, and cannot ever be used without a previous preposition to justify it. An exception to this rule is only shown with duplicate surnames linked by and (<i>e</i>), for instance when the maternal surnames come before the paternal ones: <i>Diogo Afonso da Conceição e Silva</i> (name and mother's duplicate surname)<i>Tavares da Costa</i> (paternal duplicate surname).
</p><p>From the 19th century on, it became customary for Portuguese titled nobility to socially indicate their title as a subsidiary surname: for instance, <i>Joaquim Diniz Tavares dos Santos e Silva (Paço do Lumiar)</i>. This social rule does not apply to members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Braganza" title="House of Braganza">Portuguese royal house</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_nobility" title="Spanish nobility">Spanish nobility</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, the nobiliary particle <b>de</b> is also used in two different styles. The first is a "patronymic-<i>de</i>-<a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponymic</a>" formula,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as used by, among others, the fifteenth-century general <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba</a>, the fourteenth-century chronicler and poet <a href="/wiki/Pero_L%C3%B3pez_de_Ayala" title="Pero López de Ayala">Pero López de Ayala</a>, the European discoverer of the eastern Pacific, <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Núñez de Balboa">Vasco Núñez de Balboa</a>, and many other <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The second style is use of the particle <i>de</i> before the entire surname. This style resembles but is more ambiguous than the French one, since there is no convention for a different spelling when the <i>de</i> is simply a prepositional particle in non-noble toponymic names such as De la Rúa (literally, "of the street") or De la Torre ("of the tower"). Examples of nobility particle <i>de</i> without patronymic include the sixteenth-century first Marquis of Santa Cruz, <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Baz%C3%A1n,_1st_Marquis_of_Santa_Cruz" class="mw-redirect" title="Álvaro de Bazán, 1st Marquis of Santa Cruz">Álvaro de Bazán</a>, the conquistador <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a>, a common tradition in Spanish culture. Unlike French, Spanish lacks <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">elision</a>, and so no contraction is used when the surname starts with a vowel (though exceptionally we find <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Arias_D%C3%A1vila" title="Pedro Arias Dávila">Pedro Arias Dávila</a>), but contraction is used when the surname includes the article "el" as in <a href="/wiki/Baltasar_del_Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Baltasar del Alcázar">Baltasar del Alcázar</a>.
</p><p>A Spanish law on names, from 1958 and still in force, does not allow a person to add a <i>de</i> to their surname if it does not already have it. The law does allow for one exception. A <i>de</i> may be added in front of a surname that could be otherwise misunderstood as a forename.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Conclusive proof of the nobility of a surname can be determined by establishing whether that surname is associated with a <a href="/wiki/Blazon" title="Blazon">blazon</a>, since for centuries <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coats of arms</a> have been borne legally only by persons of noble condition.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Surnames composed of two names linked by a <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphen</a> ("-"), implying that equal importance is given to both families, do not indicate nobility. For example, the hyphenated surname <i>Suárez-Llanos</i> does not indicate nobility.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Swiss_nobility" title="Swiss nobility">Swiss nobility</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <b>de</b> or <b>von</b> precedes a noble name, depending on the <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">canton</a> of origin.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_England" title="Peerage of England">Peerage of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_peers_and_baronets" title="Welsh peers and baronets">Welsh peers and baronets</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the words <b>de</b>, borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, and the English <b>of</b>, were often used in names in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England and Wales</a>, as in "<a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">Simon de Montfort</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Richard_of_Shrewsbury,_1st_Duke_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard of Shrewsbury, 1st Duke of York">Richard of Shrewsbury</a>". However, the usage of "de" is often misunderstood, as in most cases it was used only in documents written in Latin or French. At the time, in translating into English, "de" was sometimes converted into "of" and sometimes omitted; only rarely was it used in the English form of a name. It is also significant that both "de" and "of" were used simply to show geographical origin in the names of people of all classes, so that in England and Wales neither "de" nor "of" should be looked on as in themselves nobiliary.
</p><p>Despite the lack of official significance of the words "de" or "of" in names, there was sometimes a perception that these words connoted nobility. For example, on 8&#160;October 1841, a month after <a href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Trafford" title="Thomas de Trafford">Thomas Trafford</a> was created the <a href="/wiki/De_Trafford_baronets" title="De Trafford baronets">1st Baronet de Trafford</a>, Queen Victoria issued a royal licence to "Sir Thomas Joseph Trafford ... that he may henceforth resume the ancient patronymic of his family, by assuming and using the surname of De Trafford, instead of that of 'Trafford' and that such surname may be henceforth taken and used by his issue."<sup id="cite_ref-Gazette1841-10-08_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazette1841-10-08-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The anglicisation to Trafford had probably occurred in the 15th century, when the Norman article "de", signifying that a family originated from a particular place, was generally dropped in England. The resumption of such older versions of family names was a <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a> trend in 19th-century England, encouraged by a mistaken belief that the article "de" indicated nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-1911p605_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1911p605-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As in Spain, English and Welsh surnames composed of two names linked by a <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphen</a> ("-"), do not indicate nobility per se. For example, the hyphenated Welsh name <i><a href="/wiki/Rees-Jones" title="Rees-Jones">Rees-Jones</a></i> does not indicate nobility.  However, historically in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> a multi-barrelled name was indicative of good pedigree and social standing, such that there was and remains a link between hyphenated names and nobility.  The reason for this was to emphasise the ancestry of the family, where one or both sides came from aristocratic houses, or to retain a name which had come to prominence for individual achievement.  For instance, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Sir Winston Spencer-Churchill</a>'s surname evidences his descendancy from both the aristocratic <a href="/wiki/Spencer_family" title="Spencer family">Spencer family</a>, amongst whom the <a href="/wiki/Earl_Spencer_(peerage)" title="Earl Spencer (peerage)">Earls Spencer</a> are prominent, and the illustrious background of the Churchills, who hark back to their founder-hero, the prominent military leader <a href="/wiki/John_Churchill,_1st_Duke_of_Marlborough" title="John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough">John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough</a>.  Some of the grandest members of the British aristocracy have triple-barrelled names, for instance the Vane-Tempest-Stewart family, who hold the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Londonderry" title="Marquess of Londonderry">marquessate of Londonderry</a>; for a while, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Buckingham_and_Chandos" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Buckingham and Chandos">Dukes of Buckingham and Chandos</a> bore five surnames: Temple-Nugent-Brydges-Chandos-Grenville.
</p><p>However in contemporay Britain this correlation has weakened, as more middle and lower class families have started hyphenating their names on marriage, and/or passing it to their issue, with 11% of newlyweds in the 18-34 demographic hyphenating their surnames as of 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>In modern times, a nobiliary particle (as the term is widely understood on the Continent) is rarely used. More usual is the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_designation" title="Territorial designation">territorial designation</a>, which in practice is almost identical.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Scotland, there is strictly no nobiliary particle, but the use of the word <b>of</b> as a <a href="/wiki/Territorial_designation" title="Territorial designation">territorial designation</a> has a long history. In this usage, "of" and a place name follow on from a family surname, as in the name "Aeneas MacDonell of Glengarry". If the place name is identical to the surname, it is sometimes rendered as "that Ilk", e.g. "Iain Moncreiffe of that Ilk". Recognition of a territorial designation is granted in Scotland by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lyon">Lord Lyon</a> to Scottish armigers (those entitled to bear a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>) who own or were born in or are associated with named land, generally in a rural area not forming part of a town. The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lyon">Lord Lyon</a> advises that for a territorial designation to be recognised there must be "ownership of a substantial area of land to which a well-attested name attaches, that is to say, ownership of an 'estate', or farm or, at the very least, a house with policies extending to five acres or thereby".<sup id="cite_ref-Guidance_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guidance-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  The territorial designation in this case is considered to be an indivisible part of the name, not in itself necessarily indicating historical feudal nobility, but recognition in a territorial designation is usually accorded alongside the grant or matriculation of a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_heraldry" title="Scottish heraldry">Scottish coat of arms</a>, which effectively confers or recognises minor nobility status, even if not ancient. Despite this, the right to bear a territorial designation can also exist for landowners who are not <a href="/wiki/Armiger" title="Armiger">armigerous</a>, however this right is not made good until receiving official recognition; Learney comments: "mere assumption is not sufficient to warrant these territorial and chiefly names".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  A person bearing a Scottish territorial designation is either a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_feudal_barony" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish feudal barony">Feudal Baron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clan_chief#.22Clan_Chiefs.22_and_.22Clan_Chieftains.22" title="Scottish clan chief">Chief or Chieftain</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Laird" title="Laird">Laird</a>, the latter denoting "landowner", or is a descendant of one of the same.<sup id="cite_ref-Debretts_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debretts-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lyon">Lord Lyon</a> is the ultimate arbiter as to determining entitlement to a territorial designation, and his right of discretion in recognising these, and their status as a name, dignity or title, have been confirmed in the Scottish courts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  In speech or correspondence, a <a href="/wiki/Laird" title="Laird">Laird</a> is correctly addressed by the name of his estate (particularly in lowland Scotland) or his surname with designation, e.g. <a href="/wiki/William_Maitland_of_Lethington" title="William Maitland of Lethington">William Maitland of Lethington</a> would be addressed as "Lethington" or "Maitland of Lethington".<sup id="cite_ref-Debretts_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debretts-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nobiliary_particle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although many languages have nobiliary particles, their use may sometimes be misleading, as it often does not give any evidence of nobility. Some examples are:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a></b>:</li></ul>
<ol><li><b>Brazil</b> continued to recognize the distinction of <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_nobility" title="Brazilian nobility">its nobility</a> longer than its republican neighbors, since it remained a monarchy until 1889, but it has also been a republic ever since. Nobility in Brazil was based on that of Portugal, with the only distinction that in the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Brazilian Empire">Brazilian Empire</a> titles and simple nobility were conferred only on a personal basis, and could not be inherited, unlike Portugal. In fact, unlike colonial nobility, Brazilian Empire nobility was awarded as a private and personal decoration for one lifespan, though in very rare cases, some Brazilian titles were again conferred to one or two more generations. Brazilian imperial nobility did not enjoy any financial or land privileges. With the exception of the imperial family, a difference between a person with a Brazilian nobiliarchic title or descendant of someone who was a Brazilian noble from another who was not a noble and not descended from a Brazilian noble, was only the status and distinction of nobility.</li>
<li><b>Spanish-speaking countries</b> other than Spain use the particle <i>de</i> without legally meaning nobility. With only a few short-lived exceptions, all <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Spanish American</a> countries adopted a republican form of government and abolished noble distinctions (but see the article on the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_nobility" title="Mexican nobility">Mexican nobility</a>). Laws on names vary in Latin America but occasionally people are able to fashion new surnames that may sound noble: an example is <a href="/wiki/Javier_P%C3%A9rez_de_Cu%C3%A9llar" title="Javier Pérez de Cuéllar">Javier Pérez de Cuéllar</a>, whose father, Ricardo Pérez de Cuéllar, decided to permanently combine his last names as a more distinguishable paternal last name for his offspring.</li></ol>
<ul><li><b>Arabic-speaking countries</b>&#160;: The definitive article "Al" meaning "The" is added before the surname to add noblesse. For example, Suleiman Ahmad could become Suleiman Al-Ahmad.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Nobility_of_Italy" title="Nobility of Italy">Italy</a></b>: The nobiliary particles (or <i>predicati</i>) <b>de</b> or <b>di</b> are used after the surname or the name of the title. <i>Di</i> is sometimes contracted when the surname begins with a vowel, in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Italian_grammar#Prepositions" title="Italian grammar">Italian orthography</a>. An example of nobility is the name of the noted writer <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Tomasi_di_Lampedusa" title="Giuseppe Tomasi di Lampedusa">Giuseppe Tomasi di Lampedusa</a>, where <i>Tomasi</i> is the surname and <a href="/wiki/Lampedusa" title="Lampedusa">Lampedusa</a> was the family's feudal property. An ordinary use is found in the name of the American actor <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_DiCaprio" title="Leonardo DiCaprio">Leonardo DiCaprio</a>, of Italian descent (his surname is spelled as a single word, in accordance with standard <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> practice). However, the surnames of certain Italian noble families are by their nature toponymic, reflecting the names of medieval feudal properties, e.g. di Savoia, d'Aquino. Further, the use of the particle "de" or <i>de’</i> is often an abbreviation for "dei," suggesting the family is of a noble family. For instance, <i><a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%E2%80%99_Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorenzo de’ Medici">Lorenzo de’ Medici</a></i> means Lorenzo of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">Medici [family]</a>.  Nevertheless, the ordinary use of the prepositions "Di" (of) or "Da" (from), also in conjunction with articles (Del, Della, Dei, Dal, Dalla, Dai, etc. ) can easily be part of the actual name and does not indicate nobility <i>ex se</i>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/List_of_Dutch_noble_families" title="List of Dutch noble families">Netherlands</a></b>: The particle <b><a href="/wiki/Van_(Dutch)" title="Van (Dutch)">van</a></b> is not an indicator of nobility (it is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Tussenvoegsel" title="Tussenvoegsel">tussenvoegsel</a></i>): the percentage of <i>van</i>-preceded surnames that are noble is not significantly higher than that of any other surname; they are evenly spread over the social strata. However, the particles <b>tot</b> and <b>thoe</b>, historically meaning "at" and related to German <i>zu</i>, are a strong indicator of nobility if combined with <i>van</i> in a surname, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Van_Voorst_tot_Voorst" title="Van Voorst tot Voorst">van Voorst tot Voorst</a></i> (the prepositions <i>tot</i> and <i>thoe</i> were once used to denote the place of residence of a <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lord</a>, whereas <i>van</i> referred to the <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">domain</a> whence he derived his title). Note that many noble families in the Netherlands have non-noble branches that are closely related and share the same surname. Double surnames are, therefore, a weak but significant indicator of nobility; many <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">patrician</a> and other families have double names as well.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a></b>: The nobiliary particle is <b>Aw</b> meaning "honorable", "venerable", or simply "Sir". Reserved for learned Islamic clerics, and used throughout the Somali territories. During his research in the ancient town of Amud, the historian <a href="/wiki/G.W.B._Huntingford" class="mw-redirect" title="G.W.B. Huntingford">G.W.B. Huntingford</a> noticed that whenever an old site had the prefix <i><a href="/wiki/Somali_aristocratic_and_court_titles#Nobiliary_particles" title="Somali aristocratic and court titles">Aw</a></i> in its name (such as the current Regional Governor of Gedo Aw libaax), it denoted the final resting place of a local Saint..</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Belgian_nobility" title="Belgian nobility">Belgium</a></b>: The Flemish words <b>de</b>, <b>der</b>, and <b>van</b> may be nobiliary particles, but as in the Netherlands they are more often not of nobiliary origin so are not in themselves evidence of nobility. About the French word <b>de</b>, see France.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Swedish_nobility" title="Swedish nobility">Sweden</a></b>: Some noble families use 'af' as a nobiliary particle. This is the pre-1906 spelling of the modern Swedish 'av' ("of"), and corresponds to the German <i>von</i>. However, <i>von</i> has also been used in some noble families, some of German and some of Swedish origin. Thus <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a>, upon his ennoblement, took the name <i>Carl von Linné</i>. The particles <i>af</i> and <i>von</i> do not have to be used with a toponym; they can simply be attached to the pre-noble surname. The word <i>de</i> has also been used in some families descended from 17th century immigrants of craftsmen and others from <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>. A well-known example is the <a href="/wiki/De_Geer" title="De Geer">De Geer</a> family; other examples include <i>Du Rietz</i> and De Besche.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Finnish_nobility" title="Finnish nobility">Finland</a></b>: the old Swedish form 'af' and the German 'von', denoting descent or location, are used in some noble families.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a></b>: The <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B8%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:ณ">na</a></i> (of <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> origin) may be granted by a <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Thailand" title="Monarchy of Thailand">Thai monarch</a> to form a Thai family name signifying <i><b>of</b></i> a former kingdom or <a href="/wiki/Tributary_state" title="Tributary state">tributary state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Siam" class="mw-redirect" title="Siam">Siam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Example: <a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Na_Ayudhya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Na Ayudhya</a>, putative royal lineage in the <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Ayutthaya Kingdom</a> that may be granted after marriage into the royal <a href="/wiki/Chakri_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Chakri Dynasty">Chakri Dynasty</a>. The honorific particles <a href="/wiki/Sri" title="Sri">Sri</a> (<a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>: Si) and Phra (<a href="/w/index.php?title=%E0%B8%9E%E0%B8%A3%E0%B8%B0&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="พระ (page does not exist)">พระ</a>) are also used in various names of Thai nobility (e.g. Somdet Chao Phraya Sri Suriwongse, RTGS: Si Suriwong). Phra which is rooted in the Sanskrit vara (in Pali: bara) means holy or excellent and is also used as a formal address (Preah) in the royal names of several Cambodian rulers (Preah <a href="/wiki/Norodom_Sihanouk" title="Norodom Sihanouk">Norodom Sihanouk</a> and his son Preah <a href="/wiki/Norodom_Sihamoni" title="Norodom Sihamoni">Norodom Sihamoni</a>).  Furthermore the particle Sri which derives from the old indo-aryan Sanskrit word Shri - that was used in vedic scriptures to address Gods, Goddesses (e.g. Sri <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a>) and Kings (rajahs) - is also used in other countries of Southeast Asia with respective vowal shifts. Variations of it for example are found in the  federal constitutional monarchy of Malaysia (granted title datuk seri as in: Dato Seri <a href="/wiki/Najib_Razak" title="Najib Razak">Najib Razak</a>), in the Republic of Indonesia (Batak and Indonesian national hero: King <a href="/wiki/Sisingamangaraja_XII" title="Sisingamangaraja XII">Sisingamangaraja XII</a>; Javanese: <a href="/wiki/Sri_Mulyani_Indrawati" class="mw-redirect" title="Sri Mulyani Indrawati">Sri Mulyani Indrawati</a>, former finance minister of Indonesia) and in Sri Lanka (country name itself and the last ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Sinhala_Kingdom" title="Sinhala Kingdom">Sinhalese</a> Kingdom: Sri <a href="/wiki/Vikrama_Rajasinha" class="mw-redirect" title="Vikrama Rajasinha">Vikrama Rajasinha</a>).</li></ul>
