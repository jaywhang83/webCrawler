	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">City status in the United Kingdom</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Cathedral city" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_city_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Cathedral city (disambiguation)">Cathedral city (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Honorary status granted by royal charter to settlements in the United Kingdom</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:York_Minster_close.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/York_Minster_close.jpg/220px-York_Minster_close.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/York_Minster_close.jpg/330px-York_Minster_close.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/94/York_Minster_close.jpg 2x" data-file-width="408" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:York_Minster_close.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Until the nineteenth century, city status in England and Wales was associated with the presence of a cathedral, such as <a href="/wiki/York_Minster" title="York Minster">York Minster</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>City status in the United Kingdom</b> is granted by the <a href="/wiki/Monarch_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarch of the United Kingdom">monarch of the United Kingdom</a> to a select group of communities: as of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, there are <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of cities in the United Kingdom">69 cities in the United Kingdom</a> – 51 in England, six in Wales, seven in Scotland and five in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The holding of city status gives a settlement no special rights. This appellation carries its own prestige and competition for the status is hard-fought.
</p><p>The status does not apply automatically on the basis of any particular criteria, though in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> it was traditionally given to towns with <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_England_dioceses" title="List of Church of England dioceses">diocesan cathedrals</a>. This association between having a <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> and being called a city was established in the early 1540s when <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">King Henry VIII</a> founded dioceses (each having a cathedral in the <a href="/wiki/See_city" class="mw-redirect" title="See city">see city</a>) in six English towns and granted them city status by issuing <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>.
</p><p>City status in Ireland was granted to far fewer communities than in England and Wales, and there are only two pre-19th-century cities in present-day <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. In Scotland, city status did not explicitly receive any recognition by the state until the 19th century. At that time, a revival of grants of city status took place, first in England, where the grants were accompanied by the establishment of new cathedrals, and later in Scotland and Ireland. In the 20th century, it was explicitly recognised that the status of city in England and Wales would no longer be bound to the presence of a cathedral, and grants made since have been awarded to communities on a variety of criteria, including population size.
</p><p>The abolition of some corporate bodies as part of successive local-government reforms, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_(Ireland)_Act_1840" title="Municipal Corporations (Ireland) Act 1840">Municipal Corporations (Ireland) Act 1840</a>, has deprived some ancient cities of their status. However, letters patent have been issued for most of the affected cities to ensure the continuation or restoration of their status. At present, <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elgin,_Moray" title="Elgin, Moray">Elgin</a> are the only former cities in the United Kingdom. The name "City" does not, in itself, denote city status; it may be appended to place names for historic association (e.g. <a href="/wiki/White_City,_London" title="White City, London">White City</a>) or for marketing or disambiguation (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Stratford_City" title="Stratford City">Stratford City</a>). A number of large towns (such as those with over 200,000 residents) in the UK are bigger than some small cities, but cannot legitimately call themselves a <i>city</i> without the royal designation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-19th_century">Pre-19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The initial cities (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitas</a></i>) of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Britain</a> were the fortified settlements organised by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Romans">Romans</a> as the capitals of the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_tribes_of_the_British_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic tribes of the British Isles">Celtic tribes</a> under <a href="/wiki/Britannia_(Roman_province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britannia (Roman province)">Roman rule</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Christianity" title="Celtic Christianity">clerics</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">early Middle Ages</a> later preserved a traditional list of the "<a href="/wiki/28_Cities_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="28 Cities of Britain">28 Cities</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Welsh language">Old Welsh</a>: <i lang="owl"><a href="/wiki/Caer" title="Caer">cair</a></i>) which was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/De_Excidio_Britanniae" class="mw-redirect" title="De Excidio Britanniae">Gildas</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and listed by <a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Nennius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mommy_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mommy-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 16th century, a town was recognised as a city by the English Crown if it had a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_England_dioceses" title="List of Church of England dioceses">diocesan cathedral</a> within its limits, for which 22 dioceses existed in England &amp; Wales (see <a href="#City_status_conferment">City status conferment</a> further in the article). This association between having a cathedral and being called a city was established when <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> founded new dioceses (each having a cathedral in the <a href="/wiki/See_city" class="mw-redirect" title="See city">see city</a>) in six English towns and also granted them city status by issuing <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> demonstrating these were discrete procedures. Some cities today are very small because they were granted city status in or before the 16th century, then were unaffected by population growth during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>—notably <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Somerset" title="Wells, Somerset">Wells</a> (population about 10,000) and <a href="/wiki/St_Davids" title="St Davids">St Davids</a> (population about 2,000). After the 16th century, no new dioceses (and no new cities) were created until the 19th century in England (<a href="#Ireland_and_Northern_Ireland">a further city was created</a> in Ireland during the rule of King James I in the 17th century).
</p>
<h4><span id="1836.E2.80.931888"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1836–1888">1836–1888</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1836–1888">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A long-awaited resumption of <a href="/wiki/Historical_development_of_Church_of_England_dioceses#Modern_foundations" title="Historical development of Church of England dioceses">creating dioceses</a> began in 1836 with <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Ripon" title="Diocese of Ripon">Ripon</a>. Ripon Town Council assumed that this had elevated the town to the rank of a city, and started referring to itself as the <i>City and Borough of Ripon</i>. The next diocese formed was <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Diocese_of_Manchester" title="Anglican Diocese of Manchester">Manchester</a> and its Borough Council began informally to use the title <i>city</i>. When <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> visited Manchester in 1851, widespread doubts surrounding its status were raised. The pretension was ended when the borough petitioned for city status, which was granted by letters patent in 1853. This eventually forced Ripon to regularise its position; its city status was recognised by <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">Act of Parliament</a> in 1865. From this year Ripon bore city status whilst the rapidly expanding conurbation of Leeds – in the Ripon diocese – did not. The Manchester case established a precedent that any <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> in which an Anglican see was established was entitled to petition for city status. Accordingly, <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Albans" title="St Albans">St Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> were all officially designated as cities between 1877 and 1888.
</p><p>This was not without opposition from the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>, which dismissed <a href="/wiki/St_Albans" title="St Albans">St Albans</a> as "a fourth or fifth rate market town" and objected to <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>'s elevation on grounds of population. In one new diocese, <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Southwell" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Southwell">Southwell</a>, a city was not created, because it was a village without a borough corporation and therefore could not petition the Queen. The <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Southwell_and_Nottingham" title="Diocese of Southwell and Nottingham">diocese</a> covered the counties of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, and the boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> were disappointed that they would not be able to claim the title of city.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="1889.E2.80.931907"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1889–1907">1889–1907</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1889–1907">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birmingham_Council_House.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Birmingham_Council_House.jpg/220px-Birmingham_Council_House.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Birmingham_Council_House.jpg/330px-Birmingham_Council_House.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Birmingham_Council_House.jpg/440px-Birmingham_Council_House.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2020" data-file-height="1221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birmingham_Council_House.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> was the first English town without an Anglican cathedral to be granted city status. <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_City_Council" title="Birmingham City Council">Birmingham City Council</a> meets at the <a href="/wiki/Council_House,_Birmingham" title="Council House, Birmingham">Council House</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The link with Anglican dioceses was broken within England in 1889 when <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> successfully petitioned for city status (it was <a href="#Ireland_and_Northern_Ireland">pre-empted in Ireland by Belfast</a> in 1888) on the grounds of its large population and history of good local government. At the time of the grant, Birmingham lacked an Anglican cathedral, although <a href="/wiki/St_Philip%27s_Cathedral,_Birmingham" title="St Philip&#39;s Cathedral, Birmingham">the parish church</a> later became a cathedral in 1905. This new precedent was followed by other large municipalities: <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> became cities in 1893, and <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> were honoured on the occasion of Queen Victoria's <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamond Jubilee">Diamond Jubilee</a> in 1897. The last three had been the largest <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> outside the London area without city status.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1897 and 1914, applications were received from a number of other boroughs, but only <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> was successful in 1905 being designated a city and granted a <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Mayor">Lord Mayoralty</a> as "the Metropolis of Wales".
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_status_of_Westminster">The status of Westminster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The status of Westminster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1899" title="London Government Act 1899">London Government Act 1899</a> abolished the existing local authorities within the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a> and replaced them with 28 <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a>. Among the bodies to be dissolved was the Court of Burgesses of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_Burdett-Coutts" title="William Burdett-Coutts">William Burdett-Coutts</a>, one of Westminster's members of parliament, brought forward an amendment to rename the proposed borough of Greater Westminster to <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a></i>. This was intended to give "recognition to the title which the area ... had possessed for over three and a half centuries". He felt that if the status was not retained for the new borough it "must necessarily disappear altogether". The amendment was rejected by the government, however, with the <a href="/wiki/First_Lord_of_the_Treasury" title="First Lord of the Treasury">First Lord of the Treasury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Balfour" title="Arthur Balfour">Arthur Balfour</a>, believing it would be "an anomaly which, I think, would be not unnaturally resented by other districts which are as large in point of population as Westminster, although doubtless not so rich in historical associations".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The government eventually relented, with Balfour stating that "as soon as the necessary arrangements under the London Government Act have been completed, there will be conferred on the borough of Westminster, as constituted under the Act, the title of city, originally conferred in the time of Henry VIII".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">Letters patent</a> were duly issued granting the title of "city" to the newly created <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Westminster" title="Metropolitan Borough of Westminster">Metropolitan Borough of Westminster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="1907.E2.80.931953"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1907–1953">1907–1953</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1907–1953">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1907, the Home Office and <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">King Edward VII</a> agreed on a policy that future applicants would have to meet certain criteria. This policy, which was not at the time made public, had the effect of stemming the number of city creations.
</p><p>The 1907 policy contained three criteria:
</p>
<ul><li>A minimum population of 300,000.</li>
<li>A "local metropolitan character"—this implied that the town had a distinct identity of its own and was the centre of a wider area.</li>
<li>A good record of local government.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>However, well into the 20th century it was often assumed that the presence of a cathedral was sufficient to elevate a town to city status, and that for cathedral cities the city charters were recognising its city status rather than granting it. On this basis, the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition">1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i></a> said that <a href="/wiki/Southwell,_Nottinghamshire" title="Southwell, Nottinghamshire">Southwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Asaph" title="St Asaph">St Asaph</a> were cities.
</p><p>The policy laid down by Edward VII was continued by his successor, <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George V</a>, who ascended the throne in 1910. In 1911, an application for city status by <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> was refused. Explaining the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>'s reason for not recommending the King to approve the petition, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a> stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>...during the reign of his late Majesty it was found necessary, in order to maintain the value of the distinction, to lay down a rule as to the minimum population which should ordinarily, in connexion with other considerations, be regarded as qualifying a borough for that higher status.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, the King made an official visit to <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a> in 1919 to commemorate its contributions to the military victory. The borough council had made several applications for city status since 1889, and took the opportunity of the visit to renew its request. <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a> had a population of approximately 230,000 at the previous census, but its petition was granted as an exception to the policy, as it was officially a restoration of a dignity lost in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-timesleic_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timesleic-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> When the county borough of <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> applied for city status in 1925, it was initially refused as it had only 294,000 inhabitants. The decision was overturned, however, as it was felt to have outstanding importance as the centre of the pottery industry. The effective relaxation of the population rule led to applications from <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Borough_of_Salford" title="County Borough of Salford">Salford</a>. The civil servants in the Home Office were minded to refuse both applications. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Salford,_Greater_Manchester" class="mw-redirect" title="Salford, Greater Manchester">Salford</a> was felt to be "merely a scratch collection of 240,000 people cut off from Manchester by the river". Salford's case, however, was considered favourably by the Home Secretary, <a href="/wiki/William_Joynson-Hicks" class="mw-redirect" title="William Joynson-Hicks">William Joynson-Hicks</a>, who had once been <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for a neighbouring constituency of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. Following protests from <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, which felt it had better credentials as a larger town and as the "first Naval Port of the kingdom", both applications were approved in 1926.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1927, a <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(County_Boroughs_and_Adjustments)_Act_1926" title="Local Government (County Boroughs and Adjustments) Act 1926">Royal Commission on Local Government</a> was examining local council areas and functions in England and Wales. The question arose as to which towns were entitled to be called cities, and the chairman, the <a href="/wiki/Richard_Onslow,_5th_Earl_of_Onslow" title="Richard Onslow, 5th Earl of Onslow">Earl of Onslow</a>, wrote to the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> to seek clarification. The Home Office replied with a memorandum that read:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The title of a city which is borne by certain boroughs is a purely titular distinction. It has no connexion with the status of the borough in respect of local government and confers no powers or privileges. At the present time and for several centuries past the title has been obtained only by an express grant from the Sovereign effected by letters patent; but a certain number of cities possess the title by very ancient prescriptive right. There is no necessary connexion between the title of a city and the seat of a bishopric, and the creation of a new see neither constitutes the town concerned a city nor gives it any claim to the grant of letters patent creating it a city.<sup id="cite_ref-1927mem_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1927mem-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In 1928, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> submitted an application for city status. As the borough had more inhabitants than <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a> and had absorbed <a href="/wiki/Devonport,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Devonport, Devon">Devonport</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Stonehouse" class="mw-redirect" title="East Stonehouse">East Stonehouse</a>, the King agreed to the request. However, he indicated that he had "come to an end of city making", and <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton's</a> application in the following year was turned down.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The next city to be created was <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a> as part of the celebrations of the <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_King_George_VI_and_Queen_Elizabeth" class="mw-redirect" title="Coronation of King George VI and Queen Elizabeth">coronation of King George VI and Queen Elizabeth</a>. With a population of a little over 50,000, <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a> was stated to be an exception due to the town's "long association with the crown" and because it was "the county town of the King's Duchy of Lancaster". Following the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, members of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> Borough Council made contact with <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a> officials for assistance in their application. <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> became a city in 1951, again for "exceptional" reasons, as the only ancient seat of learning in the kingdom not a city or <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a> and to coincide with the 750th anniversary of the borough's first charter of incorporation.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a> also applied in 1951, but failed as it was felt not to have a sufficient identity apart from <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>, and reports on the conduct of local government in the town were unfavourable.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="1953.E2.80.931974"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1953–1974">1953–1974</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1953–1974">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>It was anticipated that the coronation of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II</a> in 1953 would lead to the creation of a city, and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> made approaches; the only civic honour given was that of a lord mayoralty to <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>. <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwark" title="Southwark">Southwark</a> made unsuccessful applications in 1955. The planned reorganisations by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1958%E2%80%931967)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1958–1967)">Local Government Commissions for England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_Wales" title="Local Government Commission for Wales">Wales</a> from 1958 effectively blocked new city grants. <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> lodged a petition in 1958. Initially refused in 1959, pending the decision of the Commission, it was eventually allowed in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In the meantime, the administration of London was reformed under the <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> was permitted to continue in existence largely unchanged, <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Westminster" title="Metropolitan Borough of Westminster">Westminster</a> was merged with two neighbouring authorities to form a new <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London borough</a> from 1 April 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In December 1963 it was announced that a charter was to be granted incorporating the new authority as "Westminster", and that the Queen had accepted the advice of the Home Secretary to raise the London borough to the title and dignity of city.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This example, of a successor local authority to a merged local government entity taking on that former entity's city status, was to be replicated in many instances as a result of the 1972/74 local government reforms across England and Wales (see below).
</p><p>With the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Report" title="Redcliffe-Maud Report">Royal Commission on Local Government in England</a> in 1966, city grants were again in abeyance in England. Attempts by <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a> to become cities were not proceeded with. In Wales, <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a> campaigned for city status throughout the 1960s. The campaign came to a successful conclusion in 1969, in conjunction with the investiture of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Charles, Prince of Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1974_reorganisation_and_new_cities">1974 reorganisation and new cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1974 reorganisation and new cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> abolished all existing local authorities outside London (other than <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a>) in England and Wales. This meant that the various local authorities that held city status ceased to exist on 1 April 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> To preserve city status new letters patent were issued to the most relevant <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">non-metropolitan district</a> or <a href="/wiki/Successor_parish" title="Successor parish">successor parish</a> councils created by the Act.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Some of these came to cover local government districts many times wider than the previous city, even taking in many square miles of rural land outside the urban areas, for example the cities of <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Leeds" title="City of Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_of_Winchester" title="City of Winchester">Winchester</a>. Three non-local authority preservations arose: here <a href="/wiki/Charter_trustees" title="Charter trustees">charter trustees</a> were established for the cities of <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salisbury" title="Salisbury">Salisbury</a> (or New Sarum) being neither districts nor civil parishes, and special letters patent for a time preserved the city of <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1977, as part of the celebrations of the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Silver Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Silver Jubilee of Elizabeth II</a>, the Home Office identified nine candidates for city status: <a href="/wiki/Blackburn" title="Blackburn">Blackburn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandwell" title="Sandwell">Sandwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> received the award as the largest non-metropolitan district not already designated a city.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In April 1980 a parish council was created for Lichfield, and the charter trustees established six years earlier were dissolved. City status was temporarily lost until new letters patent were issued in November of the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1992, on the fortieth anniversary of the monarch's accession, it was announced that another town would be elevated to a city. An innovation on this occasion was that a competition was to be held, and communities would be required to submit applications. Sunderland was the successful applicant.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was followed in 1994 by the restoration of the dignity to <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s">St David's</a>, historic see of a bishop.<sup id="cite_ref-grd94_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grd94-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Since 2000, city status has been awarded to towns or local government districts by competition on special occasions. A large number of towns have applied for the honour in recent decades including <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gateshead" title="Gateshead">Gateshead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough" title="Middlesbrough">Middlesbrough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes" title="Milton Keynes">Milton Keynes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a>. Four successful applicants in England have become cities, as well as two in Wales; in 2000 for the Millennium Celebrations, the new cities were <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton and Hove</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>; in 2002 for the Queen's Golden Jubilee it was <a href="/wiki/City_of_Preston,_Lancashire" title="City of Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a>, and in 2012 for the Queen's Diamond Jubilee it was <a href="/wiki/Chelmsford" title="Chelmsford">Chelmsford</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Asaph" title="St Asaph">St Asaph</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-grd02_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grd02-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2012results_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2012results-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_London">Greater London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Greater London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other than the cities of <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a>, no local authorities in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> area have been granted city status. The Home Office had a policy of resisting any attempt by metropolitan boroughs to become cities even when their populations, and other proposed claims as qualifying criteria, might otherwise have made them eligible. It was felt that such a grant would undermine the status of the two existing cities in the capital. The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Southwark" title="Metropolitan Borough of Southwark">Metropolitan Borough of Southwark</a> made a number of applications, but in 1955 the borough's town clerk was told not to pursue the matter any further.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Outside the boundaries of the county, the <a href="/wiki/County_Borough_of_Croydon" title="County Borough of Croydon">County Borough of Croydon</a> made three applications, all of which were dismissed as it was not seen as being sufficiently separate from London. When the successor <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Croydon" title="London Borough of Croydon">London Borough of Croydon</a> applied in 1965 the Assistant Under Secretary of State summarised the case against Croydon: "...whatever its past history, it is now just part of the London conurbation and almost indistinguishable from many of the other Greater London boroughs".<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The same objections were made when the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Croydon" title="London Borough of Croydon">London Boroughs of Croydon</a> and <a href="/wiki/2014_Southwark_London_Borough_Council_election" title="2014 Southwark London Borough Council election">Southwark</a> unsuccessfully entered the competition for city status to mark the millennium: <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a> was said to have "no particular identity of its own" while <a href="/wiki/Southwark" title="Southwark">Southwark</a> was "part of London with little individual identity".<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> When the most recent competition was held to mark the Golden Jubilee of 2002, <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a> made a sixth application, again unsuccessful. It was joined by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Borough_of_Greenwich" title="Royal Borough of Greenwich">London Borough of Greenwich</a>, which emphasised its royal and maritime connections, while claiming to be "to London what <a href="/wiki/Versailles_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Versailles (city)">Versailles</a> is to Paris".<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In this vein <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_London_Borough_Council_elections" title="Greenwich London Borough Council elections">Greenwich</a> joined <a href="/wiki/Kingston-upon-Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingston-upon-Thames">Kingston-upon-Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Borough_of_Kensington_and_Chelsea" title="Royal Borough of Kensington and Chelsea">Kensington and Chelsea</a> in London in having the title of Royal Borough in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rochester">Rochester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rochester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a> was recognised as a city from 1211 to 1998. On 1 April 1974, the city council was abolished, becoming part of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Rochester-upon-Medway" title="City of Rochester-upon-Medway">Borough of Medway</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">local government district</a> in the county of <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>. However, under <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> the former city council area was to continue to be styled the "City of Rochester" to "perpetuate the ancient name" and to recall "the long history and proud heritage of the said city".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The city was unique, as it had no council or charter trustees and no mayor or civic head. In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Borough of Medway</a> was renamed as <a href="/wiki/Rochester-upon-Medway" class="mw-redirect" title="Rochester-upon-Medway">Rochester-upon-Medway</a>, and in 1982 further letters patent transferred the city status to the entire borough.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On 1 April 1998, the existing local government districts of Rochester-upon-Medway and <a href="/wiki/Gillingham,_Kent" title="Gillingham, Kent">Gillingham</a> were abolished and became the new <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a> of <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a>. Since it was the local government district that officially held city status under the 1982 letters patent, when it was abolished, it also ceased to be a city. Whilst the two other local government districts with city status (<a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a>) that were abolished around this time decided to appoint <a href="/wiki/Charter_trustees" title="Charter trustees">charter trustees</a> to maintain the existence of the city and the mayoralty, Rochester-upon-Medway City Council did not do so. Medway Council apparently only became aware of this when, in 2002, they discovered that <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a> was not on the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor%27s_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Chancellor&#39;s Office">Lord Chancellor's Office</a>'s list of cities.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, it started to refer to the "City of Medway" in promotional material, but it was rebuked and instructed not to do so in future by the <a href="/wiki/Advertising_Standards_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Advertising Standards Authority (United Kingdom)">Advertising Standards Authority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scotland had no cities by <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> or letters patent before 1889.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The nearest equivalent in pre-Union Scotland was the <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burgh</a>. The term <i>city</i> was not always consistently applied, and there were doubts over the number of officially designated cities. The royal burghs of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> anciently used the title <i>civitas</i>, but the term <i>city</i> does not seem to have been used before the 15th century. Unlike the situation in England, in Scotland there was no link between the presence of a cathedral and the title of <i>city</i>. <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> were accepted as cities by ancient usage by the 18th century, while <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elgin,_Moray" title="Elgin, Moray">Elgin</a> also used the title.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1856, the burgh of <a href="/wiki/Dunfermline" title="Dunfermline">Dunfermline</a> resolved to use the title of <i>city</i> in all official documents in the future, based on long usage and its former status as a royal capital. The status was never officially recognised.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1889, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> was granted city status by letters patent. The grant by formal document led to doubts about the use of the title <i>city</i> by other burghs. In 1891, the city status of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> was confirmed when the burgh was enlarged by local Act of Parliament. The Royal Burgh of <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> applied for promotion to a city as part of the Jubilee honours in 1897. The request was not granted, partly because it would draw attention to the lack of any charter granting the title to existing cities.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> were constituted "counties of cities" by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1929" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1929">Local Government (Scotland) Act 1929</a>. The Act made no statement on the title <i>city</i> for any other burgh. In 1969, the Home Secretary, <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>, stated that there were six cities in Scotland (without naming them) and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elgin,_Moray" title="Elgin, Moray">Elgin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> were the only burghs listed as cities in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1973" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973">Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973</a> completely reorganised Scotland's local administration in 1975. All burghs were abolished, and a system of districts created. The four districts of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> had <i>City</i> included in their titles by the Act. The 1975 districts were replaced with the present council areas by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_etc._(Scotland)_Act_1994" title="Local Government etc. (Scotland) Act 1994">Local Government etc. (Scotland) Act 1994</a> in 1996, and the same four cities were designated. Since the 1996 reorganisation, three more Scottish cities have been designated: Inverness as part of the millennium celebrations, <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> in 2002 to commemorate <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Golden Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II's Golden Jubilee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> in 2012 to mark the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Diamond Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Queen's Diamond Jubilee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2012results_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2012results-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In the case of these three cities, there are no city councils and no formal boundaries. In January 2008, a petition to matriculate <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">armorial bearings</a> for the City of Inverness was refused by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon_King_of_Arms" title="Lord Lyon King of Arms">Lord Lyon King of Arms</a> on the grounds that there is no corporate body or legal persona to whom arms can be granted.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland_and_Northern_Ireland">Ireland and Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ireland and Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_Ireland" title="City status in Ireland">City status in Ireland</a></div>
<p>City status in Ireland tended historically to be granted by royal charter. There are many towns in Ireland with <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> cathedrals that have never been called cities. In spite of this, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> was considered a city, by virtue of its being the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Primate_of_All_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Primate of All Ireland">Primate of All Ireland</a>, until the abolition of Armagh's city corporation by the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_(Ireland)_Act_1840" title="Municipal Corporations (Ireland) Act 1840">Municipal Corporations (Ireland) Act 1840</a>. The only historic city with a charter in present-day Northern Ireland is <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>. Derry was given its first charter by <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a> in 1604, but the garrison was <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Derry" title="Burning of Derry">attacked and destroyed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cahir_O%27Doherty" title="Cahir O&#39;Doherty">Cahir O'Doherty</a> in 1608.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The present city is the result of a second charter granted in 1613 to members of the London guilds, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a>, providing for the building of a walled city, which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Londonderry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1887, the Golden Jubilee of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> was celebrated, and the Borough of Belfast submitted a memorial to the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a> seeking city status. Belfast based its claim on its similarity to two English boroughs that had received the honour—the seaport of <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> and the textile centre of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>—and the fact that it had (at the time) a larger population than the City of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. Following some legal debate, city status was conferred in 1888. The grant of the honour on the grounds of being a large industrial town, rather than a diocesan centre, was unprecedented. <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast's</a> example was soon followed by <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> in England and <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh's</a> city status was restored.<sup id="cite_ref-grd94_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grd94-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 2002, <a href="/wiki/Lisburn" title="Lisburn">Lisburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">Newry</a> were two of the five towns in the UK that were granted city status by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> to mark her Golden Jubilee. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Lisburn" title="Lisburn">Lisburn</a>, the status extends to the entire local government district.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">Newry</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> in Scotland, has no formal boundaries or city council. The letters patent were presented to representatives of <a href="/wiki/Newry_and_Mourne_District_Council" title="Newry and Mourne District Council">Newry and Mourne District Council</a> on behalf of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_practice_of_granting_city_status">Current practice of granting city status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Current practice of granting city status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a Memorandum from the Home Office issued in 1927,
</p>
<blockquote><p>If a town wishes to obtain the title of a city the proper method of procedure is to address a petition to the King through the Home Office. It is the duty of the Home Secretary to submit such petitions to his Majesty and to advise his Majesty to the reply to be returned. It is a well-established principle that the grant of the title is only recommended in the case of towns of the first rank in population, size and importance, and having a distinctive character and identity of their own. At the present day, therefore, it is only rarely and in exceptional circumstances that the title is given.<sup id="cite_ref-1927mem_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1927mem-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In fact, a town can now apply for city status by submitting an application to the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, who makes recommendations to the sovereign. Competitions for new grants of city status have been held to mark special events, such as <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_British_monarch" title="Coronation of the British monarch">coronations</a>, royal jubilees or the <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">Millennium</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_mayors">Lord mayors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lord mayors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_lord_mayoralties_and_lord_provostships_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of lord mayoralties and lord provostships in the United Kingdom">List of lord mayoralties and lord provostships in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Some cities in England, Wales and Northern Ireland have the further distinction of having a <a href="/wiki/Lord_mayor" title="Lord mayor">lord mayor</a> rather than a simple mayor — in Scotland, the equivalent is the lord provost. Lord mayors have the right to be <a href="/wiki/Style_(manner_of_address)" title="Style (manner of address)">styled</a> "The Right Worshipful The Lord Mayor". The lord mayors and provosts of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> and <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> have the further right to be styled "<a href="/wiki/The_Right_Honourable" title="The Right Honourable">The Right Honourable</a> the Lord Mayor" (or Provost), although they are not members of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Most Honourable Privy Council">Privy Council</a> as this style usually indicates. The style is associated with the office, not the person holding it, so "The Right Worshipful Joseph Bloggsworthy" would be incorrect.
</p><p>There are currently 69 recognised cities (including 30 lord mayoralties or lord provostships) in the UK: 51 cities (23 lord mayoralties) in England, six cities (two lord mayoralties) in Wales, seven cities (four lord provostships) in Scotland and five cities (one lord mayoralty) in Northern Ireland.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, the ceremonial head of the city government of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_Dublin" title="Lord Mayor of Dublin">Lord Mayor of Dublin</a>. This title was granted by Charles II in 1665 when <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a>. Whilst the 1665 letters patent provided for the Lord Mayor to hold the formal title of Right Honourable, this was repealed in 2001. There is also a <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_Cork" title="Lord Mayor of Cork">Lord Mayor of Cork</a>, a title granted in 1900 when <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> was still part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>In modern practice, competitions are held for cities that wish to gain the distinction of a lord mayor. The 2002 competition was entered by <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Carlisle" title="City of Carlisle">Carlisle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chichester" title="Chichester">Chichester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Lancaster" title="City of Lancaster">Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln, Lincolnshire">Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_and_District_of_St_Albans" class="mw-redirect" title="City and District of St Albans">St Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s">St David's</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">Salford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Sunderland" title="City of Sunderland">Sunderland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>; the successful candidate was <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In 2012 a further competition was held, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Diamond Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Diamond Jubilee</a> celebrations, with <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> receiving the distinction.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Eleven other cities entered the contest, namely: <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salford,_Greater_Manchester" class="mw-redirect" title="Salford, Greater Manchester">Salford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Albans" title="St Albans">St Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government_districts">Local government districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Local government districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since local government reorganisation in 1974 city status has been awarded to a number of local government districts which are not themselves towns.  Each includes a number of towns and villages outside the urban area from which the district takes its name.  
In some of these cases city status was awarded to districts where the largest settlement had city status before 1974.  In other cases a borough was formed to govern an area covering several towns and then city status was granted to the borough.  The largest "city" district in terms of area is the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Carlisle" title="City of Carlisle">City of Carlisle</a>, which covers some 400 square miles (1,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of mostly rural landscape in the north of England, and is larger than smaller counties such as <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>. Such cities include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Bingley" title="Bingley">Bingley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilkley" title="Ilkley">Ilkley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shipley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Shipley, West Yorkshire">Shipley</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Canterbury" title="City of Canterbury">City of Canterbury</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Herne_Bay,_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="Herne Bay, Kent">Herne Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whitstable" title="Whitstable">Whitstable</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Lancaster" title="City of Lancaster">City of Lancaster</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Carnforth" title="Carnforth">Carnforth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heysham" title="Heysham">Heysham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morecambe" title="Morecambe">Morecambe</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Lancashire" title="Lancaster, Lancashire">Lancaster</a> and surrounding rural areas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Leeds" title="City of Leeds">City of Leeds</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Otley" title="Otley">Otley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pudsey" title="Pudsey">Pudsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wetherby" title="Wetherby">Wetherby</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Preston,_Lancashire" title="City of Preston, Lancashire">City of Preston</a>, including rural areas as well as <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_St_Albans" class="mw-redirect" title="City of St Albans">City of St Albans</a>, including the town of <a href="/wiki/Harpenden" title="Harpenden">Harpenden</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/St_Albans" title="St Albans">St Albans</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">City of Salford</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Eccles,_Greater_Manchester" title="Eccles, Greater Manchester">Eccles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pendlebury" title="Pendlebury">Pendlebury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swinton,_Greater_Manchester" title="Swinton, Greater Manchester">Swinton</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Salford,_Greater_Manchester" class="mw-redirect" title="Salford, Greater Manchester">Salford</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Sunderland" title="City of Sunderland">City of Sunderland</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton-le-Hole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houghton-le-Spring" title="Houghton-le-Spring">Houghton-le-Spring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Washington, Tyne and Wear">Washington</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/City_of_Winchester" title="City of Winchester">City of Winchester</a>, including the towns of <a href="/wiki/Bishop%27s_Waltham" title="Bishop&#39;s Waltham">Bishop's Waltham</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Alresford" title="New Alresford">New Alresford</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Winchester" title="Winchester">Winchester</a></li></ul>
<p>There are some cities where the local government district is in fact smaller than the historical or natural boundaries of the city. Examples include: <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, where the traditional area associated includes areas of the neighbouring authorities of <a href="/wiki/Trafford" title="Trafford">Trafford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tameside" title="Tameside">Tameside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldham" title="Oldham">Oldham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Bury" title="Metropolitan Borough of Bury">Bury</a> and the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">City of Salford</a>; <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a>, where surrounding areas and villages that are effectively suburbs, such as <a href="/wiki/Cottingham,_East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="Cottingham, East Riding of Yorkshire">Cottingham</a>, which comes under <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire Council</a>; <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, where suburban areas of the city are located in <a href="/wiki/East_Dunbartonshire" title="East Dunbartonshire">East Dunbartonshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Renfrewshire" title="East Renfrewshire">East Renfrewshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Lanarkshire" title="North Lanarkshire">North Lanarkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renfrewshire" title="Renfrewshire">Renfrewshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Lanarkshire" title="South Lanarkshire">South Lanarkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Dunbartonshire" title="West Dunbartonshire">West Dunbartonshire</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a> and London.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_councils">City councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: City councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of cities in the United Kingdom">List of cities in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>The holding of city status gives a settlement no special rights other than that of calling itself a "city". Nonetheless, this appellation carries its own prestige and, consequently, competitions for the status are hard-fought.
</p><p>Historically, city status could only be granted to <a href="/wiki/Municipal_corporation" title="Municipal corporation">incorporated towns</a>. The grant was specifically awarded to the relevant local government area such as a civil parish or borough. However, recent grants have used a looser wording, where the status is awarded to the "town". In most cases the "town" is held to be coterminous with the relevant local government area, such that the city status holder is the corporate body of the council. Examples include the Letters Patent awarded to the "Towns of <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton and Hove</a>", the "Town of <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>" and the "Town of <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a> in the <a href="/wiki/County_Borough_of_Newport" class="mw-redirect" title="County Borough of Newport">County Borough of Newport</a>". In each case the existing borough council became the city council.
</p><p>Most cities have city councils, which have varying powers depending on the country and type of settlement.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Twelve of the 51 cities in England are in <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan counties">metropolitan counties</a> and their city councils are single-tier <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_district_councils" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan district councils">metropolitan district councils</a>.  Outside the metropolitan counties eleven cities are <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authorities</a>, and fifteen have ordinary <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">district councils</a>, which are subordinate to their local <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county council</a>. In London the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_City_Council" title="Westminster City Council">Westminster City Council</a> functions as a <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London borough</a> council, and the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a> is the council for the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>.  Nine smaller episcopal cities such as <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wells,_Somerset" title="Wells, Somerset">Wells</a> are neither local government districts nor within a 'local government district with city status', and have city councils which are <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a>, with limited powers.  Two cities (<a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a>) have no city council.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland_2">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> are themselves <a href="/wiki/Council_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Council areas">council areas</a> and have their own city councils. The cities of <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> are part of council areas which do not have city status, and have no city councils. <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Stirling Council">Stirling Council</a>'s application for city status was specifically for the urban area of the (now former) <a href="/wiki/Royal_Burgh_of_Stirling" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Burgh of Stirling">Royal Burgh of Stirling</a> and included proposed city boundaries which are much smaller than <a href="/wiki/Stirling_(council_area)" title="Stirling (council area)">Stirling council area</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a> are <a href="/wiki/Principal_areas_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Principal areas of Wales">principal areas</a> and have city councils.  The city councils of <a href="/wiki/Bangor,_Gwynedd" title="Bangor, Gwynedd">Bangor</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_Asaph" title="St Asaph">St Asaph</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Davids" title="St Davids">St Davids</a> are <a href="/wiki/Community_councils" class="mw-redirect" title="Community councils">community councils</a> with limited powers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Belfast_City_Council" title="Belfast City Council">Belfast City Council</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Local government in Northern Ireland">local government district council</a>.  Since the <a href="/wiki/Reform_of_local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Reform of local government in Northern Ireland">local government reforms of 2015</a> the four other cities form parts of wider districts and do not have their own councils.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_status_conferment">City status conferment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: City status conferment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>City status is conferred by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> and not by a royal charter (except historically in Ireland). There are twenty towns in England and Wales that were recognised as cities by "ancient prescriptive right"; none of these communities had been formally declared a city, but they had all used the title since "<a href="/wiki/Time_immemorial" title="Time immemorial">time immemorial</a>", that is, before 3 September 1189, and had been granted some form of privilege such as freedoms by way of a charter or being given borough or corporation status.<sup id="cite_ref-beckett_6-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beckett-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A list of these ancient cities was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> in 1927<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> and were:
</p>

<p>These were concurrent with 22 <a href="/wiki/Historical_development_of_Church_of_England_dioceses#Ancient_cathedrals" title="Historical development of Church of England dioceses">ancient diocese</a> (pre-<a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>) locations.<sup id="cite_ref-asaph_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asaph-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Considering that <a href="/wiki/Bath_and_Wells" class="mw-redirect" title="Bath and Wells">Bath and Wells</a> together is one diocese, the remaining three others are in Wales:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Llandaff" title="Llandaff">Llandaff</a> — had not been incorporated as a borough or granted privileges so was not deemed a city, and was merged into the existing city of Cardiff in 1922;<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>St Asaph – was never considered to be a city due to a lack of honours or charters, however it was later awarded the status in 2012;<sup id="cite_ref-asaph_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asaph-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>St Davids – had been a borough, yet <a href="/wiki/St_Davids#Governance" title="St Davids">lost the status in 1886</a> (so did not appear in the above list) but the honour was restored in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-Beckett_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beckett-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The holding of city status brings no special benefits other than the right to be called a city. All cities where a local government unit that holds that status is abolished have to be re-issued with letters patent reconfirming city status following local government reorganisation where that holder has been abolished. This process was followed by a number of cities since 1974, and <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a>'s status was confirmed twice, in 1974 and again in the 1990s. Failure to do so leads to the loss of city status as happened at <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a> in 1998 (<i>see <a href="#Former_City_of_Rochester">above</a></i>), and also previously in <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s">St David's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>, although both of these latter have regained city status since losing it. These three had been cities since time immemorial before the loss of city status.
</p><p>The formal definition of a city has been disputed,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in particular by inhabitants of towns that have been regarded as cities in the past such as <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a>, which was considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2019)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> a city from Roman times until 1542,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Dunfermline" title="Dunfermline">Dunfermline</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> although neither are legally cities today. Additionally, although the Crown clearly has the right to bestow "official" city status, some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2019)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> have doubted the right of the Crown to define the word <i>city</i> in the United Kingdom.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In informal usage, <i>city</i> can be used for large towns or conurbations that are not formally cities. The best-known example of this is London, which contains two cities (the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>) but is not itself a city.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Officially_designated_cities">Officially designated cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Officially designated cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are currently 69 officially designated cities in the UK, of which 11 have been created since 2000 in competitions to celebrate <a href="/wiki/3rd_millennium" title="3rd millennium">the new millennium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Queen Elizabeth II</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Golden Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Golden Jubilee</a> in 2002 and <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Diamond Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Diamond Jubilee</a> in 2012. The designation is highly sought after, with over 40 communities submitting bids at recent competitions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smallest_and_largest_cities">Smallest and largest cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Smallest and largest cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whilst cities are regularly ranked by the number of residents, this is not an ideal measure. <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">Population</a> can, of course vary based on the number and type of residences present, and has to be limited to an area. As <a href="#City_councils">mentioned earlier</a> in the article, the official area of a city in the UK is typically the coverage up to a local government/council boundary,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> there being a variety of council bodies. However, there are exceptions depending on the UK constituent country<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and whether the council has <a href="#City_status_conferment">since been abolished</a>.
</p><p>Such a boundary can typically contain a built up (urban) area, and a surrounding, less populated rural landscape. Inversely, the area of a city can be entirely built up, with that urban environment spilling over a boundary into another area which does not have city status. That wider urban area can still be considered in everyday parlance locally as a whole 'city' although it is not a formal designation.
</p><p>With the 'square mile' City of London being in the middle of a huge urban area, it can be suggested that 'small' should be applied to only cities with minimal urban areas that have nearby surrounding areas of countryside and so is visibly representative of the term, Wells being more suitable in this regard as the smallest standalone city council area. Therefore sizing can be interpreted in a number of ways, and below are top 5 lists of the smallest and largest cities ranked by population, city council area, and urban area.
</p><p><small><i>All statistics are 2011 census figures.</i><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><br />
<i>Population is of the total residents in the city council area. Armagh (no local council) and the Largest Urban Area table both use urban population figures.</i></small>
</p>

<p>As noted above, in ordinary discourse, <i>city</i> can refer to any large settlement, with no fixed limit.
</p><p>There are certain towns with large urban areas that could qualify for city status on the grounds of population size. Some have applied for city status and had the application turned down. <a href="/wiki/Northampton" title="Northampton">Northampton</a> is one of the most populous urban districts not to be a London Borough, metropolitan borough, unitary authority or city; on this basis, the council claims that it is the largest town in England.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cathedral_towns">Cathedral towns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Cathedral towns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p><p>In total there are 14 English and Welsh cities that have never had <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglican</a> cathedrals within their borders – <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton and Hove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Lancaster" title="City of Lancaster">Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Preston,_Lancashire" title="City of Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">Salford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Sunderland" title="City of Sunderland">Sunderland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bath_Abbey" title="Bath Abbey">Bath Abbey</a> was once a diocesan cathedral, as was <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> briefly during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>. These cities retained their city status despite their cathedrals losing that status.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland_4">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_church" title="National church">national church</a> of Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">presbyterian</a> in governance (not recognising authority of bishops), and thus has <a href="/wiki/High_kirks" class="mw-redirect" title="High kirks">high kirks</a> rather than cathedrals. However, the pre-<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Reformation</a> dioceses do have extant cathedrals, most notably at <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, which remain in use by the Church of Scotland and continue to bear the honorific title of cathedral. Others (such as <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Cathedral" title="St Andrews Cathedral">that of St Andrews</a>) are now in ruins.
</p><p>As noted above, both <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elgin,_Moray" title="Elgin, Moray">Elgin</a> were recognised as cities before 1975 (Perth's city status was restored in 2012). Additionally, five other pre-Reformation sees—<a href="/wiki/Brechin" title="Brechin">Brechin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunblane" title="Dunblane">Dunblane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunkeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunkeld">Dunkeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirkwall" title="Kirkwall">Kirkwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews" title="St Andrews">St Andrews</a>—are often referred to as cities, notably in names associated with the settlements (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Brechin_City_F.C." title="Brechin City F.C.">Brechin City F.C.</a>, City Road in <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews" title="St Andrews">St Andrews</a>). <a href="/wiki/Dornoch" title="Dornoch">Dornoch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fortrose" title="Fortrose">Fortrose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whithorn" title="Whithorn">Whithorn</a> also possess pre-Reformation cathedrals but have never been described as cities.
</p><p>Towns with non-Church of Scotland, post-Reformation cathedrals which are not recognised as cities are <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Catholic Church">R.C.</a>), <a href="/wiki/Millport,_Cumbrae" title="Millport, Cumbrae">Millport</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Episcopal</a>), <a href="/wiki/Oban" title="Oban">Oban</a> (R.C.), <a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">Motherwell</a> (R.C.) and <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a> (R.C.). Of these, <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">Motherwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a> have larger populations than <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a>, and both <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a> have formally made a bid for city status in the recent past.
</p><p>Of settlements granted city status in the 21st century, <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> (awarded 2001) possesses an Episcopal cathedral (1866), but none under the auspices of the Church of Scotland. <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> (awarded 2002) has never had a cathedral of any kind. <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> (reinstated 2012) has an Episcopal cathedral dating from 1860, but no pre-Reformation establishment.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland_3">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Northern Ireland, as noted above, possession of a diocesan cathedral has never (except in the anomalous case of <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>) been sufficient to attain this status.
</p><p>In spite of this, the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition">1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i></a> refers to <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> (<a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> had lost city status in 1840) and <a href="/wiki/Lisburn" title="Lisburn">Lisburn</a> as cities. <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> subsequently regained city status formally in 1994 and <a href="/wiki/Lisburn" title="Lisburn">Lisburn</a> achieved city status in 2002.
</p><p>There are four towns in Northern Ireland with Church of Ireland cathedrals that do not have city status—<a href="/wiki/Clogher" title="Clogher">Clogher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downpatrick" title="Downpatrick">Downpatrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dromore,_County_Down" title="Dromore, County Down">Dromore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enniskillen" title="Enniskillen">Enniskillen</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Newry" title="Newry">Newry</a> is the only city in Northern Ireland that does not have a Church of Ireland cathedral within its borders.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Claimants">Claimants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Claimants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of towns describe themselves as cities (at least in some contexts), despite not having the requisite Charter.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ballymena" title="Ballymena">Ballymena</a> in Northern Ireland has been known informally as "The City of the Seven Towers" since the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The community council for <a href="/wiki/Brechin" title="Brechin">Brechin</a> is called City of Brechin &amp; District Community Council. The local football team is known as <a href="/wiki/Brechin_City_F.C." title="Brechin City F.C.">Brechin City F.C.</a> (they were formed at a meeting on <i>City Road</i> in the town). <a href="/wiki/Brechin" title="Brechin">Brechin</a> also possesses a cathedral, and was the ancient seat of the see of <a href="/wiki/Brechin" title="Brechin">Brechin</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dunfermline" title="Dunfermline">Dunfermline</a> styles itself "A Twinned City" on the signs welcoming visitors to the town.<sup id="cite_ref-municipal_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-municipal-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Area_committee" title="Area committee">area committee</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a> council is called City of Dunfermline Area Committee. It also has "city centre" rather than "town centre" written on its road signs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dunkeld" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunkeld">Dunkeld</a>, the see of a bishop until the seventeenth century is sometimes referred to as a city. A "City Hall" was built in 1877, since converted into a holiday accommodation.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The community council for <a href="/wiki/Elgin,_Moray" title="Elgin, Moray">Elgin</a> is called City and Royal Burgh of Elgin Community Council. The local football team is known as <a href="/wiki/Elgin_City_F.C." title="Elgin City F.C.">Elgin City F.C.</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guildford" title="Guildford">Guildford</a> possesses a cathedral and the local football team is named <a href="/wiki/Guildford_City_F.C." title="Guildford City F.C.">Guildford City F.C.</a>. In 2013 the local council did not submit an application, citing low chance of success, and high time and resource against low benefits ratio.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Letchworth_Garden_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Letchworth Garden City">Letchworth Garden City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welwyn_Garden_City" title="Welwyn Garden City">Welwyn Garden City</a> are medium-sized <a href="/wiki/New_towns_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="New towns in the United Kingdom">New Towns</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> established to reduce the overcrowding of London as part of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_city_movement" title="Garden city movement">Garden city movement</a>.</li>
<li>In its planning, the government of the day intended <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes" title="Milton Keynes">Milton Keynes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, to be a "new city" in scale, and it was referred to as such in contemporary supporting papers, but was <a href="/wiki/London_Gazette" class="mw-redirect" title="London Gazette">gazetted</a> in 1967 as a New Town. It has used the term "City Centre" on its buses and road signs for many years, mainly to avoid confusion with the centres of its pre-existing constituent towns.</li>
<li>After its unsuccessful attempts to gain city status, the town of <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading, Berkshire</a>, started using the phrase "City Centre" on its buses<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and car-park signs. <a href="/wiki/Reading/Wokingham_Urban_Area" title="Reading/Wokingham Urban Area">Reading's urban area</a> has in excess of 350,000 inhabitants, making it one of the largest urban areas in the UK and larger than many sizeable cities including <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>. However, the population of the Borough of Reading was estimated as 142,800 in 2006 by the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>, as a number of the town's large eastern and southern suburbs (such as <a href="/wiki/Earley" title="Earley">Earley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodley,_Berkshire" title="Woodley, Berkshire">Woodley</a>) lie within neighbouring local authorities.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> lays claim to being the first city founded in Britain under the Romans in AD43. The city of <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a> was one of three destroyed in AD61 by Queen <a href="/wiki/Boudica" title="Boudica">Boudica</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Iceni" title="Iceni">Iceni</a> during her revolt against the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. Despite making several attempts to become the first city in the county, it was eventually beaten by the county town <a href="/wiki/Chelmsford" title="Chelmsford">Chelmsford</a> in 2012. Today, <a href="/wiki/Colchester" title="Colchester">Colchester</a> is one of the fastest-growing boroughs in the country and the largest non-metropolitan district in the county of <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> with an estimated 187,000 people.</li></ul>
