<p><b>Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties</b> are one of the four levels of <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_England" title="Subdivisions of England">subdivisions of England</a> used for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>. As originally constituted, the metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties each consisted of multiple <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">districts</a>, had a <a href="/wiki/County_council#England" title="County council">county council</a> and were also the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">counties for the purposes of Lieutenancies</a>. Later changes in legislation during the 1980s and 1990s have allowed counties without county councils and 'unitary authority' counties of a single district. Counties for the purposes of Lieutenancies are now <a href="/wiki/Lieutenancies_Act_1997" title="Lieutenancies Act 1997">defined separately</a>, based on the metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties.
</p><p>In 2009 and 2019, there were <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England#2009_structural_changes" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">further structural changes in some areas</a>, resulting in a total of 82 metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties. These 82 counties collectively consist of 283 districts or district-level subdivisions, i.e. 36 metropolitan boroughs and 247 non-metropolitan districts (192 of these are subdivisions of non-metropolitan counties with county councils; 6 are subdivisions (and also unitary authorities, but without non-metropolitan county status) of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, which is a non-metropolitan county with no county council; and the remaining 49 are unitary authorities that have non-metropolitan county status).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_counties">Metropolitan counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metropolitan counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">Metropolitan county</a></div>
<p>The metropolitan counties are <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>. The counties typically have populations of 1.2 to 2.8 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones,_B._2004_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones,_B._2004-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The county councils of these were abolished in 1986, but the counties themselves still exist legally.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They are used for some administrative and geographic purposes, and are still <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial counties</a>. Most of the powers that the former county councils had were devolved to their <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a>, which are now in effect <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitary authorities">unitary authorities</a>; however, some functions (such as emergency services, civil defence and public transport) are still run jointly on a metropolitan-county-wide basis.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-metropolitan_counties">Non-metropolitan counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-metropolitan counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">Non-metropolitan county</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shire_counties">Shire counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shire counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <i>shire county</i> is a non-metropolitan county that has multiple <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">districts</a>. Its name does not need to have <i><a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire</a></i> in it. The term <i>shire county</i> is unofficial.
</p><p>There are 28 such counties:
</p><p>Berkshire, Buckinghamshire, Cambridgeshire, Cumbria, Derbyshire, Devon, Dorset, East Sussex, Essex, Gloucestershire, Hampshire, Hertfordshire, Kent, Lancashire, Leicestershire, Lincolnshire, Norfolk, North Yorkshire, Northamptonshire, Nottinghamshire, Oxfordshire, Somerset, Staffordshire, Suffolk, Surrey, Warwickshire, West Sussex, Worcestershire.
</p><p>All, apart from Berkshire, have <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a>. Sometimes <i>shire county</i> is used to exclude Berkshire, because it has no county council. The counties have populations of 109,000 to 1.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones,_B._2004_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones,_B._2004-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England#2009_structural_changes" title="Metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties of England">local government reforms coming into effect in 2009</a>, the number of such counties was reduced. The non-metropolitan counties of Bedfordshire and Cheshire were split into two separate non-metropolitan counties respectively, while Cornwall, County Durham, Northumberland, Shropshire and Wiltshire became unitary authorities each of a single district.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unitary_authorities">Unitary authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unitary authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">Unitary authorities of England</a></div>
<p>Unitary authorities are areas with only one council, and there are 55 in total.
</p><p>49 are coterminous with a non-metropolitan county, 43 of which are defined as counties with a single district council and no county counci: <i>Bath and North East Somerset, Bedford, Blackburn with Darwen, Blackpool, Bournemouth, Brighton and Hove, Bristol, Central Bedfordshire, Cheshire East, Cheshire West and Chester, Darlington, Derby, East Riding of Yorkshire, Halton, Hartlepool, Herefordshire, Kingston upon Hull, Leicester, Luton, Medway, Middlesbrough, Borough of Milton Keynes, North East Lincolnshire, North Lincolnshire, North Somerset, Nottingham, Peterborough, Plymouth, Poole, Portsmouth, Redcar and Cleveland, Rutland, South Gloucestershire, Southampton, Southend-on-Sea, Stockton-on-Tees, Stoke-on-Trent, Swindon, Telford and Wrekin, Thurrock, Torbay, Warrington, York</i>. The other 6 are technically counties with a county council and no district councils, but the effect is the same: <i>Isle of Wight, Cornwall, Durham, Northumberland, Shropshire and Wiltshire</i>
</p><p>The remaining 6 unitary authorities (<i>West Berkshire, Reading, Wokingham, Bracknell Forest, Windsor and Maidenhead, Slough</i>) are districts of Berkshire, however they are not non-metropolitan counties, as the non-metropolitan county of Berkshire still exists albeit without a county council; this is a unique situation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions_in_England">Exceptions in England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exceptions in England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Local Government Act 1972 created the system of metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties and districts, but specifically excluded two parts of England from the new system, a situation which exists to the present.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="London">London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> was created in 1965 by the <a href="/wiki/London_Government_Act_1963" title="London Government Act 1963">London Government Act 1963</a> as a <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a></i> administrative area,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> functioning as an upper-tier local government. It consists of 33 local authority districts and spans the area which was prior made up of the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a>, most of <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, and parts of other neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrative counties">administrative counties</a>. In 1972, no metropolitan or non-metropolitan counties or districts were created in this area. However, the council was abolished along with the metropolitan county councils in 1986.
</p><p>In 1994, Greater London was designated as one of nine <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">regions of England</a>, which each had a government office up until they were abolished 2011. Since 2000, Greater London has had an elected <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">Assembly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor</a> responsible for strategic local government. In the other eight regions, plans for elected assemblies were abandoned (although they had <a href="/wiki/Regional_assembly_(England)" title="Regional assembly (England)">regional chambers</a> with limited functions between 1999 and 2010), leaving London as the only region with a conterminous authority.
</p><p>The area does however include two counties for the purposes of lieutenancies: the county of Greater London (which covers the 32 <a href="/wiki/London_boroughs" title="London boroughs">London boroughs</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isles_of_Scilly">Isles of Scilly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Isles of Scilly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> are, like Greater London, not covered by the system of metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties. The Council of the Isles of Scilly remains a district council (constituted in 1890, by way of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>) with county council powers (granted by the Isles of Scilly Order 1930) and is therefore a <i>sui generis</i> unitary authority. Some functions, such as health and economic development, are shared with Cornwall Council, and the islands form part of the ceremonial county of Cornwall.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current system of metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties came into effect on 1 April 1974 and replaced the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative counties</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a>, which were abolished at that time. Greater London was created in 1965 under separate legislation.
</p><p>In the 1990s, a new type of non-metropolitan county was created: the unitary authority, which combines the functions and powers of county and district. The existing non-metropolitan counties became known as shire counties to distinguish them from the unitary authorities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_Government_Act_1972">Local Government Act 1972</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Local Government Act 1972">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a></div>
<p>By the late 1960s, it had become obvious that the structure of local government in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> needed reforming.  <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> government set up the <a href="/wiki/Redcliffe-Maud_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Redcliffe-Maud Commission">Redcliffe-Maud Commission</a> to produce proposals for wholesale reform.
</p><p>The report proposed that for most of England the two-tier structure be abolished, and replaced with a system of 58 <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>, which would generally ignore the previous administrative boundaries in favour of changes that made geographic sense - a total redrawing of the map. In the South of <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, North East of <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">Birmingham area</a>, there would be 3 metropolitan areas, with 20 district authorities.
</p><p>These proposals were opposed by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> opposition led by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a>. They won the <a href="/wiki/1970_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1970 United Kingdom general election">1970 general election</a>, and set to work defining their own scheme. This scrapped the concept of unitary authorities (even for existing <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a>) – the entire area of England and Wales was to be divided into uniform counties and districts. In England the new divisions were to be largely modelled on the existing counties with quite radical reforms put forward, even in some non-metropolitan areas.
</p><p>Despite reassurances from the government that nobody's loyalties were expected to change as a result of the local government reform, many changes did incur significant local opposition. Most of the radical changes were withdrawn. One aspect the government stood firm on was the mergers of small counties. Campaigns for the continuation of <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> were unsuccessful, although due to its special geographic circumstances, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> was permitted to retain a separate county council, as opposed to being reunified with its historic county of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>.
</p><p>The Local Government Act was passed in 1972, and defined the English counties and metropolitan districts, but not the non-metropolitan districts. These were set by a Boundary Commission that had already begun work.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The metropolitan counties were composed as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a> - including <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, south-west Lancashire, along with the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Wirral_Peninsula" title="Wirral Peninsula">Wirral Peninsula</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> - the <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> urban area along with many surrounding towns</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a> - based upon the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>-<a href="/wiki/Rotherham" title="Rotherham">Rotherham</a> area in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a> - the <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a> conurbation based on <a href="/wiki/Newcastle-upon-Tyne" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle-upon-Tyne">Newcastle-upon-Tyne</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> - <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> conurbation, including the <a href="/wiki/Black_Country" title="Black Country">Black Country</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a> - <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> area in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a></li></ul>
<p>Other significant changes were:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/County_of_Avon" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Avon">Avon</a> formed from northern <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_England" title="Cleveland, England">Cleveland</a> formed from southern <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a> and northern part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="North Riding of Yorkshire">North Riding</a>, focusing on the <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a> conurbation along with <a href="/wiki/Guisborough" title="Guisborough">Guisborough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hartlepool" title="Hartlepool">Hartlepool</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> was formed from <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumberland, England">Cumberland</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> were merged into <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a> formed from <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">eastern Yorkshire</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a> was annexed by <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> was merged into <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">district</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vale_of_White_Horse" title="Vale of White Horse">Vale of White Horse</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>'s former county town <a href="/wiki/Abingdon,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Abingdon, England">Abingdon</a>, was ceded to <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, as was the area around <a href="/wiki/Wallingford,_Oxfordshire" title="Wallingford, Oxfordshire">Wallingford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Didcot" title="Didcot">Didcot</a> now comprising the western half of the <a href="/wiki/South_Oxfordshire" title="South Oxfordshire">South Oxfordshire</a> District</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a> was moved from <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, to join its sister town of <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a></li></ul>
<p>The changes were adopted by the Royal Mail <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">for the purposes of postal addresses</a> wherever they were able, with the notable exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>. Humberside was divided for this purpose into <a href="/wiki/North_Humberside" title="North Humberside">North Humberside</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Humberside" title="South Humberside">South Humberside</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abolition_of_metropolitan_county_councils">Abolition of metropolitan county councils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Abolition of metropolitan county councils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1985" title="Local Government Act 1985">Local Government Act 1985</a></div>
<p>In 1986, the county councils of the metropolitan counties and the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> were abolished by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>'s government following disputes with central government, but the counties themselves remained legally in existence.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_Government_Act_1992">Local Government Act 1992</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metropolitan_and_non-metropolitan_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Local Government Act 1992">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The 1990s led to the restoration of <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> under a new name, <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authorities</a>, which radically changed the administrative map of England. The changes were carried out in several waves.
</p><p>On 1 April 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> became a single unitary authority.  It had previously had a two-tier structure with an Isle of Wight County Council, <a href="/wiki/Medina,_Isle_of_Wight" title="Medina, Isle of Wight">Medina</a> Borough Council and <a href="/wiki/South_Wight" title="South Wight">South Wight</a> Borough Council.  Also on this day, two small areas were ceded from <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, giving it a border with <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>.
</p><p>On 1 April 1996, the unpopular<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="why unpopular (March 2019)">why?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> counties of <a href="/wiki/Avon_(county)" title="Avon (county)">Avon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_England" title="Cleveland, England">Cleveland</a> were abolished and their former area divided into unitary districts.  Also at this time, the city of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> was expanded and separated from <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a>.
</p><p>On 1 April 1997, the districts of <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darlington_(borough)" class="mw-redirect" title="Darlington (borough)">Darlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes_(borough)" class="mw-redirect" title="Milton Keynes (borough)">Milton Keynes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Swindon" title="Borough of Swindon">Swindon</a> (based on the former Thamesdown district) became unitary authorities.  Also, the districts of <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hove" title="Hove">Hove</a> were merged to form the new unitary authority of <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton and Hove</a>.
</p><p>On 1 April 1998, <a href="/wiki/Blackburn_with_Darwen" title="Blackburn with Darwen">Blackburn with Darwen</a> (based on the former Blackburn district), <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halton_(borough)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halton (borough)">Halton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southend-on-Sea" title="Southend-on-Sea">Southend-on-Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telford_and_Wrekin" title="Telford and Wrekin">Telford and Wrekin</a> (based on the former Wrekin district), <a href="/wiki/Torbay" title="Torbay">Torbay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thurrock" title="Thurrock">Thurrock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a> became unitary authorities. Also, the districts of <a href="/wiki/Rochester-upon-Medway" class="mw-redirect" title="Rochester-upon-Medway">Rochester-upon-Medway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gillingham,_Kent" title="Gillingham, Kent">Gillingham</a> were merged to form the new unitary authority of <a href="/wiki/Medway" title="Medway">Medway</a>, and the county of <a href="/wiki/Hereford_and_Worcester" title="Hereford and Worcester">Hereford and Worcester</a> was abolished and replaced by the unitary authority of <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and the shire county of <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>. <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> was split into six unitary authorities, but not formally abolished.
</p>
