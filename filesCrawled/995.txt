<p><b>Sir Maurice Vincent Wilkes</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">FREng</a></span><sup id="cite_ref-frs_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frs-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> (26 June 1913&#160;– 29 November 2010)<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> was a British <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> who designed and helped build the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic delay storage automatic calculator">Electronic delay storage automatic calculator</a> (EDSAC), one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Stored_program_computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Stored program computer">stored program computers</a> and invented <a href="/wiki/Microprogramming" class="mw-redirect" title="Microprogramming">microprogramming</a>, a method for using stored-program logic to operate the control unit of a <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">central processing unit</a>'s circuits. At the time of his death, Wilkes was an Emeritus Professor of the University of Cambridge.

<h2><span id="Early_life.2C_education.2C_and_military_service"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life,_education,_and_military_service">Early life, education, and military service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education, and military service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wilkes was born in <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a>, Worcestershire, England<sup id="cite_ref-cv_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Stourbridge" title="Stourbridge">Stourbridge</a>, West Midlands, England, where his father worked on the estate of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Dudley" title="Earl of Dudley">Earl of Dudley</a>. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VI_College,_Stourbridge" title="King Edward VI College, Stourbridge">King Edward VI College, Stourbridge</a> and during his school years he was introduced to amateur radio by his chemistry teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Telegraph_Obituary_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Telegraph_Obituary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg/220px-DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg/330px-DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg/440px-DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1219" data-file-height="892" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DA_Cambridge_c1937.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maurice Wilkes (right) with the <a href="/wiki/Meccano" title="Meccano">Meccano</a> <a href="/wiki/Differential_analyser" title="Differential analyser">differential analyser</a> in the Cambridge University Mathematics Laboratory, c1937. A.F. Devonshire (left) co-authored a number of papers on <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Order_and_disorder_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Order and disorder (physics)">disorder</a> with the Laboratory's first director, <a href="/wiki/John_Lennard-Jones" title="John Lennard-Jones">John Lennard-Jones</a>. The winner of the 1937 <a href="/wiki/Mayhew_Prize" title="Mayhew Prize">Mayhew Prize</a>, J. Corner, is operating the input table (centre).</div></div></div>
<p>He went on to read the <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Tripos" title="Mathematical Tripos">Mathematical Tripos</a> at <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St John&#39;s College, Cambridge">St John's College, Cambridge</a> from 1931–34, continuing to complete a PhD in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> on the topic of radio propagation of very long radio waves in the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> in 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-cl.cam_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cl.cam-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He was appointed to a junior faculty position of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> through which he was involved in the establishment of a computing laboratory. He was called up for military service during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and worked on <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Research_Establishment" title="Telecommunications Research Establishment">Telecommunications Research Establishment</a> (TRE), and in <a href="/wiki/Operational_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Operational research">operational research</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_career">Research and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiation_into_electronic_computing">Initiation into electronic computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initiation into electronic computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1945, Wilkes was appointed as the second director of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Mathematical_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory">University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory</a> (later known as the Computer Laboratory).<sup id="cite_ref-cv_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Cambridge laboratory initially had many different computing devices, including a <a href="/wiki/Differential_analyser" title="Differential analyser">differential analyser</a>. One day <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Comrie" title="Leslie Comrie">Leslie Comrie</a> visited Wilkes and lent him a copy of <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>'s <a href="/wiki/First_Draft_of_a_Report_on_the_EDVAC" title="First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC">prepress description</a> of the <a href="/wiki/EDVAC" title="EDVAC">EDVAC</a>, a successor to the <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a><sup id="cite_ref-eniac_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eniac-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> under construction by <a href="/wiki/J._Presper_Eckert" title="J. Presper Eckert">Presper Eckert</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Mauchly" title="John Mauchly">John Mauchly</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_of_Electrical_Engineering" title="Moore School of Electrical Engineering">Moore School of Electrical Engineering</a>. He had to read it overnight because he had to return it and no photocopying facilities existed. He decided immediately that the document described the logical design of future computing machines, and that he wanted to be involved in the design and construction of such machines. In August 1946 Wilkes travelled by ship to the United States to enroll in the <a href="/wiki/Moore_School_Lectures" title="Moore School Lectures">Moore School Lectures</a>, of which he was only able to attend the final two weeks because of various travel delays.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During the five-day return voyage to England, Wilkes sketched out in some detail the logical structure of the machine which would become EDSAC.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EDSAC">EDSAC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EDSAC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(9).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/EDSAC_%289%29.jpg/170px-EDSAC_%289%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/EDSAC_%289%29.jpg/255px-EDSAC_%289%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/EDSAC_%289%29.jpg/340px-EDSAC_%289%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1170" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EDSAC_(9).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maurice Wilkes inspecting the mercury <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">delay line</a> of the EDSAC in construction</div></div></div>
<p>Since his laboratory had its own funding, he was immediately able to start work on a small practical machine, the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Delay_Storage_Automatic_Calculator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic Delay Storage Automatic Calculator">Electronic Delay Storage Automatic Calculator</a> (EDSAC),<sup id="cite_ref-edsac_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edsac-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> once back at Cambridge. He decided that his mandate was not to invent a better computer, but simply to make one available to the university. Therefore, his approach was relentlessly practical. He used only proven methods for constructing each part of the computer. The resulting computer was slower and smaller than other planned contemporary computers. However, his laboratory's computer was the second practical <a href="/wiki/Stored_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Stored program">stored program</a> computer to be completed, and operated successfully from May 1949, well over a year before the much larger and more complex EDVAC. In 1950, along with David Wheeler, Wilkes used EDSAC to solve a <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equation</a> relating to <a href="/wiki/Allele_frequency" title="Allele frequency">gene frequencies</a> in a paper by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This represents the first use of a computer for a problem in the field of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_computing_developments">Other computing developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other computing developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1951, he developed the concept of <a href="/wiki/Microcode" title="Microcode">microprogramming</a><sup id="cite_ref-microprogram_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microprogram-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> from the realisation that the <a href="/wiki/Central_Processing_Unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Processing Unit">central processing unit</a> of a computer could be controlled by a miniature, highly specialised computer program in high-speed <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a>. This concept greatly simplified CPU development. Microprogramming was first described at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> Computer Inaugural Conference in 1951,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> then published in expanded form in <a href="/wiki/IEEE_Spectrum" title="IEEE Spectrum">IEEE Spectrum</a> in 1955.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This concept was implemented for the first time in <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-edsac2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edsac2-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which also used multiple identical "bit slices" to simplify design. Interchangeable, replaceable tube assemblies were used for each bit of the processor. The next computer for his laboratory was the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(1963_computer)" title="Titan (1963 computer)">Titan</a>, a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Ferranti" title="Ferranti">Ferranti</a> Ltd begun in 1963. It eventually supported the UK's first time-sharing system<sup id="cite_ref-time_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and provided wider access to computing resources in the university, including time-shared graphics systems for mechanical <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">CAD</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Control Memory (CM) which is extremely used in today's computer was developed by using the concept of this Microprogramming.
</p><p>A notable design feature of the Titan's <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> was that it provided controlled access based on the identity of the program, as well as or instead of, the identity of the user. It introduced the password encryption system used later by <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>. Its programming system also had an early version control system.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wilkes is also credited with the idea of symbolic labels, <a href="/wiki/Macro_(computer_science)" title="Macro (computer science)">macros</a> and subroutine libraries. These are fundamental developments that made programming much easier and paved the way for high-level <a href="/wiki/Programming_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Programming languages">programming languages</a>. Later, Wilkes worked on an early timesharing systems (now termed a multi-user operating system) and <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a>. Toward the end of the 1960s, Wilkes also became interested in <a href="/wiki/Capability-based_security" title="Capability-based security">capability-based computing</a>, and the laboratory assembled a unique computer, the <a href="/wiki/CAP_computer" title="CAP computer">Cambridge CAP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cap_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cap-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1974 Wilkes encountered a Swiss data network (at Hasler AG) that used a ring topology to allocate time on the network. The laboratory initially used a prototype to share peripherals. Eventually, commercial partnerships were formed, and similar technology became widely available in England.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Awards.2C_honours_and_leadership"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards,_honours_and_leadership">Awards, honours and leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards, honours and leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He received a number of distinctions: he was a knight bachelor, <a href="/wiki/DFBCS" class="mw-redirect" title="DFBCS">Distinguished Fellow of the British Computer Society</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dblp_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-microsoft_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microsoft-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-grauniad_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grauniad-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-indy_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indy-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Telegraph_Obituary_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Telegraph_Obituary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-memoirs_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-memoirs-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-numerical_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-numerical-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
Wilkes received a number of distinctions: he was a knight bachelor, <a href="/wiki/DFBCS" class="mw-redirect" title="DFBCS">Distinguished Fellow of the British Computer Society</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering</a> and was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1956" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1956">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 1956</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-frs_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frs-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He was a founder member of the <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a> (BCS) and its first president (1957–1960). Wilkes received the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> in 1967, with the following citation: "Professor Wilkes is best known as the builder and designer of the EDSAC, the first computer with an internally stored program. Built in 1949, the EDSAC used a mercury <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory" title="Delay line memory">delay line memory</a>. He is also known as the author, with <a href="/wiki/David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)" title="David Wheeler (computer scientist)">David Wheeler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Gill" title="Stanley Gill">Stanley Gill</a>, of a volume on <i>Preparation of Programs for Electronic Digital Computers</i> in 1951,<sup id="cite_ref-prep_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prep-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> in which program libraries were effectively introduced." In 1968 he received the <a href="/wiki/Harry_H._Goode_Memorial_Award" title="Harry H. Goode Memorial Award">Harry H. Goode Memorial Award</a>, with the following citation: "For his many original achievements in the computer field, both in engineering and software, and for his contributions to the growth of professional society activities and to international cooperation among computer professionals."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1972, Maurice Wilkes was awarded an honorary Doctor of Science by <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">Newcastle University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1980, he retired from his professorships and post as the head of the Computer Laboratory and joined the central engineering staff of <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maynard,_Massachusetts" title="Maynard, Massachusetts">Maynard, Massachusetts</a>, USA.<sup id="cite_ref-cv_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wilkes was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Faraday_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Faraday Medal">Faraday Medal</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Electrical_Engineers" title="Institution of Electrical Engineers">Institution of Electrical Engineers</a> in 1981. The Maurice Wilkes Award, awarded annually for an outstanding contribution to computer architecture made by a young computer scientist or engineer, is named after him.
In 1986, he returned to England, and became a member of <a href="/wiki/Olivetti_Research_Laboratory" title="Olivetti Research Laboratory">Olivetti</a>'s Research Strategy Board. In 1987, he was awarded an Honorary Degree (Doctor of Science) by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bath" title="University of Bath">University of Bath</a>. In 1993 Wilkes was presented, by Cambridge University, with an honorary Doctor of Science degree. In 1994 he was inducted as a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Mountbatten_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Mountbatten Medal">Mountbatten Medal</a> in 1997 and in 2000 presented the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Pinkerton_Lecture" title="Pinkerton Lecture">Pinkerton Lecture</a>. He was knighted in the 2000 New Year Honours List. In 2001, he was inducted as a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a> "for his contributions to computer technology, including early machine design, microprogramming, and the Cambridge Ring network."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 2002, Wilkes moved back to the Computer Laboratory, University of Cambridge, as an Emeritus Professor.<sup id="cite_ref-cv_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his memoirs Wilkes wrote:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I well remember when this realization first came on me with full force. The EDSAC was on the top floor of the building and the tape-punching and editing equipment one floor below. ... It was on one of my journeys between the EDSAC room and the punching equipment that "hesitating at the angles of stairs" the realization came over me with full force that a good part of the remainder of my life was going to be spent in finding errors in my own programs.<sup id="cite_ref-memoirs_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-memoirs-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maurice_Wilkes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wilkes married Nina Twyman in 1947 who died in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> He died in November 2010 and was survived by his son, Anthony, and two daughters, Margaret and Helen.
</p>
