<p><b>Roy Castle</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">OBE</a></span> (31 August 1932<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> – 2 September 1994<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>) was an English dancer, singer, comedian, actor, television presenter and musician. In addition to being an accomplished <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> trumpet player, he could play many other instruments. Following a versatile career as a performer on stage, television and film, he became best known to British television viewers as the long-running presenter of the children's series <i><a href="/wiki/Record_Breakers" title="Record Breakers">Record Breakers</a></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Castle was born in <a href="/wiki/Scholes,_Holme_Valley" title="Scholes, Holme Valley">Scholes</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Holmfirth" title="Holmfirth">Holmfirth</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>. The son of a railwayman, he was a <a href="/wiki/Tap_dancer" class="mw-redirect" title="Tap dancer">tap dancer</a> from an early age and trained at Nora Bray's school of dance with Audrey Spencer who later ran a big dance school,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and after leaving Holme Valley Grammar School (now <a href="/wiki/Honley_High_School" title="Honley High School">Honley High School</a>) he started his career as an entertainer in an amateur concert party. As a young performer in the 1950s, he lived in <a href="/wiki/Cleveleys" title="Cleveleys">Cleveleys</a> near <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a> and appeared there at the local Queen's Theatre, turning professional in 1953 as a stooge for <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Clitheroe" title="Jimmy Clitheroe">Jimmy Clitheroe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_James_(comedian)" title="Jimmy James (comedian)">Jimmy James</a>. By 1958 he was appearing at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Variety_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Variety Show">Royal Variety Show</a>. As a singer, he released one charting single in 1960, the Christmas song "Little White Berry".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_and_film_career">Television and film career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Television and film career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1965, Castle starred with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cushing" title="Peter Cushing">Peter Cushing</a> in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Who_and_the_Daleks" title="Dr. Who and the Daleks">Dr. Who and the Daleks</a></i>, the first of two cinematic spin-offs from the popular <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television series. He played the role of <a href="/wiki/Dr._Who_(Dalek_films)" title="Dr. Who (Dalek films)">Dr. Who</a>'s first male assistant, <a href="/wiki/List_of_companions_in_Doctor_Who_spin-offs#Ian_Chesterton" title="List of companions in Doctor Who spin-offs">Ian Chesterton</a>, and was cast to perform the role more comedically than it had been played by <a href="/wiki/William_Russell_(English_actor)" title="William Russell (English actor)">William Russell</a> in the original series. He also appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Terror%27s_House_of_Horrors" title="Dr. Terror&#39;s House of Horrors">Dr. Terror's House of Horrors</a></i> as a jazz musician suffering a curse after copying <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">voodoo</a> tunes. He also appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Carry_On_Up_the_Khyber" title="Carry On Up the Khyber">Carry On Up the Khyber</a></i> in 1968 and in the <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">TV musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Pickwick_(1969_film)" title="Pickwick (1969 film)">Pickwick</a></i> for the BBC in 1969. In the 90s he appeared again in Pickwick, touring the country, starring alongside Sir Harry Secombe and the show was recorded again. Sir Harry had originally starred in the West End  version of the show in 1963.  In 1973, Castle teamed up with the actor and comedian <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Barker" title="Ronnie Barker">Ronnie Barker</a> in an original one-off called "Another Fine Mess" (an episode from a series called <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_of_One" title="Seven of One">Seven of One</a></i>). Barker was one of Castle's best friends, and paid tribute to their work together shortly after Castle's death.
</p><p>Between 1967 and 1968 Castle co-starred with <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Edwards" title="Jimmy Edwards">Jimmy Edwards</a> in the London West End run of the comedy farce show <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Bad_Mouse" title="Big Bad Mouse">Big Bad Mouse</a></i> when <a href="/wiki/Eric_Sykes" title="Eric Sykes">Eric Sykes</a> had to withdraw because of illness. The show was resident at the <a href="/wiki/Shaftesbury_Theatre" title="Shaftesbury Theatre">Shaftesbury Theatre</a> and, being loosely scripted, it offered both Edwards and Castle the chance to freely <a href="/wiki/Ad_lib" class="mw-redirect" title="Ad lib">ad-lib</a> and generally break the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">fourth wall</a> with the audience, Castle breaking into trumpet performances while Edwards walked into a front stall seat to read a newspaper, tap dancing and firing <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">ping-pong</a> balls into the stalls.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He also once stood in for <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Forsyth" title="Bruce Forsyth">Bruce Forsyth</a> hosting <i><a href="/wiki/The_Generation_Game" title="The Generation Game">The Generation Game</a></i> in 1975 while Forsyth was ill. He made many appearances on BBC TV's long running variety show <i>The Good Old Days</i>, making huge use of his multi instrumental and performing skills.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_Breakers"><i>Record Breakers</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Record Breakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1972, he first presented <i><a href="/wiki/Record_Breakers" title="Record Breakers">Record Breakers</a></i>, a children's show, and he remained host for over 20 years. He recorded the <a href="/wiki/Theme_music" title="Theme music">theme song</a> for the show himself. While presenting the show he broke nine <a href="/wiki/World_record" title="World record">world records</a> himself, including
</p>
<ul><li>Fastest <a href="/wiki/Tap-dance" class="mw-redirect" title="Tap-dance">tap-dance</a> 1,440 taps per minute – 24 taps per second, set on 14 January 1973, a record that has never been bettered.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Longest <a href="/wiki/Wing_walking" title="Wing walking">wing walk</a> – 3 hours, 23 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Playing the same tune on 43 different instruments in four minutes.</li>
<li>On the 2nd November 1985 the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a> reported that "Twinkle-toed Roy Castle has the world at his feet...the millionth time in 24 hours. This was the moment when he tap-danced his way to a new record and raised £1 million for charity. The comedian, host of TV's <a href="/wiki/Record_Breakers" title="Record Breakers">Record Breakers</a>, averaged nearly twelve steps a second during the sponsored feat in London. Roy, 53, had already qualified as the world's fastest tap-dancer.  Last night, a million taps on, he was the tiredest, too."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>He was a host of the show up until a few months before his death in 1994, alongside <a href="/wiki/Norris_McWhirter" title="Norris McWhirter">Norris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_McWhirter" title="Ross McWhirter">Ross McWhirter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Kennedy" title="Fiona Kennedy">Fiona Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheryl_Baker" title="Cheryl Baker">Cheryl Baker</a>. From then on, hosting was taken over by Baker and former athlete <a href="/wiki/Kriss_Akabusi" title="Kriss Akabusi">Kriss Akabusi</a>. It continued for 29 years until 2001, one of Britain's longest-running shows.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singing_career">Singing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1958 and 1969, Castle recorded three <a href="/wiki/LP_album" class="mw-redirect" title="LP album">LPs</a>. One of these, <i>Songs For A Rainy Day</i> was recorded in 1966 for <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia</a> and was reissued in the UK on CD by <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> Gold, re-titled <i>Isn't This A Lovely Day</i> in 2005. The record features twelve songs with rain as the theme. British <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> players of the day <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Beck" title="Gordon Beck">Gordon Beck</a> (piano), <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Clyne" title="Jeff Clyne">Jeff Clyne</a> (bass), Leon Calvert (flugelhorn), Ike Isaacs (guitar), Ray Swinfield (flute) and <a href="/wiki/Al_Newman" title="Al Newman">Al Newman</a> (saxophone) played on the record and it features jazz arrangements by Victor Graham covering a variety of styles such as <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big band</a>, ("<a href="/wiki/Pennies_from_Heaven_(song)" title="Pennies from Heaven (song)">Pennies From Heaven</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Stormy_Weather_(song)" title="Stormy Weather (song)">Stormy Weather</a>"), ballads ("February Brings The Rain", "<a href="/wiki/Here%27s_That_Rainy_Day" title="Here&#39;s That Rainy Day">Here's That Rainy Day</a>", "Soon It's Gonna Rain") and <a href="/wiki/Bossa_nova" title="Bossa nova">bossa novas</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Everytime_It_Rains" title="Everytime It Rains">Everytime It Rains</a>", "The Gentle Rain").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Castle married dancer <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Dickson" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiona Dickson">Fiona Dickson</a> in 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> They had been introduced to each other by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Morecambe" title="Eric Morecambe">Eric Morecambe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Both Castle and his wife were committed <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> and they regularly attended the <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> church near their home. They had four children. Their youngest son, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Castle" title="Ben Castle">Ben Castle</a> (born 1973), is a jazz saxophonist who has played with a wide range of artists, including <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Cullum" title="Jamie Cullum">Jamie Cullum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carleen_Anderson" title="Carleen Anderson">Carleen Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beth_Rowley" title="Beth Rowley">Beth Rowley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marillion" title="Marillion">Marillion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>, and performed on film soundtracks.
</p><p>Castle was a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> fan and supported <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a>. Less than six months before his death, he attended the <a href="/wiki/Everton_F.C." title="Everton F.C.">Liverpool-Everton</a> <a href="/wiki/Local_derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Local derby">derby match</a> at <a href="/wiki/Anfield" title="Anfield">Anfield</a> on 14 March 1994 and stood on the Spion Kop terrace. He had also been in the crowd at Liverpool's <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> final victory over <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_A.F.C." title="Sunderland A.F.C.">Sunderland</a> in May 1992, shortly after he was first found to have cancer. At that time <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Barker" title="Ronnie Barker">Ronnie Barker</a> paid tribute to him, referring to their portrayal of characters that bore a strong resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Laurel_and_Hardy" title="Laurel and Hardy">Laurel and Hardy</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Another_Fine_Mess" title="Another Fine Mess">Another Fine Mess</a></i>.
</p><p>On 31 December 1992, Castle was awarded the <a href="/wiki/OBE" class="mw-redirect" title="OBE">OBE</a>. He was also a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Carl_Alan_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Alan Award">Carl Alan Award</a>, an honour voted for by members of the professional dance industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness_and_death">Illness and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roy_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Illness and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Castle was diagnosed with lung cancer in March 1992, and was told that his chances of recovery were slim and that it was unlikely that he would live for more than six months. He underwent <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiotherapy" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiotherapy">radiotherapy</a> and went into <a href="/wiki/Remission_(medicine)" title="Remission (medicine)">remission</a> later that year. A non-smoker, he blamed his illness on <a href="/wiki/Passive_smoking" title="Passive smoking">passive smoking</a> during his years of playing the trumpet in smoky jazz clubs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On 26 November 1993, Castle announced that his illness had returned, and once again underwent treatment in the hope of overcoming it. Several months later, he carried out the high-profile <i>Tour of Hope</i> to raise funds for the erection of the building that would become the <a href="/wiki/Roy_Castle_Lung_Cancer_Foundation" title="Roy Castle Lung Cancer Foundation">Roy Castle Lung Cancer Foundation</a>, which is the only British charity dedicated solely to defeating lung cancer. By this stage, however, his condition was deteriorating and recovery was looking highly unlikely.
</p><p>During and shortly after Castle's illness, many smoke-free restaurants and cafes were awarded the <i>Roy Castle Clean Air Award</i> to denote their adherence to a (then voluntary) smoke-free regime.
</p><p>His final contribution to <i>Record Breakers</i> was aired at the end of the series ending in December 1993, although the programme continued until 2001.
</p><p>He died in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a> on 2 September 1994, two days after his 62nd birthday.
</p><p>His widow Fiona worked with the charity after her husband's death, and campaigned for the British <a href="/wiki/Smoking_ban" title="Smoking ban">smoking ban</a> which came into effect in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in 2004, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 2006 and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> in 2007, banning smoking in virtually all enclosed <a href="/wiki/Public_space" title="Public space">public places</a>.
</p>
