<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Persian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Persian_masculine_given_names" title="Category:Persian masculine given names">Category:Persian masculine given names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Persian_feminine_given_names" title="Category:Persian feminine given names">Category:Persian feminine given names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Iranian_masculine_given_names" title="Category:Iranian masculine given names">Category:Iranian masculine given names</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Iranian_feminine_given_names" title="Category:Iranian feminine given names">Category:Iranian feminine given names</a></div>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_conquest_of_Persia" title="Muslim conquest of Persia">Muslim conquest of Persia</a>, some names in Iran have been derived from <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, although the majority are Persian in origin. Persian Christians have Arabic names indistinguishable from their Muslim neighbors. They can also use Arabic derivations of Christian names (such as saints' names), or <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neo-Aramaic_languages" title="Neo-Aramaic languages">Neo-Aramaic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a> names, as most Christian Iranians are <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Armenians" title="Iranian Armenians">Iranian Armenians</a>.
</p><p>Many Persian names come from the Persian literature book, the <i><a href="/wiki/Shahnameh" title="Shahnameh">Shahnameh</a></i> or "Epic of Kings".  It was composed in the 10th century by <a href="/wiki/Ferdowsi" title="Ferdowsi">Ferdowsi</a> and is considered by many the masterpiece of Persian literature. Approximately 10%-15% of all Persian names are from Shahnameh. A few examples are Abtin, Ardeshir, Armeen, Arzhang, Babak (Papak), Bijan, Bizhan, Bozorgmehr, Darab, Dariush (Darius), Esfandiar/Esfandyar, Javid, Faramarz, Farhad, Fariborz, Farshid, Farzad, Sam and Yazdan.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Persian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Persian-language_surnames" title="Category:Persian-language surnames">Category:Persian-language surnames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Iranian-language_surnames" title="Category:Iranian-language surnames">Category:Iranian-language surnames</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surname_law" title="Surname law">Surname law</a></div>
<p>Prior to 1919, the peoples of Persia did not use surnames. An act of the <a href="/wiki/Vosough_od-Dowleh" class="mw-redirect" title="Vosough od-Dowleh">Vosough od-Dowleh</a> government in 1919 <a href="/wiki/Surname_law" title="Surname law">introduced the use of surnames</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the practice expanded during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Reza_Shah" title="Reza Shah">Reza Shah</a> (r. 1925–1941). Prior to that, a person was often distinguished from others by a combination of prefixes and suffixes attached to his or her name. If it was omitted, that person might be taken for someone else.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Since the adoption of surnames, <a href="/wiki/Ahmadi_(surname)" title="Ahmadi (surname)">Ahmadi</a> has become the most popular surname in Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In many cases individuals were known by the name of the district, city, town, or even the village from which they came by using the locality's name as a suffix, for example: Nuri, <a href="/wiki/Khorasan_Province" title="Khorasan Province">Khorasani</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81zandar%C4%81n_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Māzandarān Province">Mazandarani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kordestan" class="mw-redirect" title="Kordestan">Kordestani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehrani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esfah%C4%81n" class="mw-redirect" title="Esfahān">Esfahani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gilani_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilani (disambiguation)">Gilani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamedan" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamedan">Hamedani</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">Shirazi</a>. The same rule is followed for the many millions of Iranians who have surnames of regions or cities of the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> region. The latter was forcefully ceded in the course of the 19th century to <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Gulistan" title="Treaty of Gulistan">Treaty of Gulistan</a> (1813) and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Turkmenchay" title="Treaty of Turkmenchay">Treaty of Turkmenchay</a> (1828). Examples of common <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iranian</a> surnames in this regard are <a href="/wiki/Dagestan" title="Dagestan">Daghestani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karabagh" class="mw-redirect" title="Karabagh">Gharabaghi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derbent" title="Derbent">Darbandi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirvan" title="Shirvan">Shirvani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yerevan" title="Yerevan">Iravani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakhchivan_Autonomous_Republic" title="Nakhchivan Autonomous Republic">Nakhjevani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lankaran" title="Lankaran">Lankarani</a>. 
</p><p>Among many other secularization and modernization reforms, surnames were required by Reza Shah, following similar contemporary patterns in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> under <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal Atatürk</a>, and later in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> under <a href="/wiki/Gamal_Abdel_Nasser" title="Gamal Abdel Nasser">Gamal Abdel Nasser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_terminology">Name terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Persian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Name terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics">Honorifics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Persian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Honorifics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of these refer to <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> titles or roles in branches of <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia Islam</a>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aga_Khan" title="Aga Khan">Aga Khan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_title" title="Hereditary title">hereditary title</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imamah_(Shia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Imamah (Shia)">Imam</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nizari" title="Nizari">Nizari</a> branch of <a href="/wiki/Isma%27ilism" title="Isma&#39;ilism">Isma'ilism</a>. As a suffix, it indicates his children, grandchildren, and/or grandchildren.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mullah" title="Mullah">Mullah</a>, Muslim cleric. The title has also been used in some Jewish communities to refer to the community's leadership, especially religious leadership.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agha_(title)" title="Agha (title)">Agha (title)</a>, Sir, mister. It is a general term of respect.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">Ayatollah</a>, high-ranking title given to <a href="/wiki/Twelver" title="Twelver">Twelver</a> Shiʻi clerics.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dervish" title="Dervish">Dervish</a>, a mystic or a spiritual guru in <a href="/wiki/Sufism" title="Sufism">Sufism</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan (title)</a>, served at one time as a title for an honored person.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ustad" title="Ustad">Ustad</a>, a master craftsperson, lecturer or a person who is the master of a profession.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">Sayyid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sharif" title="Sharif">sharif</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> titles that given to men accepted as descendants of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shah" title="Shah">Shah</a>, "king".</li></ul>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hajji" title="Hajji">Hajji</a>, one who had made the <a href="/wiki/Hajj" title="Hajj">Hajj</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jenaab" title="Jenaab">Jenaab</a>, sir, excellency.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karbala" title="Karbala">Karbala</a>'i, one who has made the pilgrimage to Karbala</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mashhad" title="Mashhad">Mashhadi</a>, one who has made the pilgrimage to Mashhad, often shortened to Mashti, or Mash.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">Mir</a>, generally indicates the person is a <a href="/wiki/Sayyid" title="Sayyid">sayyid</a>(a) or is of royal descent.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixes">Suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Persian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Suffixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>-i</b>, the most common suffix used for Persian surnames. They are, in fact, adjectives created by the adding suffix "-i" to person names, location names or other names. Surnames with "-i" are also popular in other countries of historic Greater Persia and neighboring countries like in the Caucasus, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Turkey, Iraq, and Central Asia.</li>
<li><b>-ian</b>, like the above case, but with the addition of the plural suffix "-an", common among Iranians and <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenians</a>. Examples are Shaheenian (Persian) and <a href="/wiki/Sargsyan" title="Sargsyan">Sargsyan</a> (Armenian).</li>
<li><b>-an</b>, similar to English "-s" in "Roberts".</li>
<li><b>-pour</b>, "descendant of an Army official (Title)".</li>
<li><b>-<a href="/wiki/Zadeh" title="Zadeh">zadeh</a></b>, "descendant of".</li>
<li><b>-nezhad, -nejad</b>, " of race/clan (Title)".</li>
<li><b>-nia</b>, "His/Her highness (Title)".</li>
<li><b>-far</b>, "the light of", see <a href="/wiki/Farr-e_Kiyani_(Faravahar)" class="mw-redirect" title="Farr-e Kiyani (Faravahar)">Farr-e Kiyani (Faravahar)</a></li>
<li><b>-bakhsh</b>, "granted by".</li>
<li><b>-dad</b> (Old Persian <i>dāta</i>), "given by".</li></ul>
