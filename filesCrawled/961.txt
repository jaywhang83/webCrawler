<p><b>Doncaster</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="/ŋ/: &#39;ng&#39; in &#39;sing&#39;">ŋ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>-/</a></span></span>) is a large town in <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Together with its surrounding suburbs and settlements, the town forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Doncaster" title="Metropolitan Borough of Doncaster">Metropolitan Borough of Doncaster</a>, which had a mid-2018&#160;est. population of 310,542.<sup id="cite_ref-ons.gov.uk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ons.gov.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The town itself has a population of 109,805<sup id="cite_ref-NOMIS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOMIS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Doncaster Urban Area had a population of 158,141 in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and includes Doncaster and neighbouring small villages. Part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a> until 1974, Doncaster is about 17 miles (30&#160;km) north-east of <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, with which it is served by an international airport, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Sheffield_Airport" title="Doncaster Sheffield Airport">Doncaster Sheffield Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Finningley" title="Finningley">Finningley</a>. Under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, Doncaster was incorporated into a newly created <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> in 1974, itself incorporated with other nearby boroughs in the 1974 creation of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan county</a> of South Yorkshire.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alternative_Ermine_Street.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Alternative_Ermine_Street.JPG/220px-Alternative_Ermine_Street.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="384" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/Alternative_Ermine_Street.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="252" data-file-height="440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alternative_Ermine_Street.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ermine_Street" title="Ermine Street">Ermine Street</a>'s alternative route: <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">Eboracum</a> (<a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>) to Lagecium (<a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>), 21 <a href="/wiki/Roman_mile" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman mile">miles</a>, to Danum (Doncaster), 16 miles, to Agelocum (<a href="/wiki/Littleborough,_Nottinghamshire" title="Littleborough, Nottinghamshire">Littleborough</a>), 21 miles, to <a href="/wiki/Lindum_Colonia" title="Lindum Colonia">Lindum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a>), 13 miles. A separate spur connected Danum with <a href="/wiki/Calcaria" title="Calcaria">Calcaria</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Tadcaster" title="Tadcaster">Tadcaster</a>).</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman">Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Possibly inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">earlier people</a>, Doncaster grew up at the site of a <a href="/wiki/Roman_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman fort">Roman fort</a> constructed in the 1st century at a crossing of the <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">River Don</a>. The 2nd-century <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Itinerary" title="Antonine Itinerary">Antonine Itinerary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman withdrawal from Britain">early-5th-century</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Notitia_Dignitatum" title="Notitia Dignitatum">Notitia Dignitatum</a></i> (<i>Register of Dignitaries</i>) called this fort <b><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Danum</i></b>. The first section of the road to the Doncaster fort had probably been constructed since the early 50s, while a route through the north <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> hills was opened in the latter half of the 1st century, possibly by Governor <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Gn. Julius Agricola</a> during the late 70s. Doncaster provided an alternative direct land route between <a href="/wiki/Lindum_Colonia" title="Lindum Colonia">Lincoln</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">York</a>. The main route between Lincoln and York was <a href="/wiki/Ermine_Street" title="Ermine Street">Ermine Street</a>, which required parties to break into smaller units to cross the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a> in boats. As this was not always practical, the Romans considered Doncaster to be an important staging post. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">Roman road</a> through Doncaster appears on two routes recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Itinerary" title="Antonine Itinerary">Antonine Itinerary</a>. The itinerary include the same section of road between Lincoln and York, and list three stations along the route between these two <a href="/wiki/Roman_colonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman colonia">coloniae</a>. Routes 7 and 8 (<i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Iter</i>&#160;VII &amp; VIII</i>) are entitled "the route from York to <a href="/wiki/Londinum" class="mw-redirect" title="Londinum">London</a>".
</p><p>Several areas of known intense archaeological interest have been identified in the town, although many—in particular <span class="nowrap">St Sepulchre</span> Gate—remain hidden under buildings. The Roman fort is believed to have been located on the site that is now covered by <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Minster,_Doncaster" title="St George&#39;s Minster, Doncaster">St George's Minster</a>, next to the <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">River Don</a>. The Doncaster garrison units are named in the <i>Register</i> produced near the <a href="/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman withdrawal from Britain">end of Roman rule in Britain</a>: it was the home of the Crispinian Horse, presumably named because it was originally recruited from among the tribes living near <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crispiana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crispiana (page does not exist)">Crispiana</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pannonia_Superior" title="Pannonia Superior">Pannonia Superior</a> (near present-day <a href="/wiki/Zirc" title="Zirc">Zirc</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>), but possibly owing to <a href="/wiki/Crispus" title="Crispus">Crispus</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine the Great</a>, being headquartered there while his father was based in nearby <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">York</a>. The <i>Register</i> names the unit as under the command of the "<a href="/wiki/Dux_Britanniarum" title="Dux Britanniarum">Duke of the Britons</a>".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MedievalDoncaster.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/MedievalDoncaster.gif/220px-MedievalDoncaster.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/MedievalDoncaster.gif/330px-MedievalDoncaster.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/MedievalDoncaster.gif/440px-MedievalDoncaster.gif 2x" data-file-width="563" data-file-height="659" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MedievalDoncaster.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the boundary of the fortified Medieval township of Doncaster with four Gates</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doncaster is generally believed to be the <b>Cair Daun</b><sup id="cite_ref-mommy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mommy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> listed as one of the 28 <a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">cities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">Britain</a> in the 9th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">History of the Britons</a></i> traditionally attributed to <a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nashford_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nashford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It was certainly an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Burh" title="Burh">burh</a>, during which period it received its present name: "Don-" (<a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a>&#58; <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">Donne</i>) from the Roman settlement and river and "-caster" (<i><i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">-ceaster</i></i>) from an <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castra</a></i> ("military camp; fort"). The settlement was mentioned in the 1003 <a href="/wiki/Will_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Will (law)">will</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wulfric_Spott" class="mw-redirect" title="Wulfric Spott">Wulfric Spott</a>. Shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a>, Nigel Fossard refortified the town and constructed <a href="/wiki/Conisbrough_Castle" title="Conisbrough Castle">Conisbrough Castle</a>. By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexthorpe" title="Hexthorpe">Hexthorpe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_division)" title="Hundred (county division)">wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Strafforth_and_Tickhill" title="Strafforth and Tickhill">Strafforth</a> was described as having a church and two <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">mills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The historian David Hey says that these facilities represent the settlement at Doncaster. He also suggests that the street name Frenchgate indicates that Fossard invited fellow Normans to trade in the town.<sup id="cite_ref-hey_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hey-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Doncaster was ceded to <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Durham_(1136)" title="Treaty of Durham (1136)">Treaty of Durham</a>; it was never formally returned to England.
<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg/220px-Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg/330px-Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg/440px-Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Conisbrough_Castle_Doncaster_winter_time.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>12th-century Conisbrough Castle, open to the public and property of English Heritage</div></div></div>
<p>As the 13th century approached, Doncaster matured into a busy town; in 1194 <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">King Richard I</a> granted it national recognition with a <a href="/wiki/Town_charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Town charter">town charter</a>. Doncaster had a disastrous fire in 1204, from which it slowly recovered. At the time, buildings were built of wood, and open fireplaces were used for cooking and heating. Fire was a constant hazard.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg/220px-St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg/330px-St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg/440px-St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1142" data-file-height="586" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Mary_Magdalene_Doncaster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Norman church of St Mary Magdalene, at demolition in 1846</div></div></div>
<p>In 1248, a charter was granted for Doncaster's market to be held around the Church of St Mary Magdalene, built in Norman times. In the 16th century, the church was adapted for use as the <a href="/wiki/Town_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town hall">town hall</a>. It was finally demolished in 1846.<sup id="cite_ref-hey_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hey-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Some 750 years on, the market continues to operate, with its busy traders located both under cover, at the 19th-century 'Corn Exchange' building (1873)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and in outside stalls. The Corn Exchange was extensively rebuilt in 1994 after a major fire.
</p><p>During the 14th century, numerous friars arrived in Doncaster who were known for their religious enthusiasm and preaching. In 1307 the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friars (<a href="/wiki/Greyfriar" class="mw-redirect" title="Greyfriar">Greyfriars</a>) arrived, and <a href="/wiki/Carmelites" title="Carmelites">Carmelites</a> (Whitefriars) arrived in the middle of the 14th century. In the Medieval period, other major features of the town included the Hospital of St Nicholas and the <a href="/wiki/Leper_colony" title="Leper colony">leper colony</a> of the Hospital of St James, a <a href="/wiki/Moot_hall" title="Moot hall">moot hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a>, and the five-arched stone town bridge, with a chapel dedicated to Our Lady of the Bridge. By 1334, Doncaster was the wealthiest town in southern Yorkshire and the sixth most important town in Yorkshire as a whole, even boasting its own banker. By 1379, it was recovering from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>, which had reduced its population to 1,500. In October 1536, the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage_of_Grace" title="Pilgrimage of Grace">Pilgrimage of Grace</a> ended in Doncaster. This was a rebellion led by the lawyer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Aske_(political_leader)" title="Robert Aske (political leader)">Robert Aske</a>, who commanded 40,000 people of Yorkshire against Henry VIII in protest about the monarch's <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a>. Many of Doncaster's streets are named with the suffix 'gate'. The word 'gate' is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Danish" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Danish">old Danish</a> word 'gata,' which meant street. During Medieval times, craftsmen or tradesmen with similar skills, tended to live in the same street. Baxter is an ancient word for baker: Baxtergate was the bakers' street. Historians believe that 'Frenchgate' may be named after French-speaking <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> who settled on this street.
</p><p>The Medieval township of Doncaster is known to have been protected by earthen ramparts and ditches, with four substantial gates as entrances to the town. These gates were located at Hall Gate, St Mary's Bridge (old), St Sepulchre Gate and Sunny Bar. Today the gates at Sunny Bar are commemorated by huge 'Boar Gates'; similarly, the entrance to St Sepulchre Gate is commemorated with white marble 'Roman Gates'. The boundary of the town principally extended from the River Don, along a route formed by what is now Market Road, Silver Street, Cleveland Street and Printing Office Street.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg/220px-St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg/330px-St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg/440px-St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Georges_Doncaster_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Minster,_Doncaster" title="St George&#39;s Minster, Doncaster">St George's Minster</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because access into town was restricted, some officeholders secured charters to collect tolls. In 1605, <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">King James I</a> granted to William <a href="/wiki/Levett" title="Levett">Levett</a> of Doncaster, brother of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> merchant <a href="/wiki/Percival_Levett" title="Percival Levett">Percival Levett</a>, the right to levy tolls at Friar's and St Mary's bridges.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Having served as mayors and aldermen of Doncaster, the Levetts probably believed they could control a monopoly. In 1618 the family began enforcing it but, by 1628, the populace revolted. Capt. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Levett" title="Christopher Levett">Christopher Levett</a>, Percival's son, petitioned Parliament to enforce the tolls. But Parliament disagreed, calling the tolls "a grievance to the subjects, both in creation and execution," and axed the Levett monopoly.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (Doncaster's Levet Road is named for this family, as are the nearby hamlets of <a href="/wiki/Hooton_Levitt" title="Hooton Levitt">Hooton Levitt</a> and the largely extinct <a href="/wiki/Levitt_Hagg" title="Levitt Hagg">Levitt Hagg</a>, where much of the town's early limestone was quarried.)
</p><p>During the 16th and 17th centuries, the town of Doncaster continued to expand, although it suffered several outbreaks of plague between 1562 and 1606. Each time the plague struck down significant numbers of the town's population.
</p><p>During the campaign of the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First English Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> marched by <a href="/wiki/Bridgnorth" title="Bridgnorth">Bridgnorth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashbourne,_Derbyshire" title="Ashbourne, Derbyshire">Ashbourne</a> to Doncaster, where on 18 August 1645 he was met by great numbers of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> gentlemen who had rallied to his cause. On 2 May 1664, Doncaster was rewarded with the title of 'Free Borough' by way of the King (Charles I's son, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">King Charles II</a>) expressing his gratitude for Doncaster's allegiance.
</p><p>Doncaster has traditionally been a prosperous area<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> within the wapentake of <a href="/wiki/Strafforth_and_Tickhill" title="Strafforth and Tickhill">Stafford and Tickhill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The borough was known for its rich landowners with vast estates and huge stately homes such as <a href="/wiki/Brodsworth_Hall" title="Brodsworth Hall">Brodsworth Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantley_Hall" title="Cantley Hall">Cantley Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cusworth_Hall" title="Cusworth Hall">Cusworth Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hickleton_Hall" title="Hickleton Hall">Hickleton Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nether_Hall,_Doncaster" title="Nether Hall, Doncaster">Nether Hall</a> and Wheatley Hall (demolished 1934). This wealth is evidenced in the luxurious and historic gilded 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Mansion_House,_Doncaster" title="Mansion House, Doncaster">Mansion House</a> on High Street. This land ownership developed over what is an ancient market place and large buildings were erected in the 19th century, including the Market Hall and the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Corn Exchange">Corn Exchange</a>. Perhaps the most striking building is <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Minster,_Doncaster" title="St George&#39;s Minster, Doncaster">St George's Minster</a>, constructed in the 19th century and promoted from a <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> in 2004.
</p><p>Doncaster was already a communications centre at this time. Doncaster sat on the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">Great North Road or A1</a>, due to its strategic geographical importance and essentially Roman inheritance. This was the primary route for all traffic from London to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> and Doncaster benefited from its location.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Doncaster is represented in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> by three <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MPs</a>; all three constituencies are currently held by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rosie_Winterton" title="Rosie Winterton">Rosie Winterton</a> represents <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Central_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Doncaster Central (UK Parliament constituency)">Doncaster Central</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Leader of the Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party leader</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a> represents <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_North_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Doncaster North (UK Parliament constituency)">Doncaster North</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Flint" title="Caroline Flint">Caroline Flint</a> represents <a href="/wiki/Don_Valley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Don Valley (UK Parliament constituency)">Don Valley</a>.
</p><p>At a European level, Doncaster is part of the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Yorkshire and the Humber (European Parliament constituency)">Yorkshire and the Humber</a> constituency and is represented by six <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEPs</a>.
</p><p>Doncaster is one of only twelve UK boroughs to have a directly-elected mayor, a position currently held by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ros_Jones" title="Ros Jones">Ros Jones</a>.
</p><p>In September 2014, <a href="/wiki/UKIP" class="mw-redirect" title="UKIP">UKIP</a> held its annual party conference at <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Racecourse" title="Doncaster Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</a>. UKIP party leader <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Farage" title="Nigel Farage">Nigel Farage</a> claimed that by holding the conference in Doncaster, UKIP were "now parking our tanks on the Labour Party's lawn" referring to <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_North_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Doncaster North (UK Parliament constituency)">Doncaster North</a> constituency. Shortly afterwards in the seat, at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2015 United Kingdom general election">2015 general election</a>, UKIP won 8,928 votes to Labour's 20,708. In the <a href="/wiki/2016_United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum" title="2016 United Kingdom European Union membership referendum">2016 EU Referendum</a>, however, Doncaster voted 69% to leave the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg/220px-High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg/330px-High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg/440px-High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="370" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:High_Street_and_Frenchgate_shopping_centre._-_geograph.org.uk_-_510444.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High Street and Frenchgate shopping centre</div></div></div>
<p>Doncaster is a large settlement and borough in South Yorkshire. The borough expanded dramatically in population with the development of coal mining. Closure of coal mines in the 1970s, and the early 1980s caused some economic difficulties; the town then developed its <a href="/wiki/Service_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Service industry">service industry</a>; the already good communication links with the rest of the UK supported this development.
</p><p>The Doncaster skyline is dominated by the minster in the middle of the town. The <a href="/wiki/Frenchgate_Shopping_Centre" title="Frenchgate Shopping Centre">Frenchgate Shopping Centre</a> holds an important position in the skyline, along with the Doncaster College Hub building and <a href="/wiki/Cusworth_Hall" title="Cusworth Hall">Cusworth Hall</a>. Cusworth Hall is an 18th-century Grade I listed country house in Cusworth, near Doncaster. Set in the landscaped parklands of Cusworth Park, Cusworth Hall is a good example of a Georgian country house.
</p><p>Most of the old Doncaster College, the Council House and surrounding buildings have been demolished, and work has commenced to replace them with more modern facilities including a new theatre, council house and hotel which together will form the Doncaster Civic and Cultural Quarter. There are also plans for expansion onto land north of the new college (The Hub) if it gains university status.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Potteric_Carr" title="Potteric Carr">Potteric Carr</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Potteric_Carr_Nature_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Potteric Carr Nature Reserve">Potteric Carr Nature Reserve</a>, lies to the south.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doncaster has a <a href="/wiki/Maritime_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime climate">maritime climate</a>, lacking extreme temperatures, like the rest of the British Isles. The town lies at a low elevation in the Don Valley, in the lee of the Pennines, and inland from the North Sea, meaning daytime summer temperatures are no lower than parts of South East England, despite the more northerly location. The nearest weather station is RAF Finningley, now known as Doncaster Sheffield Airport, located about 5.5 miles (8.9&#160;km) to the south-east of Doncaster town centre, and at a similar elevation.
</p><p>The Doncaster area is about as far north as the 21.5&#160;°C (70.7&#160;°F) average July/August maximum temperature isotherm reaches – Indeed, the August 1990 record high of 35.5&#160;°C (95.9&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> is the most northerly temperature above 35&#160;°C (95&#160;°F) in the British Isles. The nearby town of Bawtry just slightly further south still holds the UK's September monthly record high temperature of 35.6&#160;°C (96.1&#160;°F),<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> set in 1906. Typically, the warmest day of the year should reach 29.1&#160;°C (84.4&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and 12.58 days will report a daytime maximum of 25.1&#160;°C (77.2&#160;°F) or above.
</p><p>The absolute minimum temperature stands at −13.5&#160;°C (7.7&#160;°F),<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> set during December 1981. However, online records only relate to the period 1960–2000, so lower temperatures may have been recorded at nearby locations outside of this timeframe. According to the 1971–2000 period, 51.9 nights of the year will record an air frost on average.
</p><p>Typically 106.9 days<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> of the year will report 1mm or more of rainfall. Total annual precipitation is slightly below 560&#160;mm (22&#160;in),<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> comparable to the driest parts of the United Kingdom, due to Doncaster's location in the <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> of the Pennines.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/South_and_West_Yorkshire_Green_Belt" title="South and West Yorkshire Green Belt">South and West Yorkshire Green Belt</a></div>
<p>Doncaster is within a <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">green belt</a> region that extends into the wider surrounding counties. First defined in 1966,<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> the policy is controlled throughout the town by the <a href="/wiki/Local_planning_authority" title="Local planning authority">local planning authority</a>, Doncaster council. It is in place to reduce <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, prevent areas in the Doncaster conurbation from further uncontrolled convergence, protect the identity of outlying communities, encourage <a href="/wiki/Brownfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> reuse, and preserve nearby countryside. This is achieved by restricting inappropriate development within the designated areas, and imposing stricter conditions on permitted building.<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It surrounds the side of the urban area west of the East Coast Line, preventing suburbs such as <a href="/wiki/Sprotborough" class="mw-redirect" title="Sprotborough">Sprotborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Edlington" title="New Edlington">New Edlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Edlington" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Edlington">Old Edlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scawsby" title="Scawsby">Scawsby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rossington" title="Rossington">New Rossington</a> merging. A subsidiary aim of the green belt is to encourage recreation and leisure interests,<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> with rural landscape features, greenfield areas and facilities including the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> and valley west of Hexthorpe; Hexthorpe Park; <a href="/wiki/Cusworth_Hall" title="Cusworth Hall">Cusworth Hall</a>, museum and country park; Potteric Carr and Huxter Well Marsh; and the <a href="/wiki/Trans_Pennine_Trail" title="Trans Pennine Trail">Trans Pennine Trail</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG/220px-Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG/330px-Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG/440px-Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG 2x" data-file-width="6016" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Town_Center.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The High Street in Doncaster town centre.</div></div></div>
<p>Doncaster emerged as an industrial centre in the late 18th century to 20th century. Its communication links, particularly its waterways, meant that Doncaster became extremely busy and experienced vast migration to its centre. Underneath Doncaster lies a huge natural resource by way of deep seam coal. Recently there has been an expansion in commercial and residential developments along with good transportation links with the rest of the United Kingdom.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_centres">Distribution centres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Distribution centres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its proximity to major urban centres and motorway/rail infrastructure, Doncaster has a number of major distribution centres, including the 420 acre <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_International_Railport" title="Doncaster International Railport">Doncaster International Railport</a>, which dispatches goods to Europe by rail. Large warehousing and logistic capabilities for retailers such as <a href="/wiki/Next_(clothing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Next (clothing)">Next</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>, <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, Lidl and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Faberg%C3%A9" title="House of Fabergé">Faberge</a> are also sited there. The <a href="/wiki/B%26Q" title="B&amp;Q">B&amp;Q</a> distribution centre next to the <a href="/wiki/DFS_(British_retailer)" class="mw-redirect" title="DFS (British retailer)">DFS</a> UK headquarters at Redhouse <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)#A1(M)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1(M)</a> junction 38 was the largest free-standing warehouse in the UK<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2011)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. A significant proportion of fresh and frozen goods for northern supermarkets is dispatched by road from Doncaster.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration_initiatives">Regeneration initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regeneration initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg/220px-Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg/330px-Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg/440px-Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frenchgate_south_entrance.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Frenchgate_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Frenchgate Centre">Frenchgate</a> shopping centre</div></div></div>
<p>On 5 March 2004, Doncaster was granted <a href="/wiki/Fairtrade_Town" title="Fairtrade Town">Fairtrade Town</a> status.  In recent years, its centre has undergone redevelopment including the construction of an <i>Education City</i> campus, currently the largest education investment of its kind in the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Over the last few years the <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Lakeside" title="Doncaster Lakeside">Doncaster Lakeside</a>, which is home to the Doncaster Rovers ground, has undergone modernisation. Doncaster has a bowling alley and a cinema near Lakeside. 'The Dome', opened in 1989, by Princess Diana, contains a swimming pool, gym and ice rink, as well as a café.
The <a href="/wiki/Frenchgate_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Frenchgate Centre">Frenchgate Centre</a>, a shopping centre and transport interchange, has been extended to connect with the railway station and bus station.
Lakeside Village, a retail outlet with some 45 retail shops and restaurants is located along the <a href="/wiki/A6182" class="mw-redirect" title="A6182">A6182</a> dual carriageway.
The Waterdale area of the town centre is currently undergoing rejuvenation, with a new theatre (known as CAST), new civic offices and a new public square already having been completed, on the site of the old Waterdale car park. The old council house and civic theatre have been demolished and new housing is being constructed both in the town itself, opposite Doncaster Racecourse and in out of town suburbs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confectionery">Confectionery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Confectionery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 19th and 20th century a number of confectioners were based in Doncaster including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parkinson%27s_Doncaster_Butterscotch_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Parkinson&#39;s Doncaster Butterscotch Company (page does not exist)">Parkinson's</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Butterscotch" title="Butterscotch">Butterscotch</a> inventors, Nuttalls Mintoes<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and Murray Mints.
In August 2011 Parkinson's put their 190-year-old trademark up for sale on eBay.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The sale was completed in 2012 to Confection by Design in Harrogate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_and_industrial_expansion">Coal and industrial expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Coal and industrial expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The waterways, <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">River Don</a> and Don Navigation were used to transport coal from Doncaster to the steel production centres at <a href="/wiki/Rotherham" title="Rotherham">Rotherham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scunthorpe" title="Scunthorpe">Scunthorpe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>.
A large number of mining jobs were lost in the late 1980s, and today coal mining has ceased.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rockware_Glass" title="Rockware Glass">Rockware Glass</a> is a specialist glass manufacturer. A production facility for chemical <a href="/wiki/Polymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Polymers">polymers</a> was built on Wheatley Hall Road. It changed hands during its existence until <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> closed it in the mid-1990s.
</p><p>Steel foundries, rolling mills and wire mills were built close to the railways that brought steel from Rotherham and Sheffield. <a href="/wiki/Bridon_Ropes" title="Bridon Ropes">Bridon Ropes</a> produces wire rope, including the ropes used at coal mines to haul coal and miners. It is claimed to be the largest wire rope manufacturing plant in Europe. Bridon supplied wire rope for the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Stadium_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic Stadium (London)">Olympic Stadium</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">2012 Olympic Games</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the town became involved in munitions manufacture.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_railways_and_locomotive_works">The railways and locomotive works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The railways and locomotive works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Works" title="Doncaster Works">Doncaster Works</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> the railway came to Doncaster, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" title="Great Northern Railway (Great Britain)">Great Northern Railway</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Works" title="Doncaster Works">Doncaster Locomotive and Carriage Building Works</a>. The reasons for this were Doncaster's communication links, the necessity to transport coal quickly and efficiently and Doncaster's expertise in specialist metal products. An extensive housing programme was undertaken for the increased population. The Chairman of the Great Northern, anxious about the workers and their families' spiritual welfare, persuaded the directors to contribute towards the building of St James' Church, which became known as the "Plant Church". The railway also built St James' School. The Doncaster Plant became famous for building <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Eastern_Railway" title="London and North Eastern Railway">LNER</a> <a href="/wiki/4-6-2" title="4-6-2">4-6-2</a> locomotives <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_A4_4468_Mallard" title="LNER Class A4 4468 Mallard">Mallard</a> and the <a href="/wiki/LNER_Class_A3_4472_Flying_Scotsman" title="LNER Class A3 4472 Flying Scotsman">Flying Scotsman</a>, as well as many thousands more locomotives.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Current plant owner <a href="/wiki/Wabtec_Corporation" title="Wabtec Corporation">Wabtec</a> continues to do refurbishment in Doncaster.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg/220px-Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg/330px-Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg/440px-Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Railway_Station_-_geograph.org.uk_-_170161.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Doncaster_railway_station" title="Doncaster railway station">Doncaster railway station</a></div></div></div>
<p>Today, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_railway_station" title="Doncaster railway station">Doncaster railway station</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a>, is linked to many towns and cities across the UK such as <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, Manchester, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, London, <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darlington" title="Darlington">Darlington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>, Edinburgh, <a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">Motherwell</a>, Glasgow, and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln, Lincolnshire">Lincoln</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Doncaster_PSB" title="Doncaster PSB">Doncaster PSB</a> is one of the largest signalling centres on the UK network, controlling hundreds of route miles of railway. <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_International_Railport" title="Doncaster International Railport">Doncaster International Railport</a> is an important road-rail intermodal terminal.
</p><p>The rail freight company <a href="/wiki/DB_Cargo_UK" title="DB Cargo UK">DB Cargo UK</a> has its headquarters in Doncaster.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">Aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RAF_Doncaster" title="RAF Doncaster">RAF Doncaster</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg/220px-Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg/330px-Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg/440px-Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robin_Hood_Airport_2006-04-02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Sheffield_Airport" title="Doncaster Sheffield Airport">Doncaster Sheffield Airport</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1909 <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Racecourse" title="Doncaster Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</a> was chosen as the venue for an airshow, after the world's first international air display in <a href="/wiki/Reims" title="Reims">Reims</a>, France in 1909. Around a dozen aviators were present, the most famous being <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Delagrange" title="Léon Delagrange">Léon Delagrange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sommer" title="Roger Sommer">Roger Sommer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Cody" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel Cody">Samuel Cody</a> (no relation to <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bill" title="Buffalo Bill">William F. Cody</a>) in an attempt to win a prize offered by the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> for the first British pilot in a British aeroplane to fly a circular mile signed British <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalisation">naturalisation</a> papers in front of the crowd with the band playing both <i><a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen" title="God Save the Queen">God Save the King</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Spangled_Banner" class="mw-redirect" title="Star Spangled Banner">Star Spangled Banner</a></i>. Unfortunately, he crashed his aeroplane on the first day of the meeting and made no significant flights.
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> fighters based first from the racecourse, then a temporary airstrip near Finningley (later <a href="/wiki/RAF_Finningley" title="RAF Finningley">RAF Finningley</a> and now <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Sheffield_Airport" title="Doncaster Sheffield Airport">Doncaster Sheffield Airport</a>) and finally, in 1916, from a newly built airfield alongside the racecourse, were deployed to defend the east coast against <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelins</a>. On a number of occasions fighters took off to search for the intruders but none were ever seen. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Flying_Corps" title="Royal Flying Corps">Royal Flying Corps</a> station trained pilots for the war in France. Within months of the war ending the entire station was put up for sale and two of its three Belfast hangars, the same type of hangar that now forms the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_Museum" title="Royal Air Force Museum">Royal Air Force Museum</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hendon" title="Hendon">Hendon</a>, were sold to a Sheffield motor manufacturing company for storage and assembly at <a href="/wiki/Finningley" title="Finningley">Finningley</a>. The third of the hangars stayed in place, mainly housing buses, until the 1970s when it was knocked down and replaced with modern buildings.
</p><p>In 1920 the Government asked local authorities to assist in the formation of a chain of airfields so the country would not lag behind other nations in the provision of civil air services. Doncaster took heed and, with expert advice from <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cobham" title="Alan Cobham">Alan Cobham</a>, on 26 May 1934, opened its 'aviation centre'. Development of the airfield continued and on 1 July 1936 an international service was opened to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>. On 1 November 1938, after long discussions with the <a href="/wiki/Air_Ministry" title="Air Ministry">Air Ministry</a>, <a href="/wiki/No._616_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 616 Squadron RAF">616 (South Yorkshire) Squadron</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliary Air Force">Royal Auxiliary Air Force</a> was formed. Shortly after the outbreak of war in 1939 the squadron went to its battle station and played a part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Britain" title="Battle of Britain">Battle of Britain</a>. After the departure of 616 squadron its place was taken by the formation of <a href="/wiki/No._271_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 271 Squadron RAF">271 (Transport) Squadron</a> composed mainly of requisitioned civilian aircraft and obsolescent twin engined bombers. 616 squadron was the first Allied jet fighter squadron, equipped with the <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Meteor" title="Gloster Meteor">Gloster Meteor</a>, famed for using their wingtips to throw <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">German V-1 Flying Bombs</a> off course. In 1944, after being equipped with American-made <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a> "Dakotas", the squadron moved south to take part in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Operation 'Overlord'</a> and later in the airborne invasion at <a href="/wiki/Arnhem" title="Arnhem">Arnhem</a> where <a href="/wiki/Flight_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight Lieutenant">Flight Lieutenant</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Lord" title="David Lord">David Lord</a> was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_recognition" class="mw-redirect" title="Posthumous recognition">posthumous</a> <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a>.
</p><p>After the war the airfield reverted to civilian flying and finally closed in 1992.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tractor_production">Tractor production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tractor production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg/220px-Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg/330px-Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg/440px-Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="290" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ploughing_-_geograph.org.uk_-_280384.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ploughing by tractor</div></div></div>
<p>In 1930, <a href="/wiki/International_Harvester" title="International Harvester">International Harvester</a> (IH) started the production of agricultural implements at a factory on Wheatley Hall Road and later at another in the Carr Hill area of Doncaster. The first tractor built at the factory was a <a href="/wiki/Farmall_M" title="Farmall M">Farmall M</a>, which came off the production line on 13 September 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Tractors were initially built from parts shipped from the US. The Wheatley Hall Road factory was extended after the war with a new <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a> to make the heavy castings. The factory started Crawler tractor production in 1953. By 1960 the factory was making a range of tractors from scratch, designed specifically for British and European markets, and sold under the 'McCormick International' name. Assembly moved in 1965 to the Carr Hill plant. In 1983 tractor production was moved to IH's other Doncaster factory at Wheatley Hall. In 1985, International Harvester sold its agricultural division to Tenneco, Inc. which then merged the operation with its subsidiary J.I. Case to form <a href="/wiki/Case_IH" title="Case IH">Case IH</a>, who continued to design and build its European tractor range in Doncaster, shutting the <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_Ltd." title="David Brown Ltd.">David Brown Ltd.</a> tractor factory near <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. The 350,000th tractor came off the production line in 1999.
</p><p>In 2000, the factory was purchased by <a href="/wiki/ARGO_SpA" title="ARGO SpA">ARGO SpA</a>, an Italian-based agricultural equipment builder. Doncaster was the sole production site of the <a href="/wiki/McCormick_Tractors" title="McCormick Tractors">McCormick Tractors</a> brand, and the factory employed around 380 people (although approximately 1,100 people are employed in the worldwide McCormick group). In December 2006 the parent company, ARGO Spa, announced that the Doncaster facility was to close in 2007 with the loss of around 325 jobs. The announcement was made only one week before Christmas. 61 years of tractor production in Doncaster came to an end in 2007 when McCormick tractor production was moved to Italy. 
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Doncaster" title="Transport in Doncaster">Transport in Doncaster</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European">European</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: European">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doncaster sits on the <a href="/wiki/European_route_E15" title="European route E15">European route E15</a> and is the starting point of <a href="/wiki/European_route_E13" title="European route E13">European route E13</a>. The E13 connects Doncaster, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> to London. In the United Kingdom, European route designators are not displayed on road signs. The <a href="/wiki/M18_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M18 motorway (Great Britain)">M18</a> Junction 2 at Doncaster was the original intended starting point of the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> where the motorway meets the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)#A1(M)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1(M)</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The intended motorway design is evidenced in road maps. The M1 was extended northward to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, which is why the E13 starts at Doncaster and follows the path of the M18 and the M1.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National">National</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: National">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doncaster is situated on the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)#A1(M)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1(M)</a> and <a href="/wiki/M18_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M18 motorway (Great Britain)">M18</a> motorways, and is within 20 minutes of the key <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1</a> and <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62</a> motorways.
The 15-mile (24&#160;km) A1(M) motorway bypass cost £6&#160;million and was opened by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Marples" title="Ernest Marples">Ernest Marples</a> in 1961. The former route is now the <a href="/wiki/A638_road" title="A638 road">A638</a>, and partly the <a href="/wiki/A614_road" title="A614 road">A614</a> to <a href="/wiki/Blyth,_Nottinghamshire" title="Blyth, Nottinghamshire">Blyth</a>. Doncaster is also an important <a href="/wiki/Railway_town" title="Railway town">railway town</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_railway_station" title="Doncaster railway station">station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional">Regional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Regional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Doncaster is a European hub with a new international airport, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Sheffield_Airport" title="Doncaster Sheffield Airport">Doncaster Sheffield Airport</a> opened in 2005. Doncaster International Railport facilities link to the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Tunnel" title="Channel Tunnel">Channel Tunnel</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_borough">Metropolitan borough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Metropolitan borough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New developments include campus facilities for <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_College" title="Doncaster College">Doncaster College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Frenchgate_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Frenchgate Centre">Frenchgate Interchange</a> (a unification of bus and railway stations with the <a href="/wiki/Frenchgate_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Frenchgate Centre">Frenchgate Centre</a>). The extension to the shopping centre and the new bus station opened on 8 June 2006, when all Doncaster bus routes started to use the station.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Doncaster Museum and Art Gallery">Doncaster Museum and Art Gallery</a> is the town's main museum. It opened in 1964, and explores natural history, archaeology, local history, and fine and decorative art.<sup id="cite_ref-visitdoncaster_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visitdoncaster-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> It has a major exhibit dedicated to silverware and trophies won at <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Racecourse" title="Doncaster Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</a>. The museum houses the Regimental Museum of the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Own_Yorkshire_Light_Infantry" title="King&#39;s Own Yorkshire Light Infantry">King's Own Yorkshire Light Infantry</a>.
</p><p>Doncaster is home to <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Wildlife_Park" title="Yorkshire Wildlife Park">Yorkshire Wildlife Park</a>, the park has over 400 animals and over 70 different species of animal including the only polar bears in England and is a major tourist attraction.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire_Aircraft_Museum" title="South Yorkshire Aircraft Museum">South Yorkshire Aircraft Museum</a> (formerly known as AeroVenture) is based on the former site of <a href="/wiki/RAF_Doncaster" title="RAF Doncaster">RAF Doncaster</a> at Doncaster Lakeside. The <a href="/wiki/The_Trolleybus_Museum_at_Sandtoft" title="The Trolleybus Museum at Sandtoft">Trolleybus Museum</a> in the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/Sandtoft,_Lincolnshire" title="Sandtoft, Lincolnshire">Sandtoft</a> specialises in the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">trolleybuses</a>, and claims to have the largest collection of preserved trolleybuses in Europe, with over 60 examples. Markham Grange Steam Museum, in a garden centre in the nearby village of <a href="/wiki/Brodsworth" title="Brodsworth">Brodsworth</a>, has a private collection of steam engines.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cusworth_Hall" title="Cusworth Hall">Cusworth Hall</a> is an 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade I listed</a> country house in <a href="/wiki/Cusworth" title="Cusworth">Cusworth</a>. It is open to the public and features displays documenting the history of South Yorkshire. <a href="/wiki/Mansion_House,_Doncaster" title="Mansion House, Doncaster">Doncaster Mansion House</a> features an art gallery and displays on local history.
</p><p>Ashworth Barracks Museum is a military museum in <a href="/wiki/Balby" title="Balby">Balby</a> telling the story of the men awarded the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a>. It also houses a First World War exhibit including a "Weekers Helmet" one of only two known to exist in the UK.
</p><p>Since 1973, Doncaster has been the home of the Doncaster Youth Jazz Association (DYJA). Founded by John Ellis, DYJA has been a training ground for generations of amateur and professional jazz musicians including, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Cato" title="Andy Cato">Andy Cato</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Groove_Armada" title="Groove Armada">Groove Armada</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Rollins" title="Dennis Rollins">Denis Rollins</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Escreet" title="John Escreet">John Escreet</a>, Nadim Teimoori and Reuben Fowler, .
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre_and_cinemas">Theatre and cinemas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Theatre and cinemas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Cast is the new £22&#160;million venue opened officially on Monday 2 September 2013. Cast includes a 620-seat auditorium, a flexible studio space, drama studio, dance studio, education and ancillary space, and a large foyer with a café. Its director was Kully Thiarai, formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Contact_Theatre" title="Contact Theatre">Contact Theatre</a>, Manchester.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Doncaster Little Theatre is a 99-seat community theatre which puts on their own in-house shows including a pantomime, along with two films a month during the day. Hire companies also use the theatre space for their own shows.</li>
<li>The town has an 11-screen multiplex <a href="/wiki/Vue_Cinemas" title="Vue Cinemas">Vue</a> which was expanded as part of a £5&#160;million upgrade.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Events and concerts take place at <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Racecourse" title="Doncaster Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Dome_Leisure_Centre" title="The Dome Leisure Centre">The Dome Leisure Centre</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nightlife">Nightlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Nightlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Silver Street, Cleveland Street and High Street areas have over 40 bars and clubs within a 2–3-minute walk of each other and other bars can be found on Priory Walk, Lazarus Court, Bradford Row and around the Market Place. Various restaurants serving food from around the world can also be found in the town centre, especially in the Netherhall and Copley Road areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doncaster_Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Doncaster Racecourse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Racecourse" title="Doncaster Racecourse">Doncaster Racecourse</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg/220px-Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="90" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg/330px-Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg/440px-Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2923" data-file-height="1202" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Doncaster_Racecourse_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Doncaster Racecourse</div></div></div>
<p>From around the 16th century, Doncaster embraced the wealthy <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach" title="Stagecoach">stagecoach</a> trade. This led to horse breeding in Doncaster, which in turn led to the start of <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horseraces</a> there. The earliest important race in Doncaster's history was the Doncaster Gold Cup, first run over Cantley Common in 1766. The Doncaster Cup is the oldest continuing regulated horserace in the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_football">Rugby football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Rugby football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Founded in 1951, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_R.L.F.C." title="Doncaster R.L.F.C.">Doncaster</a> (formerly known as Doncaster Dragons RLFC and Doncaster Lakers) have played consistently in rugby league's <a href="/wiki/National_League_One" class="mw-redirect" title="National League One">National League One</a> and its successor the <a href="/wiki/Championship_(rugby_league)" class="mw-redirect" title="Championship (rugby league)">RFL Championship</a>, with its home at the <a href="/wiki/Keepmoat_Stadium" title="Keepmoat Stadium">Keepmoat Stadium</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Doncaster_R.F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Doncaster R.F.C.">Doncaster Knights</a> <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Rugby Union">Rugby</a> Football Club has shown success in recent years, being the most promoted club in English Rugby Union History,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and competing at the top of <a href="/wiki/National_League_One" class="mw-redirect" title="National League One">National League One</a>. 2008 saw the completion of the 1,650-seat De Mulder–Lloyd stand at <a href="/wiki/Castle_Park_rugby_stadium" title="Castle Park rugby stadium">Castle Park</a>. The creation of the new <a href="/wiki/RFU_Championship" title="RFU Championship">RFU Championship</a> means that the Knights compete in the newly launched second tier of professional rugby in England.
</p>

<p>Recent leisure developments include a new community sports stadium for sports teams including the towns men's <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Rovers_F.C." title="Doncaster Rovers F.C.">Doncaster Rovers F.C.</a>, and one of the most successful <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_association_football" title="Women&#39;s association football">women's football</a> clubs in the country, <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Rovers_Belles_L.F.C." title="Doncaster Rovers Belles L.F.C.">Doncaster Rovers Belles</a> who play at the <a href="/wiki/Keepmoat_Stadium" title="Keepmoat Stadium">Keepmoat Stadium</a>. It is also the home of the British <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling_promotion" title="Professional wrestling promotion">professional wrestling promotion</a> <a href="/wiki/One_Pro_Wrestling" title="One Pro Wrestling">One Pro Wrestling</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Doncaster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_speedway" title="Motorcycle speedway">Speedway</a> racing was staged at Doncaster <a href="/wiki/Greyhound" title="Greyhound">Greyhound</a> Stadium in 1969 and 1970. </p><p>Doncaster has a men's basketball team called the Doncaster Danum Eagles who compete in National League Division 2. Doncaster has an American Football team called the Doncaster Mustangs, who are in Division 1 of the British American Football League.
