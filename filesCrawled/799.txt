<p><b>Neurology</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BD%CE%B5%E1%BF%A6%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:νεῦρον">νεῦρον (neûron)</a></span>, "string, nerve" and the suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:-logia">-logia</a>, "study of") is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Specialty_(medicine)" title="Specialty (medicine)">medicine</a> dealing with <a href="/wiki/Neurological_disorder" title="Neurological disorder">disorders of the nervous system</a>. Neurology deals with the diagnosis and treatment of all categories of conditions and disease involving the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous systems</a> (and their subdivisions, the <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Somatic_nervous_system" title="Somatic nervous system">somatic nervous systems</a>), including their coverings, blood vessels, and all effector tissue, such as muscle.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Neurological practice relies heavily on the field of <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, the scientific study of the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>. 
</p><p>A <b>neurologist</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a> specializing in neurology and trained to investigate, or diagnose and treat <a href="/wiki/Neurological_disorder" title="Neurological disorder">neurological disorders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WorkWYourDoc_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorkWYourDoc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Neurologists may also be involved in <a href="/wiki/Clinical_research" title="Clinical research">clinical research</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Basic_research" title="Basic research">basic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Translational_research" title="Translational research">translational research</a>. While neurology is a nonsurgical specialty, its corresponding <a href="/wiki/Surgical" class="mw-redirect" title="Surgical">surgical</a> specialty is <a href="/wiki/Neurosurgery" title="Neurosurgery">neurosurgery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WorkWYourDoc_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorkWYourDoc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Significant overlap occurs between the fields of neurology and <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>, with the boundary between the two disciplines and the conditions they treat being somewhat nebulous.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_neurological_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="List of neurological disorders">List of neurological disorders</a></div>
<p>Many neurological disorders have been described as <a href="/wiki/List_of_neurological_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="List of neurological disorders">listed</a>. These can affect the central nervous system (brain and <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>), the peripheral nervous system, the autonomic nervous system, and the <a href="/wiki/Muscular_system" title="Muscular system">muscular system</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_neurology" class="mw-redirect" title="History of neurology">History of neurology</a></div>
<p>The academic discipline began between the 16th and 19th centuries with the work and research of many neurologists such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willis" title="Thomas Willis">Thomas Willis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Whytt" title="Robert Whytt">Robert Whytt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Baillie" title="Matthew Baillie">Matthew Baillie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bell" title="Charles Bell">Charles Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moritz_Heinrich_Romberg" title="Moritz Heinrich Romberg">Moritz Heinrich Romberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duchenne_de_Boulogne" title="Duchenne de Boulogne">Duchenne de Boulogne</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_A._Hammond" title="William A. Hammond">William A. Hammond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Martin_Charcot" title="Jean-Martin Charcot">Jean-Martin Charcot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Hughlings_Jackson" title="John Hughlings Jackson">John Hughlings Jackson</a>.
</p>

He published a human brain atlas in 1894 and wrote a fundamental book on migraines in 1912.</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charcot1893.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Charcot1893.jpg/170px-Charcot1893.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="269" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Charcot1893.jpg/255px-Charcot1893.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Charcot1893.jpg/340px-Charcot1893.jpg 2x" data-file-width="957" data-file-height="1512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charcot1893.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jean-Martin_Charcot" title="Jean-Martin Charcot">Jean-Martin Charcot</a> is considered one of the fathers of neurology.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p> Many neurologists also have additional training or interest in one area of neurology, such as stroke, epilepsy, neuromuscular, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_medicine" title="Sleep medicine">sleep medicine</a>, pain management, or <a href="/wiki/Movement_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Movement disorder">movement disorders</a>.
</p><p>In the United States and Canada, <a href="/wiki/Neurologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologists">neurologists</a> are physicians having completed postgraduate training in neurology after graduation from <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">medical school</a>. Neurologists complete, on average, about 8 years of medical college education and clinical training, which includes obtaining a four-year undergraduate degree, a medical degree (<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Osteopathic_Medicine" title="Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine">DO</a> or <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">MD</a>), which comprises an additional four years of study, then completing one year of basic clinical training and four years of <a href="/wiki/Residency_(medicine)" title="Residency (medicine)">residency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The four-year residency consists of one year of <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a> internship training followed by three years of training in neurology.
</p><p>Some neurologists receive additional subspecialty training focusing on a particular area of the field.  These training programs are called <a href="/wiki/Fellowship_(medicine)" title="Fellowship (medicine)">fellowships</a>, and are one to two years in duration.  Subspecialties include brain injury medicine, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_neurophysiology" title="Clinical neurophysiology">clinical neurophysiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hospice_and_palliative_medicine" title="Hospice and palliative medicine">hospice and palliative medicine</a>, neurodevelopmental disabilities, <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_medicine" title="Neuromuscular medicine">neuromuscular medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pain_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Pain medicine">pain medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_medicine" title="Sleep medicine">sleep medicine</a>, neurocritical care, vascular neurology (stroke),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_neurology" title="Behavioral neurology">behavioral neurology</a>, child neurology, headache, multiple sclerosis, neuroimaging, neurorehabilitation.
</p><p>In Germany, a compulsory year of psychiatry must be done to complete a residency of neurology.
</p><p>In the United Kingdom and Ireland, neurology is a subspecialty of general (internal) medicine. After five years of medical school and two years as a Foundation Trainee, an aspiring neurologist must pass the examination for <a href="/wiki/Membership_of_the_Royal_College_of_Physicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Membership of the Royal College of Physicians">Membership of the Royal College of Physicians</a> (or the Irish equivalent) and complete two years of core medical training before entering specialist training in neurology. Up to the 1960s, some intending to become neurologists would also spend two years working in psychiatric units before obtaining a diploma in psychological medicine.  However, that was uncommon and, now that the <a href="/wiki/MRCPsych" title="MRCPsych">MRCPsych</a> takes three years to obtain, would no longer be practical. A period of research is essential, and obtaining a higher degree aids career progression. Many found it was eased after an attachment to the Institute of Neurology at <a href="/wiki/Queen_Square,_London" title="Queen Square, London">Queen Square</a>, London. Some neurologists enter the field of rehabilitation medicine (known as <a href="/wiki/Physiatry" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiatry">physiatry</a> in the US) to specialise in neurological rehabilitation, which may include stroke medicine, as well as brain injuries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_examination">Physical examination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical examination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neurological_examination" title="Neurological examination">Neurological examination</a></div>
<p>During a <a href="/wiki/Neurological_examination" title="Neurological examination">neurological examination</a>, the neurologist reviews the patient's health history with special attention to the current condition. The patient then takes a neurological exam. Typically, the exam tests mental status, function of the <a href="/wiki/Cranial_nerves" title="Cranial nerves">cranial nerves</a> (including vision), strength, coordination, reflexes, and sensation. This information helps the neurologist determine whether the problem exists in the nervous system and the clinical localization. Localization of the pathology is the key process by which neurologists develop their differential diagnosis. Further tests may be needed to confirm a diagnosis and ultimately guide therapy and appropriate management.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_tasks">Clinical tasks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clinical tasks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Neurologists examine patients who are referred to them by other physicians in both the <a href="/wiki/Inpatient" class="mw-redirect" title="Inpatient">inpatient</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outpatient" class="mw-redirect" title="Outpatient">outpatient</a> settings. Neurologists begin their interactions with patients by taking a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Medical_history" title="Medical history">medical history</a>, and then performing a <a href="/wiki/Physical_examination" title="Physical examination">physical examination</a> focusing on evaluating the nervous system. Components of the <a href="/wiki/Neurological_examination" title="Neurological examination">neurological examination</a> include assessment of the patient's <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive function">cognitive function</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cranial_nerves" title="Cranial nerves">cranial nerves</a>, motor strength, <a href="/wiki/Sensory_nervous_system" title="Sensory nervous system">sensation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reflexes" class="mw-redirect" title="Reflexes">reflexes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_coordination" title="Motor coordination">coordination</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gait" title="Gait">gait</a>.
</p><p>In some instances, neurologists may order additional <a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_tests" class="mw-redirect" title="Diagnostic tests">diagnostic tests</a> as part of the evaluation. Commonly employed tests in neurology include imaging studies such as <a href="/wiki/Computed_axial_tomography" class="mw-redirect" title="Computed axial tomography">computed axial tomography</a> (CAT) scans, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI), and <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a> of major blood vessels of the head and neck. Neurophysiologic studies, including <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">electroencephalography</a> (EEG), needle <a href="/wiki/Electromyography" title="Electromyography">electromyography</a> (EMG), <a href="/wiki/Nerve_conduction_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Nerve conduction studies">nerve conduction studies</a> (NCSs) and <a href="/wiki/Evoked_potentials" class="mw-redirect" title="Evoked potentials">evoked potentials</a> are also commonly ordered. Neurologists frequently perform <a href="/wiki/Lumbar_puncture" title="Lumbar puncture">lumbar punctures</a> to assess characteristics of a patient's <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a>.  Advances in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_testing" title="Genetic testing">genetic testing</a> have made genetic testing an important tool in the classification of inherited neuromuscular disease and diagnosis of many other neurogenetic diseases.  The role of genetic influences on the development of acquired neurologic diseases is an active area of research.
</p><p>Some of the commonly encountered conditions treated by neurologists include headaches, <a href="/wiki/Radiculopathy" title="Radiculopathy">radiculopathy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuropathy" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuropathy">neuropathy</a>, stroke, <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seizure" class="mw-redirect" title="Seizure">seizures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit/hyperactivity_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder">attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson&#39;s disease">Parkinson's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourette%27s_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Tourette&#39;s syndrome">Tourette's syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Head_trauma" class="mw-redirect" title="Head trauma">head trauma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_disorder" title="Sleep disorder">sleep disorders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_disease" title="Neuromuscular disease">neuromuscular diseases</a>, and various infections and tumors of the nervous system.  Neurologists are also asked to evaluate unresponsive patients on <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a> to confirm <a href="/wiki/Brain_death" title="Brain death">brain death</a>.
</p><p>Treatment options vary depending on the neurological problem. They can include referring the patient to a <a href="/wiki/Physiotherapist" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiotherapist">physiotherapist</a>, prescribing medications, or recommending a surgical procedure.
</p><p>Some neurologists specialize in certain parts of the nervous system or in specific procedures. For example, clinical neurophysiologists specialize in the use of EEG and <a href="/wiki/Intraoperative_neurophysiological_monitoring" title="Intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring">intraoperative monitoring</a> to diagnose certain neurological disorders.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Other neurologists specialize in the use of <a href="/wiki/Electrodiagnostic_medicine" title="Electrodiagnostic medicine">electrodiagnostic medicine</a> studies – needle EMG and NCSs.  In the US, physicians do not typically specialize in all the aspects of clinical neurophysiology – i.e. sleep, EEG, EMG, and NCSs.  The American Board of Clinical Neurophysiology certifies US physicians in general clinical neurophysiology, epilepsy, and intraoperative monitoring.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The American Board of Electrodiagnostic Medicine certifies US physicians in <a href="/wiki/Electrodiagnostic_medicine" title="Electrodiagnostic medicine">electrodiagnostic medicine</a> and certifies technologists in nerve-conduction studies.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Sleep medicine is a subspecialty field in the US under several medical specialties including <a href="/wiki/Anesthesiology" title="Anesthesiology">anesthesiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Family_medicine" title="Family medicine">family medicine</a>, and neurology.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Neurosurgery is a distinct specialty that involves a different training path, and emphasizes the surgical treatment of neurological disorders.
</p><p>Also, many nonmedical doctors, those with doctoral degrees(usually PhDs) in subjects such as biology and chemistry, study and research the nervous system. Working in laboratories in universities, hospitals, and private companies, these neuroscientists perform clinical and laboratory experiments and tests to learn more about the nervous system and find cures or new treatments for diseases and disorders.
</p><p>A great deal of overlap occurs between <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a> and neurology. Many neurologists work in academic training hospitals, where they conduct research as neuroscientists in addition to treating patients and teaching neurology to <a href="/wiki/Medical_student" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical student">medical students</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_caseload">General caseload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: General caseload">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neurologists are responsible for the diagnosis, treatment, and management of all the conditions mentioned above. When surgical or endovascular intervention is required, the neurologist may refer the patient to a <a href="/wiki/Neurosurgeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurosurgeon">neurosurgeon</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Interventional_neuroradiology" title="Interventional neuroradiology">interventional neuroradiologist</a>. In some countries, additional legal responsibilities of a neurologist may include making a finding of brain death when it is suspected that a patient has died. Neurologists frequently care for people with hereditary (<a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a>) diseases when the major manifestations are neurological, as is frequently the case. <a href="/wiki/Lumbar_puncture" title="Lumbar puncture">Lumbar punctures</a> are frequently performed by <a href="/wiki/Neurologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologists">neurologists</a>. Some neurologists may develop an interest in particular subfields, such as stroke, <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Movement_disorders" title="Movement disorders">movement disorders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurointensive_care" title="Neurointensive care">neurointensive care</a>, headaches, <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleep disorders">sleep disorders</a>, chronic <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> management, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuromuscular">neuromuscular</a> diseases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overlapping_areas">Overlapping areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overlapping areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some overlap also occurs with other specialties, varying from country to country and even within a local geographic area. Acute <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">head trauma</a> is most often treated by neurosurgeons, whereas <a href="/wiki/Sequelae" class="mw-redirect" title="Sequelae">sequelae</a> of head trauma may be treated by neurologists or <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">specialists in rehabilitation medicine</a>. Although stroke cases have been traditionally managed by internal medicine or hospitalists, the emergence of vascular neurology and <a href="/wiki/Interventional_neuroradiology" title="Interventional neuroradiology">interventional neuroradiology</a> has created a demand for stroke specialists. The establishment of <a href="/wiki/Joint_Commission" title="Joint Commission">Joint Commission</a>-certified stroke centers has increased the role of neurologists in stroke care in many primary, as well as tertiary, hospitals. Some cases of nervous system <a href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> are treated by infectious disease specialists. Most cases of headache are diagnosed and treated primarily by <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioners</a>, at least the less severe cases. Likewise, most cases of <a href="/wiki/Sciatica" title="Sciatica">sciatica</a> are treated by general practitioners, though they may be referred to neurologists or  surgeons (neurosurgeons or <a href="/wiki/Orthopedic_surgery" title="Orthopedic surgery">orthopedic surgeons</a>). <a href="/wiki/Sleep_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleep disorders">Sleep disorders</a> are also treated by <a href="/wiki/Pulmonology" title="Pulmonology">pulmonologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrists" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatrists">psychiatrists</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">Cerebral palsy</a> is initially treated by <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">pediatricians</a>, but care may be transferred to an adult neurologist after the patient reaches a certain age. <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">Physical medicine and rehabilitation</a> physicians also in the US diagnosis and treat patients with neuromuscular diseases through the use of electrodiagnostic studies (needle EMG and nerve-conduction studies) and other diagnostic tools.  In the United Kingdom and other countries, many of the conditions encountered by older patients such as movement disorders, including Parkinson's disease, stroke, dementia, or gait disorders, are managed predominantly by specialists in <a href="/wiki/Geriatric_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Geriatric medicine">geriatric medicine</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Clinical_neuropsychology" title="Clinical neuropsychology">Clinical neuropsychologists</a> are often called upon to <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychological_assessment" title="Neuropsychological assessment">evaluate</a> brain-<a href="/wiki/Human_behavior" title="Human behavior">behavior</a> relationships for the purpose of assisting with <a href="/wiki/Differential_diagnosis" title="Differential diagnosis">differential diagnosis</a>, planning <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">rehabilitation</a> strategies, documenting <a href="/wiki/Cognitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> strengths and weaknesses, and measuring change over time (e.g., for identifying abnormal <a href="/wiki/Aging" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging">aging</a> or tracking the progression of a <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_clinical_neurophysiology">Relationship to clinical neurophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relationship to clinical neurophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some countries, e.g. US and Germany, <a href="/wiki/Neurologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologists">neurologists</a> may subspecialize in <a href="/wiki/Clinical_neurophysiology" title="Clinical neurophysiology">clinical neurophysiology</a>, the field responsible for EEG and <a href="/wiki/Intraoperative_neurophysiological_monitoring" title="Intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring">intraoperative monitoring</a>, or in electrodiagnostic medicine <a href="/wiki/Nerve_conduction_study" title="Nerve conduction study">nerve conduction studies</a>, EMG, and <a href="/wiki/Evoked_potentials" class="mw-redirect" title="Evoked potentials">evoked potentials</a>. In other countries, this is an autonomous <a href="/wiki/Specialty_(medicine)" title="Specialty (medicine)">specialty</a> (e.g., United Kingdom, Sweden, Spain).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overlap_with_psychiatry">Overlap with psychiatry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Overlap with psychiatry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Psychoneuroimmunology" title="Psychoneuroimmunology">Psychoneuroimmunology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychiatry" title="Neuropsychiatry">Neuropsychiatry</a></div>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">mental illnesses</a> are believed by many to be neurological disorders affecting the central nervous system, traditionally they are classified separately, and treated by <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrists" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatrists">psychiatrists</a>. In a 2002 review article in the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Journal_of_Psychiatry" class="mw-redirect" title="American Journal of Psychiatry">American Journal of Psychiatry</a></i>, Professor Joseph B. Martin, Dean of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a> and a neurologist by training, wrote, "the separation of the two categories is arbitrary, often influenced by beliefs rather than proven scientific observations. And the fact that the brain and mind are one makes the separation artificial anyway".<sup id="cite_ref-pmid11986119_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11986119-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Neurological_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurological disorders">Neurological disorders</a> often have <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatric">psychiatric</a> manifestations, such as poststroke depression, depression and <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson&#39;s disease">Parkinson's disease</a>, mood and cognitive dysfunctions in Alzheimer's disease, and <a href="/wiki/Huntington_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Huntington disease">Huntington disease</a>, to name a few. Hence, the sharp distinction between neurology and psychiatry is not always on a biological basis. The dominance of <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalytic theory</a> in the first three-quarters of the 20th century has since then been largely replaced by a focus on pharmacology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="many evidence based psychotherapies still in use (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Despite the shift to a medical <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_model" title="Conceptual model">model</a>, brain science has not advanced to the point where scientists or <a href="/wiki/Clinicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinicians">clinicians</a> can point to readily discernible pathologic lesions or genetic abnormalities that in and of themselves serve as reliable or predictive <a href="/wiki/Biomarkers" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomarkers">biomarkers</a> of a given mental disorder.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurological_enhancement">Neurological enhancement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neurology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Neurological enhancement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The emerging field of neurological enhancement highlights the potential of therapies to improve such things as workplace efficacy, attention in school, and overall happiness in personal lives.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, this field has also given rise to questions about <a href="/wiki/Neuroethics" title="Neuroethics">neuroethics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Psychopharmacology" title="Psychopharmacology">psychopharmacology</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="Lifestyle drugs">lifestyle drugs</a>.
</p>
