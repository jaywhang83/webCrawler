<p>A <b> code name</b> or <b>cryptonym</b> is a word or name used, sometimes clandestinely, to refer to another name, word, project, or person. Names are often used for <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> purposes, or in <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>. They may also be used in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_espionage" title="Industrial espionage">industrial counter-espionage</a> to protect secret projects and the like from business rivals, or to give names to projects whose marketing name has not yet been determined. Another reason for the use of names and phrases in the military is that they transmit with a lower level of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_whispers" title="Chinese whispers">cumulative errors</a> over a <a href="/wiki/Walkie-talkie" title="Walkie-talkie">walkie-talkie</a> or radio link than actual names.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_origins">Military origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Military origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">World War I</a>, names common to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allies</a> referring to nations, cities, geographical features, military units, military operations, diplomatic meetings, places, and individual persons were agreed upon, adapting pre-war naming procedures in use by the governments concerned. In the British case names were administered and controlled by the Inter Services Security Board (ISSB) staffed by the <a href="/wiki/War_Office" title="War Office">War Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This procedure was coordinated with the United States when <a href="/wiki/American_entry_into_World_War_I" title="American entry into World War I">America entered</a> the war. Random lists of names were issued to users in alphabetical blocks of ten words and were selected as required.  Words became available for re-use after six months and unused allocations could be reassigned at discretion and according to need. Judicious selection from the available allocation could result in clever meanings and result in an <a href="/wiki/Aptronym" title="Aptronym">aptronym</a> or <a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a>, although policy was to select words that had no obviously deducible connection with what they were supposed to be concealing. Those for the major <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_War_II_conferences" class="mw-redirect" title="List of World War II conferences">conference meetings</a> had a partial naming sequence referring to devices or instruments which had an <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_number" title="Ordinal number">ordinal number</a> as part of their meaning, e.g., the third meeting was "TRIDENT". <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, whose last name means "man of steel", was given the name "GLYPTIC", meaning "an image carved out of stone".
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_code_names">German code names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: German code names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ewen_Montagu" title="Ewen Montagu">Ewen Montagu</a>, a British Naval intelligence officer, discloses in <i>Beyond Top Secret Ultra</i> that during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> habitually used <i>ad hoc</i> code names as <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a> which often openly revealed or strongly hinted at their content or function.
</p><p>Some German code names:
</p>
<ul><li>Golfplatz (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for "golf course") – Britain, employed by the <a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a></li>
<li>Samland – The United States (from <a href="/wiki/Uncle_Sam" title="Uncle Sam">Uncle Sam</a>), employed by the <a href="/wiki/Abwehr" title="Abwehr">Abwehr</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heimdall" class="mw-redirect" title="Heimdall">Heimdall</a> (a god whose power was "to see for a hundred miles") – long-range radar</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wotan_(deity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wotan (deity)">Wotan</a> – an <a href="/wiki/Y-Ger%C3%A4t" class="mw-redirect" title="Y-Gerät">aerial bombing navigation system</a>. Knowing that the god Wotan had only one eye, <a href="/wiki/R._V._Jones" class="mw-redirect" title="R. V. Jones">R. V. Jones</a>, a British scientist working for <a href="/wiki/RAF_Intelligence" title="RAF Intelligence">Air Intelligence</a> of the British <a href="/wiki/Air_Ministry" title="Air Ministry">Air Ministry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secret_Intelligence_Service" title="Secret Intelligence Service">SIS</a> inferred that the device used a single beam and from that determined, correctly, how it must work. A counter-system was quickly created which made Wotan useless.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Operation_Seel%C3%B6we" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Seelöwe">Operation Seelöwe</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sea-lion" title="Sea-lion">Sea-lion</a>) – plans to invade Britain (lions being prominent in the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Coat of arms of the United Kingdom">coat of arms of the United Kingdom</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Operation Barbarossa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Frederick_Barbarossa" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick Barbarossa">Frederick Barbarossa</a>) – plans to go east and invade the Soviet Union</li></ul>
<p>Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Wacht_am_Rhein" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Wacht am Rhein">Operation Wacht am Rhein</a> (Watch on the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>) was deliberately named to suggest the opposite of its purpose&#160;&#8211;&#32; a defensive "watch" as opposed to a massive <a href="/wiki/Blitzkrieg" title="Blitzkrieg">blitzkrieg</a> operation, just as was <a href="/wiki/Operation_Weser%C3%BCbung" title="Operation Weserübung">Operation Weserübung</a> (<a href="/wiki/Weser" title="Weser">Weser</a>-exercise), which signified the plans to invade <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> in April 1940.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Code_names_of_other_powers">Code names of other powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Code names of other powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Britain and the United States developed the security policy of assigning code names intended to give no such clues to the uninitiated. For example, the British counter measures against the <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a> was called <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Crossbow</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">atomic bomb</a> project centered in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> was called the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Engineer_District" class="mw-redirect" title="Manhattan Engineer District">Manhattan Engineer District</a> which managed the program. The code name for the American <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_A-12" title="Lockheed A-12">A-12</a> / <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">SR-71</a> spy plane project, producing the fastest, highest-flying aircraft in the world, was <i>Oxcart</i>. The American group that planned that country's first <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBM</a> was called the <a href="/wiki/Teapot_Committee" title="Teapot Committee">Teapot Committee</a>.
</p><p>Although the word could stand for a menace to shipping (in this case, that of Japan), the American code name for the attack on the subtropical island of <a href="/wiki/Okinawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a> in World War II was <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Okinawa" title="Battle of Okinawa">Operation Iceberg</a></i>. The Soviet Union's project to base missiles in Cuba was named <i><a href="/wiki/Operation_Anadyr" title="Operation Anadyr">Operation Anadyr</a></i> after their closest bomber base to the US (just across the Bering Strait from Nome, Alaska). The names of colors are generally avoided in American practice to avoid confusion with meteorological reporting practices. Britain, in contrast, made deliberately non-meaningful use of them, through the system of <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow codes">rainbow codes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_recognition_reporting_names">Aircraft recognition reporting names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aircraft recognition reporting names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/World_War_II_Allied_names_for_Japanese_aircraft" title="World War II Allied names for Japanese aircraft">World War II Allied names for Japanese aircraft</a></div>
<p>Although German and Italian aircraft were not given code names by their Allied opponents, in 1942, Captain Frank T. McCoy, an intelligence officer of the <a href="/wiki/USAAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAAF">USAAF</a>, invented a system for the identification of Japanese military aircraft. Initially using short, "<a href="/wiki/Hillbilly" title="Hillbilly">hillbilly</a>" boys' names such as "<a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_F1M" title="Mitsubishi F1M">Pete</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Aichi_E13A" title="Aichi E13A">Jake</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Nakajima_A6M2-N" title="Nakajima A6M2-N">Rufe</a>", the system was later extended to include girls' names and names of trees and birds, and became widely used by the Allies throughout the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean_theater_of_World_War_II" title="Pacific Ocean theater of World War II">Pacific theater</a> of war. This type of naming scheme differs from the other use of code names in that it does not have to be kept secret, but is a means of identification where the official nomenclature is unknown or uncertain.
</p><p>The policy of recognition reporting names was continued into the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> for Soviet, other <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a>, and Communist Chinese aircraft. Although this was started by the Air Standards Co-ordinating Committee (ASCC) formed by the United States, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, it was extended throughout <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> as the <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> for aircraft, rockets and missiles. These names were considered by the Soviets as being like a nickname given to one's unit by the opponents in a battle, such as the U.S. Marines were called by the Germans in France "Devil Dogs"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, which they appreciated as a feather in their cap. The Soviets did not like the <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-25" title="Sukhoi Su-25">Sukhoi Su-25</a> getting the code name "<a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-25" title="Sukhoi Su-25">Frogfoot</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, some names were appropriate, such as "Condor" for the <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonov An-124">Antonov An-124</a>, or, most famously, "Fulcrum" for the <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan_MiG-29" title="Mikoyan MiG-29">Mikoyan MiG-29</a>, which had a "pivotal" role in Soviet air-strategy.
</p><p>Code names were adopted by the following process. Aerial or space reconnaissance would note a new aircraft at a <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> airbase. The intelligence units would then assign it a code name consisting of the official abbreviation of the base, then a letter, for example, "Ram-A", signifying an aircraft sighted at <a href="/wiki/Ramenskoye_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramenskoye Airport">Ramenskoye Airport</a>. Missiles were given designations like "TT-5", for the fifth rocket seen at <a href="/wiki/Baikonur" title="Baikonur">Tyura-Tam</a>. When more information resulted in knowing a bit about what a missile was used for, it would be given a designation like "SS-6", for the sixth surface-to-surface missile design reported. Finally, when either an aircraft or a missile was able to be photographed with a hand-held camera, instead of a reconnaissance aircraft, it was given a name like "<a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-27" title="Sukhoi Su-27">Flanker</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Scud" title="Scud">Scud</a>"&#160;&#8211;&#32; always an <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> word, as international pilots worldwide are required to learn English. The Soviet manufacturer or designation – which may be mistakenly inferred by NATO – has nothing to do with it.
</p><p>Jet-powered aircraft received two-syllable names like <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-25" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-25">Foxbat</a>, while propeller aircraft were designated with short names like <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-4" title="Tupolev Tu-4">Bull</a>. Fighter names began with an "F", bombers with a "B", cargo aircraft with a "C". Training aircraft and reconnaissance aircraft were grouped under the word "miscellaneous", and received "M". The same convention applies to missiles, with air-launched ground attack missiles beginning with the letter "K" and surface-to-surface missiles (ranging from <a href="/wiki/ICBM" class="mw-redirect" title="ICBM">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a> to <a href="/wiki/Antitank" class="mw-redirect" title="Antitank">antitank</a> rockets) with the letter "S", air-to-air missiles "A", and surface-to-air missiles "G".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_operations_since_Churchill">Military operations since Churchill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military operations since Churchill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the Second World War, the British allocation practice favored one-word code names (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Jubilee" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Jubilee">Jubilee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Frankton" title="Operation Frankton">Frankton</a>). That of the Americans favored longer compound words, although the name <a href="/wiki/Operation_Overlord" title="Operation Overlord">Overlord</a> was personally chosen by <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> himself. Many examples of both types can be cited, as can exceptions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> was particular about the quality of code names. He insisted that code words, especially for dangerous operations, would be not overly grand nor petty nor common. One emotional goal he mentions is to never have to report to anyone that their son "was killed in an operation called 'Bunnyhug' or 'Ballyhoo'."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Presently, British forces tend to use one-word names, presumably in keeping with their post-World War II policy of reserving single words for operations and two-word names for exercises. British operation code names are usually randomly generated by a computer and rarely reveal its components or any political implications unlike the American names (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> was called "Operation Telic" compared to Americans' "Operation Iraqi Freedom", obviously chosen for propaganda rather than secrecy). Americans prefer two-word names, whereas the Canadians and Australians use either. The French military currently prefer names drawn from nature (such as colors or the names of animals), for instance <i><a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ration_Daguet" title="Opération Daguet">Opération Daguet</a></i> ("brocket deer") or <i><a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ration_Baliste" title="Opération Baliste">Opération Baliste</a></i> ("Triggerfish"). The CIA uses alphabetical prefixes to designate the part of the agency supporting an operation.
</p><p>In many cases with the United States, the first word of the name has to do with the intent of the program. Programs with "have" as the first word, such as <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Have_Blue" title="Lockheed Have Blue">Have Blue</a> for the stealth fighter development, are developmental programs, not meant to produce a production aircraft. Programs that start with Senior, such as Senior Trend for the F-117, are for aircraft in testing meant to enter production.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the United States code names are commonly set entirely in upper case. This is not done in other countries, though for the UK in British documents the code name is in upper case while operation is shortened to OP e.g., "Op. TELIC".
</p><p>This presents an opportunity for a bit of public-relations (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Just_Cause" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Just Cause">Operation Just Cause</a>), or for controversy over the naming choice (Operation Infinite Justice, renamed <a href="/wiki/Operation_Enduring_Freedom" title="Operation Enduring Freedom">Operation Enduring Freedom</a>). Computers are now used to aid in the selection. And further, there is a distinction between the <i>secret</i> names during former wars and the <i>published</i> names of recent ones.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_code_name">Project code name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Code_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Project code name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b>project code name</b> is a code name (usually a single word, short phrase or <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>) which is given to a <a href="/wiki/Project" title="Project">project</a> being developed by <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">academia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, and other concerns.
</p><p>Project code names are typically used for several reasons:
</p>
<ul><li>To uniquely identify the project within the organization. Code names are frequently chosen to be outside the normal business/domain jargon that the organization uses, in order to not conflict with established terminology.</li>
<li>To assist with maintaining <a href="/wiki/Secrecy" title="Secrecy">secrecy</a> of the project against rival concerns. Some corporations routinely change project names in order to further confuse competitors.</li>
<li>When the goal of the project is to develop one or more commercial <i>products</i>, use of a code name allows the eventual choice of product nomenclature (the name the product(s) are marketed and sold under) to be decoupled from the development effort. This is especially important when one project generates multiple products, or multiple projects are needed to produce a single product. This allows for <i>subprojects</i> to be given a separate identity from the main project.</li>
<li>To decouple an early phase of a development effort (which may have failed) from a subsequent phase (which may be given a "fresh start") as a political tool.</li>
<li>To prevent casual observers from concluding that a pre-release version is a new release of the product, thus helping reduce confusion.</li></ul>
<p>Different organizations have different policies regarding the use and publication of project code names. Some companies take great pains to <i>never</i> discuss or disclose project code names outside of the company (other than with outside entities who have a need to know, and typically are bound with a <a href="/wiki/Non-disclosure_agreement" title="Non-disclosure agreement">non-disclosure agreement</a>). Other companies never use them in official or formal communications, but widely disseminate project code names through informal channels (often in an attempt to create a <a href="/wiki/Marketing_buzz" title="Marketing buzz">marketing buzz</a> for the project). Still others (such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>) discuss code names publicly, and routinely use project code names on beta releases and such, but remove them from final product(s). At the other end of the spectrum, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a> includes the project code names for <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> as part of the official name of the final product, a practice that was started in 2002 with <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X_v10.2" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X v10.2">Mac OS X v10.2</a> "Jaguar".
</p>

See <a href="/wiki/List_of_Intel_codenames" title="List of Intel codenames">List of Intel codenames</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> often names projects (in particular, versions of the <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> operating systems) after place names. Examples include <b>Chicago</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a>), <b>Daytona</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_NT_3.5" title="Windows NT 3.5">Windows NT 3.5</a>), <b>Memphis</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_98" title="Windows 98">Windows 98</a>), <b>Whistler</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_XP" title="Windows XP">Windows XP</a>), <b>Longhorn</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_Vista" title="Windows Vista">Windows Vista</a>), <b>Blackcomb/Vienna</b> (<a href="/wiki/Windows_7" title="Windows 7">Windows 7</a>), and <b>Cairo</b> <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT_4.0" title="Windows NT 4.0">Windows NT 4.0</a>.</li>
<li>For a period of time, <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> used code names which are mostly named after <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> to reference different versions of the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a> browser:
<ul><li>Firefox 2.0: <b><a href="/wiki/Bon_Echo_Provincial_Park" title="Bon Echo Provincial Park">Bon Echo</a></b></li>
<li>Firefox 3.0: <b><a href="/wiki/Gran_Paradiso" title="Gran Paradiso">Gran Paradiso</a></b></li>
<li>Firefox 3.5: <b><a href="/wiki/Shiretoko_National_Park" title="Shiretoko National Park">Shiretoko</a></b></li>
<li>Firefox 3.6: <b><a href="/wiki/Tsingy_de_Namoroka_Strict_Nature_Reserve" title="Tsingy de Namoroka Strict Nature Reserve">Namoroka</a></b></li>
<li>Firefox 4.0: <b><a href="/wiki/Tumucumaque_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Tumucumaque National Park">Tumucumaque</a></b></li>
<li>Firefox pre-beta: <b>Aurora</b></li>
<li>Firefox trunk builds: <b>Nightly</b></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> often uses code names for new consoles. The best-known is that of <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a>, which was code-named <b>Revolution</b> for over a year. Others include the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" class="mw-redirect" title="Nintendo GameCube">GameCube</a>'s code name of <b>Dolphin</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Advance" title="Game Boy Advance">Game Boy Advance</a>'s code name of <b><a href="/wiki/Atlantis" title="Atlantis">Atlantis</a></b>, the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a> as <b>Project Reality</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_DS" title="Nintendo DS">DS</a> code name <b>Project Nitro</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Micro" title="Game Boy Micro">Game Boy Micro</a> code name <b>Oxygen</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Wii_U" title="Wii U">Wii U</a> code name <b>Project Cafe</b>, and Nintendo's latest console <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Switch" title="Nintendo Switch">Nintendo Switch</a> as <b>NX.</b></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Jedi" title="Return of the Jedi">Return of the Jedi</a></i> was code-named "Blue Harvest" while in production and principal photography. This was reportedly to prevent disruption by fans and the media as well as to avoid <a href="/wiki/Price_gouging" title="Price gouging">price gouging</a> by local merchants and vendors.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Chamber_of_Secrets_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets (film)">The Chamber of Secrets</a></i> sequel of the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_(film_series)" title="Harry Potter (film series)"><i>Harry Potter</i> film series</a> was code-named "Incident of 57th Street" to disguise the production from its increasingly rabid fanbase, who would seek out filming locations and disrupt production.</li></ul>
