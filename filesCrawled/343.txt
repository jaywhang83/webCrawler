<p>A <b>title</b> is one or more words used before or after a person's name, in certain contexts. It may signify either veneration, an official position, or a professional or academic qualification. In some languages, titles may be inserted between the first and last name (for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Graf</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">Cardinal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Catholic_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic church">Catholic</a> usage (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Cushing#Legacy" title="Richard Cushing">Richard Cardinal Cushing</a>) or clerical titles such as <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a>). Some titles are <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_title" title="Hereditary title">hereditary</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Titles include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">Honorific titles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Style_(manner_of_address)" title="Style (manner of address)">styles of address</a>, a phrase used to convey respect to the recipient of a communication, or to recognize an attribute such as:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks" title="Imperial, royal and noble ranks">Imperial, royal and noble ranks</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">Academic degree</a></li>
<li>Other accomplishment, as with a <a href="/wiki/Title_of_honor" title="Title of honor">title of honor</a></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Title_of_authority" title="Title of authority">Title of authority</a>, an identifier that specifies the office or position held by an <a href="/wiki/Official" title="Official">official</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_in_English-speaking_areas">Titles in English-speaking areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Titles in English-speaking areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following titles are the default titles:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mr." title="Mr.">Mr.</a> – Adult man (regardless of marital status)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs.</a> – Adult woman (usually just for married women, widows, and divorcées)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mx." class="mw-redirect" title="Mx.">Mx.</a> – Gender neutral (regardless of marital status)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms.</a> – Adult woman (regardless of marital status)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a> – Formal title for unmarried women and for female children</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Master_(form_of_address)" title="Master (form of address)">Master</a> – For male children: Young boys were formerly addressed as "Master [first name]." This was the standard form for servants to use in addressing their employer's minor sons.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maid_(title)" title="Maid (title)">Maid</a> – Archaic: When used as a title before a name (and not as a general term for a young <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">domestic worker housemaid girl</a>), this was a way to denote an unmarried woman, such as the character <a href="/wiki/Maid_Marian" title="Maid Marian">Maid Marian</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Madam" title="Madam">Madam</a> (also madame) –Adult woman</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aunt" title="Aunt">Aunt</a>, Auntie, or <a href="/wiki/Uncle" title="Uncle">Uncle</a> may be used as titles by nieces and nephews, or by children to adults whom they know.
</p><p>Other titles are used for various reasons, such as to show aristocratic status or one's role in government, in a religious organization, or in a branch of the military.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aristocratic_titles">Aristocratic titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aristocratic titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Royal_and_noble_ranks" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal and noble ranks">Royal and noble ranks</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">Prince</a>/<a href="/wiki/Princess" title="Princess">Princess</a> – From the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Princeps" title="Princeps">princeps</a></i>, meaning "first person" or "first citizen." The title was originally used by <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> at the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> to avoid the political risk of assuming the title <i>Rex</i> ("King") in what was technically still a republic.  In modern times, the title is often given to the sons and daughters of ruling monarchs. Also a title of certain ruling monarchs under the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and its subsidiary territories until 1918 (still survives in <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>, and also in Monaco although that is elsewhere), and in <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a> before 1917. The German title is <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrst" title="Fürst">Fürst</a> ("first"), a translation of the Latin term;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;A&#93;</a></sup> the equivalent Russian term is <i>князь (knyaz)</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Archduke" title="Archduke">Archduke</a>/<a href="/wiki/Archduchess" class="mw-redirect" title="Archduchess">Archduchess</a> – A title derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Archon" title="Archon">Archon</a></i> ("ruler; higher") and the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Dux" title="Dux">Dux</a></i>("leader").  It was used most notably by the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Dynasty">Habsburg Dynasty</a> that ruled <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> until 1918.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke">Grand Duke</a>/<a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchess" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duchess">Grand Duchess</a> - "Big; large" + Latin <i>Dux</i> (leader).  A variant of "Archduke," used particularly in English translations <a href="/wiki/Romanov_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanov Dynasty">Romanov Dynasty</a> Russian titles. Also used in various Germanic territories until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Still survives in <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a>/<a href="/wiki/Duchess" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchess">Duchess</a> - From the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Dux" title="Dux">Dux</a></i>, a military title used in  the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, especially in its early <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine period</a> when it designated the military commander for a specific zone.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marquis" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquis">Marquis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">Marquess</a> (the feminine equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Marquise" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquise">Marquise</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marchioness" class="mw-redirect" title="Marchioness">Marchioness</a>) from the French <i>marchis</i>, literally "ruler of a border area," (from Old French <i>marche</i> meaning "border"); exact English translation is "March Lord," or "Lord of the March."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Count</a>/<a href="/wiki/Countess" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess">Countess</a> - From the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Comes" title="Comes">comes</a></i> meaning "companion."  The word was used by the Roman Empire in its Byzantine period as an honorific with a meaning roughly equivalent to modern English "peer."  It became the title of those who commanded field armies in the Empire, as opposed to "Dux" which commanded locally based forces.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">Earl</a> (used in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Count</a>, but the feminine equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Countess" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess">Countess</a>)  From the Germanic <i>jarl</i>, meaning "chieftain," the title was brought to the British Isles by the Anglo-Saxons and survives in use only there, having been superseded in Scandinavia and on the European continent.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">Viscount</a>/<a href="/wiki/Viscountess" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscountess">Viscountess</a> - From the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Vicarius" title="Vicarius">vicarius</a></i> (Deputy; substitute.  Hence "vicar" and prefix "vice-") appended to Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Comes" title="Comes">comes</a></i>.  Literally: "Deputy Count".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a>/<a href="/wiki/Baroness" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroness">Baroness</a> - From the <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>Baro</i>, meaning "man, servant, soldier" the title originally designated the chief feudal tenant of a place, who was in vassalage to a greater lord.</li></ul>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, "Lord" and "Lady" are <a href="/wiki/Forms_of_address_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Forms of address in the United Kingdom">used as titles</a> for members of the nobility. Unlike titles such as "Mr" and "Mrs", they are not used before first names except in certain circumstances, for example as courtesy titles for younger sons, etc., of peers. In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> "<a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Parliament" title="Lord of Parliament">Lord of Parliament</a>" and "Lady of Parliament" are the equivalents of Baron and Baroness in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">Lord</a> from Old English <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hl%C4%81ford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hlāford (page does not exist)">hlāford</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hl%C4%81fweard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hlāfweard (page does not exist)">hlāfweard</a></i>, meaning, literally, “bread-keeper," from hlāf (“bread”) + weard (“guardian, keeper”) and by extension husband, father, or chief.  (From which comes modified titles such as  <a href="/wiki/First_Sea_Lord" title="First Sea Lord">First Sea Lord</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Manor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of the Manor">Lord of the Manor</a>.)  The feminine equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Lady" title="Lady">Lady</a> from the related Old English <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hl%C7%A3fd%C4%AB%C4%A1e&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hlǣfdīġe (page does not exist)">hlǣfdīġe</a></i> meaning, literally, “bread-kneader”, from hlāf (“bread”) + dīġe (“maid”), and by extension wife, daughter, or mistress of the house.  (From which comes <a href="/wiki/First_Lady" title="First Lady">First Lady</a>, the anachronistic <a href="/wiki/Second_Lady" title="Second Lady">Second Lady</a>, etc.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a>/<a href="/wiki/Empress" class="mw-redirect" title="Empress">Empress</a> – From the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Imperator" title="Imperator">Imperator</a></i>, meaning he/she who holds the authority to command (<i>imperium</i>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">King</a>/<a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">Queen</a> – Derived from <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>/Germanic words.  The original meaning of the root of "king" apparently meant "leader of the family" or "descendant of the leader of the family," and the original meaning of "queen," "wife."  By the time the words came into English they already meant "ruler."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a>/<a href="/wiki/Tsarina" title="Tsarina">Tsarina</a> (Tsaritsa) – Slavonic loan-word from Latin.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caesar" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar">Caesar</a><i>: the name of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> taken by his heir <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> and thereafter by Augustus' successors as <a href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">fall of Constantinople</a> in 1453.  Germanic loan-word for Caesar is <a href="/wiki/Kaiser" title="Kaiser">Kaiser</a>.</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">Leader</a> – From Old English <i>lædan</i>, meaning "to guide", derived from Old Norse and Germanic. The head of state of <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> is titled Great Leader. The <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> head of state of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> is titled <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Leader" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Leader">Supreme Leader</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chieftain" class="mw-redirect" title="Chieftain">Chief</a> - A variation of the English "Prince", used as the short form of the word "Chieftain" (except for in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, where "Chieftain" is a title held by a titleholder subordinate to a chief). Generally used to refer to a recognised leader within a <a href="/wiki/Chieftaincy" class="mw-redirect" title="Chieftaincy">chieftaincy</a> system. From this come the variations <a href="/wiki/Paramount_chief" title="Paramount chief">paramount chief</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clan_chief" class="mw-redirect" title="Clan chief">clan chief</a> and <a href="/wiki/Village_headman" class="mw-redirect" title="Village headman">village chief</a>. The feminine equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Chieftain" class="mw-redirect" title="Chieftain">Chieftess</a>.</li></ul>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Popess" class="mw-redirect" title="Popess">Popess</a><br />The title of a character found in <a href="/wiki/Tarot_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="Tarot cards">Tarot cards</a> based upon the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> on the Roman Catholic Church. As the Bishop of Rome is an office always forbidden to women there is no formal feminine of Pope, which comes from the Latin word <i>papa</i> (an affectionate form of the Latin for <i>father</i>). <br />The mythical <a href="/wiki/Pope_Joan" title="Pope Joan">Pope Joan</a>, who was reportedly a woman, is always referred to with the masculine title <i>Pope</i>, even when her female identity is known. Further, even if a woman were to become Bishop of Rome it is unclear if she would take the title <i>Popess</i>. A parallel might be drawn with the Anglican Communion, whose female clergy use the masculine titles of <i>priest</i> and <i>bishop</i> as opposed to <i>priestess</i> or <i>bishopess</i>.<br />Nonetheless some European languages, along with English, have formed a feminine form of the word <i>pope</i>, such as the Italian <i>papessa</i>, the French <i>papesse</i>, the Portuguese <i>papisa</i>, and the German <i>Päpstin</i>.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Titles_used_by_knights.2C_dames.2C_baronets_and_baronetesses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_used_by_knights,_dames,_baronets_and_baronetesses">Titles used by knights, dames, baronets and baronetesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Titles used by knights, dames, baronets and baronetesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These do not belong to the nobility.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">Sir</a> – Used by <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baronets" class="mw-redirect" title="Baronets">baronets</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dame_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame (title)">Dame</a> – Used by dames and <a href="/wiki/Baronetess" class="mw-redirect" title="Baronetess">baronetesses</a></li></ul>
<p>"Sir" and "Dame" differ from titles such as "Mr" and "Mrs" in that they can only be used before a person's first name, and not immediately before their surname.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judicial_titles">Judicial titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judicial titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The names of shipboard officers, certain shipping line employees and <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime Academy">Maritime Academy</a> faculty/staff are preceded by their title when acting in performance of their duties.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_professional_titles">Protected professional titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Title&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Protected professional titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In North America, several jurisdictions restrict the use of some professional titles to those individuals holding a valid and recognised license to practice. Individuals not authorised to use these reserved titles may be fined or jailed.  Protected titles are often reserved to those professions that require a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or higher and a state, provincial, or national license.
