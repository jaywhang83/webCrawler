	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">-onym</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> <i><b>-onym</b></i>, in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and other languages, means "word, name", and <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a> ending in <i>-onym</i> refer to a specified kind of name or word, most of which are <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">classical compounds</a>. For example, an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> is a word formed from the initial letter or letters of each of the successive parts or major parts of a compound term (as <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>). The use of <i>-onym</i> words provides a means of classifying, often to a fine degree of resolution, sets of nouns with common attributes.
</p><p>In some words, the <i>-onym</i> form has been modified by replacing (or dropping) the "o". In the examples <i><a href="/wiki/Ananym" title="Ananym">ananym</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metanym&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Metanym (page does not exist)">metanym</a></i>, the correct forms (<i><a href="/wiki/Anonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Anonym">anonym</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Metonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Metonym">metonym</a></i>) were pre-occupied by other meanings. Other, late 20th century examples, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Hypernym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypernym">hypernym</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Characternym&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Characternym (page does not exist)">characternym</a></i>, are typically incorrectly formed <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologisms</a> for which there are more traditional words formed in <i>-onym</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Hyperonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyperonym">hyperonym</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Charactonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Charactonym">charactonym</a></i>).
</p><p>The English suffix <i>-onym</i> is from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> suffix <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">-ώνυμον</span> (<i>ōnymon</i>), neuter of the suffix <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ώνυμος</span> (<i>ōnymos</i>), having a specified kind of name, from the Greek <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ὄνομα</span> (<i>ónoma</i>), <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">Aeolic Greek</a> ὄνυμα (<i>ónyma</i>), "name". The form <i>-ōnymos</i> is that taken by <i>ónoma</i> when it is the end component of a <a href="/wiki/Bahuvrihi" title="Bahuvrihi">bahuvrihi</a> compound, but in English its use is extended to <a href="/wiki/Tatpurusa" class="mw-redirect" title="Tatpurusa">tatpurusa</a> compounds.
</p><p>The suffix is found in many modern languages with various spellings. Examples are: <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>synoniem</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Synonym</i>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> <i>sinónimo</i>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> синоним (<i>sinonim</i>), <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> <i>synonim</i>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> <i>synonyymi</i>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> <i>sinonim</i>.
</p><p>According to a 1988 study<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> of words ending in <i>-onym</i>, there are four discernible classes of <i>-onym</i> words: (1) historic, classic, or, for want of better terms, naturally occurring or common words; (2) scientific terminology, occurring in particular in linguistics, onomastics, etc.; (3) language games; and (4) <a href="/wiki/Nonce_word" title="Nonce word">nonce words</a>. Older terms are known to gain new, sometimes contradictory, meanings (e.g., <i>eponym</i> and <i>cryptonym</i>). In many cases, two or more words describe the same phenomenon, but no precedence is discernible (e.g., <i>necronym</i> and <i>penthonym</i>). New words are sometimes created, the meaning of which duplicating existing terms. On occasion, new words are formed with little regard to historical principles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Words_that_end_in_-onym">Words that end in -onym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=-onym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Words that end in -onym">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Onomastics" title="Onomastics">Onomastics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature" title="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</a></div>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a></b>: considered to be a "word" in its original sense formed from the initials of one or more words that is pronounceable like a normal word, such as <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, sometimes in distinction to <a href="/wiki/Initialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Initialism">initialism</a>; reflecting a historical development from its component word initials</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Allonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Allonym">allonym</a></b>: an author's name of another person's, often a well-known person's, name; an alternative term for a <b>pseudonym</b></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anacronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Anacronym">anacronym</a></b>: an acronym so well-established that its origin as an abbreviation is no longer widely known and its component initials are in danger of no longer being recognized (a blend of <a href="/wiki/Anachronism" title="Anachronism">anachronism</a> and acronym)</li>
<li><b>andronym</b>: a man's name or a word derived from a man's name; the name of the husband, taken on by the wife; a male name adopted by a woman</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Anonym">anonym</a></b>: something created anonymously, or its creator; an unknown author; this term now generally replaced by pseudonym</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" class="mw-redirect" title="Genericized trademark">anepronym</a></b>: a portmanteau of anacronym and <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a>; an original eponym of a trademark term that becomes so well established that it is used to define other objects that share its own definition (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Anthroponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthroponym">anthroponym</a></b>: a name of a human being; as reflected in surnames or proper names of people</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Antonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Antonym">antonym</a></b>: a word with the exact opposite meaning of another word; an <a href="/wiki/Antithesis" title="Antithesis">antithesis</a>: often shown in opposite word pairs such as "high" and "low" (compare with "synonym")</li>
<li><b>apronym</b>: a word which, as an acronym or backronym, has a meaning related to the meaning of the words constituting the acronym or backronym; such as <a href="/wiki/PLATO_(computer_system)" title="PLATO (computer system)">PLATO</a> for "Programmed Logic for Automated Teaching" alluding to <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, the philosopher and teacher</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aptronym" title="Aptronym">aptronym</a></b>: a name appropriate to its owner's <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">occupation</a> or physical properties, such as "Goldsmith" or "Longman" (compare with "charactonym") — coined by Franklin P. Adams</li>
<li><b>astronym</b>: a name of a star (or more loosely of a constellation or other heavenly body)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Autonym_(botany)" title="Autonym (botany)">autonym</a></b>: Botanical nomenclature for an automatically created name</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Backronym" title="Backronym">backronym</a></b>: an ordinary word understood as an (usually amusing or ironic) acronym (a portmanteau of back + acronym), such as <a href="/wiki/Fiat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiat">Fiat</a> understood as "Fix It Again Tomorrow"</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Basionym" title="Basionym">basionym</a></b>: the first name published for a biological taxon (species, genus, etc.), which remains the defining name for the taxon even when the taxon has been transferred to a new name</li>
<li><b>caconym</b>: a bad name, either from poor formation (as through mixing Greek and Latin) or unpleasantness (as through lengthiness or cacophony)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Capitonym" title="Capitonym">capitonym</a></b>: a word that changes its meaning (and sometimes pronunciation) when it is capitalized, such as March and march or Polish and polish.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Charactonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Charactonym">charactonym</a></b>: a name of a fictional character reflected in his personality traits, such as Shakespeare's <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Pistol" title="Ancient Pistol">Pistol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bottom" title="Nick Bottom">Bottom</a> (compare with "aptronym")</li>
<li><b>chrematonym</b>: a name of a politico-economic or commercial or cultural institution or thing; a catch-all category</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Chresonym" title="Chresonym">chresonym</a></b>: Biol. use of a taxonomic name; historically sometimes referred to as a synonym. Sometimes divided into orthochresonyms (correct usages) and heterochresonyms (names incorrectly applied).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Contronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Contronym">contronym</a></b> or <b>antagonym</b> or <b>autoantonym</b>: a word that may have opposite meanings in different contexts, such as <i>cleave</i> meaning <i>"stick together"</i> or <i>"split apart"</i></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cryptonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryptonym">cryptonym</a></b>: a <a href="/wiki/Code_name" title="Code name">code name</a>; a word or name used clandestinely to refer to another name or word</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a></b>: a name, derived from a place name, for residents of that place (e.g., <i>Utahn</i>, from Utah, or <i>Sioux Cityan</i>, from Sioux City) — coined by George H. Scheetz, according to Paul Dickson in <i>What Do You Call a Person From...? A Dictionary of Resident Names</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The term first appeared in print in 1988 in <i>Names' Names: A Descriptive and Prescriptive Onymicon</i> by George H. Scheetz.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Taxonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxonym">taxonym</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Endonym">endonym</a></b>: A self-assigned name by locals of a place. Also known as an <b>autonym</b> (not to be confused with the autonym in botany).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponym</a></b>: a botanical, zoological, artwork, or place name that derives from a real or legendary person; a name for a real or hypothetical person from whom a botanical, geographical, artwork or zoological name is derived; a person after whom a <a href="/wiki/Medical" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical">medical</a> condition is named, or the condition so named. A type of taxonym.</li>
<li><b>ergonym</b>: sometimes used for the name of an institution or commercial firm</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">ethnonym</a></b>: a name of an ethnic group. A type of taxonym.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Exonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym">exonym</a></b>: a name used by one group of people for another group, but who call themselves by a different name, such as "Germans" for "Deutsche"; a place name used by one group that differs from the name used by the people who live there, such as "<a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>" for "<i>Köln</i>"</li>
<li><b>gamonym</b>: a name bestowed as a consequence of marriage. Judy Jones married Count Stephen Smith: her gamonyms include Mrs. Stephen Smith, Judy Smith, and Countess Smith.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Geonym" title="Geonym">geonym</a></b>: a name of a geographic feature</li>
<li><span id="glottonym"></span><b>glossonym</b> <i>or</i> <b>glottonym</b>: a name of a language</li>
<li><b>heterochresonym</b>: (biological taxonomy) see chresonym.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Heteronym_(linguistics)" title="Heteronym (linguistics)">heteronym</a></b>: a word that is spelled in the same way as another but that has a different sound and meaning, for example "bow" as in "bow of a ship" or "bow and arrow" (compare "homonym")</li>
<li><b>hodonym</b>: a name of a street or road (also <a href="/wiki/Odonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Odonym">odonym</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Holonymy" title="Holonymy">holonym</a></b>: a word for the whole of which other words are part, in the way <i>house</i> contains <i>roof</i>, <i>door</i> and <i>window</i>; or <i>car</i> contains <i>steering-wheel</i> and <i>engine</i> (compare "meronym")</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonym</a></b>: 1: a: a word pronounced like another, but differing in meaning or derivation or spelling—also known as homophone (to, too, two). b: a word spelled like another, but differing in derivation or meaning or pronunciation—also known as homograph or heteronym (lead, to conduct, and lead, the metal). Compare autantonym, contronym, and heteronym. c: a word spelled and pronounced like another, but differing in meaning (pool of water, and pool, the game). 2: a namesake. 3: Biol. a taxonomic designation that is identical to another one of the same rank, but based on a different type; only one of the homonyms is considered a valid designation (see <a href="/wiki/Homonym_(biology)" title="Homonym (biology)">homonym (biology)</a>). Compare to synonym.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hydronym" title="Hydronym">hydronym</a></b>: a name of river, lake, or other body of water</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hypernym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypernym">hypernym</a></b>: sometimes spelled hyperonym, a generic word that stands for a class or group of equally ranked items, such as "tree" for "beech" or "elm," or "house" for "chalet" or "bungalow." A hypernym is said to be "superordinate" to a hyponym.</li>
<li><b>hypocoronym</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Hypocorism" title="Hypocorism">hypocorism</a></b>, or <b>hypocoristic</b>: a colloquial, usually unofficial, name of an entity; a pet-name or "nickname"</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hyponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyponym">hyponym</a></b>: an item that belongs to and is equally ranked in a generic class or group, for example "lily" or "violet" in the class of "flowers"; or "limousine" or "hatchback" in the class of "automobiles." A hyponym is said to be "subordinate" to a hyperonym.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Isonym" title="Isonym">isonym</a></b>: 1: a word having the same root or stem as another — also known as paronym. Compare exonym, heteronym, paronym, and synonym. 2: one person's surname that is the same as another person's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 3: Biol. a taxonomic designation that is identical to another designation, and based on the same type, but published at a different time by the same or other authors (see <a href="/wiki/Isonym_(taxonomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isonym (taxonomy)">isonym (taxonomy)</a>).</li>
<li><b><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/linguonym" class="extiw" title="wikt:linguonym">linguonym</a></b>: a name of a language</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Meronymy" title="Meronymy">meronym</a></b>: a word that names a part that belongs to and is therefore subordinate to a larger entity; a part-whole relationship, such as "door" or "window" in "house", or "engine" or "steering-wheel" in "car" (compare "holonym")</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Metonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Metonym">metonym</a></b>: a word that substitutes a part for the whole it is associated with, for example "crown" for "monarch"; <a href="/wiki/Metonymy" title="Metonymy">metonymy</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_speech" title="Figure of speech">figure of speech</a> incorporating a metonym</li>
<li><b>matronym</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a></b>: a name of a human being making reference to that person's mother (contrast "patronym")</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">mononym</a></b>: a word indicating the "single name" as generally applied to people e.g. Madonna or Plato.</li>
<li><b>microtoponym</b>: a word indicating an uninhabited place cf. <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Necronym" title="Necronym">necronym</a></b>: a reference to or name of a person who has died.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Numeronym" title="Numeronym">numeronym</a></b>: is a number-based word.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Odonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Odonym">odonym</a></b>: a name of a street or road (also <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hodonym&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hodonym (page does not exist)">hodonym</a>).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Oeconym" title="Oeconym">oikonym</a></b> or (Latinized) <b><a href="/wiki/Oeconym" title="Oeconym">oeconym</a></b>: a name of a house or other building</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Oronym_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Oronym (disambiguation)">oronym</a></b>: 1: a name of a hill, mountain, or mountain-range; 2: a neologism for homophonic words or phrases.</li>
<li><b>orthochresonym</b>: (biological taxonomy) see chresonym.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Paedonymic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paedonymic">paedonymic</a></b>: a name adopted from one's child's name, as in the <a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">kunya</a> of <a href="/wiki/Islamic_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic name">Islamic names</a> or when one is identified by means of one's child's name ("Tim's dad").<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Paronym" title="Paronym">paronym</a></b>: a word that is related to another word and derives from the same root; a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> word, such as <i>dubious</i> and <i>doubtful</i></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronym</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a></b>: a name adopted from the father's or ancestor's name, for example "Johnson" from "John," "MacDonald" from "Donald," "O'Brien" from "Brien," or "Ivanovich" from "Ivan"</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Phantonym" title="Phantonym">Phantonym</a></b>: a word that looks like it would mean one thing, when in reality it means something completely different. Such as "noisome" meaning "smelly" or "unhealthy" and not "noisy".</li>
<li><b><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phytonym" class="extiw" title="wikt:phytonym">phytonym</a></b>: a name of a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plesionyms&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plesionyms (page does not exist)"><b>plesionym</b></a> or <b><a href="/w/index.php?title=Near-synonyms&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Near-synonyms (page does not exist)">near-synonym</a></b>: word that is almost synonymous with another word, but not quite</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a></b>: a false and fictitious name, especially one adopted by an author; a <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a></b>: a compound or modified noun that replaces an original simple noun, for example "analog watch" now means what "watch" used to mean before the invention of the digital watch; and motorcycles became "solo motorcycles" when others were built with sidecars</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a></b>: 1: a word equivalent in meaning or nearly so to another word; a word that may be substituted for another word that has the same or a similar meaning, such as <i>near</i> and <i>close</i> (compare "antonym"). 2: In Biology, one or more names given to the same taxon, and so considered equivalent. Usually only one of them in considered as correct (senior synonym in animal taxonomy, accepted name in plant taxonomy), while the other are considered deprecated (see <a href="/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)" title="Synonym (taxonomy)">synonym (taxonomy)</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tautonym" title="Tautonym">tautonym</a></b>: a <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scientific_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific name">scientific name</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> of living things in which the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">generic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">specific</a> names are the same, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">Gorilla</a> gorilla</i>; a scientific name in which the specific name is repeated, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">Homo sapiens sapiens</a></i> as distinct from <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens_neanderthalensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Homo sapiens neanderthalensis">Homo sapiens neanderthalensis</a></i>; a noun component that is repeated, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Aye-aye" title="Aye-aye">aye-aye</a></i> or <i>tom-tom</i>; a <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a> where both <a href="/wiki/First_name" class="mw-redirect" title="First name">forename</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> are identical, such as <i>Francis Francis</i></li>
<li><b>taxonym</b>: a name used for classification or identification purposes, usually signifying a relationship to something. Taxonyms include <a href="/wiki/Binomial_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Binomial name">binomens</a>, names of <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> or <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Demonyms">demonyms</a>, ethnonyms, and <a href="/wiki/Eponyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Eponyms">eponyms</a>. Examples include <i><a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">canine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">hominid</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Dryad" title="Dryad">Dryad</a></i>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Textonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Textonym">textonym</a></b>: a word that is generated by a single sequence of numerals keyed in to a mobile telephone; for example, <i>726</i> produces <i>pam</i>, <i>ram</i>, <i>sam</i>, and <i>ran</i>. Also called homonumeric words.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Theonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Theonym">theonym</a></b>: a name of a god. The names societies give their gods at times is useful in understanding the origin of their language as well as their view of a particular deity. Analysis of theonyms has been useful in understanding the connections of <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a>, and possibly their religions, in particular. In <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_faiths" class="mw-redirect" title="Abrahamic faiths">Abrahamic faiths</a> the origin and meaning of the <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Tetragrammaton</a> is sometimes deemed to have important historical or even metaphysical meaning.</li>
<li><b>theronym</b>: a name — especially a product name — that has been derived from the name of an animal.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a></b>: a <a href="/wiki/Place_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Place name">place</a> or <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> name; the name of an area of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body</a>, as distinguished from the name of an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Troponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Troponym">troponym</a></b>: a verb conveying a meaning that is a particular case of the meaning of another verb. For example, <i>to duel</i> is a troponym of <i>to fight</i>; <i>to write</i> is a troponym of <i>to communicate</i>; etc.  The concept of <i>troponym</i> is to verbs as that of <i>hyponym</i> is to nouns.</li>
<li><b>zoonym</b>: a name of an animal</li></ul>
